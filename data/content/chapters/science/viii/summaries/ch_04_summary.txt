Chapter 4: Electricity: Magnetic and Heating Effects

Section 4.1 opens with a contextual story of students observing a lifting electromagnet at a science exhibition — an iron nail wrapped with wire and connected to a battery picks up iron clips when the circuit is closed, and releases them when the circuit is opened. This motivates the central question of whether electric current has a magnetic effect.

4.1 Does an Electric Current Have a Magnetic Effect?

This section investigates the magnetic effect of electric current through a student activity (Activity 4.1) in which a magnetic compass is placed under a current-carrying wire. Students observe that when the switch is closed and current flows, the compass needle deflects; when the switch is opened, the needle returns to its original position. The section introduces the term magnetic field as the region around a magnet or current-carrying wire where magnetic effects can be detected. The key concept established is the magnetic effect of electric current: a current-carrying conductor produces a magnetic field around it, which disappears when the current stops. The section contains a "Be a scientist" sidebar about Hans Christian Oersted (1777–1851), who first discovered the link between electricity and magnetism in 1820. The section also notes that this magnetic effect has practical applications in devices such as electromagnets, electric bells, motors, fans, and loudspeakers. There is a student activity present.

4.1.1 Electromagnets

This subsection builds on the magnetic effect to introduce electromagnets. Activity 4.2 ("Let us explore") has students wrap an insulated wire around an iron nail, connect it to a cell, and test whether the nail attracts iron paper clips; clips cling when current flows and fall off when current is stopped. Activity 4.3 ("Let us experiment") extends this by forming a cylindrical coil on a paper cylinder, placing magnetic compasses at both ends, and inserting an iron nail into the core. Students observe that the coil with current behaves like a magnet and that inserting the iron nail increases the magnetic strength significantly. The section defines an electromagnet as a current-carrying coil that behaves as a magnet, noting that practical electromagnets have an iron core for greater strength. Activity 4.4 ("Let us investigate") has students identify the poles of the electromagnet using a compass, establishing that an electromagnet has a North pole and a South pole, just like a bar magnet. A "Think like a scientist" box guides students to vary the number of cells and the number of turns to discover that electromagnet strength depends on the magnitude of current and the number of coil turns, and that its poles can be reversed by reversing current direction. A "A step further" sidebar explains Earth's magnetic field as arising from movement of liquid iron in Earth's core, linking the chapter's physics to a real-world natural phenomenon. There are multiple student activities and one teacher-guided investigation present.

4.1.2 Lifting Electromagnets

This short subsection connects the electromagnet concept to an industrial application. It describes lifting electromagnets mounted on cranes that are used in factories and scrap yards to move, lift, and sort heavy metal objects. The crane operator controls the magnet by switching the current on and off — when current is on, the electromagnet holds objects; when current is off, the magnetic field disappears and objects are released. A "step further" sidebar notes that in higher grades students will learn about electromagnetic induction — that just as electricity produces magnetism, a moving magnet can produce an electric current. There is no student activity in this subsection.

4.2 Does a Current Carrying Wire Get Hot?

This section introduces the heating effect of electric current. Activity 4.5 ("Let us observe") uses a nichrome wire connected in a circuit: students touch the wire before and after current flows to observe that it becomes warm when current passes through it. The section explains the mechanism: when current flows through a conductor, the conductor offers resistance to the flow, and this resistance converts some electrical energy into heat energy. The term heating effect of electric current is introduced to describe this phenomenon. A "Think like a scientist" box has students compare heating with one cell versus two cells, leading to the conclusion that the amount of heat generated depends on the magnitude of electric current, as well as the material, thickness, length of the wire, and duration of current flow. The section connects to everyday appliances — electric room heaters, stoves, kettles, irons, immersion rods, and hair dryers — all of which use a heating element (a rod or coil of wire). The section also notes the dual nature of the heating effect: it is useful in appliances but can cause problems such as energy loss during transmission, overheating, melting of plastic parts, or fires. Industrial applications such as electric furnaces for melting scrap steel are mentioned in a sidebar. There is a student activity present.

4.3 How Does a Battery Generate Electricity?

This section shifts focus from the effects of electric current to the source of electric current in portable form. It opens the question of what is inside cells and batteries that produces electricity.

4.3.1 Voltaic Cell

This subsection describes the Voltaic cell (also called Galvanic cell), one of the earliest electric cells. It consists of two metal electrodes (rods of different materials) partially immersed in a liquid electrolyte — typically a weak acid or salt solution — held in a glass or plastic container. A chemical reaction between the electrodes and the electrolyte produces electricity. When the circuit is connected, current flows from the positive terminal through the circuit to the negative terminal. Over time the chemicals are consumed and the cell becomes "dead." An "Ever heard of" sidebar narrates the historical discovery by Alessandro Volta and Luigi Galvani in the late 1700s, including Galvani's observation that a dead frog's leg twitched when touched with two different metals and Volta's counter-experiment using saltwater-soaked paper to prove it was the metal-liquid combination — not the frog — that generated current. Activity 4.6 ("Let us construct") has students build a Voltaic cell using lemons, copper wires, and iron nails to power a LED, reinforcing the concept that lemon juice acts as the electrolyte. There is a student activity present.

4.3.2 Dry Cells

This subsection describes the dry cell as the most widely used type of electric cell today. Unlike the Voltaic cell, the electrolyte in a dry cell is not a liquid but a thick moist paste, making it more portable and practical. The structure consists of a zinc container (negative terminal), a central carbon rod with a metal cap (positive terminal), and the paste-like electrolyte surrounding the carbon rod. The dry cell is a single-use cell — once the chemicals are used up it must be disposed of. The subsection notes that rechargeable batteries are increasingly replacing single-use cells for many applications. There is no student activity in this subsection.

4.3.3 Rechargeable Batteries

This subsection introduces rechargeable batteries, which can be recharged and reused multiple times, preventing waste and reducing cost over time. Different types are mentioned — from small batteries in watches and phones to larger ones in laptops, inverters, and electric vehicles. The section notes that even rechargeable batteries wear out after many charge-discharge cycles. A "step further" sidebar focuses on lithium-ion (Li-ion) batteries as the most common type today, mentions resource scarcity concerns around lithium and cobalt, and introduces solid-state batteries as a future technology promising greater safety and longer life. A safety sidebar notes that "dead" batteries are not completely harmless and should be disposed of at e-waste recycling facilities, as they may contain acids and toxic metals. There is no student activity in this subsection.
