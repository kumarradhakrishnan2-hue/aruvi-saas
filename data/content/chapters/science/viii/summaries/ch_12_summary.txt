Chapter 12: How Nature Works in Harmony

Section 12.1 introduces the concept of a habitat as the specific place where an organism lives and describes how every habitat contains both living organisms (biotic components) and non-living things (abiotic components) such as air, sunlight, water, temperature, and soil. Using two contrasting habitats — a pond and a forest — the section illustrates that organisms in each habitat interact with both living and non-living elements to survive. Different organisms may coexist in the same habitat while using its resources in different ways, such as a nocturnal snake and a diurnal rodent sharing a forest. The section contains a student activity (Activity 12.1) that asks students to explore two nearby habitats and record their biotic and abiotic components in a table.

Section 12.2 introduces the concept of a population as a group of organisms of the same kind living together in a habitat at a given time. The section explains that a single habitat typically hosts multiple populations of different organisms. It also introduces the concept of a community as an assemblage of different populations sharing the same habitat, including plants, animals, and microorganisms, all of which interact and depend on one another. The section contains a student activity (Activity 12.2) in which students mark a 1 m × 1 m area in the school garden and count the number of individuals of different plant and animal species present.

Section 12.3 explores the role of individual organisms within a community through a reading activity based on a real research study. The study compared two ponds — one with fish and one without — and traced the indirect effects on dragonfly populations, pollinator populations, and ultimately on seed production in nearby flowering plants. Fish ate dragonfly larvae, fewer dragonflies meant more bees and butterflies, and more pollinators led to greater seed production near the pond with fish. The section uses this example to establish how both biotic and abiotic components interact and affect each other across an entire system. The section contains a student activity (Activity 12.3) that asks students to read the study and analyse the cascading interactions.

Section 12.4 defines an ecosystem as the system formed when biotic components (plants, animals, and microorganisms) and abiotic components (air, water, soil, sunlight, and temperature) in a habitat interact with each other. The section distinguishes between interactions within biotic components, between biotic and abiotic components, and between two abiotic components. It introduces the two broad types of ecosystems: aquatic (ponds, rivers, lakes) and terrestrial (forests, farms, large trees). It also notes that farmland is a human-made ecosystem and that ecosystems can be large or small and may overlap. The section emphasises that abiotic components depend on biotic components as well — for example, plants release oxygen and their roots prevent soil erosion. The section contains a student activity (Activity 12.4) that asks students to classify interactions from a diagram of a pond habitat according to three criteria: biotic-abiotic, abiotic-abiotic, and biotic-biotic.

Section 12.5 introduces the feeding relationships among organisms in an ecosystem. It distinguishes producers (autotrophs, such as plants that make food through photosynthesis) from consumers (heterotrophs that depend on other organisms for food). Consumers are further classified as herbivores (eating only plants), carnivores (eating only animals), and omnivores (eating both). The section introduces the concept of a food chain as a linear sequence showing who eats whom, and the concept of trophic levels — the position each organism occupies in a food chain. It further introduces the food web as a network of interconnected food chains in an ecosystem, recognising that most organisms are part of multiple feeding relationships. The section contains two student activities: Activity 12.5 asks students to classify organisms from a forest ecosystem image by their eating habits, and Activity 12.6 asks students to draw feeding relationships among grassland organisms including grass, grasshopper, frog, snake, and eagle. Activity 12.7 asks students to construct a trophic pyramid from a crop field food chain, and Activity 12.8 asks students to complete a food web diagram by adding arrows for missing feeding relationships.

Section 12.6 introduces decomposers (also called saprotrophs) as microorganisms — particularly fungi and bacteria — that break down complex substances in dead plants and animals into simpler ones, returning nutrients to the soil. The section explains that this process, called decomposition, is essential for nutrient recycling, noting that nothing is truly wasted in nature. It cites examples such as mushrooms growing on dead trees during the rainy season and beetles and flies breaking down animal dung.

Section 12.7 explains how a single change in an ecosystem can trigger a cascade of consequences throughout the system. Using the example of plant death due to pond pollution, the section traces a chain: fewer plants → less oxygen → fish die → fewer consumers → insect populations rise → insects spread to farmland → farmers use pesticides → further environmental harm. A reading activity (Activity 12.9) uses the real historical case of India's large-scale export of frog legs in the 1980s, which reduced frog populations, increased agricultural pests, forced use of synthetic pesticides, and ultimately led the Government of India to ban the export of frog legs to prevent further ecological damage.

Section 12.8 explains that beyond feeding relationships, organisms also compete for shared resources such as food, water, space, and sunlight, and that this competition helps regulate population size and maintain ecosystem balance. The section introduces three additional types of inter-organism relationships: mutualism (both organisms benefit, e.g., honeybees and flowers), commensalism (one organism benefits while the other is unaffected, e.g., orchids on tree branches), and parasitism (one organism benefits while the other is harmed, e.g., ticks on dogs). It notes that ecosystem balance is dynamic rather than fixed, and can be disrupted by natural or human-made changes.

Section 12.9 addresses the benefits that ecosystems provide to humans, including fresh air, fertile soil, food, fibres, timber, medicines, water, and aesthetic and recreational value. It uses the Sundarbans — the world's largest mangrove forest, declared a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1987 — as a case study of a threatened ecosystem, describing how deforestation, illegal hunting, and industrial pollution are disrupting its natural function. The section notes that similar threats — deforestation, invasive species, unsustainable land use, and pollution — affect other ecosystems across India, including forests, rivers, wetlands, and grasslands.

Section 12.9.1 covers human-made ecosystems such as fish ponds, farms, and parks, noting that these can support biodiversity and reduce pollution when well designed, but unlike natural ecosystems they require ongoing human care and management.

Section 12.9.2 examines how farming practices interact with ecosystem health. The section traces the history of the Green Revolution in India — the mid-20th century adoption of tractors, synthetic fertilisers, and pesticides that raised food production — and identifies its long-term ecological costs, including soil degradation, loss of soil microorganisms and humus, increased pest resistance, and reduction of pollinators through monoculture. The section introduces organic and natural farming as alternatives that aim to reduce synthetic chemical use and support sustainable agriculture with minimal interference in natural ecosystems. The section contains a student survey activity (Activity 12.10) in which students interact with local farmers to learn about their farming practices and record observations on synthetic fertiliser and pesticide use.
