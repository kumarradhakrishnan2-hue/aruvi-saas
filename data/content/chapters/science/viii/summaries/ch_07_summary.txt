Chapter 7: Particulate Nature of Matter

Section 7.1 introduces the central question of what matter is made up of. Through Activity 7.1 (breaking and grinding chalk), students explore whether matter can be subdivided indefinitely. The activity establishes that even after grinding chalk to fine powder, each speck remains chalk — a physical change only. The key term introduced is constituent particle: the basic indivisible unit that makes up a larger piece of a substance. Activity 7.2 (dissolving sugar in water) reinforces this idea. After stirring, sugar particles become invisible yet their presence is confirmed by taste; the chapter explains that sugar breaks into its constituent particles, which occupy the interparticle spaces between water molecules. These two activities together establish the foundational idea that all matter is composed of an extremely large number of very small particles invisible even under an ordinary microscope.

Section 7.2 addresses what determines the physical state of matter. The chapter explains that constituent particles are held together by interparticle attractions — forces that are attractive in nature. The strength of these attractions depends on the nature of the substance and the interparticle distance, with even a slight increase in distance causing a drastic weakening of the force. A sidebar, "Our Scientific Heritage," notes that Acharya Kanad, the ancient Indian philosopher, first proposed the concept of Parmanu (atom) — tiny, indivisible, eternal particles — in his work Vaisheshika Sutras.

Subsection 7.2.1 covers the solid state. Activity 7.3 (collecting and hammering solid objects — iron nail, rock salt, stone, wood, key, aluminium) leads to the observation that all have a definite shape and volume. This is because particles in solids are tightly packed with very strong interparticle attractions that hold them in fixed positions; particles can only vibrate about their positions and cannot move past each other. When heated, vibrations intensify until the particles abandon their fixed positions and the interparticle forces weaken — this is the melting point, defined as the minimum temperature at which a solid becomes a liquid at atmospheric pressure. A data table gives melting points of ice (0°C), urea (133°C), and iron (1538°C) to illustrate how the strength of interparticle forces varies across substances.

Subsection 7.2.2 examines the liquid state. Activity 7.4 (transferring 200 mL of water across three differently shaped containers A, B, C) demonstrates that liquids take the shape of their container but maintain a fixed volume. Liquids' particles are free to move but only within a limited space, making interparticle attractions slightly weaker than in solids. The section defines boiling point as the temperature at which a liquid converts to vapour at atmospheric pressure; at this point, particles have enough energy to escape the liquid entirely, and bubble formation occurs throughout the bulk. Evaporation — which occurs at all temperatures at the surface — is distinguished as a slower process occurring even below the boiling point.

Subsection 7.2.3 explores the gaseous state. Activity 7.5 (collecting smoke from a burning incense stick in Gas Jar A, then allowing it to spread into an inverted Gas Jar B) shows that gas fills all available space. This demonstrates that gas particles move freely in all directions, interparticle attractions are negligible, and gases have neither a fixed shape nor a fixed volume. An alternative demonstration using iodine vapour (solid iodine subliming inside a closed jar) is mentioned. The section concludes by classifying both liquids and gases as fluids because both flow and do not retain a fixed shape.

Section 7.3 investigates how interparticle spacing differs across the three states. Activity 7.6 (syringe experiment: compressing air by pushing a plunger, then repeating with water) establishes that gases are highly compressible because their particles have large spaces between them, while water is practically incompressible. Activity 7.7 (adding sugar to water, marking the water level before and after dissolution) shows that the solution volume is less than the sum of the individual volumes, proving that interparticle spaces exist in liquids and that dissolved particles fit into those spaces. Repeating with insoluble sand confirms the contrast: sand particles settle and add volume rather than dissolving. A summary diagram (Fig. 7.12) illustrates particle packing in the three states: minimum spacing in solids, moderate spacing in liquids, and maximum spacing in gases. A "A Step Further" sidebar clarifies that the term "particle" in Suspended Particulate Matter (SPM, referring to dust in air pollution) is different in scale from constituent particles like atoms and molecules.

Section 7.4 examines how particles move in different states. Activity 7.8 (dropping potassium permanganate into water) shows the spread of pink colour from the grain eventually reaching the entire tumbler, demonstrating that liquid particles are in constant motion. A "Think Like a Scientist" box extends this: repeating the activity with hot, room-temperature, and ice-cold water shows that particle movement — and therefore rate of spreading — increases with temperature. Activity 7.9 (burning an incense stick in one corner of a room) demonstrates that fragrance spreads throughout the space, providing evidence that gas particles are in constant, rapid, random motion that carries scent particles in all directions. The chapter closes with a synthesis paragraph explaining that thermal energy is the underlying driver: low thermal energy in solids means strong interparticle attraction and only vibration; at the melting point thermal energy overcomes attractions allowing liquid-state movement; in the gaseous state thermal energy is sufficient to overcome attractions entirely, enabling free movement.
