Chapter 11: Keeping Time with the Skies

11.1 How Does the Moon's Appearance Change and Why?

The chapter opens with a student noticing the Moon visible during the daytime at a kite festival, which prompts inquiry into why the Moon's visible shape changes each night. The section sets up a sustained observation activity (Activity 11.1) in which students track the Moon over a full month, documenting its appearance, the size of the bright portion, and its angular separation from the Sun each day. Students are directed to begin this observation after a full Moon day and to switch from sunrise to sunset viewing midway through the cycle.

11.1.1 Phases of the Moon

This subsection introduces the terminology and cycle of lunar phases. Over roughly two weeks, the bright portion of the Moon decreases from a full circle to a half circle to invisibility — this is the waning period, called Krishna Paksha in India. The Moon then brightens over the next two weeks through the waxing period (Shukla Paksha), returning to a full Moon. The specific shapes encountered during the cycle are named: full Moon (Purnima), new Moon (Amavasya), gibbous (more than half illuminated), and crescent (less than half illuminated). The complete cycle from one full Moon to the next takes about one month. The key definition introduced is that the changing shapes of the bright portion of the Moon as seen from Earth are called the phases of the Moon.

11.1.2 Locating the Moon

This subsection explains how knowing a Moon's phase helps predict where and when to find it in the sky. On a full Moon day, the Moon is nearly opposite the Sun; as the bright portion shrinks during waning, the Moon appears progressively closer to the Sun at sunrise. A half-circle waning Moon is overhead at sunrise; a crescent waning Moon is very close to the Sun. A waxing Moon is easiest to spot at sunset, and a waning Moon at sunrise. The Moon rises about 50 minutes later each day because the Moon advances in its orbit while Earth completes one rotation, and Earth must rotate a little further for the Moon to return to the same apparent position. A student activity is noted (checking moonrise times from the Positional Astronomy Centre website over several consecutive days).

11.1.3 Making sense of our observations

This subsection provides the causal explanation for lunar phases. The Moon does not emit its own light but shines by reflecting sunlight; the hemisphere facing the Sun is always illuminated, while the hemisphere facing away is dark. As the Moon revolves around the Earth, the portion of the illuminated hemisphere visible to an observer on Earth changes continuously, producing the phases. Activity 11.2 models this using a ball on a stick, with a torch representing the Sun and the student's head representing the Earth: rotating with the ball outstretched, the student observes the same progression of illuminated shapes seen in the lunar cycle. The section also clarifies a common misconception — Earth's shadow on the Moon causes a lunar eclipse, not the phases. Lunar eclipses occur only on full Moon days and solar eclipses only on new Moon days, but not every month, because the Moon's orbit is slightly tilted relative to Earth's orbit around the Sun.

11.2 How Did Calendars Come into Existence?

This section explains how three recurring astronomical cycles gave rise to the three fundamental units of calendar time. Earth's rotation on its axis causes the Sun to appear to move across the sky daily; the average time from one solar noon to the next is 24 hours, called the mean solar day. Activity 11.3 has students measure this by tracking the shortest shadow cast by a vertical stick over several days. The Moon's phase cycle of about 29.5 days provides the basis for the month. Earth's revolution around the Sun in approximately 365.25 days, producing one cycle of seasons, defines the solar year. Fig. 11.8 illustrates these three nested cycles as the foundation of the calendar system.

11.2.1 Lunar calendars

Lunar calendars were developed by tracking 12 cycles of Moon phases within a season cycle. A lunar month is about 29.5 days, so a lunar year of 12 months totals approximately 354 days. The key limitation introduced is that the lunar year falls about 11 days short of the solar year, so the seasons gradually drift out of alignment with the lunar months over successive years.

11.2.2 Solar calendars

Solar calendars were created to keep the months synchronised with the seasons, which was important for agriculture. The Gregorian calendar, the most widely used today, is a solar calendar of 365 days, with month lengths adjusted (28, 30, or 31 days) to sum to this total. Because Earth's revolution takes approximately 365.25 days, an extra day is added every four years as a leap year (February gains a 29th day). Fine corrections are needed: century years (e.g. 1700, 1800, 1900) skip the leap year, but every 400 years (e.g. 1600, 2000) the leap year is restored, keeping the calendar accurately aligned with the seasons over long periods. The section also introduces the tropical year (time between successive spring equinoxes, the basis of the Gregorian calendar) and the sidereal year (time for the same stars to rise again at sunset, about 20 minutes longer), which astronomers use to track Earth's orbital position.

11.2.3 Luni-solar calendars

Luni-solar calendars use the Moon's phases to count months but add a corrective intercalary month (Adhika Maasa) every two to three years when the accumulated gap between the lunar year (354 days) and the solar year (365 days) reaches roughly a full month. This keeps the calendar broadly synchronised with the seasons while retaining lunar months as the primary unit. Many Indian regional calendars are luni-solar. The section notes two conventions within luni-solar calendars: Amant (month starts after new Moon, ends at next new Moon) and Purnimant (month starts after full Moon, ends at next full Moon). The names of the twelve Indian lunar months are listed: Chaitra, Vaisakha, Jyeshtha, Ashadha, Shravana, Bhadrapada, Ashwin, Kartika, Margashirsha, Pausha, Magha, and Phalguna. The section also includes a scientific heritage box describing how ancient Indian observers, including the Taittiriya Samhita and the Surya Siddhanta, recorded the Sun's northward (Uttarayan) and southward (Dakshinayan) movement and tracked solstices using background star patterns.

11.2.4 The Indian National Calendar

The Indian National Calendar is a solar calendar established by the Government of India following the recommendations of the Calendar Reform Committee (1952), chaired by astrophysicist Meghnad Saha. It has been in official use since 21 March 1956. The year begins on 22 March (the day after the spring equinox) and consists of 365 days. Months have either 30 or 31 days; the second through sixth months have 31 days and the rest have 30. In leap years (aligned with the Gregorian calendar), a day is added to Chaitra, and the new year begins on 21 March. The calendar's month names are drawn from traditional Indian calendars, and its principles follow the Surya Siddhanta.

11.3 Are Festivals Related to Astronomical Phenomena?

This section connects calendar systems to Indian festivals. Many festivals fall on specific lunar phases: Diwali on the new Moon of Kartika, Holi on the full Moon of Phalguna, Buddha Purnima on the full Moon of Vaisakha, Eid-ul-Fitr after sighting the crescent Moon at the end of Ramazan, and Dussehra on the tenth day of Ashwina. Because these are lunar or luni-solar dates, they fall on different Gregorian calendar dates each year. Festivals based on luni-solar calendars shift by less than a month across years (because the intercalary month corrects the drift), while purely lunar festivals like Eid can shift across different Gregorian months entirely. A small number of Indian festivals (Makar Sankranti, Pongal, Bihu, Vaisakhi) follow a solar sidereal calendar and therefore occur on nearly the same Gregorian date each year, though they slowly drift due to the difference between the sidereal and tropical years (about one day per 71 years). The Positional Astronomy Center publishes the Rashtriya Panchang annually to standardise festival dates across India's time zones.

11.4 Why Do We Launch Artificial Satellites in Space?

The Moon is Earth's natural satellite. Beyond it, many human-made artificial satellites orbit Earth, typically about 800 km above the surface, completing one orbit in roughly 100 minutes. They are visible as fast-moving points of light just before sunrise or after sunset. Artificial satellites serve purposes including communication, navigation, weather monitoring, disaster management, and scientific research. Activity 11.4 is a sky-watching activity directing students to spot satellites with the naked eye or binoculars, and to use mobile apps or websites for satellite pass predictions. The section highlights ISRO contributions: the Cartosat series for Earth imaging and the Bhuvan platform, AstroSat for astronomical observation, and missions Chandrayaan 1/2/3, Aditya L1, and Mangalyaan. Student-built satellites such as AzaadiSat, InspireSat-1, and Jugnu are also noted. A step-further box raises the issue of space debris — defunct satellites and rocket parts that can collide with active satellites and require international cooperation to manage.
