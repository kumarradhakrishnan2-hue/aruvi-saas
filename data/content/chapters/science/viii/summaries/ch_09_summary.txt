Chapter 9: The Amazing World of Solutes, Solvents, and Solutions

9.1 What Are Solute, Solvent, and Solution?

This section introduces the distinction between uniform and non-uniform mixtures, using everyday examples such as salt-in-water versus sand-in-water. A uniform mixture of two or more substances is defined as a solution. When a solid dissolves in a liquid to form a solution, the solid component is called the solute and the liquid is called the solvent. For solutions formed by mixing two liquids, the component present in smaller amount is the solute and the one in larger amount is the solvent. The section notes that gases mixed with liquids can also form solutions, since the gas dissolves evenly throughout.

9.2 How Much Solute Can a Fixed Amount of Solvent Dissolve?

This section explores the limit of dissolution through Activity 9.1, in which students progressively add salt to a fixed volume of water and record whether it dissolves. The key concepts introduced are unsaturated solution (one in which more solute can still be dissolved at a given temperature), saturated solution (one in which no additional solute can dissolve at that temperature), concentration (amount of solute in a fixed quantity of solution), dilute solution (less solute), and concentrated solution (more solute). The section establishes that solubility is the maximum amount of solute that can dissolve in a fixed quantity of solvent at a particular temperature. It contains a student activity (Activity 9.1).

9.2.1 How does temperature affect the solubility of a solute?

This subsection investigates the effect of temperature on solubility through Activity 9.2, a teacher-demonstrated experiment. Students create a saturated solution of baking soda at 20 °C, then observe that additional baking soda dissolves as the temperature is raised first to 50 °C and then to 70 °C. The finding is that for most solids, solubility increases with increasing temperature, and a saturated solution at one temperature behaves as an unsaturated solution if the temperature is raised. The activity is explicitly labelled as a demonstration activity.

9.3 Solubility of Gases

This section extends the concept of solubility to gases dissolved in liquids, using dissolved oxygen in water as the primary example. Gases form uniform mixtures when dissolved in water, qualifying them as solutions. The section introduces a key contrast with solid solubility: for gases, solubility generally decreases as temperature increases. The ecological consequence highlighted is that colder water holds more dissolved oxygen, sustaining aquatic life, while warming water reduces oxygen availability for fish and other organisms.

9.4 Why Do Objects Float or Sink in Water?

This section raises the observation that some objects float while others sink in the same liquid, giving everyday examples such as husk floating and rice sinking during washing, and oil floating on water. It introduces the concept that the property determining whether an object floats or sinks is related to its heaviness relative to the liquid — described as density — while also noting that density alone does not fully determine floating or sinking behaviour.

9.5 What Is Density?

This section provides the scientific definition of density as mass per unit volume, expressed by the formula Density = Mass ÷ Volume. Intuitive analogies (a crowded versus empty bus, a dense versus sparse forest) are used to build the concept. The section explains that density is independent of shape and size but depends on temperature and pressure. It introduces the SI unit of density as kg/m³ and also commonly used units g/mL and g/cm³, with a conversion table. The concept of relative density is introduced as a dimensionless ratio: the density of a substance divided by the density of water at the same temperature.

9.5.1 Determination of density

This subsection teaches students how to measure the two quantities needed to calculate density — mass and volume — and contains three student activities and one calculation activity. Activity 9.3 (Let us measure) guides students to use a digital weighing balance, including the tare function, to measure the mass of a solid object. It distinguishes mass (quantity of matter, measured in grams or kilograms) from weight (the Earth's gravitational force on an object, measured in newtons). The subsection then addresses volume measurement, explaining the measuring cylinder as the standard instrument for liquids, available in sizes from 5 mL to 500 mL. Activity 9.4 (Let us observe and calculate) develops the concept of least count of a measuring cylinder. Activity 9.5 (Let us measure 50 mL of water) teaches correct technique for reading a measuring cylinder, including identifying and reading the meniscus at eye level, and the difference in reading technique for colourless versus coloured liquids. For regular solid objects, Activity 9.6 (Let us calculate) uses the formula Volume = l × w × h. For irregular solids, Activity 9.7 (Let us measure) uses the water displacement method: the rise in water level in a measuring cylinder when the object is submerged equals the object's volume. The subsection concludes by combining mass and volume to calculate density using a worked example.

9.5.2 Effect of temperature on density

This subsection explains that density generally decreases when a substance is heated and increases when it is cooled. The mechanism is grounded in the particulate nature of matter: rising temperature causes particles to spread further apart, increasing volume while mass remains constant, so density decreases. The section applies this principle to explain why hot air rises (it is less dense than cool surrounding air) and how hot air balloons work. It also notes the anomalous behaviour of water, whose density is highest at 4 °C; below this, water expands as it freezes into ice at 0 °C, making ice less dense than liquid water and causing it to float. The ecological significance of floating ice — forming an insulating layer that keeps water beneath warm enough for aquatic life in cold climates — is highlighted.

9.5.3 Effect of pressure on density

This subsection examines how pressure affects density differently depending on the state of matter. For gases, increased pressure compresses particles closer together, reducing volume and increasing density. Liquids are nearly incompressible, so pressure has only a small effect on their density. Solids are even less affected by pressure than liquids, and changes in density of solids under normal pressure conditions are negligible.
