Chapter 8: Nature of Matter: Elements, Compounds, and Mixtures

This chapter explores how all matter around us is composed of substances that can be classified as mixtures, pure substances (elements or compounds), and minerals. Beginning from familiar everyday objects, the chapter builds a hierarchical understanding of the classification of matter.

8.1 What Are Mixtures?
This section introduces the concept of a mixture using familiar examples such as poha, sprout salad, sugar dissolved in water, soups, and lemonade. A mixture is defined as a combination of two or more substances where each substance retains its own properties and the components do not react chemically with each other. The individual substances in a mixture are called its components. The section distinguishes between two types of mixtures: non-uniform mixtures, where different components are visible to the naked eye or with a magnifying device (e.g., sprout salad), and uniform mixtures, where components are evenly distributed and cannot be distinguished even under a microscope (e.g., sugar dissolved in water). A sidebar introduces alloys (stainless steel, brass, bronze) as examples of uniform solid-solid mixtures, and a cultural heritage note mentions the ancient Indian concept of Mishraloha, referring to alloys described in texts like the Charaka Samhita and used for medicinal purposes.

8.1.1 Is air a mixture?
This subsection revisits the composition of air, confirmed from earlier learning, as a uniform mixture of nitrogen, oxygen, argon, carbon dioxide, and water vapour. It contains a student activity (Activity 8.1) in which students prepare lime water from calcium oxide and water, then expose it to air in a petri dish to observe it turning milky — demonstrating the presence of carbon dioxide through the reaction of calcium hydroxide with carbon dioxide to form calcium carbonate. A second activity (Activity 8.2) involves placing a black sheet of paper near an open window to collect and examine airborne dust particles with a magnifying glass, showing that dust is suspended in air as a pollutant. A sidebar notes major air pollutants including particulate matter and gases like carbon monoxide, ozone, nitrogen dioxide, and sulfur dioxide, and introduces the Air Quality Index (AQI).

8.1.2 Types of mixtures
This subsection extends the classification of mixtures based on the physical states of their components. A table (Table 8.1) lists six mixture types — gas-gas (e.g., air), gas-liquid (e.g., aerated water, oxygen in water), solid-gas (e.g., carbon particles in air), liquid-liquid (e.g., vinegar, oil and water), solid-liquid (e.g., sand in water, seawater), and solid-solid (e.g., baking powder, alloys) — and asks students to classify each as uniform or non-uniform. The section also clarifies that in science, all components of a mixture must be pure substances, and that the purpose of separating a mixture in science is to obtain pure substances.

8.2 What Are Pure Substances?
This section distinguishes between the common usage of the word "pure" (meaning unadulterated) and its scientific meaning. In science, a pure substance is one that contains only one kind of particle and cannot be separated into other kinds of matter by any physical process. The section notes that everyday "pure" products like milk, ghee, or spices may still be scientifically impure if they contain more than one substance. A sidebar prompts students to classify milk, packed fruit juice, baking soda, sugar, and soil as mixtures or pure substances.

8.3 What Are the Types of Pure Substances?
This section introduces the two categories of pure substances — elements and compounds — through a demonstration activity (Activity 8.3) in which electricity is passed through water (with dilute sulfuric acid added) using a 9V battery and two inverted test tubes. Unequal volumes of gas collect in the two test tubes; testing with a burning candle confirms one is hydrogen (pop sound) and the other is oxygen (flame glows brighter). The activity shows that water decomposes into two distinct gases, establishing that water is composed of two different substances.

8.3.1 Elements
The two gases produced — hydrogen and oxygen — are identified as elements, defined as pure substances made of identical particles called atoms that cannot be broken down further into simpler substances. Elements are described as the building blocks of all matter, with examples including gold, silver, sulfur, and carbon. The section notes that atoms of most elements exist as molecules: two hydrogen atoms form a molecule of hydrogen; two oxygen atoms form a molecule of oxygen. A sidebar classifies elements as metals and non-metals, mentions metalloids (like silicon and boron), and provides facts about the 118 known elements — including that eleven are gaseous at room temperature, two are liquid (mercury and bromine), and most are solid.

8.3.2 Compounds
This section explains why water cannot be separated into hydrogen and oxygen by physical means: because the particles of hydrogen and oxygen are chemically bonded in a fixed 2:1 ratio, making water a compound. Compounds are defined as substances formed when different elements combine chemically in fixed ratios, having properties entirely different from the constituent elements. Activity 8.4 (student activity) involves heating sugar in a boiling tube, which turns brown and then chars to leave carbon behind, with water droplets forming at the open end — demonstrating that sugar is a compound of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen. Activity 8.5 (demonstration activity) compares a mixture of iron filings and sulfur (Sample A) with iron sulfide produced by heating that mixture (Sample B). Students test both samples for colour, texture, magnetic attraction, and response to dilute hydrochloric acid: Sample A (mixture) retains the individual properties of iron and sulfur — iron is attracted to a magnet and reacts with HCl to produce hydrogen gas (colourless, no odour, burns with pop sound); Sample B (compound, iron sulfide) is uniformly black, not attracted to a magnet, and reacts with HCl to produce hydrogen sulfide gas (colourless, rotten egg odour). This contrast establishes the key distinction between mixtures and compounds.

8.4 How Do We Use Elements, Compounds, and Mixtures?
This section contextualises the practical importance of elements, compounds, and mixtures across everyday life and technology. Air is cited as a mixture of oxygen, nitrogen, and carbon dioxide; water as a compound of hydrogen and oxygen. Elements like iron and aluminium are used in construction and vehicles. Chemists combine elements into compounds to create medicines, vaccines, and fertilisers. Engineers and material scientists use knowledge of compounds and mixtures to develop advanced materials such as alloys (e.g., stainless steel stronger than pure iron) and building materials (wood, steel, concrete — all mixtures). A sidebar highlights graphene aerogel, a carbon-based material considered the lightest material on earth, with potential for oil-spill cleanup and energy-saving devices. A heritage note describes Dhokra art from Bihar and Odisha, where brass or bronze (alloys of elements) are cast using the lost-wax method to create traditional figures.

8.5 What Are Minerals?
This section introduces minerals as natural solid substances with a fixed chemical composition, found in rocks. Most rocks are mixtures of minerals. Some minerals called native minerals are pure elements (metals like gold, silver, copper, or non-metals like sulfur and carbon). Most minerals are compounds — examples include quartz, calcite, mica, pyroxene, and olivine. The section connects minerals to everyday materials: cement is made from calcite, quartz, alumina, and iron oxide; talcum powder comes from the mineral talc. The chapter closes by reinforcing that elements and compounds are the building blocks of matter, and that not everything (e.g., light, heat, electricity, thoughts) constitutes matter.
