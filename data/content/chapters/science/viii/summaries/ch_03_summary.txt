Chapter 3: Health: The Ultimate Treasure

3.1 Health: Is It More Than Not Falling Sick?

This section challenges the assumption that health means simply not having a disease, broadening the concept to include physical, mental, and social dimensions. The World Health Organization defines health as "a state of complete physical, mental, and social well-being, and not merely the absence of disease." A healthy person performs tasks efficiently, copes well with difficult situations, and adjusts successfully with peers and society. The section also introduces the Ayurvedic perspective — that true health involves a balance of body, mind, and surroundings, supported by dinacharya, ritucharya, and practices like yoga and meditation. Activity 3.1 presents a student case study in which social isolation and excessive screen time lead to physical and mental symptoms, prompting reflection on how habits and surroundings affect well-being.

3.2 How Can We Stay Healthy?

This section frames good health as the outcome of both lifestyle choices and environmental conditions. Nutritious food, personal hygiene, clean surroundings, regular exercise, adequate sleep, positive relationships, and a positive attitude all contribute to staying healthy. Activity 3.2 asks students to list good and harmful habits, then discuss findings with peers to recognise that health is shaped by lifestyle and environment.

3.2.1 Maintain a healthy lifestyle

This subsection lists specific lifestyle practices that support health: eating a balanced diet rich in fruits, vegetables, and whole grains; avoiding processed, fatty, and sugary food; staying physically active through outdoor play, walking, or cycling; limiting screen time; getting enough sleep; practising yoga or pranayama regularly; and refusing harmful substances such as tobacco, alcohol, and addictive drugs. The key terms introduced are lifestyle and the specific categories of healthy and harmful habits.

3.2.2 Keep the environment clean

This subsection establishes that a clean environment is as essential to health as personal habits. Activity 3.3 has students compare two playground images — one clean and one polluted — to understand why people in polluted surroundings fall sick more often. It introduces the Air Quality Index (AQI) as a monitoring tool and notes that vehicle and factory pollution can cause asthma and respiratory problems. The section also notes that even a clean environment cannot compensate for poor emotional or social well-being — relationships and feelings matter equally.

3.3 How Do We Know That We Are Unwell?

This section introduces the distinction between symptoms and signs of illness. A symptom is defined as what a person feels subjectively, such as pain, tiredness, or dizziness. A sign is something that can be observed or measured by others, such as fever, rash, high blood pressure, or swelling. The section frames these two categories as the tools that help doctors understand the cause of illness and notes that the body's deviation from its normal functioning is what constitutes being unwell.

3.4 Diseases: What Are the Causes and Types?

This section introduces the concept of disease as a condition that disrupts the normal functioning of the body or mind, often affecting one or more organ systems. The key term pathogen is introduced to describe disease-causing organisms including bacteria, viruses, fungi, worms, and protozoa. The chapter establishes a fundamental classification of diseases into two major types: communicable diseases, which are caused by pathogens and can spread from person to person, and non-communicable diseases (NCDs), which are linked to lifestyle, diet, or environment and do not spread between people. Examples given for communicable diseases include typhoid, dengue, flu, chickenpox, and COVID-19. Examples of NCDs include cancer, diabetes, and asthma. The section notes that NCDs have become the leading cause of death in India due to changes in lifestyle such as consumption of processed food, reduced physical activity, and an aging population.

3.4.1 How are communicable diseases caused and spread?

This subsection examines how pathogens spread, identifying five routes: airborne transmission (coughs or sneezes), direct contact (handshaking), indirect contact (shared personal items), contaminated food or water, and vectors (insects such as mosquitoes and houseflies). Activity 3.4 presents Table 3.1, a student-completed table grouping common communicable diseases by transmission mode — airborne diseases (common cold, influenza, chickenpox, measles, tuberculosis), waterborne and foodborne diseases (hepatitis A, cholera, typhoid, ascariasis), and insect-transmitted diseases (malaria, dengue). For each disease the table records the causal agent, site of infection, symptoms, and preventive measures. The subsection also introduces parasites — organisms such as intestinal worms that live in or on a host and feed on its nutrients. Key preventive practices include handwashing, covering the mouth while coughing, wearing masks, avoiding shared personal items, and maintaining food and water hygiene.

3.4.2 How are non-communicable diseases caused?

This subsection explains that NCDs such as cancer, diabetes, and asthma arise from lifestyle, diet, and environmental factors rather than pathogens. Deficiency diseases — such as scurvy, anaemia, and goitre — are also classified as NCDs caused by nutrient deficiencies. Chronic diseases are defined as conditions persisting for more than three months. Diabetes is highlighted as a major NCD in India, driven by hormonal imbalances, unhealthy diet, physical inactivity, and obesity. Activity 3.5 asks students to survey their neighbourhood for common lifestyle-related diseases and fill in a table of disease names, symptoms, and suggested lifestyle changes.

3.5 How to Prevent and Control Diseases?

This section establishes the principle that prevention is preferable to cure for both communicable and non-communicable diseases. Activity 3.6 presents a case study from Bhadrak district, Odisha, where a community sanitation campaign improved toilet use, reduced open defecation, and led to fewer cases of diarrhoea and infections in children, illustrating the power of simple sanitation steps.

Ability of the body to fight diseases

This subsection introduces immunity — the body's natural ability to resist disease — and the immune system that provides it. Vaccines train the immune system by exposing it to weakened, dead, or harmless parts of a pathogen, producing acquired immunity. The historical example of Edward Jenner's smallpox vaccine is developed through a "Think like a scientist" framework: his observation that cowpox-infected milkmaids did not contract smallpox, his hypothesis, his inoculation experiment, and the eventual global eradication of smallpox by 1979. India's traditional practice of variolation by teekedaars is noted as a heritage precursor to vaccination, and India's current role as a major global vaccine producer is highlighted. Vaccines are characterised as preventive, not curative.

3.5.1 Treatment of diseases

This subsection explains that when the immune system fails, antibiotics — medicines that kill bacteria by targeting bacterial cell structures absent in human cells — may be used. They are ineffective against viruses or protozoa. The discovery of penicillin by Alexander Fleming in 1928 is presented as a "Think like a scientist" example. The subsection then addresses antibiotic resistance, where bacteria survive and multiply despite antibiotic treatment due to overuse or incomplete dosing. Activity 3.7 asks students to infer from an infographic how resistance develops and what precautions reduce it. Traditional medicine systems such as Ayurveda, Siddha, and Unani use natural substances like herbs and oils and can support everyday well-being, but may not be effective for all diseases or stages. For NCDs, treatment focuses on managing symptoms through medication, lifestyle changes, and rehabilitation, with early diagnosis and continuous care being key.
