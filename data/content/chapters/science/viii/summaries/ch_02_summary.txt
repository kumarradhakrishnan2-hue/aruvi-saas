Chapter 2: The Invisible Living World: Beyond Our Naked Eye

The chapter opens by explaining that the human eye cannot see objects below a certain size, and that the invention of the lens — shaped like a lentil seed — and subsequently the microscope, revealed a hidden world of tiny living creatures. Robert Hooke (1665) used a microscope to observe cork slices, naming the compartments he saw "cells." Around the same time Antonie van Leeuwenhoek built improved microscopes and became the first to clearly see and describe bacteria and blood cells, earning him the title Father of Microbiology. The chapter introduces student Activity 2.1, in which a water-filled round-bottom flask is used to demonstrate magnification before a real magnifying glass is used.

2.1 What Is a Cell?

All living beings are made up of cells. This section establishes the basic structure of a cell through Activity 2.2 (a teacher demonstration involving an onion peel stained with safranin and observed under a microscope) and Activity 2.3 (a student-executed investigation in which cheek cells are scraped, stained with methylene blue, and observed). Both activities show three fundamental components: the cell membrane (outer boundary, porous, controls entry and exit of materials), the cytoplasm (fills the interior, contains carbohydrates, proteins, fats, mineral salts, site of most life processes), and the nucleus (regulates all cellular activities and growth). Plant cells additionally have a cell wall that provides rigidity and strength, and a large vacuole that stores substances, removes waste, and maintains cell shape. A "step further" box introduces plastids (including chloroplasts that contain chlorophyll and enable photosynthesis) and mitochondria as additional plant cell components. Animal cells generally lack a cell wall and large vacuoles.

2.1.1 Variation in shape and structure of cells

Different cell types have distinct shapes that reflect their functions. Muscle cells are spindle-shaped and flexible, enabling wave-like contractions that push food through the food pipe and churn food in the stomach; other stomach cells produce digestive juices and acid. Nerve cells (neurons) are very long with branches that allow them to carry messages rapidly across the body. Cheek cells are thin and flat, forming a protective lining. Plant cells vary from rectangular to elongated to tube-like; some form tubes that carry water throughout the plant. The section establishes the principle that cell shape, size, and structure are directly related to the function the cell performs.

2.2 What Are the Levels of Organisation in the Body of a Living Organism?

This section introduces the hierarchy of biological organisation: Cell → Tissue → Organ → Organ system → Organism. A cell is the basic unit, analogous to a brick; similar cells group into tissues; tissues combine to form organs; organs cooperate in organ systems; all systems together constitute the organism. Complex organisms begin life as a single egg cell that divides repeatedly to produce many cells — such organisms are called multicellular organisms. Animals, plants, and humans are all multicellular. An "Ever heard of" box notes that the ostrich egg yolk is the largest known single cell, measuring 130–170 mm in diameter.

2.3 What Are Microorganisms?

Some living organisms are made up of just one or very few cells and are too small to see with the naked eye; these are called microorganisms or microbes. Bacteria and Amoeba are unicellular; some fungi and algae are multicellular microorganisms. Microorganisms are found in water, soil, air, and inside the body. Observing them requires a microscope that magnifies 100–400 times, though low-cost foldable paper microscopes are also referenced. Activity 2.4 involves collecting and observing pond or stagnant water under a microscope; Activity 2.5 involves observing soil suspension. Activity 2.6 presents data tables identifying organisms found in pond water (Amoeba, Paramecium — protozoa; algae) and soil suspension (bread mould and mould — fungi; algae; bacteria of spherical, comma, spiral, and rod shapes). Viruses are introduced in an "Ever heard of" box as microscopic, acellular entities that multiply only inside living cells and can infect plants, animals, and bacteria.

2.4 How Are We Connected to Microbes?

This section explores the ubiquitous presence of microorganisms — in water, soil, air, food, extreme environments such as hot springs and snow, and inside the human gut (helping with digestion, as introduced in Grade 7). The chapter uses the example of fruit or food rotting when left out to show that microbes are ever-present and can infect food. It notes that high concentrations of salt or sugar (used in pickles and murabbas) inhibit microbial growth.

2.4.1 Key players in cleaning the environment

Activity 2.7 has students layer fruit and vegetable peels with soil and observe after 2–3 weeks, finding that the peels decompose into dark, nutrient-rich manure. Bacteria and fungi in the soil break down complex plant waste into simpler substances through decomposition — returning nutrients to the soil and supporting plant growth. Microbes also decompose dead animals and animal dung. The section references the "Our scientific heritage" box noting that ancient Indian Vedic texts (particularly the Atharvaveda) reference tiny entities called 'Krimi' — both visible (Drishya) and invisible (Adrishya) — and describe their beneficial and harmful effects. A "step further" box explains that some bacteria and fungi living in oxygen-free environments decompose organic waste and release biogas (primarily methane and carbon dioxide), used as fuel for cooking, heating, electricity generation, and vehicles.

2.4.2 Microorganisms and food

Activity 2.8 compares yeast-added dough (bowl A) with plain dough (bowl B) after several hours in a warm place, showing that yeast (a fungus) breaks down sugar in warm conditions, releasing carbon dioxide that makes dough rise and become fluffy, with a small amount of alcohol responsible for altered smell. This property is used in making breads, cakes, idlis, dosas, and bhatura (the latter with Lactobacillus). Activity 2.9 tests curd formation by adding a spoonful of curd to lukewarm milk (bowl A) and cold milk (bowl B): Lactobacillus in warm bowl A ferments milk sugar (lactose), producing lactic acid that sets the curd and makes it sour; cold bowl B does not curdle. Rhizobium bacteria, living in root nodules of legumes (beans, peas, lentils), trap atmospheric nitrogen and make it available to plants, reducing the need for chemical fertilisers and explaining why farmers rotate legumes with other crops.

2.4.3 Amazing microalgae: tiny helpers in water

Microalgae are microscopic, plant-like organisms found in water, soil, air, and on trees; they produce food through photosynthesis and in doing so supply more than half of Earth's oxygen. They are nutrient-rich food sources for aquatic animals. Specific examples — Spirulina, Chlorella, and Diatoms — are used as health supplements and medicines; microalgae also help clean water and serve as biofuel feedstock. An "Ever heard of" box highlights Spirulina as a superfood rich in protein (over 60% of body weight), vitamin B12, and low in fat and sugar, and describes how it can be cultivated in a glass tank. The section warns that pollution, climate change, and habitat destruction threaten microalgal diversity and that their conservation is important for environmental protection and oxygen balance.

2.5 Why Is Cell Considered to Be a Basic Unit of Life?

This concluding section consolidates the chapter's core argument: all living organisms — plants, animals, and microorganisms — are built from cells. Multicellular organisms have specialised cells that cooperate; unicellular organisms (bacteria, protozoa) carry out all life functions in a single cell. Fungi can be either (yeast = unicellular; mould = multicellular). The cells of microorganisms share a cell membrane with plant and animal cells; fungal cells additionally have a cell wall but lack chloroplasts; bacteria lack a well-defined nucleus and nuclear membrane, possessing instead a nucleoid region — this is the key structural distinction between bacteria and all other cell types. The section notes that an electron microscope (magnification ~10,00,000 times) is needed to observe sub-cellular components that will be studied in higher classes.
