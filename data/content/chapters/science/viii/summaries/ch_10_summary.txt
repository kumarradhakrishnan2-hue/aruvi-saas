Chapter 10: Light: Mirrors and Lenses

Chapter 10 introduces students to curved mirrors (concave and convex) and lenses (convex and concave), building on prior learning about plane mirrors and straight-line travel of light from Grade 7. The chapter is structured as a progression from mirror types and their image characteristics, through the laws of reflection, to the parallel world of lenses, with real-world applications woven throughout.

10.1 What Are Spherical Mirrors?

This section introduces spherical mirrors as a type of curved mirror whose reflecting surface forms part of a hollow glass sphere. The section opens with a student activity using a shiny metallic spoon as an improvised curved mirror to observe the difference between images formed by its inward-curving (inner) and outward-curving (outer) surfaces. The inner surface produces an inverted image while the outer surface produces an erect but smaller image. The chapter defines a concave mirror as one whose reflecting surface curves inwards and a convex mirror as one whose reflecting surface curves outwards, providing schematic representations for each. A "step further" box clarifies that spherical mirrors are manufactured by grinding a flat glass surface into a curve and applying a reflective coating, not by slicing a hollow sphere. Activity 10.2 asks students to distinguish between concave and convex mirrors by viewing them from the side to identify the direction of curvature.

10.2 What Are the Characteristics of Images Formed by Spherical Mirrors?

This section examines how image characteristics change with the distance of the object from a concave or convex mirror. Activity 10.3 guides students to place a small object in front of both mirror types at a short distance, then gradually move it farther away while observing changes in image size and orientation. For a concave mirror, the image is erect and enlarged when the object is close, but becomes inverted and progressively smaller as the object moves away. For a convex mirror, the image is always erect and diminished regardless of object distance, though the size decreases slightly as the object moves farther. The section distinguishes this behaviour from a plane mirror, which always produces an erect image of the same size, and notes that lateral inversion is seen in all three mirror types. Real-world uses of concave mirrors covered include torch reflectors, car and scooter headlights, and the dentist's mirror (which produces an enlarged view when held close). Real-world uses of convex mirrors include vehicle side-view mirrors (which provide a wider field of view and erect image of traffic behind), road safety mirrors at intersections, and surveillance mirrors in large stores. A "step further" box notes that modern reflecting telescopes use a large concave mirror as the primary mirror.

10.3 What Are the Laws of Reflection?

This section revisits the reflection activity from Grade 7 and extends it to formally establish the two laws of reflection. Activity 10.4 uses a plane mirror, a comb-and-torch setup to produce a narrow beam of light, and a white paper sheet to trace incident and reflected beams at multiple angles. Students draw the normal at the point of incidence and measure the angle of incidence and angle of reflection for several trials, discovering that these angles are always equal — this is the first law of reflection. Activity 10.5 extends this by using a sheet of chart paper with part bent at the edge of the table; the reflected beam disappears when the paper is bent, demonstrating that the incident ray, normal, and reflected ray all lie in the same plane — this is the second law. The section states that both laws are valid for all mirror types, plane and spherical. Activity 10.6 uses multiple parallel beams (multiple comb slits) to show that parallel beams reflecting off a plane mirror remain parallel, off a concave mirror they converge, and off a convex mirror they diverge. Activity 10.7 exploits concave mirror convergence by focusing sunlight onto paper to demonstrate that the concentrated light generates enough heat to ignite the paper; a "Safety First" warning is included. A "step further" box introduces solar concentrators as devices that use mirrors or lenses to concentrate sunlight for generating steam, electricity, and industrial heat, including solar furnaces that can melt steel.

10.4 What Is a Lens?

This section transitions from reflective to refractive optics, introducing lenses as pieces of transparent material with curved surfaces that refract rather than reflect light. Activity 10.8 demonstrates the lens effect using a water drop on an oiled glass strip: the curved outer surface of the water drop causes letters placed beneath it to appear enlarged, just as a magnifying glass does. The section defines a convex lens as one thicker at the middle than the edges (a converging lens) and a concave lens as one thicker at the edges than the middle (a diverging lens). Activity 10.9 explores image characteristics through both lens types: a convex lens produces an erect and enlarged image when the object is close but an inverted image when the object is farther away; a concave lens always produces an erect and diminished image. Activity 10.10 uses parallel beams through a flat glass plate, a convex lens, and a concave lens in sequence to confirm that the flat plate leaves the beam unchanged, the convex lens converges it, and the concave lens diverges it. Activity 10.11 repeats the paper-burning demonstration from Activity 10.7 using a convex lens in place of the concave mirror, confirming that a convex lens can also concentrate sunlight sufficiently to ignite paper. The section closes with everyday applications of lenses: eyeglasses for vision correction, cameras, telescopes, microscopes, and the flexible convex lens in the human eye. The end-of-chapter "Keep the curiosity alive" section includes exercises requiring angle calculations, matching images to mirror types, matching images to lens types, assertion-reason questions about convex mirrors, and a practical magnifying glass activity.
