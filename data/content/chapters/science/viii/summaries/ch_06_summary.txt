Chapter 6: Pressure, Winds, Storms, and Cyclones

6.1 Pressure

This section introduces the concept of pressure as force per unit area, motivated by a relatable scenario of two children carrying bags with narrow and broad straps. The key term introduced is pressure, defined as Pressure = Force / Area. The section establishes that when the same force is distributed over a smaller area, the resulting pressure is higher — which is why narrow-strap bags feel more uncomfortable than broad-strap bags. The SI unit of pressure is newton per metre squared (N/m²), also called pascal (Pa). The section includes a student activity (Table 6.1) in which students compare driving an iron nail by its head versus its pointed end, and cutting an apple with the sharp versus the blunt edge of a knife, to observe how area affects pressure.

The section then extends pressure to liquids, using the example of overhead water tanks placed at a height to supply good water pressure. Activity 6.1 involves two pipes of different diameters with balloons attached; when filled to the same water level, both balloons bulge equally, demonstrating that liquid pressure depends on the height of the water column, not on the volume or weight of water above. Activity 6.2 involves holes made near the bottom of a plastic bottle — when unsealed, water sprays outward in all directions, showing that liquids exert pressure not only at the bottom of a container but also on its sides and in all directions. A sidebar ("Ever heard of…") explains why dam bases are broader than their tops — to withstand the large horizontal water pressure near the bottom.

6.2 Pressure Exerted by Air

This section establishes that the atmosphere exerts pressure on all objects. Activity 6.3 involves an inverted paper plate covered first with a folded and then an unfolded chart paper sheet, lifted by a stick — more effort is needed with the unfolded (larger-area) sheet, demonstrating that air exerts a force that increases with area, which is the definition of atmospheric pressure. The section introduces the term atmospheric pressure — the pressure exerted by the air around us. Activity 6.4 uses a rubber sucker pressed on a smooth surface; it sticks because pressing pushes out the air inside, making the surrounding atmospheric pressure higher than the pressure inside the sucker, so it becomes difficult to pull off. The section quantifies atmospheric pressure by noting that the force exerted by the air column over an area of 15 cm × 15 cm is nearly equal to the weight of a 225 kg object (2250 N). It explains that humans are not crushed because the pressure inside the body equals the atmospheric pressure. A "step further" box notes that practical units for air pressure are millibar (mb) and hectopascal (hPa), both equal to 100 Pa.

6.3 Formation of Wind

This section establishes that winds form because air moves from regions of high pressure to regions of low pressure. Activity 6.5 connects an inflated balloon to an uninflated one via a straw; air moves from the inflated (higher pressure) balloon to the uninflated (lower pressure) one, and the flow stops when pressures equalise — demonstrating the directional movement of air driven by pressure differences. The section then connects this principle to sea breeze and land breeze: during the day, land heats faster than the sea, air above land rises and creates a low-pressure area, and cooler sea air flows in (sea breeze); at night, the sea is warmer, so the reverse occurs (land breeze). It also notes that a larger pressure difference produces higher wind speeds.

6.4 High-Speed Winds Result in Lowering of Air Pressure

This section introduces the inverse relationship between wind speed and air pressure. Activity 6.6 involves blowing air between two inflated balloons hanging from a stick; the balloons move towards each other, because the high-speed air between them creates a low-pressure zone while surrounding higher-pressure air pushes them inward — and blowing harder accelerates this effect. The section applies this principle to explain why roofs of houses may be blown off during storms: high-speed winds over a roof create low pressure above it, while pressure inside the house remains higher, generating an upward net force. The safety recommendation given is to keep doors and windows open during storms so wind can pass through the house, reducing this pressure difference and protecting the roof.

6.5 Storms, Thunderstorms, and Lightning

This section explains how storms, thunderstorms, and lightning form through a chain of atmospheric processes. When land is heated, warm moist air rises to form a low-pressure area; cooler surrounding air rushes in, gets heated, and rises in turn, creating a continuous wind circulation. As rising air expands and cools, water vapour condenses to form clouds; when droplets merge into heavy drops, they fall as rain, hail, or snow — and the resulting strong winds accompanying rain constitute a storm. Under certain conditions, rising warm air reaches heights cold enough to convert water droplets into ice particles. Strong upward and downward winds cause water droplets and ice particles to rub against each other, generating static electric charges within clouds — lighter positively charged ice particles rise to the upper cloud layers while heavier negatively charged water droplets occupy the lower layers. This charge separation eventually causes the ground beneath to become positively charged. When the charge build-up is large enough to break down air's insulating property, a sudden discharge — lightning — occurs within clouds, between clouds, or between clouds and the ground. The rapid heating and expansion of air by lightning produces the sound of thunder, and a storm with both lightning and thunder is called a thunderstorm. A "step further" box notes regional names for pre-monsoon thunderstorms in India (Kalboishakhi, Bordoisila, mango showers). The section closes with safety precautions during lightning: crouch low in open areas, avoid tall objects and metallic umbrellas, leave water bodies, and stay inside vehicles. A sidebar describes lightning conductors — metallic rods installed on buildings with a pointed end above the highest point and the other end buried in the ground — which provide a safe discharge path for electrical charges.

6.6 Cyclone

This section explains the formation, structure, and effects of cyclones. Cyclones form over warm ocean water: heating causes warm moist air to rise, water vapour condenses and releases latent heat back into the atmosphere, further warming the ascending air and deepening the low-pressure centre. Air from surrounding high-pressure regions rushes in, Earth's rotation causes the incoming air to spin, and the self-reinforcing cycle builds a very low-pressure area with high-speed revolving winds — forming a cyclone. The calm, lowest-pressure centre of a cyclone is called the eye of the cyclone; the surrounding region experiences intense winds and heavy rainfall. A cyclone intensifies over the ocean but weakens once it reaches land, as the source of moist air is cut off. The section describes the destructive effects of cyclones: storm surges of 3–12 metres flooding coastal areas, river flooding and landslides from heavy rainfall, contamination of drinking water by seawater, reduced soil fertility from salt deposition, blocked roads, and extended power outages. The section emphasises cyclone preparedness — monitoring IMD alerts and weather satellites, keeping emergency kits, and moving to designated cyclone shelters. The example of Cyclone Amphan (2020) with peak winds of 270 km/h is cited to illustrate the scale of cyclone destruction.
