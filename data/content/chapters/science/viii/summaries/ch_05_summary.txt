Chapter 5: Exploring Forces

Section 5.1 — What Is a Force? introduces the concept of force through a simple student activity. Activity 5.1 asks students to move a large cardboard box in as many ways as they can, leading them to discover that every mode of movement — pushing, pulling, lifting, carrying — involves a push or pull. The section establishes the foundational definition: a push or pull applied on an object is called force in science.

Section 5.2 — What Can a Force Do to the Objects on Which It Is Applied? uses Activity 5.2, a structured analytical exercise in which students examine everyday force situations (a friend pulling a moving bicycle to stop it, hitting a ball with a bat, pressing an inflated balloon) and record what effect the force has in each case. Through this analysis, the section identifies the four possible effects of force: making an object at rest begin to move, changing the speed of a moving object, changing the direction of motion of an object, and bringing about a change in the shape of an object.

Section 5.3 — Are Forces an Interaction Between Two or More Objects? establishes the interactional nature of force. The section asks students to count how many objects are involved in each force situation from Section 5.2, guiding them to infer that at least two objects must interact for a force to come into play. It formally defines force as a push or pull on an object resulting from that object's interaction with another object, and introduces the SI unit of force: newton (N), symbolised as N. A Step Further extension notes that when two objects interact each experiences a force from the other, and that these forces disappear the moment interaction ceases.

Section 5.4 — What Are the Different Types of Forces? organises forces into two broad categories: contact forces and non-contact forces.

Section 5.4.1 — Contact forces are those that require physical contact — direct (hand, body parts) or indirect (stick, rope) — between the objects involved. Muscular force is the first contact force introduced: it is the force produced by the contraction and elongation of muscles during any physical activity (walking, running, lifting, pushing). Animals, birds, fish, and insects also rely on muscular force for movement and survival; humans historically harnessed animal muscular force for tasks like ploughing and transport. A sidebar notes that muscular force also drives internal body functions such as digestion and circulation of blood through the heart. The second contact force is friction. Introduced through observations of objects slowing and stopping on their own, the section poses the question of what hidden force causes this. Activity 5.3 asks students to push an object across a surface and observe that it stops, while Activity 5.4 repeats the push on different surfaces (glass, cloth, wood, ceramic tile, sand) and observes that the stopping distance differs. These activities establish that friction is the contact force that acts between two surfaces in contact and always opposes the direction of motion. The mechanism of friction is explained: even apparently smooth surfaces have minute irregularities that interlock with each other. A Step Further note extends friction to objects moving through liquids and gases (air drag on aeroplanes, ships) and explains streamlining.

Section 5.4.2 — Non-contact forces are those that act at a distance without physical contact. Magnetic force is introduced first: students already know from Grade 6 that magnets attract magnetic materials and that like poles repel while unlike poles attract. Activity 5.5 asks students to stack two ring magnets on a vertical stick with like poles facing each other, observe that the upper magnet floats, and feel the repulsive force when pushed down. The formal definition establishes that magnetic force is a non-contact force exerted by a magnet on another magnet or a magnetic material. Electrostatic force follows: Activity 5.6 has students rub a plastic scale or straw with polythene and bring it near small paper pieces without touching them — the paper is attracted. Activity 5.7 has students rub two balloons with a woollen cloth, observe that the two identically charged balloons repel each other, and then bring the woollen cloth near one balloon, observing attraction. The section explains that rubbing causes static charges to build up; similar charges repel and unlike charges attract. The formal definition is that electrostatic force is the force exerted by a charged body on another charged or uncharged body; it is a non-contact force. A Step Further connects moving charges to electric current. Gravitational force is the final non-contact force. Activity 5.8 prompts students to throw a ball upwards and observe that it always returns to the ground regardless of how hard it is thrown. The section defines gravitational force as the force with which the Earth attracts objects towards itself, also called force of gravity or gravity. Unlike magnetic and electrostatic force, gravitational force is always attractive, never repulsive. The section describes the vertical motion of objects under gravity — rising objects slow down, stop momentarily, reverse direction, and accelerate downward.

Section 5.5 — Weight and Its Measurement distinguishes weight from mass. Weight is defined as the force with which the Earth pulls an object towards itself; it is measured in newtons. Activity 5.9 has students hang different objects (pencil box, tiffin box, small stone) from a spring one by one and observe that different objects stretch the spring by different amounts, demonstrating that the Earth pulls different objects with different forces. A Step Further box describes a spring balance — its hook, spring, and graduated scale in N — and explains how to read it. Activities 5.10 and 5.11 guide students to identify the range and the least count of a spring balance, and Activity 5.12 uses the spring balance to measure the weight of different objects. The section then distinguishes mass (the amount of matter in an object, measured in kg, constant everywhere) from weight (the gravitational force on an object, which varies with location and can be very different on other planets). A Step Further table shows that the same 1 kg object weighs 10 N on Earth, 1.6 N on the Moon, 3.8 N on Mars, 9 N on Venus, and 25.4 N on Jupiter.

Section 5.6 — Floating and Sinking addresses the upward force exerted by liquids on submerged objects. Activity 5.13 has students push a sealed empty bottle into a bucket of water, feel the upward push, release the bottle, and observe it bounce back to the surface. This leads to the definition of upthrust or buoyant force as the force applied by a liquid on an object in the upward direction. The section explains that when an object is placed in a liquid, gravitational force acts downward on it while buoyant force acts upward; if gravitational force exceeds buoyant force the object sinks, if they are equal the object floats. The density of the liquid is mentioned as one factor that determines buoyant force. A Step Further introduces Archimedes' Principle: the upward force on an immersed object equals the weight of the liquid it displaces. A sidebar notes that pumice, a volcanic rock full of air pockets, floats on water because its density is less than that of water.
