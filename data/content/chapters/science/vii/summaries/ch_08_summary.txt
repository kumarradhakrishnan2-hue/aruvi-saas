Chapter 8 — Measurement of Time and Motion opens with Prerna, a district-level 100 metre sprint champion, watching Olympic race footage and marvelling at how precisely time is measured in modern sports compared to the humble stopwatch at her school. Her curiosity about how time was measured before clocks and watches existed frames the chapter's two-part investigation: first, the history and science of timekeeping culminating in the pendulum and the SI unit of time; and second, the concepts of speed and types of linear motion.

8.1 Measurement of Time traces the development of timekeeping from ancient observations of repeating natural events — the rising and setting of the Sun, lunar phases, and changing seasons — to the invention of mechanical clocks. Early human civilisations used these natural cycles to create calendars, defining a day by the Sun's rising and setting. To measure smaller intervals within a day, they devised sundials (where a shadow's position marks the time), two types of water clocks (the outflow type, where water drains from a marked vessel, and the floating bowl or Ghatika-yantra type, where a bowl with a fine hole slowly fills and sinks), hourglasses (where sand flows from one bulb to another), and candle clocks (candles with time-indicating markings). A Fascinating Facts box highlights the Samrat Yantra at the Jantar Mantar in Jaipur, Rajasthan — the world's largest stone sundial, built around 300 years ago, whose 27-metre-high shadow moves at about 1 millimetre per second and can measure intervals as short as 2 seconds. A second Fascinating Facts box describes ancient Indian timekeeping: shadow-based methods referenced in Kautilya's Arthasastra (second century BCE–third century CE), Varahamihira's accurate shadow formulations around 530 CE, and the Ghatika-yantra mentioned by Aryabhata and used continuously at Buddhist monasteries, royal palaces, and town squares until the late nineteenth century, with each sinking cycle lasting 24 minutes (one ghatika or ghati), giving 60 ghatis to a day. The development of mechanical clocks driven by weights, gears, and springs from the fourteenth century onwards led to the invention of the pendulum clock. A Know a Scientist feature profiles Christiaan Huygens (1629–1695), who invented the pendulum clock in 1656–1657, building on Galileo Galilei's (1564–1642) discovery that the time for each swing of a pendulum is constant. The section contains a student activity. Activity 8.1 guides students to construct a simple water clock from a cut plastic bottle, calibrate it by marking water levels at one-minute intervals, and use it to measure short time intervals.

8.1.1 A simple pendulum introduces the pendulum as a device consisting of a small metallic ball (the bob) suspended by a long thread from a rigid support. At rest, the bob hangs at its mean position. When displaced and released, it swings back and forth in oscillatory motion that is periodic — it repeats its path after a fixed interval of time. One oscillation is defined as the motion of the bob from the mean position to one extreme position, then to the other extreme position, and back to the mean position. The time taken to complete one oscillation is defined as the time period of the pendulum. Through Activity 8.2, students measure the time taken for 10 oscillations of a pendulum of 100 cm string length and calculate the time period by dividing by 10; repeated trials show the time period is nearly constant. A Think Like a Scientist box guides further investigations to establish that the time period of a pendulum depends on its length but not on the mass of the bob, and that all pendulums of the same length at the same location have the same time period. This constancy is the property exploited in mechanical timekeeping. All clocks — ancient or modern — are based on some process repeating continuously at equal time intervals. A Dive Deeper box notes that modern quartz clocks use the vibrations of a quartz crystal, and atomic clocks use vibrations of specific atoms; today's atomic clocks lose only one second in millions of years.

8.1.2 SI unit of time establishes that the SI unit of time is the second, with symbol s. Larger units are the minute (min; 60 s = 1 min) and the hour (h; 60 min = 1 h). A Dive Deeper box clarifies writing conventions for units: time unit names and symbols are written in lowercase letters, no full stop after the symbol, a space between the number and unit, and the abbreviations 'sec' and 'hrs' are incorrect. A Fascinating Facts box reinforces that the Ghatika-yantra established the ghatika as the standard time unit in India until the nineteenth century. The section contains a student activity. Activity 8.3 asks students to examine a wall clock and identify the smallest time interval it can measure, establishing that one second is the smallest measurable interval on a standard wall clock. A Science and Society box describes precision timekeeping in sports (milliseconds), medicine (ECG machines measuring millisecond heartbeat variations), music (digital recordings capturing thousands of samples per second), and computing (smartphones and computers processing signals in microseconds).

8.2 Slow or Fast transitions from timekeeping to the concept of comparing motion. The section uses the example of runners in a straight-line race: to decide who is faster when all run the same distance, one measures the time taken; a runner who covers the same distance in less time is moving faster. The section extends this to comparing motions over different distances, motivating the need for a single combined measure of how fast or slow an object moves.

8.3 Speed defines speed as the distance covered by an object in unit time. The formula is: Speed = Total distance covered ÷ Total time taken. The SI unit of speed is metre per second (m/s); when distance is in kilometres and time in hours, speed is expressed in kilometre per hour (km/h). Three worked examples (Examples 8.1, 8.2, and 8.3) practise calculating speed, distance, and time from given quantities. A Science and Society box describes the speedometer (which measures and displays a vehicle's speed in km/h) and the odometer (which measures the total distance travelled) as instruments fitted in most vehicles. The section contains a student activity. Activity 8.4 asks students to look up a railway timetable, identify trains near their locality, and calculate and compare the speeds of different train types (Passenger, Express, Superfast) between two stations.

8.3.1 Relationship between speed, distance, and time formalises the three rearrangements of the speed formula: Speed = Distance ÷ Time; Distance = Speed × Time; Time = Distance ÷ Speed. Additional worked examples reinforce the use of each form. The section also introduces the term average speed for the speed calculated over the entire journey, noting that in reality objects rarely move at exactly constant speed throughout.

8.4 Uniform and Non-uniform Linear Motion revisits the concept of linear motion from Grade 6 — motion along a straight line — and introduces two types. When an object moving along a straight line covers equal distances in equal intervals of time, it is in uniform linear motion. When its speed keeps changing so that it covers unequal distances in equal time intervals, it is in non-uniform linear motion. A tabulated data set for two trains, X and Y, travelling between the same two stations in the same overall time demonstrates that Train X, covering 20 km in every 10-minute interval, is in uniform linear motion, while Train Y, whose per-interval distances vary, is in non-uniform linear motion. The section notes that uniform linear motion is an idealisation; in everyday life, most motion is non-uniform, which is why average speeds are used. A Fascinating Facts box closes the chapter by reflecting that, despite our ability to measure time with extraordinary precision, the fundamental nature of time remains a deep philosophical and scientific question.
