Chapter 4 — The World of Metals and Non-metals opens with two students, Yashwant and Anandi, visiting an ironsmith (Sudarshan) in a village in Rajasthan who crafts daily-use items such as tawas, baltis, chimtas, phawras, kulhadis, khurpis, and jelees from iron. Observing that iron can be beaten into shapes motivates the chapter's core inquiry into what properties make metals and non-metals distinct, how they behave with air and water, and what roles they play in daily life.

4.1 Properties of Materials introduces the physical properties used to distinguish metals from non-metals, organised across five subsections.

4.1.1 Malleability establishes that metals such as copper, aluminium, and iron are lustrous (showing metallic lustre) and hard, whereas materials like coal, sulfur, and wood are non-lustrous and not as hard. The property by which materials can be beaten into thin sheets is called malleability, and most metals possess it; gold and silver are identified as the most malleable metals. Materials that break into pieces rather than flatten are called brittle — coal and sulfur are brittle. A Holistic Lens feature connects the prominence of iron in ancient Indian civilisation to the superior strength of iron agricultural tools, noting that copper was likely discovered earlier. The section contains a student activity. Activity 4.1 directs students to collect pieces of copper, aluminium, iron, coal, sulfur, and wood, observe their lustre and hardness, and then hammer each piece to determine whether it flattens or breaks.

4.1.2 Ductility introduces the property by which materials can be drawn into wires as ductility, noting that this property is mainly possessed by metals. Copper and aluminium wires are common in electrical fittings and musical instruments; gold is so ductile that one gram can be drawn into a two-kilometre-long wire. A Dive Deeper box explains that steel ropes — a mixture of iron and carbon — are used in suspension bridges and cranes because of their ability to support heavy loads.

4.1.3 Sonority defines sonority as the property by which metals produce a ringing sound when struck or dropped, as opposed to the dull sounds produced by coal and wood. Metals are described as sonorous in nature; everyday examples include a school bell and the ghungroos worn by dancers. The section contains a student activity. Activity 4.2 asks students to drop a metal spoon, a coin, a piece of coal, and a block of wood and compare the sounds produced.

4.1.4 Conduction of heat establishes through a comparative experiment that metals are good conductors of heat while wood is a poor conductor. The transfer of heat from one point to another through a material is called conduction, and materials that allow this transfer are called conductors. This explains why cooking vessels are made of metal while their handles are made of wood or other materials that do not conduct heat. The section contains a student activity. Activity 4.3 has students immerse a metal spoon and a wooden spoon simultaneously in hot water and feel which spoon becomes hotter at its upper end.

4.1.5 Conduction of electricity extends the investigation of conductivity to electricity. The activity requires students to build a tester circuit (as in Chapter 3) and test various materials including aluminium foil, iron nail, sulfur, copper wire, coal, wood, stone, rubber eraser, and nylon rope. The observation that only metals make the bulb glow leads to the conclusion that metals are good conductors of electricity and non-metals are generally poor conductors. This explains why screwdriver handles and electrician's gloves are made of plastic or rubber. The section contains a student activity.

4.2 Effect of Air and Water on Metals: Iron investigates the conditions under which iron develops brown deposits (rust). A controlled experiment places iron nails in three conditions: dry air only, boiled water covered with oil (water only, no air), and open water in contact with air. Only the nail exposed to both water and air develops rust, demonstrating that moist air is responsible. The brown deposit is called rust and the process is called rusting. The broader deterioration of any metal's surface due to air, water, or other substances is called corrosion; examples include green coating on copper (verdigris) and black coating on silver. Methods to prevent rusting include painting, oiling, greasing, and galvanisation (applying a protective zinc layer). A Fascinating Facts box features the Iron Pillar of Delhi — more than 1,600 years old and still largely rust-free — as a testament to ancient Indian metallurgical skill. The section contains a student activity.

4.3 Effect of Air and Water on Other Metals demonstrates through a teacher-led activity that burning magnesium ribbon in air produces magnesium oxide, a white powder whose solution turns red litmus paper blue, confirming that oxides of metals are basic in nature. The section notes that sodium is so reactive it is stored in kerosene to prevent contact with oxygen and water. The section contains a student activity.

4.4 Substances that Behave Differently from Metals in Air and Water contrasts the behaviour of non-metals such as sulfur and phosphorus with metals. Burning sulfur in oxygen produces sulfur dioxide gas, which dissolves in water to form sulfurous acid, an acidic solution — establishing that oxides of non-metals are acidic in nature. Sulfur itself does not react with water. Phosphorus is so reactive it catches fire in atmospheric air and must be stored in water. Non-metals are characterised as generally soft, dull in appearance, neither malleable nor ductile, non-sonorous, and poor conductors of heat and electricity. A Dive Deeper box clarifies that metals and non-metals are sub-categories of elements — substances that cannot be broken down into simpler substances — and notes that 118 elements are currently known. Plastic, glass, wood, rubber, and paper are not classified as metals or non-metals because they are not elements. The section contains student activities.

4.5 Are Non-metals Essential in Everyday Life? challenges the impression that non-metals are unimportant by highlighting the vital roles of specific non-metals: oxygen is essential for all life; carbon is the building block of proteins, fats, and carbohydrates; nitrogen is used in fertilisers and is an essential plant nutrient; chlorine is used in water purification; iodine solution is applied as an antiseptic. A Science and Society box highlights the importance of metals and their alloys in everyday utensils, modern technologies, aerospace, and atomic energy, and notes that recycling of iron and aluminium in India helps minimise waste and support sustainability.
