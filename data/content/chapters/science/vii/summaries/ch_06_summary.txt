Chapter 6 — Adolescence: A Stage of Growth and Change opens by drawing a parallel between the growth of a plant — from seed to sapling to a plant capable of producing new seeds — and the growth of animals and humans, who must also mature before they can reproduce. The chapter maps the human life journey through infancy, childhood, adolescence, adulthood, and old age, establishes that adolescence typically spans ages 10 to 19 and is a period of rapid physical, biological, and emotional change, and guides students to understand these changes responsibly.

6.1 Growing With Age: The Teenage Years introduces the concept of adolescence through a conversation between two cousins, Venkatesh and Devyani, who notice changes in each other — a thin moustache, a changed voice, and increased height. The section defines adolescence as the stage of development between childhood and adulthood, generally starting around age 10 and continuing until 19. It then describes the most commonly observable physical changes occurring during this period. Increase in height becomes more prominent during adolescence even though height growth is continuous from birth. Changes in body structure, weight gain, and strength describe how boys may experience broader shoulders and wider chests while girls experience changes in height, weight, and breast development. Changes in voice explain that growth of the voice box (larynx) leads to a hoarse voice in boys, visible as the Adam's apple, while girls experience only slight voice changes as their voice box grows more slowly. Appearance of hair in different parts of the body notes that both boys and girls develop hair in the armpits and pubic region, while boys additionally develop facial hair in the form of moustaches and beards; variations in the timing of these changes are completely normal. Changes in facial skin: emergence of pimples explains that increased oily secretions from skin pores during adolescence can cause the skin condition acne, of which pimples are a manifestation, typically appearing on the face. The section introduces the term secondary sexual characteristics for changes that help distinguish males from females but are not directly involved in reproduction, and defines puberty as the stage in which the body undergoes external and internal changes to develop into an adult capable of reproduction. The section contains a student activity. Activity 6.1 asks students to anonymously write down changes they observe among classmates from Grades 5 to 8 related to height, strength, behaviour, or appearance, place the slips in a jar, and discuss the most commonly noted changes as a class.

6.2 Changes that Indicate Reproductive Capability covers internal changes associated with adolescence, particularly those related to reproductive maturation. In adolescent girls, a key internal change is the onset of the menstrual cycle, a recurring cycle of approximately 28 to 30 days (though a healthy range of 21 to 35 days is also noted) associated with reproductive health. The phase during which blood discharge occurs is called menstruation, which may last three to seven days. Menstruation begins at puberty and typically ends naturally between ages 45 and 55. A Breaking Myths about Menstruation box addresses the widespread social taboos and myths — including physical isolation of menstruating girls — that have no scientific basis, and encourages a scientific outlook to support reproductive health and a healthier lifestyle for women.

6.3 Emotional and Behavioural Changes in Adolescents establishes that adolescence involves not only physical changes but also heightened emotions and behavioural shifts. Adolescents may experience mood swings, strong emotions, and increased sensitivity, which can express themselves through engagement in music, dance, sports, or social activities. Understanding the relationship between emotions and behaviour helps adolescents respond thoughtfully to situations. The section contains a student activity. Activity 6.2 asks students to reflect on emotional and behavioural changes they or their classmates have experienced over the past year or two, and to complete Table 6.2 identifying key emotional changes, their probable effects on behaviour, and constructive ways to channel them.

6.4 Making Adolescence a Joyful Experience addresses four practical dimensions of healthy adolescent living, each covered in a subsection.

6.4.1 Meeting nutritional needs emphasises that adolescence, as a period of rapid physical change, requires an especially nutritious diet rich in proteins, carbohydrates, fats, vitamins, and minerals. Calcium from milk, millets, curd, cheese, and paneer supports optimal bone growth, proteins and carbohydrates support strength and energy, and iron from spinach, kidney beans, and dried fruits is essential for blood formation. A Science and Society box highlights that adolescent girls are particularly susceptible to blood-related health problems due to iron or Vitamin B12 deficiency, and invites students to explore government schemes addressing these deficiencies. A Know a Scientist feature profiles Dorothy Hodgkin, who determined the structure of Vitamin B12 and was the third woman to win the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1964. The section contains a student activity.

6.4.2 Personal hygiene explains that maintaining body hygiene — particularly in the armpits and pubic region — is important during adolescence to prevent infections. For girls, maintaining menstrual hygiene using sanitary pads or reusable cloth pads is essential for both comfort and health. Used sanitary pads should be wrapped and disposed of in a dustbin. A Science and Society box describes four government schemes to support menstrual hygiene: the Menstrual Hygiene Scheme (MHS), which provides sanitary pads free or at reduced cost in rural areas; the Rashtriya Kishor Swasthya Karyakram (RKSK), which promotes peer education on menstrual health; the Suvidha Sanitary Napkin Initiative, which provides biodegradable pads through Jan Aushadhi Kendras; and various State-Level Initiatives such as the Shuchi Scheme in Karnataka and free napkin distribution in Tamil Nadu and Odisha.

6.4.3 Physical activities establishes that regular exercise and participation in games and sports during adolescence keeps the body and mind healthy, builds stamina, and improves mood, making physical activity an essential part of adolescent wellness.

6.4.4 Balanced social life emphasises that adolescents should be polite and respectful in their interactions both in person and online, and should use social media platforms responsibly. A Science and Society box on cyberbullying defines it as the use of digital devices to harass others through misleading messages, false rumours, or sharing personal information without consent, and advises students to seek help from parents or teachers if bullied rather than responding with fear. The section contains a student activity. Activity 6.4 asks students to work in groups to design posters and pamphlets about responsible social media behaviour and to complete a dos and don'ts table (Table 6.4).

6.4.5 Avoiding harmful substances — learn to say NO addresses the risk of peer pressure to try addictive substances such as tobacco, gutka, cigarettes, beedi, alcohol, and illegal drugs during adolescence. The section explains that these substances are addictive, meaning that once consumed, they create a compulsive urge to continue, a pattern called substance abuse. Health consequences include breathing difficulties, memory loss, and lung damage. The section urges students to confidently say no and seek support from family, friends, or counsellors if needed. A Science and Society box describes the Nasha Mukt Bharat Abhiyaan, a government initiative focused on spreading awareness about substance abuse prevention, particularly among early-age children, and mentions the National De-addiction Helpline number 14446.

6.5 The 'Why' Question for Adolescence addresses the underlying biological cause of the changes described throughout the chapter. The key concept introduced is that of hormones — chemical substances produced in different parts of the body, released in response to signals from the brain, that regulate growth, development, reproduction, and behaviour. Puberty, menstruation, and mood changes during adolescence are all primarily driven by hormonal activity. The chapter closes by affirming that staying informed, seeking support, and making healthy decisions helps adolescents build a strong foundation for life ahead.
