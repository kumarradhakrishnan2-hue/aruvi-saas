Chapter 7 — Heat Transfer in Nature opens with siblings Pema and Palden sitting around a fireplace in Gangtok on a winter evening. Their conversation — about why Kerala is warmer than Sikkim and why cooking utensils are made of metal — launches the chapter's inquiry into the three modes of heat transfer: conduction, convection, and radiation. The chapter then extends these ideas to natural large-scale phenomena, including land and sea breezes, the water cycle, and groundwater seepage.

7.1 Conduction of Heat establishes through an experimental investigation that heat travels along a metal strip from its hotter end towards its cooler end. The process of heat transfer from the hotter part of an object to the cooler part, where the particles themselves do not move from their positions but pass heat on to neighbouring particles, is defined as conduction. Materials that allow heat to pass through them easily — such as metals — are called good conductors of heat, and this explains why cooking utensils are made of metal. Materials such as glass, wood, clay, and porcelain do not allow heat to pass through easily and are called poor conductors or insulators of heat; this is why clay and porcelain cups keep tea or coffee hot for longer. Air is also a poor conductor of heat, which is why woollen clothes (whose fibres trap air in their pores) keep us warm in winter and why two thin blankets are warmer than one thick blanket of the same total thickness. A Fascinating Facts box describes traditional houses in the Mori block of Uttarkashi (Himalayas), whose walls consist of two wooden layers filled with cow dung and mud — both poor conductors — to retain warmth. Hollow bricks used in modern construction serve the same purpose. The section contains a student activity. Activity 7.1 directs students to attach four wax-held pins at equal intervals on a metal strip, heat one end, predict the order in which pins will fall, and record observations.

7.2 Convection addresses how heat is transferred through fluids — both liquids and gases. The observation of smoke rising from burning firewood motivates the investigation. Because air near a flame heats up, expands, becomes lighter, and rises, cooler air moves in to take its place, establishing a circulating current. This process of heat transfer by the actual movement of particles of a liquid or gas is defined as convection. The section confirms through a potassium permanganate experiment in heated water that liquids also transfer heat by convection: heated water at the base expands, becomes lighter, and rises, while cooler water from the sides descends, creating a visible circulation loop. The section contains student activities.

7.2.1 Land and Sea Breeze extends convection to the meteorological scale. Because land heats up faster than water during the day, warm air above the land rises and cooler air from the sea moves in to replace it; this movement of cooler air from sea to land is called a sea breeze. At night the process reverses — land cools faster than water, so air above the sea is now warmer and rises, and cooler air moves from the land towards the sea, producing a land breeze. This explains why people in coastal areas experience a reversal of wind direction between day and night, and why houses in coastal areas are built with windows facing the sea. The section contains a student activity. Activity 7.4 asks students to compare the rates of heating and cooling of soil and water by placing thermometers in two bowls set in sunlight and recording temperature every five minutes.

7.3 Radiation identifies the third mode of heat transfer as radiation — the process by which heat travels directly from a hot object to cooler surroundings without requiring any material medium. This is how heat from the Sun reaches the Earth across space, and how warmth from a fireplace reaches people sitting near it. All objects radiate heat; a hot utensil removed from a flame eventually cools because it radiates heat to its surroundings. A Dive Deeper box explains that light-coloured clothes reflect most heat and are therefore more comfortable in summer, while dark-coloured clothes absorb more heat and are preferred in winter. The section notes that in many everyday situations — such as water being heated in a pan — all three processes operate simultaneously: heat passes from the flame to the metal pan by conduction, water inside heats up by convection, and the warmth felt around the flame and hot pan is due to radiation. A Fascinating Facts box describes the bukhari, a traditional iron stove used in the Himalayan region for cooking and room heating, which involves all three modes of heat transfer.

7.4 Water Cycle connects heat transfer, specifically solar radiation and evaporation, to the continuous movement of water on Earth. Water exists in three states in nature: as liquid in oceans, rivers, and lakes; as solid snow, ice sheets, and glaciers in mountains and polar regions; and as water vapour in the atmosphere. Solar radiation drives evaporation from water bodies and transpiration from plants, converting liquid water into water vapour that rises into the atmosphere. As it rises and cools, it condenses to form clouds, which return water to the Earth's surface through precipitation as rain, snow, or hail. The continuous movement of water upward as vapour and downward through precipitation, passing through soil, rocks, and plants and finally returning to water bodies, is called the water cycle. A Know a Scientist feature profiles Varahamihira, a sixth-century CE astronomer and mathematician from Ujjaini, whose work Brihatsamhita gave methods for predicting seasonal rainfall based on observations of cloud formation, wind patterns, and celestial phenomena.

7.4.1 Seepage of water beneath the Earth investigates how rainfall that reaches the Earth's surface seeps downward through soil and rock. Water seeps fastest through gravel, slower through sand, and slowest through clay, because the spaces between particles are largest in gravel and most interconnected. The process of surface water seeping through soil and rocks is called infiltration. Water that seeps through and accumulates in the pore spaces of sediments and openings in rocks beneath the surface is called groundwater. The underground layers of sediments and rocks that store groundwater are called aquifers, which are accessed by wells and bore wells. The section notes that groundwater is being depleted by excessive extraction and reduced infiltration due to increased urban concretisation, and that rainwater harvesting and recharge pits help replenish groundwater. A Science and Society box describes the ice stupa — a cone-shaped artificial glacier built in Ladakh during winter by channelling mountain stream water through underground pipes and spraying it into the cold air, where it freezes layer by layer. The ice stupa melts slowly during spring, providing water for farming and daily needs throughout the summer season. The section contains a student activity.
