Chapter 10 — Life Processes in Plants connects to Grade 6 learning that all living beings need food for growth, and to Chapter 9's discussion of how animals obtain nutrition. The chapter opens with the question of how plants — which do not eat food as animals do — obtain the nutrients they need. The four major sections progressively establish that plants make their own food through photosynthesis using sunlight, water, carbon dioxide, and chlorophyll; transport water and food through xylem and phloem respectively; and, like all living organisms, also respire to release energy. A Fascinating Facts box quotes from the ancient Indian text Vrikshayurveda, which recorded practical observations on plant growth, soil, and agricultural practices including methods of organic manure preparation.

10.1 How Do Plants Grow? builds on prior knowledge that plants require water and sunlight, testing these claims through a controlled experiment. The section contains a student activity. Activity 10.1 asks students to set up three pots with similar saplings under different conditions — Pot A in sunlight with water, Pot B in sunlight without water, and Pot C in the dark with water — observe changes in height, number of leaves, and leaf colour over two weeks, and infer that plants require both sunlight and water for healthy growth.

10.2 How Do Plants Get Food for their Growth? explores the mechanism by which plants synthesise their own food, organised into four subsections.

10.2.1 Leaves: the 'food factories' of plants introduces the concept that plants store food in the form of starch and that starch is produced in the leaves. Leaves are typically broad and flat, and are mostly green due to the presence of a pigment called chlorophyll, which efficiently captures sunlight. The iodine test — turning a decolourised leaf blue-black if starch is present — is introduced as a method to detect starch. Activity 10.2 is a teacher-led demonstration in which a leaf is first boiled in water, then in alcohol (to remove chlorophyll and enable observation of colour change), before being tested with dilute iodine solution; a blue-black colour indicates the presence of starch. Activity 10.3 investigates the relationship between chlorophyll, sunlight, and starch production using leaves with green and non-green patches from plants kept in sunlight and in the dark; the result shows that only green patches of sunlit plants test positive for starch, confirming that chlorophyll and sunlight are both essential for starch preparation, and that leaves are therefore the food factories of plants. A Fascinating Facts box notes that some plant leaves appear red, violet, or brown because they contain more of these coloured pigments than green chlorophyll; these pigments also assist in photosynthesis, and starch can be detected in such leaves using the iodine test.

10.2.2 Role of air in the preparation of food investigates which gas from the air is essential for food preparation. The section contains a student activity. Activity 10.4 is a teacher-led demonstration in which half a leaf of a destarched plant is placed inside a bottle containing sodium hydroxide solution (which absorbs carbon dioxide from the air), while the other half remains outside. After placing the setup in sunlight and conducting a starch test, only the half outside the bottle turns blue-black, showing that carbon dioxide from the air is essential for the plant to prepare starch. The section concludes that four things — sunlight, water, chlorophyll, and carbon dioxide — are all essential for the synthesis of food in plants, and names this process photosynthesis.

10.2.3 Photosynthesis: in a nutshell consolidates the process of photosynthesis. Food is produced in the form of glucose (a simple carbohydrate), which serves as an instant energy source and is later converted to starch for storage. The word equation of photosynthesis is: Carbon dioxide + Water → (in the presence of sunlight and chlorophyll) → Glucose + Oxygen. Other green parts of the plant besides leaves that contain chlorophyll also perform photosynthesis. A Know a Scientist feature profiles Rustom Hormusji Dastur (1896–1961), an Indian plant scientist who studied the effects of water, temperature, and light colour on photosynthesis and headed the Botany Department at the Royal Institute of Science, Bombay. Activity 10.5 investigates the gas released during photosynthesis using an aquatic plant in a beaker of water; set-up A placed in sunlight produces gas bubbles that accumulate in an inverted test tube, and a glowing splint introduced into the tube bursts into flame, confirming that oxygen is released during photosynthesis, which only occurs in sunlight.

10.2.4 How do leaves exchange gases during photosynthesis? introduces the structural feature that enables leaves to take in carbon dioxide and release oxygen. Activity 10.6 is a demonstration in which a thin peel from the lower surface of a leaf (such as rhoeo, money plant, or onion) is placed on a microscope slide and examined under a microscope; students observe numerous tiny pores called stomata. Stomata, present on the surface of leaves, facilitate the exchange of gases during both photosynthesis and respiration.

10.3 Transport in Plants examines how substances move within a plant, covered in two subsections.

10.3.1 Transport of water and minerals explains that roots absorb water and dissolved minerals from the soil and that these must be transported to leaves and other parts of the plant. Activity 10.7 demonstrates this by placing a stem cutting in red-inked water; after one day, the red colour is visible in the stem, leaves, and flowers. Cutting the stem and examining it with a magnifying glass reveals red-coloured tube-like structures called xylem present in the stem, branches, and leaves of plants. Water and minerals dissolved in water travel upward through the xylem.

10.3.2 Transport of food explains that the food prepared in the leaves during photosynthesis must also be transported to all parts of the plant, including roots, stems, and seeds where it may be stored. This food is transported through a different set of thin tube-like structures called phloem.

10.4 Do Plants Respire? addresses the question of whether plants, like animals, also carry out respiration. Activity 10.8 is a teacher-led demonstration in which germinating moong bean seeds are sealed in a conical flask for 24 hours; gas channelled from the flask through rubber tubing into lime water turns the lime water milky, confirming that the seeds have released carbon dioxide. This demonstrates that plants (and their seeds) respire. During respiration, glucose is broken down in the presence of oxygen, releasing carbon dioxide, water, and energy: Glucose + Oxygen → Carbon dioxide + Water + Energy. The energy released is used for plant growth and development. All parts of a plant — both green and non-green — carry out respiration. The chapter concludes by affirming that plants have distinct mechanisms for synthesising food (photosynthesis), transporting it (through xylem and phloem), and releasing energy from it (respiration). A Know a Scientist feature profiles Kamala Sohonie (1911–1998), who received a Ph.D. from Cambridge University for her work on respiration in plants and later became Director of the Royal Institute of Science, Bombay; much of her work improved the nutritive value of plant foods and she investigated the sap of the coconut palm as a nutritive drink called Neera.
