Chapter 2 — Exploring Substances: Acidic, Basic, and Neutral introduces students to the classification of everyday substances based on their chemical nature, using a National Science Day science fair narrative involving two siblings, Ashwin and Keerthi, as the motivating context. The chapter establishes that substances around us can be sorted into three groups — acidic, basic, and neutral — and that certain natural materials can be used to identify which group a substance belongs to. The chapter is organised around hands-on exploration of three natural indicators and the phenomenon of neutralisation.

2.1 Nature — Our Science Laboratory frames nature as the primary source of tools for scientific investigation. It establishes the concept of an indicator as a substance that shows different colours in the presence of acids and bases, and introduces the term acid-base indicator. This section is organised into three subsections, each focused on a different natural indicator.

2.1.1 Litmus as an indicator explains that litmus is a natural dye obtained from lichens and is available as a solution and as paper strips in two colours — blue and red. The key concepts are that acidic substances turn blue litmus paper red, while basic substances turn red litmus paper blue, and neutral substances cause no colour change in either paper. A Holistic Lens feature notes that lichens are symbiotic associations of a fungus and an alga, found in areas with clean air. The section contains a student activity. Activity 2.1 directs students to test a range of household samples — lemon juice, soap solution, amla juice, tamarind water, vinegar, baking soda solution, lime water, tap water, washing powder solution, sugar solution, and salt solution — with both blue and red litmus papers and sort them into acidic (Group A), basic (Group B), and neutral (Group C) groups. Activity 2.2 asks students to relate taste to acid content, establishing that sour-tasting substances tend to be acidic, and to observe that basic substances are generally soapy or slippery to touch and taste bitter. It introduces citric acid, tartaric acid, ascorbic acid (Vitamin C), and oxalic acid as examples of naturally occurring acids in foods.

2.1.2 Red rose as an indicator extends the concept of natural indicators by showing that an extract prepared from red rose petals can also serve as an acid-base indicator, giving a red colour in acidic solutions and a green colour in basic solutions. Other plant materials mentioned as potential indicators include beetroot, purple cabbage, turmeric, jamun, and red hibiscus (gudhal). A Fascinating Facts box describes how the hydrangea plant produces flowers of different colours depending on whether the soil is acidic or basic. The section contains student activities. Activity 2.3 guides students to prepare a red rose petal extract by crushing petals and filtering the coloured liquid. Activity 2.4 has students test the same set of samples using the red rose extract and compare results with the litmus paper groupings from Activity 2.1.

2.1.3 Turmeric as an indicator demonstrates that turmeric (haldi) paper, prepared by soaking filter paper in turmeric paste and drying it, can be used to detect basic substances. Basic solutions turn turmeric paper from yellow to red, but the paper cannot distinguish between acidic and neutral substances, which both leave it unchanged. A Fascinating Facts box describes turmeric's traditional significance as a member of the ginger family and its use in Ayurvedic medicine. The section contains student activities. Activity 2.5 guides students to prepare turmeric paper strips. Activity 2.6 introduces olfactory indicators — substances whose odour changes in the presence of an acid or a base — and uses onion-soaked strips to investigate this phenomenon. A Know a Scientist feature introduces Acharya Prafulla Chandra Ray, known as the Father of Modern Indian Chemistry, who established India's first pharmaceutical company and advanced scientific research.

2.2 What Happens When Acidic Substances Mix with Basic Substances? introduces the neutralisation reaction through an experiment in which lemon juice (acidic) is progressively neutralised by lime water (basic), observed through the colour change of blue litmus solution. The section establishes that when an acid and a base react in sufficient quantities, the resulting solution is neither acidic nor basic. This type of reaction is defined as a neutralisation reaction, and its products are identified as salt, water, and heat, expressed as: Acid + Base → Salt + Water + Heat. The section contains a student activity.

2.3 Neutralisation in Daily Life applies the concept of neutralisation to three real-world scenarios. Situation 1 explains that a red ant bite injects formic acid into the skin, and applying baking soda (a base) neutralises the acid and relieves the pain. Situation 2 addresses soil that has become too acidic due to excessive use of chemical fertilisers, which can be corrected by adding lime; if soil is too basic, organic matter like compost is added to release acids that neutralise the base. Situation 3 describes how acidic factory waste released into a lake can harm fish, and explains that adding basic substances before release can neutralise the waste. The chapter concludes by revisiting the opening science fair mystery, suggesting that turmeric solution was used to coat the paper and a soap solution was used for writing, so the basic soap turned the turmeric paper red to reveal the message.
