Chapter 11 — Light: Shadows and Reflections opens with Reza and her grandmother preparing a shadow puppet performance using a diya (oil lamp) and cutout puppets, and noticing how the shadow of a hand changes when moved closer to or farther from the lamp. These everyday observations motivate the chapter's four core topics: sources of light, the straight-line travel of light, shadow formation and transparency, and the pinhole camera and reflection of light.

11.1 Sources of Light distinguishes between two categories of objects based on their relationship with light. Objects that produce their own light — such as the Sun, fire, burning diyas, candles, electric bulbs, LEDs, and glow-worms — are called luminous objects. Objects that do not produce their own light but are visible only because they reflect light falling on them — such as the Moon, tables, books, and people — are called non-luminous objects. The section contains a student activity. Activity 11.1 presents a list of objects and asks students to classify each as luminous or non-luminous by deciding whether it produces its own light. A Fascinating Facts box describes bioluminescence, the ability of certain living organisms — including some deep-sea fish such as the anglerfish, fireflies, glow-worms, and certain bacteria and fungi — to produce their own light through chemical reactions in their bodies.

11.2 Does Light Travel in a Straight Line? investigates the path taken by light using two activities. Activity 11.2 places three cardboard sheets each with a small hole in alignment, with a candle at one end; when the holes are aligned, the flame is visible, but when any card is shifted, the flame disappears. Activity 11.3 shines a torch through a straight hollow tube (where light passes through) and then through a bent tube (where no light emerges at the other end). Both activities lead to the conclusion that light always travels in a straight line. This property is called the rectilinear propagation of light, and a ray of light is represented diagrammatically as a straight line with an arrowhead showing the direction of travel.

11.3 Shadows establishes the conditions under which a shadow is formed. When an opaque object is placed in the path of light, it blocks the light and casts a dark region behind it — this dark region is called a shadow. The section uses this observation to classify materials on the basis of how much light they allow to pass through. Materials that allow almost all light to pass through and through which objects are clearly visible are called transparent — examples include clear glass, clean water, and air. Materials that do not allow any light to pass through are called opaque — examples include wood, metal, cardboard, and stone. Materials that allow some light to pass through but scatter it so that objects behind them appear blurry are called translucent — examples include frosted glass, butter paper, and thin cloth. The section also explores how the size of a shadow varies: moving the object closer to the light source makes the shadow larger, while moving it farther from the source or closer to the screen makes the shadow smaller. The section contains student activities. Activity 11.4 uses a torch to cast the shadow of a hand, varying the distance between the hand and the wall, to observe shadow size changes. Activity 11.5 asks students to classify a set of objects — including clear glass, wood, cardboard, a glass tumbler, thin cloth, and a plastic bag — as transparent, translucent, or opaque based on the clarity of the shadows they cast.

11.4 Pinhole Camera explains a simple device that demonstrates the rectilinear propagation of light. A pinhole camera consists of two nested boxes — an inner box with a small pinhole on one face and an outer box covered on the opposite side with tracing paper or translucent material as a screen. Light from an object enters through the tiny pinhole and forms an image on the screen inside. Because light from the top of the object travels in a straight line through the pinhole and reaches the bottom of the screen, and vice versa, the image formed is inverted (upside down). The section contains a student activity. Activity 11.6 guides students to construct a pinhole camera from two cardboard boxes, one sliding inside the other, make a pinhole on the outer closed face and the translucent screen on the open inner face, point it at a bright source or outdoor scene, and observe and sketch the image. A Fascinating Facts box describes the camera obscura, a large room-sized ancestor of the pinhole camera used by artists in the past to trace scenes, and notes that some early photographers used pinhole cameras to capture images on photographic paper.

11.5 Mirrors and Reflections covers the behaviour of light when it strikes a smooth, polished surface, organised into four subsections.

11.5.1 Reflection of light introduces reflection as the bouncing back of light when it strikes a surface. Activity 11.7 directs a torch beam at a mirror in a dark room and observes a reflected beam on the adjacent wall, establishing that smooth surfaces such as mirrors reflect light strongly, while rough or uneven surfaces scatter light in many directions without producing a clear reflected beam. Common examples of reflection in everyday life include seeing a reflection in still water or a polished metal surface.

11.5.2 Laws of reflection establishes the two fundamental laws governing how light reflects. The perpendicular to the mirror surface at the point of incidence is called the normal. The angle between the incoming ray (incident ray) and the normal is called the angle of incidence, and the angle between the reflected ray and the normal is called the angle of reflection. Activity 11.8 guides students to direct a ray of light at a mirror at various angles, trace the incident and reflected rays, measure both angles, and record results in a table. The observations establish the two laws of reflection: the angle of incidence is always equal to the angle of reflection; and the incident ray, the normal, and the reflected ray all lie in the same plane. A Know a Scientist feature profiles Ibn al-Haytham (965–1040 CE), also known as Alhazen, an Arab scientist whose Book of Optics made foundational contributions to the understanding of vision, light, reflection, and refraction, establishing him as one of the founders of modern optics.

11.5.3 Image in a plane mirror investigates the nature and position of the image formed when an object is placed in front of a flat (plane) mirror. Activity 11.9 uses two identical pins and a plane mirror to locate the image of one pin by finding the point where the other pin exactly coincides with the reflected image. The key observations are that the image appears to be behind the mirror at the same distance as the object is in front, is the same size as the object, and cannot be formed on a screen — such an image is called a virtual image. The image in a plane mirror also appears laterally inverted, meaning the left side of the object appears as the right side of the image. Activity 11.10 demonstrates lateral inversion by writing text in front of a mirror and noting the reversed appearance; the word AMBULANCE on emergency vehicles is printed in reverse so that drivers ahead can read it correctly in their rear-view mirrors. A Fascinating Facts box describes the periscope, a device using two plane mirrors at 45° angles to allow observation over or around obstacles such as walls or submarine hulls.

11.5.4 Multiple reflections explores what happens when an object is placed between two mirrors. Activity 11.11 places an object between two plane mirrors held at different angles and observes multiple images formed by repeated reflections. When the mirrors are kept parallel to each other, the number of images becomes very large. The kaleidoscope is introduced as a device based on multiple reflections — three mirrors arranged in a triangular prism create multiple reflections of coloured beads or glass pieces, producing symmetrical and ever-changing patterns. Students are guided to construct a simple kaleidoscope.
