Chapter 5 — Changes Around Us: Physical and Chemical opens with four students noticing changes in their everyday surroundings — ice melting into water, a rose bud blooming into a flower, a bottle of cold water warming up, and a banana developing more brown spots and a strong smell. These observations frame the central question: can the changes around us be sorted into meaningful categories? The chapter progressively builds the classification of changes into physical and chemical, examines reversibility, discusses desirability, and concludes with two slow natural change processes.

The introductory Activity 5.1 asks students to observe and record changes — including melting ice cubes, chopping vegetables, boiling water, making popcorn, cutting paper, burning wood, and drying wet clothes — and reflect on what is changing in each case, noting that changes may involve size, shape, smell, or other properties observed through the senses.

5.1 A Substance May Change in Appearance but Remain the Same! explores changes in which the underlying substance is not transformed into anything new. Through three sub-experiments in Activity 5.2 — folding paper into origami shapes, inflating and deflating a balloon, and crushing chalk into powder — students observe that the paper, rubber, and chalk remain the same substances even though their shape or size changed. The same principle connects to water existing in solid, liquid, and gaseous states while still being water. Changes in which only physical properties such as shape, size, and state change, and no new substance is formed, are defined as physical changes.

5.2 A Substance May Change in Appearance and Not Remain the Same! introduces chemical changes through a comparative experiment in Activity 5.3, in which students blow exhaled air through straws into tap water and lime water. Blowing into tap water produces only bubbles with no colour change, while blowing into lime water turns it milky due to the formation of insoluble calcium carbonate. Since a new substance is formed, this is identified as a chemical change brought about through a chemical reaction. The word equation is: Calcium hydroxide + Carbon dioxide → Calcium carbonate + Water. The section also contains Activity 5.4, in which vinegar (or lemon juice) is mixed with baking soda; the fizzing and gas produced turns lime water milky, confirming the gas is carbon dioxide — another chemical change: Vinegar + Baking soda → Carbon dioxide + Other substances.

5.3 Some Other Processes Involving Chemical Changes extends the concept of chemical change to three further phenomena, organised into two subsections.

5.3.1 Rusting revisits the formation of rust from Chapter 4, explicitly classifying it as a chemical change because a new substance — iron oxide — is formed.

5.3.2 Combustion identifies the burning of magnesium ribbon as a chemical change because a new white substance, magnesium oxide, is formed along with heat and light. A chemical reaction in which a substance reacts with oxygen and produces heat and/or light is defined as combustion, and the substances that undergo it are called combustible substances — examples include wood, paper, cotton, and kerosene. Activity 5.5 establishes that oxygen from air supports combustion by showing that a candle covered by a glass tumbler goes out while the uncovered one continues to burn; the carbon dioxide trapped inside can be confirmed with lime water. The sub-section then addresses whether air alone is sufficient for combustion, introducing the concept of ignition temperature — the minimum temperature at which a substance catches fire — through Activity 5.6, in which sunrays focused through a magnifying glass ignite paper. The conclusion is that combustion requires three things simultaneously, represented by the fire triangle: a combustible substance (fuel), oxygen, and heat sufficient to reach the ignition temperature. A Science and Society box explains that wrapping a cloth around a person whose clothes are on fire cuts off air supply and extinguishes the fire. A Fascinating Facts box introduces bioluminescence — the production of light without heat by fireflies through a chemical change.

5.4 Can Physical and Chemical Changes Occur in the Same Process? uses the burning candle as a case study to show that both types of change can co-exist in a single process. Activity 5.7 presents student dialogue about the burning candle. The chapter explains that the melting, flow, solidification, and evaporation of wax are physical changes, while the burning of wax vapour to produce new substances is a chemical change. A Know a Scientist feature profiles Michael Faraday, who delivered the famous lecture series Chemical History of a Candle in the nineteenth century to illustrate physical and chemical processes.

5.5 Are Changes Permanent? introduces the concept of reversibility. Activity 5.8 asks students to revisit the changes from Activity 5.1 and determine which can be reversed. The section establishes that some changes can be reversed — ice melting can be reversed by refreezing, and evaporated water can be condensed — while others cannot be reversed, such as chopping vegetables or making popcorn. Changes are thus grouped into reversible and irreversible changes.

5.6 Are All Changes Desirable? distinguishes changes that are useful or beneficial (desirable), such as ripening of fruits, cooking food, or converting food waste into compost, from those that cause harm or inconvenience (undesirable), such as rusting or food decay. The section also highlights the long-term environmental impact of human-driven changes: increased burning of fuels raises atmospheric carbon dioxide levels, and drying paints release substances that cause atmospheric pollution.

5.7 Some Slow Natural Changes examines two natural processes that involve both physical and chemical changes but unfold over very long time scales.

5.7.1 Weathering of rocks explains that large rocks break down into smaller pieces called sediments through physical changes driven by temperature variation, growing tree roots, and water freezing in cracks. Additionally, water and dissolved chemicals can react with rock surfaces, producing chemical changes in their composition; an example is basalt, which contains iron and develops a red iron oxide layer when exposed to water vapour or air over time. The combined physical and chemical breakdown of rocks is called weathering and eventually leads to the formation of soil.

5.7.2 Erosion describes the process by which rock pebbles, soil, and sediments are broken down and carried from one location to another by natural forces such as wind and flowing water. This is classified as a physical change. Sediments that settle in water bodies eventually harden into new rocks, a process that takes thousands of years and cannot be reversed.
