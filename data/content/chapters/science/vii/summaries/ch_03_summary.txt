Chapter 3 — Electricity: Circuits and their Components uses a school trip to the Bhakra Nangal Dam hydroelectric powerhouse as its opening context, framing the broad pervasiveness of electricity in daily life across categories such as cooking, lighting, transportation, heating and cooling, entertainment, and communication. The chapter focuses on the components of a simple electric circuit — electric cell, battery, electric lamp, LED, switch, and wires — and progresses from exploring a familiar object (a torchlight) to understanding how its components work together, how circuits are drawn using symbols, and which materials allow current to flow.

3.1 A Torchlight introduces the torchlight (also called a torch or flashlight) as the entry point for studying electricity. The section uses the familiar observation that the torch lamp glows when the switch is in one position and not the other, prompting students to open the torch and examine what is inside. The section contains a student activity. Activity 3.1 asks students to take a torchlight, observe it carefully for its lamp and switch, slide the switch and observe whether the lamp glows, and then open the torch to find the electric cells inside.

3.2 A Simple Electrical Circuit introduces the internal components of the torch and explains how they function together to form a circuit. It is divided into six subsections.

3.2.1 Electric cell establishes that an electric cell is a portable source of electrical energy with two terminals — a positive terminal (the metal cap, marked +) and a negative terminal (the metal disc, marked –). The section contains a student activity. Activity 3.2 asks students to examine an electric cell carefully and identify its two terminals and their physical features.

3.2.2 Battery explains that when two or more cells are connected so that the positive terminal of one cell connects to the negative terminal of the next, the combination is called a battery. Connecting cells in this way provides electrical energy to the circuit for a longer time or at greater capacity. A Fascinating Facts box notes that the term "battery" is also commonly used for a single cell. The section contains a student activity. Activity 3.3 asks students to take a two-cell torch, remove the cells, reinsert them in different orders and orientations, and observe which arrangement makes the lamp glow, leading to the discovery of the correct series arrangement.

3.2.3 Electric lamp covers two types of lamps. The Incandescent Lamp subsection explains that inside the glass bulb of an incandescent lamp is a thin wire called a filament, which gets hot and glows when current passes through it. The filament is supported by two thicker wires connected to the lamp's two terminals — the metal case and the metal tip. The LED Lamp subsection explains that a Light Emitting Diode (LED) does not contain a filament, has two terminals identified by wire length (the longer wire is positive, the shorter is negative), and can emit light in different colours. Both subsections contain student activities.

3.2.4 Making an electric lamp glow using an electric cell or battery presents the core construction activity of the chapter, where students assemble a circuit using a cell, lamp, cell holder, lamp holder, and wires. The key learning is that a lamp glows only in specific arrangements, prompting comparison between glowing and non-glowing configurations. A separate experiment with an LED highlights that, unlike incandescent lamps, LEDs allow current to flow in one direction only, so the polarity of connections must be correct for the LED to glow. The section contains student activities.

3.2.5 An electrical circuit defines an electrical circuit as a complete path for electric current to flow. The section explains that the direction of current in a circuit is taken to be from the positive to the negative terminal of the electric cell. Current passes through the filament of an incandescent lamp and causes it to glow; the lamp will glow as long as the circuit is complete. A Fascinating Facts box notes that a lamp with a broken filament has "fused" and stops current flow.

3.2.6 Electric switch introduces the switch as a device that either completes (closes) or breaks (opens) a circuit. When the circuit is closed, current flows and the lamp glows (ON position); when the circuit is open, there is a gap and current cannot flow (OFF position). The section guides students to construct a simple switch using drawing pins and a safety pin, and then test it in a circuit. The section contains student activities.

3.3 Circuit Diagrams introduces the use of standard symbols to represent electrical components — electric cell, battery, electric lamp, LED, switch in ON and OFF positions, and wire — so that circuits can be drawn and understood universally. A representation of a circuit using these symbols is called a circuit diagram. The long line in the cell symbol represents the positive terminal, the short line the negative terminal; in the LED symbol, the triangle indicates the direction of current flow and two arrows indicate light emission. International bodies such as the IEC, ANSI, and IEEE establish these standard symbols. The section contains a student activity. Activity 3.10 asks students to draw circuit diagrams for two given circuits — one with an incandescent lamp and one with an LED.

3.4 Electrical Conductors and Insulators investigates why metal wires are used in circuits and why they are covered with plastic or rubber. The section introduces the concept that some materials allow electric current to pass through them easily (good conductors or conductors of electricity) while others do not allow current to pass (insulators or poor conductors of electricity). Experiments show that metals are conductors; plastic, rubber, glass, wood, paper, and wax are insulators. Conductors are used for wires, switches, and connectors, while insulators are used as coverings to protect people from electric shocks. A Dive Deeper box distinguishes Direct Current (DC) from batteries from Alternating Current (AC) from power plants. A safety caution notes that the human body is a conductor and electric current can cause severe injury or death. The section contains a student activity. Activity 3.11 guides students to build a conduction tester and use it to test various objects, recording results in a table and concluding which materials are conductors and which are insulators.
