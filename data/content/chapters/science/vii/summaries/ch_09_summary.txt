9.1 Nutrition in Animals

Animals obtain energy from food by breaking down complex components such as carbohydrates, proteins, and fats into simpler, absorbable forms through a process called digestion. This breakdown takes place along a long continuous tube called the alimentary canal, which runs from the mouth to the anus. The simpler molecules produced through digestion are absorbed into the blood and transported to every cell in the body to fuel life processes.

9.1.1 Digestion in human beings

This subsection traces the step-by-step journey of food through the human alimentary canal, explaining how each organ contributes to the progressive breakdown and absorption of nutrients.

Beginning with the mouth cavity

Digestion begins in the mouth, where teeth perform mechanical digestion by crushing and grinding food into smaller pieces. Saliva, produced by salivary glands in the mouth, contains a digestive juice that chemically breaks down starch into simpler sugars — which is why starchy foods like chapati or rice taste sweeter when chewed for a long time. The chapter includes Activity 9.1, in which students test boiled rice for the presence of starch using iodine solution, comparing unchewed rice to rice that has been thoroughly chewed, to observe starch breakdown in the mouth.

Food pipe (Oesophagus): A passage from the mouth to the stomach

Once food is chewed, saliva moistens it into a soft ball and the tongue pushes it into the oesophagus, a long flexible muscular tube that connects the mouth cavity to the stomach. The walls of the oesophagus contract and relax in rhythmic wave-like movements that push the food downward; this motion continues throughout the entire length of the alimentary canal.

Stomach

In the stomach, the muscular walls contract and relax to churn the food and mix it thoroughly with a secretion containing digestive juice, acid, and mucus. The digestive juice breaks down proteins, the acid kills harmful bacteria that may have entered with the food, and the mucus protects the stomach's own lining from being damaged by the acid. Food remains in the stomach until it is reduced to a semi-liquid mass before passing to the next stage.

Small Intestine

The partially digested food moves into the small intestine, which is approximately 6 metres long and is the longest part of the alimentary canal. The small intestine receives digestive secretions from three sources: its own inner lining, the liver (which secretes bile to neutralise the acidic food mass and to help break down fats), and the pancreas (which secretes pancreatic juice to further break down carbohydrates, proteins, and fats). The inner lining of the small intestine carries thousands of tiny finger-like projections that greatly increase its surface area; through these projections, digested nutrients are absorbed into the blood vessels in the intestinal wall and transported to the rest of the body, a process the chapter calls absorption.

Large intestine

Undigested material that the small intestine cannot absorb passes into the large intestine, which is about 1.5 metres long and considerably wider than the small intestine. Here, water and some salts are absorbed from the remaining material, which gradually becomes solid waste called stool. The stool is stored in the rectum until it is expelled from the body through the anus, a process called egestion.

9.1.2 Do all animals digest food the same way as humans do?

Animals show considerable variation in their digestive adaptations depending on their diet and feeding habits. Grass-eating animals such as cows and buffaloes are called ruminants; they quickly swallow partially chewed grass, store it in a part of their stomach, and later return it to the mouth to chew it thoroughly before swallowing again — a process called rumination or chewing the cud. Birds do not have teeth and therefore cannot chew food; instead they have a muscular chamber called the gizzard in which food is broken down by powerful contractions of its walls, sometimes aided by small stones the birds swallow deliberately.

9.2 Respiration in Animals

The nutrients absorbed from food must be converted into usable energy inside the body's cells, and this conversion is achieved through respiration. The chapter draws a clear distinction between breathing and respiration: breathing is the physical act of moving air in and out of the lungs, whereas respiration is the chemical process by which oxygen reacts with glucose inside cells to release carbon dioxide, water, and energy.

9.2.1 Respiration in humans

How do we breathe?

The human respiratory system consists of the nostrils, nasal passages, windpipe, and two lungs, working together to bring fresh air into the body and expel waste gases. Air inhaled through the nostrils passes through the nasal passages, where fine hairs and mucus filter out dust and dirt, before travelling down the windpipe into the lungs; inside the lungs the windpipe branches repeatedly into smaller passages that end in millions of tiny balloon-like sacs called alveoli. The chapter includes Activity 9.2, in which students construct a model using a plastic bottle and balloons to observe how changes in the volume of the chest cavity drive the movement of air in and out of the lungs, demonstrating the role of the diaphragm and rib muscles. During inhalation the ribs move upward and outward while the dome-shaped diaphragm moves downward, increasing the volume of the chest cavity so that air rushes in; during exhalation the ribs move down and inward and the diaphragm moves upward, compressing the cavity and pushing air out.

What do we breathe out?

Activity 9.3 demonstrates that exhaled air contains a higher concentration of carbon dioxide than inhaled air, confirmed when exhaled air causes lime water to turn milky while inhaled air does not. Inhaled air contains approximately 21% oxygen and 0.04% carbon dioxide, whereas exhaled air contains 16–17% oxygen and 4–5% carbon dioxide, showing that the body consumes oxygen and produces carbon dioxide during respiration.

How does the exchange of gases happen?

Gas exchange between air and blood takes place in the alveoli, which have extremely thin walls surrounded by a dense network of fine blood vessels. Blood arriving at the alveoli carries carbon dioxide collected from the body's cells; this carbon dioxide passes into the air inside the alveoli and is breathed out, while oxygen from freshly inhaled air passes in the opposite direction into the blood and is carried to all tissues of the body. The word equation given for this process is: Glucose + Oxygen → Carbon dioxide + Water + Energy.

9.2.2 Do other animals breathe the same way as humans do?

While many animals — including birds, mammals such as elephants and lions, and reptiles such as snakes — breathe through lungs, the precise structure of their lungs varies across groups. Aquatic animals such as fish breathe through gills, which are richly supplied with blood vessels that allow dissolved oxygen in the surrounding water to pass into the blood and carbon dioxide from the blood to pass out into the water. Frogs and other amphibians use gills for breathing as tadpoles, develop lungs for life on land as adults, and can also exchange gases through their moist skin when in water; earthworms, which have no specialised respiratory organs, exchange gases entirely through their moist skin surface.
