Chapter 12 — Earth, Moon, and the Sun opens with Rashmika, a 12-year-old in Kanniyakumari, who notices that coconut tree shadows are long in the morning and shorter in the afternoon. She wonders whether this happens because the Sun is moving across the sky, or because the Earth itself is moving. The chapter addresses this question by investigating the Earth's rotation and revolution, the phenomena of seasons, and the formation of solar and lunar eclipses.

12.1 Rotation of the Earth introduces the Earth's rotation through the analogy of riding a merry-go-round: a person sitting on a merry-go-round rotating anti-clockwise sees the surrounding trees and objects appearing to rotate clockwise. In the same way, the Sun appears to rise in the East and move westward across the sky not because the Sun is moving, but because the Earth itself is rotating from West to East. The Earth spins on its own axis — an imaginary line passing through its geographic North Pole and South Pole — and completes one full rotation in approximately 24 hours. When viewed from above the North Pole, the Earth rotates in the anti-clockwise direction, that is, from West to East. A Dive Deeper box defines rotation as the motion of an object in which all its parts move in circles around an imaginary line (the axis of rotation) passing through it. The rotation of the Earth from West to East causes the day-night cycle: the side of the Earth facing the Sun experiences daytime, and the opposite side experiences night. As Earth rotates, locations move alternately into sunlight and darkness, producing sunrise and sunset. The section contains student activities. Activity 12.1 uses a merry-go-round to help students experience how rotation causes surrounding objects to appear to move in the opposite direction. Activity 12.2 uses a globe, a small sticker to mark the student's location, and a torch to demonstrate how one half of the Earth is illuminated at any time, and how rotating the globe from West to East produces the cycle of day and night. Activity 12.3 asks students to observe the Big Dipper (Saptarishi) constellation over several hours at night and notice that it appears to move around the Pole Star (Dhruva Tara). Since the Earth's axis of rotation points very close to the Pole Star, that star appears nearly stationary, while all other stars appear to circle around it, rising in the East and setting in the West. The Moon also rises in the East and sets in the West for the same reason. A Fascinating Facts box describes the Foucault pendulum, a long pendulum with a heavy bob whose swinging plane appears to rotate over time, providing simple proof of the Earth's rotation. A 22-metre Foucault pendulum hangs from the skylight of the Constitution Hall in India's new Parliament building in New Delhi. A second Fascinating Facts box describes star trails — circular arcs recorded when astrophotographers keep camera shutters open for a long time — as a visual record of apparent star motion due to Earth's rotation. A third Fascinating Facts box highlights Aryabhata (fifth century CE), whose treatise Aryabhatiya contained a verse explaining that the apparent westward movement of stars is caused by the eastward rotation of the Earth; his calculated value for the Earth's rotation period was approximately 23 hours, 56 minutes, and 4.1 seconds — impressively close to the currently accepted value.

12.2 Revolution of the Earth establishes that while the Earth rotates on its own axis, it also simultaneously revolves around the Sun. Revolution is defined as the motion of an object around another object, and the path an object takes while revolving is called its orbit. The Earth's orbit around the Sun is nearly circular when viewed from above, and the Earth completes one revolution in approximately 365 days and 6 hours.

12.2.1 Changing view of night sky from the Earth explains that as the Earth revolves around the Sun, the direction in which we face at night (away from the Sun) gradually changes over the year. Consequently, different stars and constellations are visible at different times of the year — stars that rise at sunset in March are different from those that rise at sunset in September. This is why certain constellations are associated with particular seasons. A Fascinating Facts box notes that the Bhil and Pawara communities of the Tapi Valley in western India traditionally used the appearance of specific star patterns in the sky as indicators of the approaching monsoon season.

12.2.2 Seasons on the Earth explains that the Earth's axis of rotation is not perpendicular to its orbital plane but is tilted at an angle, and the Earth maintains this tilt consistently as it revolves around the Sun. The combination of this axial tilt and the spherical shape of the Earth gives rise to the seasons. In June, the Northern Hemisphere is tilted towards the Sun: sunlight strikes the Northern Hemisphere at a steeper angle and is concentrated over a smaller area, making it more intense; additionally, the Northern Hemisphere receives more than 12 hours of sunlight per day. Both effects cause higher temperatures, producing the summer season in the Northern Hemisphere. In December, the Northern Hemisphere is tilted away from the Sun, sunlight is spread over a larger area and is less intense, and the day is shorter than 12 hours, producing winter. The Southern Hemisphere experiences the reverse — winter in June and summer in December. A Dive Deeper box corrects two common misconceptions: that the Northern Hemisphere is warmer in summer because it is physically closer to the Sun when tilted towards it, or that seasonal temperature changes are caused by variation in Earth–Sun distance over the elliptical orbit. Both factors create distance differences that are too small to cause the observed temperature changes; in fact, the Earth is actually closest to the Sun in January. A second Dive Deeper box introduces the special dates in the yearly cycle: the summer solstice (around 21 June in the Northern Hemisphere, the longest day), the winter solstice (around 22 December, the shortest day), and the spring and autumn equinoxes (around 21 March and 23 September, when daytime lasts exactly 12 hours). A Fascinating Facts box notes that at the North Pole, the Sun rises on the equinox (21 March) and remains continuously visible for six months, setting on 22 September; the polar regions therefore experience six consecutive months of daylight followed by six months of darkness. The equator always receives 12 hours of sunlight, so the effect of seasons is minimal for regions of southern India close to the equator.

12.3 Eclipses introduces the Moon's role in creating spectacular alignment events. The Moon is a natural satellite of the Earth and revolves around the Earth as the Earth revolves around the Sun; these simultaneous orbital motions occasionally bring the Sun, Moon, and Earth into near-perfect alignment, producing eclipses.

12.3.1 Solar eclipse occurs when the Moon comes between the Sun and the Earth, obstructing the Sun's light from reaching part of the Earth. The section introduces the key concept of apparent size — the size of an object as perceived by an observer, which depends on both its actual physical size and its distance. Although the Moon is much smaller than the Sun, it is also much closer to the Earth, so their apparent sizes as seen from the Earth are nearly equal. Activity 12.4 demonstrates apparent size by asking students to cover a friend's head with an outstretched thumb — possible despite the thumb being much smaller than the head, because the thumb is much closer. When the Moon's shadow falls on a small area of the Earth's surface, observers in that area see the Sun completely blocked — a total solar eclipse — and it turns dark for a few minutes during the day. In the surrounding area where the Moon only partially blocks the Sun, observers see a partial solar eclipse. A Dive Deeper box explains why Mercury and Venus, despite being larger than the Moon, cannot block the Sun: they are so much farther from the Earth that their apparent sizes are far smaller than the Sun's, so they can only appear as tiny black dots crossing the Sun's face (a Transit of Venus). The section includes a caution that directly viewing the Sun during an eclipse — with the naked eye, sunglasses, binoculars, or a telescope — is extremely dangerous and can cause permanent blindness; the safe way to observe is to attend events organised by planetaria or astronomy clubs, or to project the Sun's image onto a screen using a mirror. A Fascinating Facts box notes that eclipses are known as grahan in Sanskrit and many Indian languages, and that ancient Indian astronomical texts — most notably the Surya Siddhanta — provided precise calculations to predict them. A Know a Scientist feature profiles M.K. Vainu Bappu, known as the father of modern Indian astronomy, who established telescopes at Nainital and Kavalur and travelled around the world to observe solar eclipses; the observatory at Kavalur is named in his honour.

12.3.2 Lunar eclipse occurs when the Earth comes between the Sun and the Moon, and the Earth's shadow falls on the Moon, blocking sunlight from reaching it. A total lunar eclipse occurs when the Moon is completely inside the Earth's shadow; the Moon does not disappear entirely but takes on a dark red colour because some sunlight is refracted through the Earth's atmosphere and reaches the Moon. A partial lunar eclipse occurs when only part of the Moon enters the Earth's shadow while the rest remains visible. Unlike solar eclipses, lunar eclipses can be safely observed with the naked eye. A Science and Society box recommends the free Stellarium computer application for finding upcoming solar and lunar eclipses visible from any location. A Fascinating Facts box describes the Kodaikanal Solar Observatory, established in 1899 in the Palani hills of southern India, which has provided over 125 years of solar observational data and is operated by the Indian Institute of Astrophysics (IIA), Bengaluru.
