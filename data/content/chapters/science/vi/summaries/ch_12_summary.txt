Chapter 12: Beyond Earth

The chapter opens with a narrative of two children in Nubra, Ladakh, who observe the night sky and experience a sense of wonder about stars, setting the context for exploring objects beyond Earth.

12.1 Stars and Constellations

Stars shine with their own light; some are bright and others dim. Groups of stars that appear to form recognisable patterns are called constellations — a term that in modern usage refers to defined regions of the sky rather than just the star patterns themselves. These patterns were historically used for navigation by sailors and travellers before modern technology. The International Astronomical Union (IAU) officially listed 88 constellations in the early twentieth century, dividing the entire sky into 88 regions. Notable constellations include Orion (depicted as a hunter with a three-star belt), Canis Major (which contains Sirius, the brightest star in the night sky), and Taurus. In Indian astronomy, the term nakshatra denotes specific stars or star groups — Betelgeuse in Orion is known as Ardra, the Pleiades cluster in Taurus as Krittika, and Aldebaran in Taurus as Rohini. Two widely known star patterns are the Big Dipper (in constellation Ursa Major, known in India as Saptarishi) and the Little Dipper (in Ursa Minor), which includes the Pole Star (Polaris, or Dhruva tara). The Pole Star appears nearly stationary in the North direction and is used to locate north in the Northern hemisphere. There is a student activity (Activity 12.1) in which students draw imaginary lines between stars to form their own patterns and invent stories about them.

12.2 Night Sky Watching

The visibility of stars depends on light pollution, smoke, dust, and obstructions such as tall buildings and trees; the night sky is best observed from open, dark locations away from city lights. Light pollution — excessive artificial light at night — reduces the number of stars visible and is a growing global concern; dark sky reserves and parks have been established to preserve dark skies for research. Not all stars and constellations are visible from all locations or at all times of the year; the Pole Star, for example, is not visible from the Southern Hemisphere. Sky mapping apps such as Sky Map and Stellarium help identify stars, constellations, and planets from any given location. The section provides practical preparation guidance for night sky watching: choosing a clear moonless night, waiting about half an hour for eyes to adjust to the dark, and using a compass and printed constellation images. Activities 12.2 and 12.3 are student activities in which learners attempt to locate the Big Dipper, the Pole Star, and the constellation Orion with the star Sirius in the actual night sky.

12.3 Our Solar System

The Sun

The Sun is the star closest to Earth — an extremely hot, spherical ball of gases that produces heat and light, making it the primary source of energy on Earth. Its distance from Earth is about 150 million km, defined as one astronomical unit (au), the standard unit for distances within the solar system. The Sun is about 100 times larger than Earth in diameter; it appears large due to its relative proximity, while far more distant stars appear only as points of light. The Sun regulates climate, seasons, weather, the water cycle, and winds, and is essential for plant growth and all life on Earth.

Planets

A planet is a large, nearly spherical object that revolves around the Sun; this movement is called revolution. The eight planets in order of increasing distance from the Sun are Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune. The inner four planets are smaller and have solid rocky surfaces; the outer four are much larger, mostly made of gas and ice, and have ring-like structures of dust and rocky material around them. The presence of an atmosphere affects a planet's temperature — Venus is hotter than Mercury despite being farther from the Sun because its thick atmosphere traps heat. Venus is known as the Morning Star or Evening Star; Mars is the Red Planet due to its reddish soil; Earth appears blue from space and is called the Blue Planet. Objects reclassified as dwarf planets — including Pluto — were redefined by the IAU in 2006 when similar small objects were discovered beyond Neptune. Activity 12.4 guides students to locate Venus at dawn (eastern horizon before sunrise) or dusk (western horizon after sunset).

Natural Satellites

Objects that move around planets are commonly called satellites; moons are the natural satellites of planets. Earth has one Moon, Mars has two moons, and Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune have large numbers of moons.

The Moon

Earth's natural satellite, the Moon, is about 3,84,000 km away and takes approximately 27 days to complete one revolution around the Earth. The Moon is about a quarter the size of Earth in diameter and has virtually no atmosphere. Its surface features circular bowl-like structures called craters, formed by the impact of asteroids and rocks from space; without atmosphere, water, or life, these features persist for very long periods. India launched Chandrayaan-1 (2008), Chandrayaan-2 (2019), and Chandrayaan-3 (July 2023); the Vikram lander of Chandrayaan-3 successfully soft-landed near the Moon's south pole on 23 August 2023, making India the first country to achieve this, and 23 August is now celebrated as National Space Day.

Asteroids

Asteroids are small, rocky, irregularly shaped objects that revolve around the Sun; many orbit in the region between Mars and Jupiter called the asteroid belt. Their sizes range from about 10 m to approximately 500 km.

Comets

Comets are objects from the outer Solar System composed of dust, gases, rocks, and ice; as they approach the Sun, frozen material evaporates to form a visible tail, but they appear dim as they move away. Many comets orbit the Sun periodically — Halley's Comet, for example, appears every 76 years and was last seen in 1986. The Solar System as a whole consists of the Sun, eight planets, their moons, and many smaller objects including asteroids and comets; all objects other than the Sun shine by reflecting sunlight.

12.4 The Milky Way Galaxy

The Milky Way Galaxy (Akasha Ganga) is visible from very dark locations as a faint extended band of light stretching across the night sky. A galaxy contains millions to billions of stars; our entire Solar System is a small part of the Milky Way Galaxy.

12.5 The Universe

Beyond the Milky Way, there are many other galaxies in outer space that scientists study to understand stars, galaxies, and the Universe as a whole. The search for life elsewhere in the Universe is ongoing and directed mainly toward exoplanets — planets discovered orbiting stars other than our Sun — but no evidence of life has been found yet.
