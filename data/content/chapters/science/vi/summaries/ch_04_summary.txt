Chapter 4: Exploring Magnets

The chapter opens with a narrative about Reshma, a student from coastal Kerala, who is writing a story about ancient sailors navigating by stars. When clouds obscure the stars in her story, her research leads her to discover the magnetic compass — a device used by travellers for centuries to find directions. This curiosity about compasses and magnets drives the chapter's inquiry. The chapter introduces the history of magnets: naturally occurring lodestones were the first magnets known in ancient times, followed by iron-based artificial magnets. Today, magnets are made from various materials and come in several shapes, including bar magnets, U-shaped magnets, and ring magnets.

4.1 Magnetic and Non-magnetic Materials

This section introduces the fundamental distinction between materials that are attracted to a magnet and those that are not. Students carry out Activity 4.1 ("Let us explore") in which they collect everyday objects, predict whether each will be attracted to a magnet, then test their predictions by bringing a magnet close to each object and recording the outcome in a table. Through this hands-on exploration, students discover that not all materials respond to a magnet. The section establishes the key terms: materials attracted towards a magnet are called magnetic materials (iron, nickel, cobalt, and some of their alloys are examples), while materials that are not attracted are called non-magnetic materials (wood, rubber, plastic, glass, etc.). The activity contains a student-executed investigation component.

4.2 Poles of Magnet

This section focuses on the unequal distribution of magnetic force along a magnet. Activity 4.2 ("Let us investigate") directs students to spread iron filings on a sheet of paper, place a bar magnet over them, and tap the paper to observe where the filings cluster. Students discover that the filings gather most densely near the two ends of the magnet, establishing that magnetic attraction is strongest at these ends. The chapter names these ends the two poles of the magnet — the North pole and the South pole — and notes that this concentration of filings at the poles is true for magnets of any shape, not just bar magnets. Crucially, the section establishes that poles always exist in pairs: a magnet cannot be broken to produce a single pole — every fragment, however small, always has both a North and a South pole. This activity is student-executed.

4.3 Finding Directions

This section develops the directional property of magnets. Activity 4.3 ("Let us experiment") asks students to suspend a bar magnet freely by a thread, allow it to come to rest, mark the line along which it rests, then disturb and repeat. Students find that the magnet always comes to rest along the same north–south line. The section explains that the end pointing north is the North-seeking pole (North pole) and the end pointing south is the South-seeking pole (South pole). Earth's own magnet-like nature is offered as the reason for this consistent alignment. A comparison with a non-magnetised iron bar (which rests in any direction) teaches students how to distinguish a magnet from an unmagnetised piece of metal. The section then explains the structure and use of a magnetic compass: a freely rotating magnetised needle mounted in a circular box with a directional dial, whose North-pointing end is typically painted red. Activity 4.4 ("Let us construct") guides students to magnetise an iron sewing needle by stroking it with a magnet, mount it through a cork floating on water, and use this homemade compass to confirm the north–south alignment. A "Do you know?" sidebar mentions the Indian historical device matsya-yantra, a fish-shaped magnetised iron piece used for sea navigation. Activities 4.3 and 4.4 are both student-executed.

4.4 Attraction and Repulsion between Magnets

This section explores how two magnets interact when brought close to each other. Activity 4.5 ("Let us experiment") uses two labelled bar magnets, one resting on pencils to roll freely, to test what happens when like and unlike poles are brought near each other. Students observe that unlike poles (North and South) attract each other, causing the resting magnet to move towards the approaching pole, while like poles (North–North or South–South) repel each other, causing the magnet to move away. Replacing one magnet with an iron bar reveals that both poles of the iron bar are attracted to both poles of the magnet — establishing repulsion as the definitive test for identifying a magnet. Activity 4.6 ("Let us experiment") uses a magnetic compass and a bar magnet: students bring the North pole of the bar magnet near the compass needle's North pole and observe repulsion (needle moves away), then bring the South pole near the North pole and observe attraction (needle moves closer). Activity 4.7 ("Let us investigate") probes whether non-magnetic materials — wood, cardboard, plastic, glass — block or diminish the magnetic effect on the compass needle. Students find no appreciable change in the needle's deflection when these materials are interposed, leading to the conclusion that magnetic effects can act through non-magnetic materials. Activities 4.5, 4.6, and 4.7 are all student-executed.

4.5 Fun with Magnets

The final section presents three playful applications that reinforce the chapter's concepts through informal experimentation: constructing a magnetic garland by hanging iron filings or pins from a magnet; guiding steel balls through a cardboard maze using a magnet held beneath; and extracting a steel paperclip from water using a magnet without wetting the fingers. A matchbox-car activity using two cars with like poles facing demonstrates repulsion in a dynamic context. A "More to know!" sidebar covers proper magnet storage — keeping pairs with unlike poles adjacent, separated by a wooden piece, with soft-iron keeper bars across the ends — and cautions against heating, dropping, hammering, or placing magnets near electronic devices. The final page illustrates various magnet shapes (bar, disc, cylindrical, ring, spherical) to show that poles always occur in pairs regardless of shape.
