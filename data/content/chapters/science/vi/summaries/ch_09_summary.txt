Chapter 9: Methods of Separation in Everyday Life

The chapter introduces a variety of everyday separation methods through a narrative of two siblings, Malli and Valli, travelling across India and encountering different separation techniques in each location. The concept of a mixture — when two or more substances are combined — is established as the context for why separation is needed.

Handpicking is the first method introduced. It is the process of manually picking out components from a mixture on the basis of differences in size, colour, and shape. The chapter uses the example of removing small stones and husk from grains, as well as picking out black pepper from pulao, to illustrate when handpicking is convenient — specifically when the quantity of the component to be removed is small and it can easily be distinguished by eye and hand.

Threshing is presented next as the method used to separate grains from harvested stalks. Farmers beat the stalks against a large wooden log so that the grains detach. The chapter notes that modern threshing machines (threshers) perform both threshing and winnowing simultaneously.

Winnowing follows threshing in the narrative. Activity 9.1 (Let us explore) guides students to rub peanuts between their palms and blow away the lighter skin, demonstrating that air separates heavier and lighter components. Winnowing is then defined as the method of separating heavier and lighter components of a mixture by wind or by blowing air. A traditional soop (bamboo tray) is used to demonstrate how farmers separate grain from husk in the open air.

Sieving is introduced when Mami uses a sieve to separate bran from wheat flour before kneading dough. The chapter explains that sieving allows fine particles to pass through holes in the sieve while larger particles are retained on top. Sieving is applicable when the components of a solid–solid mixture differ in particle size.

Evaporation is explained in the context of obtaining common salt from seawater. Seawater kept in shallow pits is exposed to sunlight and air; the water evaporates, leaving behind a solid mixture from which salt is purified. Activity 9.2 (Let us observe and create) has students spread a salt solution on dark paper, allow it to dry, and observe the salt residue left behind as the water evaporates. Activity 9.3 (Let us investigate) extends this by heating a salt solution in a china dish over a spirit lamp until the water boils away, leaving the salt as residue in the dish. Evaporation is defined as the process in which a liquid is converted into its vapour and is used to separate a dissolved solid from a liquid.

Sedimentation and Decantation are introduced through the tea-making scene at Dada's home. Sedimentation is the process of heavier insoluble components settling to the bottom of a liquid. Decantation is the process of carefully tilting and pouring off the liquid while the sediment remains at the bottom. The chapter notes that decantation does not completely separate all particles and is used in everyday tasks such as washing rice and pulses. It is also noted that oil and water can be separated by decantation since oil forms a separate layer.

Filtration is introduced as a more complete method of separating insoluble solid particles from a liquid. A tea strainer is the everyday example — it collects tea leaves while allowing liquid tea to pass through. The chapter discusses how cloth (with fine pores between woven threads) can also act as a filter. Activity 9.4 (Let us experiment) demonstrates laboratory filtration: a filter paper folded into a cone is placed in a funnel over a conical flask; muddy water poured through the filter paper yields mud as the residue on the paper and clear water (filtrate) in the flask. Activity 9.5 (Let us design and create) extends this by asking students to design a working water filter from low-cost materials. The chapter notes that other materials — cotton, charcoal, and sand — can also serve as filters depending on particle size.

Churning is the method used to separate butter from curd. A large mathni (churner) is rotated in curd; the butter, being lighter, floats to the top and is skimmed off while the buttermilk remains behind. Chhach (buttermilk) encountered at a dhaba in Bhopal prompts this discussion.

Magnetic Separation is the final method presented. In Shillong, iron nails accidentally dropped in sawdust are retrieved by passing a magnet through the mixture; all iron nails are attracted to the magnet while the sawdust is not. Substances attracted to a magnet are called magnetic substances; iron is the primary example. Separation of magnetic substances from non-magnetic substances using a magnet is called magnetic separation. The chapter notes industrial use: cranes fitted with magnets separate scrap iron from heaps of industrial waste for recycling.

Activity 9.6 (Let us play) is a consolidation game where students match separation scenarios (e.g., separating small stones from pulses, separating salt from salt solution, separating oil from water) to the appropriate method, helping them review all nine methods covered in the chapter.
