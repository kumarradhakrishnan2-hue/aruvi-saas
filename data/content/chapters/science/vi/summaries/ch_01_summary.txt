Chapter 1: The Wonderful World of Science

Science is defined in this chapter as a way of thinking, observing and doing things to understand the world and to uncover the secrets of the universe. The chapter opens by positioning science as a continuation of the curiosity and exploration that children practise from childhood, framing the Grade 6 textbook — titled "Curiosity" — as the vehicle for this journey. The central metaphor used is that of a giant, unending jigsaw puzzle: every new discovery adds a piece, every piece leads to more questions, and sometimes earlier pieces need to be repositioned as understanding improves.

The section "What is Science?" introduces science as omnipresent — present from ocean depths to outer space, from the kitchen to the playground. It emphasises that science is not a fixed body of facts but a living, evolving enterprise. The chapter stresses that the single most important attitude for a science learner is curiosity, and that new discoveries frequently revise earlier understanding rather than merely confirming it.

The section "What will we explore with the help of this book?" previews the thematic sweep of the Grade 6 course. It introduces the living world — the diversity of plants and animals on Earth, and how organisms grow and change. It then moves to food and nutrition, noting India's diverse cuisines and the question of what food is made of. The chapter previews the study of materials — paper, metal, plastic, rubber, magnets, cloth — and raises the question of how different materials are identified and separated. Water is introduced as a topic of inquiry: its states (liquid, ice, steam), the phenomenon of rain, and temperature measurement. The solar system and beyond are also briefly flagged as part of the course scope. This preview section functions to demonstrate that science connects to everyday life rather than being an abstract academic exercise.

The section "How can we try to find answers to our questions on our own?" introduces the scientific method through the relatable example of a pen that stops writing. The student's natural response — forming a guess (ink finished), testing it (checking the refill), revising the guess if wrong (ink dried up), and testing again — is identified as a direct analogy for how science works. The five steps of the scientific method are presented in sequence: observation of something interesting or puzzling; formulation of a question; proposing a possible answer (hypothesis/guess); testing through experiment or further observation; and analysis of results to evaluate the guess. The chapter widens the frame by showing that the scientific method is not exclusive to laboratories — a cook wondering why dal spilled from a pressure cooker, a bicycle repair person locating an air leak, and an electrician diagnosing a faulty bulb are all offered as examples of everyday scientific thinking. The chapter emphasises that collaborative inquiry — working with peers — is central to science, and that not all questions will be answered in Grade 6; the course is the beginning of a five-year or longer journey.

Three student activities are embedded in the chapter. Activity 1.1 asks students to write about a problem they personally tried to solve and describe the steps they took — a reflective writing task. Activity 1.2 asks students to describe a daily-life situation in which someone was following the scientific method — a recognition and application task. Activity 1.3 asks students to pose a "Why?" question of their own and attempt to describe how they would find the answer — an inquiry-generation task. All three activities are discussion and writing tasks; none involve physical materials or apparatus.
