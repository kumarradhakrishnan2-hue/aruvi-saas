Chapter 6: Materials Around Us

6.1 Observing Objects Around Us

This section introduces students to the idea that all objects around us are made from materials such as paper, wood, cloth, glass, metal, plastic, and clay. Through a classroom conversation between characters Ghulan, Sheeta, and their teacher Madam Vidya, students are prompted to notice and compare the objects they bring to school every day. The key term introduced is material, defined as any substance used to create an object. The section includes Activity 6.1, a listing activity where students identify objects around them and the materials they are made of.

6.2 How to Group Materials?

This section establishes that objects and materials can be grouped based on shared properties such as shape, colour, hardness, softness, shine, or material composition. Activity 6.2 asks students to group a set of pictured objects using any common property they choose, prompting reflection on how different students may group the same objects differently. The section introduces the term classification, defined as the method of arranging objects into groups based on a common property. Students learn that an object can be made from more than one material, and that a single material can be used to make more than one object. The purpose of choosing a material for an object depends on the material's properties and the intended use of the object. Activity 6.3 asks students to think about materials suitable for making a tumbler, and Activity 6.4 compares the bounce of tennis, cricket, and hand exercise balls to explore how material determines an object's behaviour.

6.3 What are the Different Properties of Materials?

This section is the core of the chapter and explores several measurable and observable properties that distinguish materials from one another.

6.3.1 Observe and identify appearance of materials

This subsection teaches students to classify materials based on their appearance, particularly their lustre. Materials with shiny surfaces are described as lustrous; these are typically metals such as iron, copper, zinc, aluminium, and gold. However, the section notes that metals can lose their lustre over time due to exposure to air and moisture, and lustre is often visible only on freshly cut surfaces. Non-lustrous materials such as paper, wood, rubber, and jute do not have a shiny surface. The section cautions students that not all shiny materials are metals, since some surfaces are made shiny by polishing or coating with plastic or wax. Students sort collected material samples based on appearance in the sorting challenge activity.

6.3.2 Which materials are hard?

This subsection distinguishes between hard and soft materials. Materials that are difficult to compress or scratch are called hard, while those that can be compressed or scratched easily are called soft. Students test hardness by pressing materials with their hands and scratching surfaces using a metal key. The section notes that hardness is a relative property — for example, rubber is harder than sponge but softer than iron. Activity 6.5 asks students to feel a set of everyday objects and classify them as hard or soft while identifying their constituent materials.

6.3.3 Explore materials through which one can see or cannot see

This subsection introduces three categories of materials based on how much light passes through them. Materials through which objects can be seen clearly are called transparent; examples include glass, water, air, and cellophane paper. Materials through which objects cannot be seen at all are called opaque; examples include wood, cardboard, and metals. Materials through which objects can be seen but not clearly are called translucent; examples include butter paper and frosted glass. A narrative involving children playing hide-and-seek is used to contextualise the three categories. Activity 6.6 asks students to classify a set of given objects — glass tumbler, butter paper, eraser, frosted glass, wooden board, and window glass — into the three categories.

6.3.4 What is soluble in water; what is not?

This subsection explores how materials behave when mixed with water, distinguishing between soluble and insoluble materials. Materials that dissolve completely in water are described as soluble, while those that do not mix with water even after stirring are described as insoluble. Activity 6.7 uses five substances — sugar, salt, chalk powder, sand, and sawdust — stirred separately in water to demonstrate this difference through direct observation. The section notes that some liquids also mix completely with water while others form a separate layer, and that some gases such as oxygen dissolve in water, making aquatic life possible. The role of water as a solvent in the human body is briefly highlighted. A contextual "Make your own ORS!" box explains how sugar and salt dissolve to make an Oral Rehydration Solution.

6.3.5 How heavy or light?

This subsection introduces the concept of mass as the property that determines whether an object is heavier or lighter. Activity 6.8 has students fill three identical paper cups with water, sand, and pebbles respectively, predict and then measure which is heaviest using a balance. From this activity, students learn that the property of heaviness or lightness is measured in terms of mass — more mass means heavier. The section notes that weight is commonly used in everyday language to mean mass, and that the distinction between the two will be explored in higher classes.

6.3.6 Space and volume

This subsection introduces the concept of volume as the space occupied by matter. Using a classroom scenario where students cannot sit because bags occupy their seats, Madam Vidya introduces the idea that all materials occupy space. An activity comparing two identical glass tumblers filled to different levels with water demonstrates that the amount of water — and therefore its volume — differs. Students observe that the water in the half-filled tumbler occupies less space than in the nearly full tumbler. The section connects volume to everyday labelling: water bottles marked as 1 L, 500 mL, or 200 mL indicate the volume of water they contain. Units introduced include litre (L) and millilitre (mL).

6.4 What is Matter?

This section consolidates the chapter by introducing the concept of matter. Students have encountered several properties — lustre, hardness, transparency, solubility — not all of which apply to every material. However, mass and volume are identified as two properties shared by all materials. The section defines matter as anything that occupies space and has mass. Materials are described as types of matter used in the creation of objects. Units of mass (gram and kilogram; SI unit is kilogram, kg) and volume (litre and millilitre; SI unit is cubic metre, m³; 1 m³ = 1000 L) are introduced along with correct notation conventions. The section concludes by connecting classification of the non-living world to prior learning about classification of the living world, and includes a "Do you know?" box on Ayurveda's ancient system of twenty paired properties (gunas) used to describe all physical matter.
