Chapter 7: Temperature and its Measurement

7.1 Hot or Cold?

This section establishes that our sense of touch is unreliable as a measure of hotness or coldness. Using a narrative about Lambok and his sister Phiban, the chapter opens with the question of whether fever can always be detected by touch alone, prompting the need for a more reliable method. Activity 7.1 ("Let us investigate") is a student-executed hands-on activity in which students dip their hands into containers of warm water, tap water, and ice-cold water in sequence and record both predictions and observations. The activity demonstrates that the same sample of water can feel warm to one hand and cool to another simultaneously, establishing through direct experience that sensory perception of temperature is subjective and context-dependent. The key inference is that we cannot always rely upon our sense of touch to correctly determine whether a body is hot or cold.

7.2 Temperature

This section introduces temperature as the reliable, objective measure of the hotness or coldness of a body. It defines temperature conceptually: a hotter body has a higher temperature than a colder body, and the difference in temperature between two bodies quantifies how hot one is relative to the other. A thermometer is defined as any device that measures temperature. The section also introduces two main types of thermometers students will encounter: clinical thermometers, used specifically for measuring human body temperature, and laboratory thermometers, used for a wider range of purposes.

7.3 Measuring Temperature

This is the principal section of the chapter, covering three subsections on different thermometer types and temperature scales.

7.3.1 Clinical thermometer

This subsection describes the digital clinical thermometer as the type students are most likely to use. It explains that the thermometer measures body temperature when its tip is placed in contact with the body, typically under the tongue, and displays readings digitally using heat sensors. The unit used is degree Celsius (°C) on the Celsius scale. A "Do you know?" sidebar notes that earlier mercury thermometers have been largely replaced by digital ones due to mercury's toxicity; it also introduces infrared (non-contact) thermometers used during events such as the COVID-19 pandemic. Activity 7.2 ("Let us measure") is a student-executed activity in which students measure their own and their classmates' body temperatures using a digital clinical thermometer, recording readings in a table, and following precautions around hygiene and handling. The subsection discusses why not every healthy person reads exactly 37.0 °C — body temperature is an average across a large population and is influenced by age, time of day, and activity level — and notes that human body temperature does not normally fall below 35 °C or rise above 42 °C. It also introduces the Fahrenheit scale (°F), where normal body temperature is 98.6 °F, and the Kelvin scale (K), noting that kelvin is the SI unit of temperature. The conversion formula from Celsius to Kelvin (K = °C + 273.15) is provided in a "More to know!" box. Conventions for writing temperature units and scale names are explained, including capitalisation rules and the absence of the degree sign with kelvin.

7.3.2 Laboratory thermometer

This subsection describes the structure of a typical school laboratory thermometer: a sealed, long, narrow glass tube with a liquid-filled bulb at one end, a narrow liquid column visible above the bulb, and a Celsius scale marked along the tube. The liquid column rises or falls with temperature and the reading is taken at the top of the column. The liquid used is typically alcohol (coloured red) or mercury. Activity 7.3 ("Let us observe") is a student observation activity in which students identify the lowest and highest temperatures a given thermometer can measure, establishing its range (typically −10 °C to 110 °C for the example shown). Activity 7.4 ("Let us observe and calculate") requires students to calculate the smallest temperature value one division of the thermometer represents by dividing the temperature difference between two numbered marks by the number of divisions between them — for the example thermometer this is 1 °C per division. Both activities develop skills in reading and interpreting a scale. Activity 7.5 ("Let us measure") is a student activity in which students dip the laboratory thermometer into warm water and read the temperature while it is still immersed; the subsection emphasises that the reading must be taken while immersed, the thermometer held vertically, and the eye kept level with the liquid surface. Precautions for handling the fragile glass thermometer are provided. Activity 7.6 ("Let us compare") presents temperature readings of boiling water recorded by multiple students in Shillong, all slightly below 100 °C, and asks students to discuss why differences exist between readings and why the boiling point differs from the textbook value of 100 °C — implicitly raising the concept that boiling point varies with altitude. A "More to do!" prompt asks students to observe the constant temperature of both boiling water and melting ice using teacher-set-up apparatus.

7.3.3 Air temperature

This subsection introduces the room thermometer as a device for measuring approximate room temperature. It connects temperature measurement to weather reporting, explaining that weather services record maximum and minimum daily air temperatures. Activity 7.7 ("Let us analyse") is a student data-collection and analysis activity in which students record maximum and minimum air temperatures from weather reports over ten successive days and examine variation patterns. The subsection notes that air temperature typically rises towards summer and falls in winter. A "More to know!" sidebar explains that air temperature is a key weather parameter monitored globally and used in weather forecasting. The section closes with a profile of Anna Mani (1918–2001), the Indian scientist known as the "Weather Woman of India," who designed numerous weather instruments, reducing India's dependence on imported instruments, and contributed to wind and solar energy research.
