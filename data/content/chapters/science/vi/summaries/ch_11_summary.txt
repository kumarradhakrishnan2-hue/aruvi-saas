Chapter 11: Nature's Treasures

Section 11.1 introduces air as a vital natural resource essential for the survival of all living beings. Students perform a breathing exercise (Activity 11.1) to experience first-hand how quickly the body requires oxygen—holding the breath causes discomfort because the body cannot function without it. Air is described as a mixture of gases that surrounds the Earth: nitrogen makes up 78%, oxygen 21%, and argon, carbon dioxide, and other gases together account for the remaining 1%. The section introduces the concept that moving air is called wind. Students construct a paper pinwheel called a firki (Activity 11.2) to observe wind's ability to cause rotation, and this is connected to the working of a windmill. Windmills convert wind energy into useful work—running flour mills, pumping water from wells, and generating electricity. Major windmill farms in India are cited: Muppandal in Tamil Nadu, Jaisalmer Wind Park in Rajasthan, and Brahmanvel Farm in Maharashtra.

Section 11.2 introduces water as another essential and precious natural resource. Although water covers about two-thirds of the Earth's surface, most of it is saline—found in oceans and seas—and is not fit for drinking, agriculture, or industrial use. Freshwater is available as ice sheets and snow, rivers, lakes, ponds, wells, and underground sources, but only a very small fraction of this is easily accessible. The section includes Activity 11.3, in which students examine daily activities such as hand washing, cooking, and gardening to identify how water is wasted and suggest ways to reduce that wastage. Conservation strategies highlighted include turning off taps, fixing leakages, recycling water, and rainwater harvesting. Rainwater harvesting is described as an age-old practice in India; traditional stepwells—known as Bawadi in Rajasthan and Vav in Gujarat—are given as examples that store both rainwater and water seeping from nearby ponds and lakes. The section stresses that access to freshwater is unequal across India and that responsible, judicious use is necessary to prevent pollution and shortage.

Section 11.3 establishes the Sun as the primary source of energy on Earth. Sunlight enables plants to produce food through photosynthesis, making the Sun the foundational energy source for all life—animals eat plants, and both animals and humans ultimately depend on solar energy. A discussion between characters about a grazing cow clarifies the distinction between direct and indirect solar energy. The section identifies practical uses of solar energy: solar panels on rooftops, street lights, and traffic signals capture sunlight and generate electricity; solar cookers use the Sun's heat directly for cooking; and solar water heaters warm water for domestic use.

Section 11.4 introduces forests as large areas with dense growth of various types of plants—herbs, shrubs, and trees. Forests are natural habitats for wild animals, birds, and insects, providing food and shelter. The diversity of life forms in forests ensures that food is available for every living being in the ecosystem. However, forest cover has been declining due to large-scale cutting of trees, and it takes many years to grow a new forest or restore a lost one. The Chipko movement—started in the early 1970s in Uttarakhand where local women hugged trees to prevent them from being felled—is presented as an example of community-led forest conservation. Van Mahotsav, a week-long national festival in July during which new trees are planted, is mentioned as an organised effort to increase green cover. The section also notes that fallen leaves decompose on the forest floor, enriching the soil with nutrients and cycling materials back to new plants.

Section 11.5 introduces soil, rocks, and minerals as important and irreplaceable natural resources. Soil is formed over thousands of years by the disintegration of rocks caused by the combined action of the Sun, water, and living organisms. Activity 11.4 asks students to collect soil samples from different locations, observe their colour and texture with the naked eye and a magnifying lens, and record differences in a table—noting that soils from different places vary because they contain different materials. Rocks are used widely in construction: slate for roofing, laterite as bricks, and granite, sandstone, and marble in buildings and monuments. Rocks have also been used since ancient times to make tools. Rocks are composed of minerals, from which important metals such as aluminium, gold, copper, and iron are extracted. Minerals are used in manufacturing airplanes, cars, jewellery, cosmetics, and electronic equipment—a basic mobile phone alone contains about a dozen minerals. Because rocks take thousands to millions of years to form, the section stresses the need to conserve and use them responsibly.

Section 11.6 introduces fossil fuels—petroleum, natural gas, and coal—as resources formed over millions of years from the remains of ancient microorganisms and plants buried deep underground. Activity 11.5 asks students to survey vehicles in their neighbourhood and record the types of fuels each uses. Petrol and diesel are derived from petroleum; natural gas is used for cooking, electricity generation, and as Compressed Natural Gas (CNG) in vehicles; and coal is used primarily for producing electricity. The section notes that burning fossil fuels releases smoke and carbon dioxide, contributing to large-scale air pollution. Since fossil fuels exist in limited quantities and take millions of years to form, the section urges reduced dependence through actions such as walking, cycling, and using public transport.

Section 11.7 introduces the classification of natural resources as renewable or non-renewable. Natural resources are all resources provided by nature—air, water, sunlight, forests, soil, rocks, minerals, and fossil fuels; resources created by human beings using natural resources are called human-made resources. Renewable resources are those that are renewed, replenished, or restored by natural processes within a reasonable period of time; air, water, and forests are given as examples. Non-renewable resources exist in limited quantities and are not replenished within a reasonable time frame; minerals, soil, rocks, coal, petroleum, and natural gas are examples.

Section 11.8 closes the chapter by connecting resource knowledge to everyday life. Students observe through Activity 11.6 that almost every daily activity—washing clothes, making clay toys, cooking breakfast—relies on natural resources directly or indirectly. The section reinforces that all living beings depend on natural resources for survival, and that responsible, waste-free use is essential to meet present needs without harming the environment for future generations. The chapter ends with the Gandhian quote: "Earth provides enough to satisfy every man's need but not for every man's greed."
