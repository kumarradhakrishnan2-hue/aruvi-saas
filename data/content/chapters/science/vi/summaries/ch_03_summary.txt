Chapter 3: Mindful Eating: A Path to a Healthy Body

3.1 What Do We Eat?

The chapter opens by establishing that food is an essential component of daily life and that people eat a variety of food items across the week. Students are invited to record their own food intake over a week and compare it with classmates, discovering that there is considerable variety in the food eaten by different individuals. This variety in food habits is then extended to a national scale, prompting enquiry into why food differs across India's states.

3.1.1 Food in different regions

This subsection explores the diversity of traditional food across Indian states. Students investigate locally grown crops and traditional dishes in different states, using examples such as Punjab (makki di roti, sarson da saag, lassi), Karnataka (idli, dosa, sambhar, ragi mudde, buttermilk, coffee), and Manipur (rice, eromba, singju, black tea). The key explanatory idea is that the traditional food of any state is typically based on the crops grown in that region — a relationship between local agriculture, soil type, and climate. Beyond crop availability, the chapter notes that food choices also vary according to taste preferences, culture, and traditions. A student activity (Activity 3.2) requires researching food and crops of several states and filling a comparison table.

3.1.2 How have cooking practices changed over time?

This subsection examines how culinary practices have evolved. Students conduct interviews with elderly people to collect firsthand accounts of changes in food habits and cooking methods. The chapter uses contrasting image pairs to illustrate the shift from traditional tools — the chulha (clay stove) and sil-batta (stone grinder) — to modern equivalents — the gas stove and electric grinder. The section attributes these changes to technological development, improved transportation, and better communication. Students are asked to find out other earlier forms of cooking and grinding and to reflect on why practices changed.

3.2 What are the Components of Food?

Using the setting of a school Traditional Food Festival, the chapter introduces the concept of nutrients. The opening question — why do we feel tired when we skip a meal — leads to the identification of carbohydrates as a primary energy source. Glucose is given as an example of a carbohydrate that provides instant energy. Cereals (wheat, rice, maize, bajra), vegetables (potato, sweet potato), and fruits (banana, pineapple, mango, sugarcane) are listed as sources of carbohydrates, including common sugar. Fats are introduced through the example of laddoos made with ghee, nuts, and seeds eaten in winter for warmth and energy. Fat sources from both plants (groundnuts, walnuts, almonds, pumpkin seeds, sunflower seeds, soya bean, sesame, coconut) and animals (butter, curd) are identified. Carbohydrates and fats are together called energy-giving foods. A sidebar notes that polar bears store fat for hibernation, illustrating fat as stored energy.

Proteins are introduced next as body-building foods that help in growth and repair. Plant sources include pulses, beans, peas, and nuts (gram, moong, tuar dal, kidney beans, soya bean, peas); animal sources include milk, paneer, eggs, fish, and chicken. Edible mushrooms are noted as a protein source. The importance of adequate protein for growing children and sportspersons is highlighted.

Two historical cases lead into vitamins and minerals. Case 1 describes how Scottish physician James Lind (1746) observed that sailors suffering from scurvy — bleeding and swollen gums — recovered when they ate lemons and oranges, establishing that Vitamin C prevents scurvy. Case 2 describes how Indian scientists in the 1960s found swelling at the front of the neck (goitre) prevalent in Himalayan and northern plains populations due to iodine deficiency in soil; supplementing common salt with iodine (iodised salt) reduced the condition. A comprehensive chart (Fig. 3.5) presents seven vitamins and minerals: Vitamin A (sources — papaya, carrot, mango, milk; deficiency — loss of vision/night blindness), Vitamin B1 (sources — legumes, nuts, whole grains, milk products; deficiency — beriberi), Vitamin C (sources — amla, guava, green chilli, orange, lemon; deficiency — scurvy), Vitamin D (sources — sunlight, milk, butter, fish, eggs; deficiency — rickets), Calcium (sources — milk, soya milk, curd, cheese, paneer; deficiency — bone and tooth decay), Iodine (sources — seaweed, water chestnut, iodised salt; deficiency — goitre), and Iron (sources — green leafy vegetables, beetroot, pomegranate; deficiency — anaemia). The chapter notes that not all nutrients come from plants and animals — salt comes from seawater or rocks, and Vitamin D is synthesised by the body on exposure to sunlight. The collective term nutrients is defined: food components that provide energy, support growth, help repair, protect the body from diseases, and maintain bodily functions. Vitamins and minerals are specifically called protective nutrients, required in small amounts but essential for health. The chapter also cautions that some nutrients like Vitamin C are lost during cooking with high heat, and washing cut vegetables may cause vitamin loss — though washing before consumption is still strongly recommended. A student survey activity (Activity 3.4) asks students to study the chart, visit their neighbourhood to identify any signs of deficiency diseases, correlate symptoms with diet, and suggest dietary improvements.

3.3 How to Test Different Components of Food?

This section introduces simple food-testing procedures for starch, fat, and protein. The iodine test for starch (Activity 3.5) uses diluted iodine solution on food samples; a blue-black colour change confirms the presence of starch in items like potato and boiled rice, while cucumber and oil do not react. The paper-patch test for fat (Activity 3.6) involves pressing food items on paper; an oily patch that remains translucent against light confirms fat, as in peanuts and butter. The protein test (Activity 3.7) is described as a teacher demonstration: food samples are treated with copper sulfate solution and then caustic soda solution; a violet colour indicates protein, seen in boiled gram, peanuts, and soya bean. Students record results in a comparison table (Table 3.3), noting that peanuts test positive for both fat and protein, illustrating that a single food can contain multiple nutrients.

3.4 Balanced Diet

The chapter distinguishes that nutritional requirements vary by age, gender, physical activity, health status, and lifestyle — individuals do not all need the same type or quantity of nutrients. A balanced diet is defined as one that contains all essential nutrients, roughage, and water in the right amount for proper growth and development. Students check whether their own weekly food list (from Activity 3.1) qualifies as balanced and consider what they would need to add. The concept of junk food is introduced: foods high in sugar and fat but low in protein, minerals, vitamins, and dietary fibre — examples include potato wafers, candy bars, and carbonated drinks. A comparative nutritional table for potato wafers versus roasted chana illustrates the difference: wafers have 536 kcal, 35 g fat, and low dietary fibre per 100 g versus roasted chana's 355 kcal, 6.26 g fat, 18.64 g protein, and 16.8 g dietary fibre. Frequent consumption of junk food is linked to obesity and health problems.

3.5 Millets: Nutrition-rich Cereals

Millets — including jowar, bajra, ragi, and sanwa — are identified as small-grained native crops of India that can be cultivated across a range of climatic conditions. They are rich in vitamins, minerals (iron and calcium), and dietary fibres, earning them the name nutri-cereals. The section notes that millets have been a part of the Indian diet for centuries and have regained popularity for their health benefits. A sidebar explains food fortification — the addition of nutrients during food processing — and mentions FSSAI as the government agency regulating food quality in India.

3.6 Food Miles: From Farm to Our Plate

This section traces the journey of food from production to consumption using wheat and chapati as an example. The steps include farming, threshing and winnowing, storage, grinding and packing, and transport to retail shops. The total distance a food item travels from producer to consumer is defined as its food miles. The chapter argues for reducing food miles because shorter supply chains lower transport costs and pollution, support local farmers, and keep food fresher and more nutritious. The section concludes with a call to eat locally grown and plant-based food wherever possible, noting this benefits both personal health and the environment. Students are encouraged to value the labour of farmers and to avoid food wastage.
