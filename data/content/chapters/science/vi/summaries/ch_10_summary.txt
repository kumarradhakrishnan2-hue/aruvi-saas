Chapter 10: Living Creatures: Exploring their Characteristics

The chapter opens with a narrative in which students Avadhi and Aayush encounter shells during a morning walk and wonder whether a non-moving shell could be home to a living snail. This curiosity drives the class discussion that forms the backbone of the chapter — how to distinguish the living from the non-living.

Activity 10.1: Let us record
Students look around the classroom and list objects — a pencil, a book, a pigeon, a car, and a plant — then classify each as living or non-living and give reasons. Columns for initial guesses and correct answers are provided. The activity surfaces the core question: what shared characteristics define all living beings?

10.1 What Sets the Living Apart from the Non-living?
This section establishes the essential characteristics that define living beings by guiding students to compare themselves with non-living objects such as cars. Movement is examined first — students note that even plants, which do not move from place to place, do exhibit movement. Examples include the insectivorous plant Drosera, whose hair-like sticky projections trap insects through inward movement, and climber plants that wind around nearby objects. Growth is introduced next: living beings increase in size over time (a child cannot wear clothes worn four years ago), and plants also grow. Nutrition is identified as the requirement for food to support growth and development.

The section continues with respiration — the process of inhaling and exhaling. Students count breaths before and after different levels of physical activity. Breathing is explained as part of respiration; plants respire too, taking in and releasing air through tiny pores called stomata on leaf surfaces. Excretion is then introduced as the removal of waste products from the body — sweat and urine in animals; excess water and minerals appearing as droplets on leaves (as seen in grasses and roses) in plants. All living beings excrete.

Sensitivity to stimuli is explained through the example of reacting to stepping on a thorn or touching a hot object — any event that prompts a living being to respond is called a stimulus. Plants also respond: the touch-me-not (chhui-mui) plant folds its leaves when touched; certain plants including the amla tree fold their leaves after sunset. Finally, reproduction — the production of new individuals of one's own kind — is identified as necessary for the continuity of life. The section closes by defining death as the inability to exhibit any of these characteristics despite the availability of all necessary resources.

10.2 Essential Conditions for Germination of a Seed
This section investigates what conditions a seed needs in order to germinate. Students are asked to consider a seed's classification as living or non-living before performing the activity. Activity 10.2 (Let us experiment) sets up four pots of bean seeds under contrasting conditions: Pot A in sunlight without water, Pot B in sunlight with excess water, Pot C in darkness with moist soil, and Pot D in sunlight with moist soil. Students predict and observe which pots show germination over 7–10 days, recording results in Table 10.2.

The analysis establishes that water and air (not sunlight) are the essential conditions for seed germination. Water softens the seed coat and enables the embryo inside to develop into a plant. Seeds use the air trapped between soil particles for germination, and those soil spaces also allow roots to grow easily. Light is not generally required for germination; after germination, however, sunlight is needed for the seedling to grow further. A "Do you know?" sidebar notes exceptions — Coleus and Petunia require light to germinate, while Calendula and Zinnia require darkness.

10.3 Growth and Movement in Plants
This section examines how roots and shoots respond to gravity and light. Activity 10.3 (Let us design) uses three glass beakers, each holding a germinated bean or gram seedling attached to a glass plate with wet blotting paper. Beaker A holds an upright seedling in all-round sunlight; Beaker B holds an inverted seedling in all-round sunlight; Beaker C holds an upright seedling in a closed box where light enters only from one direction. Students predict then observe the direction of root and shoot growth in each setup.

The results show three principles: (1) an upright plant's root grows downward and shoot grows upward; (2) an inverted plant's root bends and grows downward while the shoot bends and grows upward; (3) when sunlight comes from one direction only, the shoot grows toward the light source while the root continues to grow downward. The section concludes that shoots grow toward sunlight (phototropism) while roots grow downward (gravitropism). A "Know a scientist" sidebar profiles Jagadish Chandra Bose (1858–1937), who built the crescograph to measure plant responses to stimuli including light, heat, electricity, and gravity.

10.4 Life Cycle of a Plant
This section traces the complete life cycle of a plant using the bean plant as the example. Activity 10.4 (Let us explore) asks students to plant a bean seed and observe it over three months, recording dates, observations, and sketches in Table 10.4. Students track when the first shoot appears, when the first flower appears, when the flower dries and a pod forms, and what happens to the plant after the pod containing seeds is produced.

The chapter presents the five-stage life cycle: Stage I (seed), Stage II (seed germination), Stage III (appearance of leaves), Stage IV (appearance of flowers), Stage V (appearance of fruits/pod with seeds), followed by death of the plant. The pod produces seeds that germinate into the next generation of bean plants, completing the cycle. The life cycle is defined as the entire process from seed to plant to the next generation of seeds. A plant is considered dead when it stops growing and all life activities cease despite favourable conditions.

10.5 Life Cycle of Animals
This section introduces the concept that animals, like plants, undergo distinct changes through their life cycles. Two animal examples are developed in detail: the mosquito and the frog.

10.5.1 Life cycle of a mosquito
Students are introduced to mosquitoes as disease-transmitting insects and to the public health advice not to allow stagnant water — the breeding habitat of mosquitoes. Activity 10.5 (Let us analyse) involves a safety audit for stagnant water in school or home surroundings, where students may observe larvae and pupae in water bodies such as desert coolers and open containers. Students analyse the sequence of the larval and pupal stages through observation. The mosquito life cycle has four stages: egg (Stage I), larva (Stage II), pupa (Stage III), and adult mosquito (Stage IV). The adult female lays eggs on or near water and lives 10–15 days. Significant changes in body shape and structure occur across each stage. Mosquito larvae and pupae come to the water surface repeatedly to access air for respiration; spraying kerosene on stagnant water blocks air access at the surface, killing the larvae and pupae. A "Do you know?" sidebar connects this to the silk moth, which also passes through four identical life stages; larvae secrete thread wrapped around themselves before becoming pupae, and these fibres are used to make silk fabric.

10.5.2 Life cycle of a frog
Activity 10.6 (Let us analyse) takes students (and teacher narration) to a shallow pond during the rainy season, where they observe a white jelly-like cluster of frog eggs called spawn. Students observe images (Fig. 10.7) of six stages (labelled A through F) and determine their correct sequence by recording visible changes in Table 10.5. The frog life cycle has four broad stages: Stage IA (spawn, Day 1) → Stage IB (embryo, Day 3–4) → Stage IIA (tadpole with tail, Day 7–10) → Stage IIB (tadpole with legs, 8–10 weeks) → Stage III (froglet, 12 weeks) → Stage IV (adult frog, 14 weeks). Tadpoles use tails to swim in water; as froglets develop, legs strengthen for jumping and tails disappear. Adult frogs are amphibians — living both on land and in water. The section invites discussion of how body features match each stage's habitat.

The chapter closes by emphasising that the changes living beings undergo across their life cycles are essential for survival and continuity of their kind, and calls on students to care for the living world and its habitats.
