Chapter 2 — Diversity in the Living World
Science | Grade VI | Curiosity (NCERT)

2.1 Diversity in Plants and Animals Around Us

The chapter opens with a nature walk organised by science teacher Madam Sulekha, accompanied by Dr Raghu, a scientist, and Maniram chacha, an expert in identifying plants and animals and mimicking bird calls. The walk serves as an immersive introduction to the variety of living things: grasses, bushes, large trees, birds with unique chirps, butterflies, and monkeys. Students are guided to observe with all senses — sight, smell, and sound — while respecting living creatures and not disturbing them. The key concept introduced is biodiversity: the variety of plants and animals found in a particular region. Plants observed during the walk exhibit variation in stem type (hard or soft, thick or thin), leaf shape and arrangement, and flower colour, shape, and scent. Animals differ in where they live, what they eat, and how they move — some on land, some on trees, some in water, and some like frogs in both. The section contains two student activities: Activity 2.1 (nature walk observation recorded in tables for plants and animals) and Activity 2.2 (class appreciation exercise where students draw a favourite plant or animal, leading to a discussion on how many varieties exist). The section concludes by noting that plants and animals within a region are interdependent — trees provide food and shelter to birds and animals, while animals help spread seeds.

2.2 How to Group Plants and Animals?

This section introduces the concept and value of grouping as a tool for understanding diversity. Grouping means arranging things based on common features and makes it easier to study similarities and differences. Activity 2.3 asks students to collect pictures of plants and animals, observe their features, and group them — leading to the discovery that different people may choose different criteria such as height, presence of flowers, eating habits, or place of living.

2.2.1 How to group plants?

Plants can vary in the features of stems, leaves, flowers, and roots. The section walks students through several criteria for grouping plants. Based on height and stem type, plants are grouped into three categories: trees (tall, hard, thick, woody brown stems, branches starting high up — e.g., mango), shrubs (medium height, multiple hard but thin woody stems branching close to ground — e.g., rose), and herbs (small, soft green stems — e.g., tomato). Plants with weak stems that need support are called climbers, while those that spread along the ground are called creepers. Activity 2.4 guides systematic observation of stem colour, hardness, thickness, and branching patterns to arrive at these groupings. Leaf venation is then introduced: the pattern of veins on a leaf is called venation. Reticulate venation (net-like pattern on both sides of a central vein) is shown in hibiscus; parallel venation (veins running parallel) is shown in banana and grasses. Activity 2.5 asks students to compare collected leaves for venation type. Root types are examined next in Activity 2.6: a mustard plant shows a taproot system (one main root with side roots), while common grass shows fibrous roots (a bunch of similar thin roots from the base of the stem). Activity 2.7 guides students to relate leaf venation and root type, arriving at a general rule: plants with reticulate venation typically have taproots (e.g., chickpea, hibiscus, sadabahar), and plants with parallel venation typically have fibrous roots (e.g., lemongrass, wheat). Activity 2.8 introduces seed structure — soaking chickpea and maize reveals that chickpea splits into two cotyledons (dicotyledon or dicot), while maize has a single thin cotyledon (monocotyledon or monocot). The integrated principle established is that dicot plants have reticulate venation and a taproot system, while monocot plants have parallel venation and a fibrous root system.

2.2.2 How to group animals?

Animals show significant differences in shape, size, colour, structure, and movement. Activity 2.9 asks students to observe animals and record the body parts they use to move: ants and goats use legs; pigeons and houseflies use legs and wings; fish use fins. Animals can fly, run, crawl, walk, hop, or jump. Grouping animals by type of movement and body parts used for movement is one valid approach to classifying animal diversity. The section also introduces the scientist Janaki Ammal (1897–1984), an Indian botanist who documented and helped preserve India's plant biodiversity, led the Save Silent Valley movement, and headed the Botanical Survey of India.

2.3 Plants and Animals in Different Surroundings

Different plants and animals live in different surroundings and their features are shaped by those surroundings. Fish have streamlined bodies and fins suited for water; goats have legs suited for grassy terrain. Activity 2.10 asks students to list plants and animals across regions — desert, mountains, ocean, forest — and observe that each region has a distinct set of species. The reasons for this variation are environmental conditions: desert plants like cactus have thick fleshy stems to store water; mountain trees like deodar are conical with flexible, sloping branches to shed snow. The special features that enable plants and animals to survive in a particular region are called adaptations. The place where a plant or animal lives is its habitat — the habitat provides food, water, air, and shelter. Hot-desert camels have long legs, wide hooves, and one hump for food storage; cold-desert camels from Ladakh have shorter legs, two humps, and long fur for insulation. Rhododendrons in the Nilgiris grow shorter with smaller leaves due to heavy winds, while those in Sikkim grow taller — showing that even the same species adapts differently across regions. Animals and plants living on land are in terrestrial habitats (forests, deserts, grasslands, mountains); those in water are in aquatic habitats (ponds, lakes, rivers, oceans). Animals like frogs that live in both are called amphibians. Damage to habitats causes loss of food, shelter, and other resources for plants and animals, leading to loss of biodiversity. The section highlights conservation efforts including Project Tiger (1973), the Cheetah Reintroduction Project (2022), and Protected Area status for the Great Indian Bustard's habitat. Sacred groves — community-protected undisturbed forest patches found across India — are presented as a traditional form of biodiversity conservation. The chapter closes with the scientist Salim Ali (1896–1987), the Birdman of India, who documented bird diversity and habitats across India, wrote a landmark ten-volume series on birds of the Indian Subcontinent, and helped preserve regions like Keoladeo National Park and Ranganathittu Bird Sanctuary.
