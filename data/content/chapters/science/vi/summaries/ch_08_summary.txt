Chapter 8: A Journey through States of Water

8.1 Investigating Water's Disappearing Act

This section opens with children noticing that water from playground puddles disappears after rain. It introduces the concept that water on surfaces—utensils, mopped floors, wet clothes—dries up over time, prompting students to investigate whether water seeps through surfaces or disappears in another way. Through an activity using a steel plate, students establish that water does not seep through a solid surface. The section introduces the key term evaporation: the process of conversion of water into its vapour state. Water vapour is named as a third state of water, distinct from liquid water and ice. The section notes that evaporation occurs continuously even at room temperature and provides everyday examples such as drying clothes, mopped floors, and sweat evaporating from skin. Students are asked to consider whether puddles disappear due to seepage, evaporation, or both. A student activity (Activity 8.2) involves investigating water on a steel plate; a drawing activity asks students to sketch the process of water disappearing.

8.2 Another Mystery

This section builds on 8.1 by presenting a new puzzle: water droplets appearing on the outer surface of a cold glass tumbler containing ice water. Students record observations and propose possible reasons—including seepage from the glass or melted ice escaping. Through chain-of-reasoning discussions, the seepage hypothesis is tested and rejected using evidence (water level in the glass does not decrease; a narrow bottle confirms even small drops are detectable). The section introduces the term condensation: the process of conversion of water vapour into its liquid state. When water vapour in the surrounding air contacts the cold surface of the tumbler, it condenses into visible droplets. Related phenomena—dew drops on plants in the morning, water drops on the underside of a lid placed over boiling water—are cited as further examples of condensation. An activity (Activity 8.4) involves measuring the increasing mass of a cold-water tumbler on a digital weighing balance over 30 minutes, providing quantitative evidence that external water is being added to the tumbler through condensation. A follow-up modification marking the internal water level confirms that water inside the tumbler has not decreased. The term humidity is introduced as the measure of water vapour in the air.

8.3 What are the Different States of Water?

This section consolidates the three states of water established in the preceding sections: solid (ice), liquid (water), and gaseous (water vapour). It identifies the distinguishing properties of each state. Ice retains a fixed shape regardless of the container it is placed in, does not flow, and does not spread. Liquid water has no fixed shape—it takes the shape of its container while maintaining constant volume—and it can flow and spread. Water vapour spreads out to fill the entire available space and has no fixed shape; it is invisible and present in the air around us even at room temperature. An activity (Activity 8.5) asks students to compare the three states by transferring ice and water between containers of different shapes and observing the spreading behaviour of water vapour. The section broadens the concept to note that other substances such as wax, oil, and ghee also exhibit solid, liquid, and gaseous states, and invites students to identify everyday examples of solids, liquids, and gases in their surroundings.

8.4 How can We Change the States of Water?

This section addresses the role of heating and cooling in bringing about changes of state. Supplying heat converts ice (solid) to water (liquid) and water to water vapour (gas). Removing heat—placing water in a cold environment such as a freezer—converts liquid water to ice. These two processes are named: the conversion of a solid into its liquid state is called melting, and the conversion of liquid into solid state is called freezing. The section draws a parallel with wax (candle) and coconut oil to show that state changes on heating and cooling are not unique to water. An activity (Activity 8.6) asks students to complete a diagram labelling the three states and the four transition processes (melts, freezes, evaporates, condenses). A "More to Know" note introduces the Atmospheric Water Generator (AWG) as a real-world application of condensation—collecting water from humid air by cooling it, analogous to the cold-tumbler experiment.

8.5 How can Water be Evaporated Faster or Slower?

This section systematically explores factors that affect the rate of evaporation. Students are first prompted to recall everyday observations—clothes drying faster on a hot sunny day versus a rainy day, the cooling sensation of a fan—before conducting controlled investigations. Activity 8.7 compares evaporation from a small bottle cap (less exposed surface area) with evaporation from a plate (greater exposed area), keeping all other conditions constant; students measure the time taken for complete evaporation in each case. The conclusion established is that greater exposed surface area leads to faster evaporation. An open investigation (using Table 8.5) challenges students to design their own activity changing a different condition while keeping surface area fixed. Activity 8.8 places equal amounts of water in sunlight versus shade and records the time to complete evaporation, demonstrating that higher temperature accelerates evaporation. The section also addresses humidity as a factor: on rainy days, when the air already holds more water vapour, evaporation is slower; clothes dry more slowly in high-humidity conditions. Increased air movement (wind or fan) is identified as another factor that speeds up evaporation.

8.6 Cooling Effect

This section explains why evaporation produces a cooling effect, using the everyday example of an earthen pot (matka) keeping water cooler than a stainless steel pot. Water seeps through the porous surface of the earthen pot and evaporates, removing heat from the water inside and lowering its temperature. Additional examples of evaporative cooling are given: sprinkling water on a floor or roof in summer, and the cooling sensation when sanitiser is rubbed on hands. A student activity (Activity 8.9) involves constructing a pot-in-pot cooler from two earthen pots of different sizes with moist sand between them; students cover the inner pot with a wet jute sack and observe the cooling effect over several days, testing whether vegetables and fruits stored inside remain fresh. The section connects evaporative cooling to the earlier discussion of humidity by noting that higher humidity slows evaporation and therefore reduces cooling—explaining why fans provide relief in dry heat but less so in humid conditions. The traditional clay Surahi is cited as a related example of evaporative cooling used in Indian summers.

8.7 How Do Clouds Give Us Rain?

This final section explains how the water cycle operates, connecting evaporation and condensation at a planetary scale. As water vapour rises higher in the atmosphere, the air becomes cooler. At sufficient altitude, the air cools to the point where water vapour condenses around tiny dust particles to form small water droplets; these droplets float in the air and collectively form clouds. A "Do you know" note explains why water vapour rises: it is lighter than air, just as gas balloons containing lighter gases rise. Many droplets combine to form larger drops until they become heavy enough to fall as rain; under special conditions, water may fall as hail or snow. Activity 8.10 demonstrates the role of dust particles in cloud formation by squeezing a plastic bottle containing water and a small piece of burnt newspaper—the smoke provides invisible dust particles around which condensation occurs, producing a visible haze (clouds) inside the bottle. Activity 8.11 asks students to label a diagram of the full water cycle, identifying where water is stored (ocean, lake, river, groundwater, cloud) and how it moves (evaporation, condensation, rain, snow). The section concludes by noting that only a small fraction of Earth's water is available for use by living things, the rest being in the oceans, and stresses the importance of using water wisely and preventing pollution of water bodies.
