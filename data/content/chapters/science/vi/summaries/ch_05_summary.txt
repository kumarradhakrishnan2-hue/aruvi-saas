Chapter 5: Measurement of Length and Motion

5.1 How do we Measure?

The chapter opens with Deepa and her friends comparing different ways of measuring the length of their classroom table using their handspans (called balisht by Deepa's mother). The five students—Deepa, Anish, Hardeep, Padma, and Tasneem—record different counts for the same table because their handspans are of different sizes. This hands-on experience leads them to the core insight: a measurement consists of two parts, a number and a unit. The handspan serves as an informal unit, but since body-part units such as the hand, arm, foot, stride, and fist vary from person to person, they cannot serve as reliable shared standards. The section establishes the need for a unit that gives the same result regardless of who performs the measurement.

5.2 Standard Units

This section explains how different measurement systems evolved independently in different parts of the world, creating confusion as trade and travel increased across regions. Countries came together to adopt a common framework called the International System of Units, or SI units. For length, the SI unit is the metre, symbolised as m. One metre is divided into 100 equal divisions called centimetres (cm), and each centimetre is further divided into 10 equal parts called millimetres (mm), making 1 mm the smallest graduation on a standard 15-cm school scale. For larger distances, the kilometre (km) is used, equal to 1000 metres. The unit relationships are: 1 km = 1000 m, 1 m = 100 cm, 1 cm = 10 mm. A "More to know" box situates this in Indian history, noting that ancient units such as angula (finger width), dhanusa, and yojana appear in classical literature and were used in architecture and town planning, with the angula still used by traditional craftspeople such as tailors and carpenters.

5.3 Correct Way of Measuring Length

This section moves from choosing the right unit to choosing the right instrument and using it correctly. The appropriate measuring device depends on what is being measured: a 15-cm scale for a pencil, a metre scale or tape for room dimensions, and a flexible measuring tape for curved surfaces such as a tree girth or a person's chest. Three practical sub-questions are addressed. First, the scale must be placed flat along the object's length with the zero mark aligned to one end. Second, the eye must be positioned directly above the tip of the object being measured—the correct vertical position eliminates parallax error; viewing from an angle gives a false reading. Third, if the ends of the scale are broken or the zero marking is unclear, any clear full-centimetre mark can be used as the starting reference; the length is then the difference between the two readings (for example, if one end aligns with 1.0 cm and the other with 10.4 cm, the object's length is 9.4 cm). A "Do you know?" note mentions that visually challenged students use scales with raised markings that can be read by touch. Activity 5.1 asks students to measure everyday objects such as a comb, pen, pencil, and eraser, and to compare results with classmates to appreciate residual variation.

5.4 Measuring the Length of a Curved Line

A straight rigid scale cannot follow a curved surface. This section introduces two methods for measuring curved lengths. A flexible measuring tape can be laid along the curve directly. Alternatively, a thread can be placed carefully along the curve, then straightened and measured against a metre scale. The practical example used is measuring the length of the string-light decorations on the arched verandah of Anish's house.

5.5 Describing Position

This section develops the concept of a reference point. Deepa and her friends disagree about whether the school or the garden is closer, because each child is measuring distances from their own house as an implicit reference. The section shows that distances are only comparable when calculated from the same fixed point. When distance is stated with respect to a fixed object or point, that point is called a reference point. Two concrete examples reinforce this: drawing lines for a Kabaddi court on the playground by marking a reference point from which all measurements start, and reading kilometre stones on the road to Delhi that state distances from Delhi as a fixed reference point.

5.6 Moving Things

Having established what a reference point is, the section defines motion: an object is said to be in motion if its position changes with respect to the reference point with time; if its position does not change with time, the object is at rest. The example of Deepa observing fellow passengers on a bus illustrates that motion and rest are relative. Relative to Deepa (or the bus interior), the seated passengers are at rest because their positions do not change. Relative to a building outside the bus, the same passengers are in motion because the bus is moving. This shows that the same object can be simultaneously at rest relative to one reference point and in motion relative to another. Activity 5.2 asks students to identify five objects in motion and five at rest in their surroundings, and to write justifications for each classification.

5.7 Types of Motion

The chapter concludes by classifying motion into three types based on the path an object follows.

Linear motion is when an object moves along a straight line. Examples include a dropped eraser falling vertically, an orange falling from a tree, students marching in the Republic Day parade, and a heavy box being pushed across the floor.

Circular motion is when an object moves along a circular path. Activity 5.4 has students tie an eraser to a thread and whirl it; the eraser traces a circle, similar to the motion of a merry-go-round.

Oscillatory motion is when an object moves to and fro about a fixed position. Activity 5.5 uses a pendulum made of an eraser on a thread: when displaced slightly and released, it swings back and forth like a playground swing. Activity 5.6 shows that a thin metal strip clamped at one end and pressed at the free end also vibrates in oscillatory motion.

A "More to know" note explains that both circular and oscillatory motion are periodic because the object repeats its path after a fixed interval of time. Activity 5.7 asks students to observe a children's park and classify the motions of swings, slides, merry-go-rounds, and other equipment as linear, circular, or oscillatory.
