Chapter 2: Cell: The Building Block of Life

Section 2.1 introduces the challenge of studying cells given their sub-millimetre size. The human eye can resolve two points only if they are at least 0.1 mm apart — the limit of resolution — which means most plant and animal cells, lying in the range of 10–100 µm, require optical assistance to study. Robert Hooke first observed cells in 1665 using a self-designed microscope with 200–300× magnification, identifying box-like compartments in cork. Light microscopes used in school laboratories combine an objective lens and eyepiece to achieve magnification and improved resolution under visible light; key parameters improved over centuries are resolution, contrast, and magnification. (Activity 2.1) Students use a transparent ruler to measure the field-of-view diameter in micrometres, then count onion peel cells along the diameter and compute estimated cell size using the formula: estimated cell size = diameter of visible field (µm) ÷ number of cells along the diameter.

Section 2.2 establishes that cell structure is fundamentally organised around the boundary between a cell and its environment. Regardless of whether a cell is unicellular or part of a multicellular organism, its interactions with surroundings and neighbouring cells occur at the cell membrane. This section introduces the two main boundary structures: the universal cell membrane and the plant/fungal/bacterial cell wall.

Section 2.2.1 describes the cell membrane as the thin, selectively permeable boundary — also called the plasma membrane — that defines cellular individuality by allowing some substances to pass while blocking others. The fluid-mosaic model explains its structure: a lipid bilayer (hydrophilic heads outward, hydrophobic tails inward) with embedded proteins that act as gatekeepers, all about 7–10 nm thick. The selective permeability drives osmosis — the diffusion of water across the membrane from a dilute (hypotonic) solution to a concentrated (hypertonic) solution — and diffusion more broadly from higher to lower concentration without need for a membrane. Isotonic, hypotonic, and hypertonic conditions produce predictable cell volume changes. (Activity 2.2) Students place two equal potato pieces — one in plain water, one in 20% salt/sugar solution — and measure weight before and after to observe and infer osmotic water movement across the cell membrane. (What if…) Students consider what happens to mung bean seeds soaked in water then transferred to a concentrated solution — predicting that osmotic water loss would cause shrinkage. (What if…) Students examine a diagram showing the effect of isotonic, hypotonic, and hypertonic solutions on a cell to predict size changes based on relative solute concentrations.

Section 2.2.2 explains that plant cells possess a cell wall outside the cell membrane — a rigid, permeable layer made primarily of cellulose that maintains shape, supports the plant against environmental stresses like wind and rain, and keeps leaves and flowers firm. Unlike the cell membrane, the cell wall is permeable to water and dissolved minerals. When a plant cell loses water by osmosis in a concentrated solution, the cell wall prevents collapse: the inner cytoplasmic content shrinks and the membrane pulls away from the wall (plasmolysis), while the cell's outer dimensions remain unchanged. Animal cells lack a cell wall and therefore shrink and lose shape under hypertonic conditions. Fungi and bacteria also have cell walls for protection and structural support. (Activity 2.3) Students prepare temporary microscope slides of onion peel or Rhoeo leaf cells (stained with safranin) alongside human cheek cells (stained with methylene blue), observe their shapes under a microscope, and then repeat with 20% sugar solution to compare how plant cells maintain outer boundary while the inner content shrinks, versus how cheek cells shrink overall. (Pause and Ponder) Students reason about why fixed plants need a cell wall while mobile animals do not, predict consequences if the cell wall became as flexible as the membrane, and evaluate experimental design by explaining why equal initial potato piece size and weight measurement matter.

Section 2.3 introduces the internal organisation of eukaryotic cells as a coordinated system. Most cells share three fundamental components: the plasma membrane, the semi-fluid cytoplasm, and the nucleus. The cytoplasm of eukaryotic cells also contains numerous membrane-bound organelles that divide cellular labour and allow multiple processes to run simultaneously. Prokaryotic cells (1–10 µm), typified by bacteria, lack a membrane-bound nucleus and membrane-bound organelles; their genetic material exists as a circular DNA molecule in a region called the nucleoid, and cellular activities occur directly in the cytoplasm. Eukaryotic cells (10–100 µm), such as plant and animal cells, have a true membrane-bound nucleus and a full complement of organelles. In eukaryotic cells, a network of fine protein fibres called the cytoskeleton provides structural support and enables internal transport. (Activity 2.4) Students study labelled diagrams of a bacterial cell, a plant cell, and an animal cell and record in a comparison table which structures — cell membrane, cell wall, cytoplasm, well-defined nucleus, nucleoid, and membrane-bound organelles — are present or absent in each.

Section 2.3.1 explains why eukaryotic cells require organelles by framing the cell as a tiny living factory in which each part performs a specialised job. Organelles enable building new materials, removing waste, and generating energy — all simultaneously.

Nucleus — House of coded instructions: The nucleus is enclosed by a double-layered nuclear membrane perforated by pores that allow exchange of material with the cytoplasm. Inside, the nucleolus synthesises ribosomal subunits that exit to the cytoplasm for final ribosome assembly. Chromosomes — visible as rod-shaped structures only during cell division — are composed of DNA and specific proteins; the functional segments of DNA are called genes. Between divisions, the genetic material exists as diffuse chromatin threads. Prokaryotes store their circular DNA molecule, associated with specific proteins, in the unenclosed nucleoid region. (Threads of Curiosity) Mature red blood cells (RBCs) in humans are enucleate, allowing more space for haemoglobin and thus greater oxygen-carrying capacity, but as a consequence they cannot repair or divide, giving them a lifespan of approximately 120 days.

Ribosomes — The protein factories: Ribosomes are tiny structures found either free in the cytoplasm or attached to the endoplasmic reticulum; they are the sole sites of protein synthesis in the cell.

Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER) — Manufacturing factory: The ER is a large network of membranes continuous with the outer nuclear envelope, extending throughout the cytoplasm. Rough Endoplasmic Reticulum (RER) is studded with ribosomes on its surface and is the primary site of protein synthesis and secretion, as in pancreatic gland cells. Smooth Endoplasmic Reticulum (SER) lacks surface ribosomes and is involved in the synthesis and storage of lipids and hormones. The ER plays a key role in the transport of proteins and lipids to other parts of the cell.

Golgi apparatus — The packaging and shipping centres: The Golgi apparatus consists of stacks of flattened, sac-like membranes functionally linked to the ER, the cell membrane, and other organelles. It acts as the cell's post office: receiving material from the ER, modifying, sorting, and packaging proteins and lipids into vesicles for secretion, transport to the cell membrane, or lysosome formation.

Lysosomes — The clean-up system: Lysosomes are single-membrane-bound organelles filled with digestive enzymes capable of breaking down unwanted proteins, carbohydrates, fats, and damaged organelles. The breakdown products are released into the cytoplasm for reuse. (Threads of Curiosity) Human sperm cells carry lysosomal enzymes in a specialised region; these enzymes break down the outer layer of the egg at fertilisation, enabling the sperm to enter.

Mitochondria — The powerhouse of the cell: Mitochondria generate most of the cell's usable energy through cellular respiration, breaking down glucose and other molecules. Each mitochondrion has two membranes: a smooth outer membrane and an inner membrane folded into finger-like projections called cristae that greatly increase surface area for energy-producing reactions. The energy released is stored as ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate), the universal energy currency of the cell. Mitochondria contain their own DNA and ribosomes, enabling partial self-replication, a feature shared with plastids and suggestive of an evolutionary origin from ancient bacteria.

Plastids — Centre for food synthesis in the plant cells and beyond: Plastids are a family of double-membrane-bound organelles unique to plant cells (and some other photosynthetic eukaryotes) that serve synthesis and storage functions. Chloroplasts contain the green pigment chlorophyll, which absorbs light for photosynthesis; inside the chloroplast is a semi-fluid stroma in which disc-shaped membrane structures carrying chlorophyll are embedded, and synthesised sugars and starch granules are stored in the stroma. Like mitochondria, chloroplasts carry their own DNA and ribosomes.

How do flowers, fruits, and vegetables acquire varied colours?: Chromoplasts are plastids that contain pigments other than chlorophyll — yellow, orange, or red — responsible for the vivid colours of flower petals and ripe fruits; these colours attract pollinators and seed-dispersing animals. Leucoplasts are colourless plastids that store food materials such as starch, oils, or proteins; for example, leucoplasts in potato and taro cells store starch.

Vacuoles — The organelles for storage and support: In mature plant cells, a single large central vacuole occupies much of the cell volume, surrounded by a selectively permeable membrane. The vacuole's watery cell sap stores water, minerals, sugars, and waste products, and the turgor pressure it creates keeps plant cells firm; water deficit causes the vacuole to deflate and the plant to wilt. Animal cells may contain smaller vacuoles that assist in temporary material storage. (Pause and Ponder) Students are asked why white flowers may still contain pigment, and to draw a labelled cell diagram using structural clues about the nucleus, ER, mitochondria, and chloroplasts.

Section 2.4 introduces cell division as the mechanism behind growth and repair. Cell growth is limited in a single cell — cells divide rather than simply enlarge — and division replaces old, dead, or damaged cells. Both prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells divide, but eukaryotic division is more controlled and orderly, governed by the cell cycle. (Activity 2.5) Students grow onion root tips in water for 5–6 days, fix root tips in aceto-alcohol, stain with aceto-carmine, prepare squash slides, and observe different stages of cell division under a microscope — seeing that cells at different stages appear structurally distinct.

Section 2.4.1 defines cell division as the process producing new cells from pre-existing ones, enabling growth, tissue repair, and reproduction. There are two major types: mitosis and meiosis.

Mitosis: Mitosis is the most common form of cell division, producing two genetically identical daughter cells from one parent cell. Each daughter cell receives the same DNA and the same chromosome number as the parent, preserving genetic continuity across body cells. Mitosis is essential for normal growth, tissue repair and maintenance, and asexual reproduction.

Meiosis: Meiosis is a two-stage division occurring only in the reproductive organs, producing four daughter cells each with half the chromosome number of the parent. In the first division, chromosome number is halved; the second division is mitosis-like, dividing each intermediate cell again. In animals, meiosis occurs in the testes (producing sperm) and ovaries (producing eggs); in plants, in the anthers (producing pollen) and ovaries (producing egg cells). At fertilisation, when two gametes fuse, the full chromosome number is restored. Meiosis is the basis of sexual reproduction and introduces the genetic diversity that means offspring resemble but are not identical to their parents. (Threads of Curiosity) J. Craig Venter's 2010 experiment synthesised a complete copy of the DNA of Mycoplasma mycoides, transplanted it into a cell whose own DNA had been removed, and the cell then grew and divided according to the synthetic DNA — demonstrating that DNA controls cell structure and activity, while noting that only the DNA was synthetic; the cellular machinery was from a living cell. (Pause and Ponder) Students reason about why multiple small mitochondria are functionally superior to one large one (surface area argument), and what would happen if skin cells divided by meiosis instead of mitosis.

Section 2.5 presents the Cell Theory as the unifying principle of biology. Matthias Schleiden (1838) established that all plants are made of cells; Theodor Schwann (1839) extended this to animals; Rudolf Virchow (1855) added that all cells arise only from pre-existing cells. The classical Cell Theory holds: (1) all living organisms consist of one or more cells; (2) the cell is the basic structural and functional unit of life; (3) all cells arise from pre-existing cells. This framework unifies biology from bacteria to humans.

Section 2.5.1 addresses cell lifespan and the limits of division. Cells grow, divide in a controlled manner, perform their functions, and eventually die, with dead cells replaced by new ones. In animal cells, division normally ceases when a cell contacts neighbouring cells — a process called contact inhibition. Cancer cells lose this control and divide uncontrollably, forming tumours; plant cells, constrained by their rigid walls, do not show contact inhibition and grow by a different pattern. (Threads of Curiosity) Cancer cells break free of normal growth controls, forming tumours that may be benign or malignant; malignant tumours can invade nearby tissues and metastasise to other parts of the body.

The chapter also connects cell biology to real-world biotechnology. Scientists have developed cell culture techniques — growing plant and animal cells outside the body in nutrient-rich media under controlled temperature, pH, and sterile conditions. Cell culture is used to study cell function and for the production of biochemicals, food, medicines, and vaccines. The Bridging Science and Society box also notes that errors in mitosis lead to uncontrolled division and tumour formation, while errors in meiosis can cause genetic disorders, early pregnancy loss, or reduced fertility.
