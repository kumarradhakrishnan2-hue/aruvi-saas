Chapter 13: Earth as a System: Energy, Matter, and Life

This chapter integrates ideas about energy flow, atmospheric processes, biogeochemical cycles, and human impact that students explored separately in earlier grades, presenting them as parts of one interconnected Earth system made up of five interacting spheres: the geosphere (solid rocks, soil, and landforms), hydrosphere (surface water and groundwater), cryosphere (ice and snow), atmosphere (the surrounding air), and biosphere (all living organisms and their habitats). A disturbance in any one sphere propagates to the others — for example, reduced snowfall lowers lake levels, while warmer Arabian Sea water intensifies the southwest monsoon and accelerates glacier melt, ultimately threatening coastal cities and disrupting ecosystems. The chapter uses this systems framing to unify solar radiation, wind, ocean currents, biogeochemical cycles, and human impact into a single coherent picture.

(Activity 13.1: Let us explore) Students observe a landscape diagram identifying one example from each Earth sphere, trace how snow becomes lake water, reason about how reduced snowfall would affect lake level and vegetation, and discuss with classmates how a disturbance in one sphere leads to changes in others.

13.1 Uneven Heating of the Earth

Solar radiation, the primary energy source on Earth, arrives as electromagnetic (EM) waves travelling at 3 × 10⁸ ms⁻¹ and spans the full electromagnetic spectrum from gamma rays and X-rays at short wavelengths to infrared and radio waves at long wavelengths. Approximately 99 per cent of the Sun's energy reaching Earth falls within the ultraviolet (UV), visible, and infrared (IR) range. The solar constant — the average solar energy received per unit time per unit area perpendicular to the Sun's rays at the top of the atmosphere — is approximately 1.4 kWm⁻², though atmospheric absorption and scattering reduce the maximum surface insolation to about 1 kWm⁻² under clear-sky conditions. UV is mostly absorbed by the ozone layer, visible light drives photosynthesis and partly warms land and water, and infrared radiation warms the surface, which then re-radiates heat that greenhouse gases (CO₂, CH₄, water vapour) trap to keep the Earth warm enough for life.

(Think as a Scientist) Students estimate how much of India's surface area covered with solar panels could supply the country's total electricity needs, using insolation data and an assumed efficiency fraction, and discover that a fraction of the Thar desert's area would suffice.

13.1.1 Interaction of solar radiation on the Earth's surface

Different materials absorb and re-radiate heat at different rates under sunlight, and the fraction of solar radiation reflected by a surface is called its albedo. High-albedo surfaces such as snow (0.80–0.90) and ice (0.50–0.70) reflect most incoming radiation and stay cool, making polar regions very cold, while low-albedo surfaces like black soil and ocean water absorb more and warm more quickly. Dark-coloured built-up surfaces — concrete, asphalt, and brick — absorb and retain heat, causing cities to be warmer than surrounding rural areas during summer and at night, a phenomenon called the urban heat island effect; forests and vegetation stay cooler through shade and transpiration. This differential heating by surface material and colour is fundamental to local and regional climate patterns.

(Threads of Curiosity — Urban Heat Island Effect) Students consider how the replacement of vegetation with concrete and asphalt in cities amplifies daytime and night-time warming through heat absorption, retention, and re-radiation from built materials.

13.1.2 Latitude and Earth's shape

Because the Earth is spherical, solar radiation strikes equatorial regions more directly and is concentrated over a smaller area, while at higher latitudes the same solar energy is spread over a larger area, resulting in lower intensity and cooler temperatures. This geometric effect, combined with the tilt of the Earth's rotation axis, gives rise to seasons, varying day lengths through the year, and a systematic decrease in average temperature from the equator toward the poles. The resulting temperature gradient between equatorial and polar regions is the primary driver of global atmospheric circulation and ocean currents.

13.1.3 Role of the atmosphere

The atmosphere, held by gravity and composed mainly of nitrogen (78%) and oxygen (21%) with trace amounts of argon, CO₂, water vapour, and other gases, has a layered structure with distinct properties at different altitudes. The troposphere (0–12 km), heated from the Earth's surface, is where nearly all weather occurs; temperature decreases with height at about 6.5 °C/km and rising warm air drives winds and storms. The stratosphere (12–50 km) contains the ozone layer, which absorbs UV radiation causing temperature to increase with height, suppressing vertical mixing and confining weather to the troposphere below. The atmosphere protects life in two ways: firstly by absorbing harmful UV in the ozone layer and scattering some sunlight in clouds and gases before it reaches the surface; secondly by trapping outgoing infrared heat through the greenhouse effect of CO₂, CH₄, and water vapour — without which Earth would be too cold for life, but excess CO₂ from human activities enhances warming to dangerous levels.

13.2 Uneven Heating Causes Wind and Ocean Currents

Pressure differences caused by the uneven heating of the Earth's surface drive the movement of air from high-pressure to low-pressure regions, producing winds at local, regional, and planetary scales, as well as setting ocean surface water in motion. The same basic principle — differential heating creates pressure gradients that drive flow — operates from the scale of a mountain valley to the scale of the entire globe. Understanding these wind and current systems is essential for understanding climate, agriculture, trade routes, and the distribution of ecosystems.

13.2.1 Local winds

In mountainous regions, slopes and valley floors heat and cool at different rates, giving rise to daily reversals in wind direction called valley and mountain breezes. During the day, mountain slopes facing the Sun heat faster than the valley floor, causing air over the slopes to rise and creating low pressure that draws cooler valley air upward — this is the valley breeze. After sunset, the slopes cool faster than the valley floor, making slope air cooler and denser, so it flows down into the valley as a mountain breeze. These local winds, experienced in hilly areas like Shimla, Dehradun, and other Himalayan valleys, regulate temperature and moisture conditions, supporting soil health and agriculture.

13.2.2 Planetary winds

Large-scale uneven heating between the equator and poles creates a system of pressure belts that drives planetary-scale wind circulation. Near the equator, intense solar heating causes warm air to rise, forming an equatorial low-pressure belt; this air moves poleward at altitude, cools, and sinks around 30° North and South, creating subtropical high-pressure belts from which air returns along the surface toward the equator to complete one circulation cell. Some of this sinking air moves poleward instead, rising again around 60° N and S where it meets cold polar air, forming sub-polar low-pressure belts; in polar regions, very cold dense air sinks to create polar high-pressure belts, completing a second circulation cell. Earth's rotation deflects these winds from straight paths, causing them to curve rightward in the Northern Hemisphere and leftward in the Southern Hemisphere.

13.2.3 Ocean currents

Ocean currents are the continuous large-scale movement of ocean water, driven primarily by planetary winds dragging the surface through friction, and additionally by temperature and salinity differences, Earth's rotation, and the distribution of continents. Warm, less dense equatorial water moves poleward at the surface while colder, denser water returns toward the equator through deeper ocean levels; water with higher salinity sinks and moves at depth while lower-salinity water stays near the surface. Earth's rotation deflects these moving water masses into large circular gyres — clockwise in the Northern Hemisphere and counter-clockwise in the Southern Hemisphere — and continents redirect currents further. Ocean currents regulate climate by transporting heat from the equator toward the poles, as illustrated by the North Atlantic Drift (an extension of the Gulf Stream) which keeps northwestern European ports ice-free at high latitudes; currents also support marine ecosystems by transporting nutrients.

13.3 Biogeochemical Cycles

Living organisms continuously exchange matter and energy with the abiotic environment — air, water, soil, and rocks — and this exchange ensures that essential nutrients such as carbon, nitrogen, and oxygen are recycled and remain available to support life. The cyclic movement of matter and energy between abiotic and biotic components is called the biogeochemical cycle, and these cycles interconnect all five Earth spheres, helping ecosystems recover from disturbances and maintain environmental balance. The chapter examines the water, carbon, nitrogen, and oxygen cycles, exploring why their balance matters and how human activities are disrupting them.

13.3.1 Water cycle

The water cycle involves evaporation from rivers, lakes, and oceans, condensation to form clouds, precipitation as rain, hail, or snow, surface runoff back to the ocean, and infiltration into soil and rock where water dissolves and transports minerals that support both terrestrial and marine organisms. Climate change is intensifying the water cycle: a warmer atmosphere holds more moisture, causing heavier rains in some areas and droughts elsewhere; melting glaciers add water to rivers and raise sea levels, threatening coastal cities like Mumbai and Chennai; intense rainfall increases surface runoff that erodes soil while reducing groundwater recharge, making agriculture harder. In this way, the water cycle directly links all five Earth spheres — cryosphere, hydrosphere, atmosphere, geosphere, and biosphere — and disruption of any one affects the others.

13.3.2 Carbon cycle

Carbon forms the structural backbone of all biological molecules — proteins, carbohydrates, fats, and DNA — and circulates between the atmosphere (CO₂), biosphere (plants and animals), geosphere (carbonate rocks and fossil fuels), and hydrosphere (dissolved CO₂ and marine shells) at vastly different time scales. In the fast cycle (days to years), plants fix atmospheric CO₂ into glucose through photosynthesis, animals obtain carbon by consumption, and decomposition returns CO₂ to the atmosphere through respiration; ocean water continuously exchanges CO₂ with the atmosphere, with phytoplankton and marine organisms incorporating carbon into shells that sink to the ocean floor. In the slow cycle (millions of years), buried organic matter becomes coal, oil, and gas — fossil fuels — whose combustion on human timescales releases carbon far faster than natural processes can reabsorb it, raising atmospheric CO₂ by about 35% since 1960 (from 315 ppm to 420 ppm), intensifying the greenhouse effect, accelerating glacier melt, raising sea levels, and threatening to intensify Indian monsoons and disrupt agriculture.

13.3.3 Nitrogen cycle

Nitrogen is essential for synthesising proteins and nucleic acids, and the atmosphere is the largest nitrogen reservoir, but atmospheric N₂ is non-reactive and cannot be used directly by most organisms — it must first be converted to soluble compounds through several microbial processes collectively constituting the nitrogen cycle. Nitrogen-fixing bacteria (Rhizobium in legume root nodules, Azotobacter in soil) convert N₂ to ammonia (NH₃); nitrifying bacteria (Nitrosomonas, Nitrobacter) convert ammonia first to nitrite then to nitrate that plants can absorb; animals obtain nitrogen by eating plants or other animals. When organisms die or produce waste, decomposers (bacteria and fungi) break down organic matter in ammonification, returning ammonia to the soil; denitrifying bacteria (Pseudomonas) convert some nitrates back to N₂ in denitrification, completing the cycle and maintaining ecosystem nitrogen balance. Lightning also fixes small amounts of nitrogen oxides.

13.3.4 Oxygen cycle

Oxygen constitutes about 21% of the atmosphere as free O₂, is an essential component of most biological molecules, and also exists in combined forms in the Earth's crust (metal oxides and minerals) and in the air as CO₂. Animals and plants consume O₂ in respiration and release CO₂; combustion of fuels also consumes O₂ and releases CO₂; photosynthesis by plants restores O₂ using sunlight, water, and CO₂ to produce glucose and O₂. The balance between O₂ consumption (respiration and combustion) and O₂ production (photosynthesis) circulates oxygen between the atmosphere, land, oceans, and living organisms, sustaining life across all Earth's spheres.

13.4 Human Impact on Earth's Processes

Human activities are disrupting biogeochemical cycles across all Earth spheres at a rate unprecedented in human history. Burning fossil fuels and deforestation saturate natural carbon sinks (forests and oceans), raising CO₂ levels, enhancing the greenhouse effect, and warming ocean water — which reduces the ocean's capacity to absorb CO₂ and acidifies seawater, threatening plankton and coral reefs. Overuse of nitrogen fertilisers in agriculture adds excess nitrates to rivers and lakes, triggering algal blooms that deplete oxygen and kill fish — a process called eutrophication. Deforestation reduces photosynthesis and transpiration, lowering local rainfall, altering surface albedo, increasing soil erosion, and destroying habitats, leading to biodiversity loss; vehicular emissions react with sunlight to form ground-level smog and harmful tropospheric ozone. While global agreements like the Montreal Protocol have successfully begun ozone layer recovery, efforts to reduce CO₂ emissions through the Kyoto Protocol and Paris Agreement have been less effective; individual and community actions — conserving energy, switching to renewables, planting trees, saving water, and practising sustainable farming — combined with India's rapid expansion of solar power and afforestation efforts offer pathways to restoring environmental balance.

(Pause and Ponder #6) Students discuss how human activities increase greenhouse gas concentrations and identify individual actions to reduce greenhouse gas emissions.

The chapter also connects this science to India's contributions and real-world applications. Anna Mani, India's pioneering atmospheric scientist, mapped solar insolation across India in the 1950s and co-published Solar Radiation Over India in 1982, creating the country's first insolation atlas whose findings now underpin India's large-scale solar energy deployment. Scientists at the Indian Institute of Tropical Meteorology (IITM), Pune, run advanced coupled atmosphere-ocean-land-ice computer models using satellite, Indian Ocean buoy, and Antarctic station data to simulate the Indian monsoon, improve seasonal forecasts, and study how global warming may alter monsoon rainfall patterns.
