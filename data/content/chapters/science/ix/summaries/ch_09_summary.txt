Chapter 9: Atomic Foundations of Matter

9.1 Law of Conservation of Mass

This section opens by asking whether mass changes during physical and chemical changes, using the example of water whose properties differ entirely from its constituent elements hydrogen and oxygen, yet whose mass equals the sum of their masses. Three numbered activities anchor the section's central claim. Activity 9.1 investigates a physical change: students dissolve common salt in water and find that the mass of the resulting solution equals the sum of the masses of water and salt taken, establishing that mass is conserved in physical changes. (Activity 9.1) Students dissolve a spatula of common salt in about 50 mL of water in a tared beaker and confirm that the reading before and after dissolution does not change.

Activity 9.2 tests whether mass is also conserved in chemical changes by reacting vinegar with baking soda (sodium hydrogencarbonate), which produces carbon dioxide. Two experimental set-ups are used: in set-up 1, the balloon with baking soda sits beside the conical flask and gas escapes after mixing, producing a lower final reading; in set-up 2, the balloon is fixed to the flask mouth so all gases are captured, and the final reading matches the initial reading, confirming mass conservation even in chemical reactions. (Activity 9.2) Students in two set-ups weigh vinegar and baking soda before and after reaction, discovering that only when the system is closed—gas captured in the balloon—do the initial and final masses agree.

Activity 9.3 further verifies the law using sodium sulfate and barium chloride solutions: when mixed, a white precipitate of barium sulfate and sodium chloride forms, yet the total mass of both flasks is unchanged. The Law of Conservation of Mass, proposed by Antoine Lavoisier in 1789, states that matter can neither be created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction. The section includes two worked examples: one verifying the law numerically for calcium carbonate reacting with hydrochloric acid, and another applying it proportionally to calculate carbon dioxide produced when carbon burns. (Think as a Scientist) Students design and perform their own experiment using zinc and dilute hydrochloric acid to test whether mass is conserved during that reaction, using a set-up of their own choice.

9.2 Law of Constant Proportions

This section introduces Joseph Proust's Law of Constant Proportions (also called the Law of Definite Proportions or Proust's Law), which states that in any compound formed by two or more elements, the elements combine in a fixed ratio by mass regardless of the compound's source. The key example is water: purified water from rivers, borewells, or the ocean always contains hydrogen and oxygen in a mass ratio of 1:8, so 9 g of water always yields 1 g of hydrogen and 8 g of oxygen on decomposition. A worked example applies the law to sodium chloride, in which sodium and chlorine are always in a mass ratio of 23:35.5, to find how much chlorine reacts with 46 g of sodium. (Threads of Curiosity) Students read that cinnabar—known in ancient India as hingula—always yields mercury and sulfur in mass percentages of approximately 86.22% and 13.78% regardless of which civilisation processed it, illustrating the law through a historical cross-cultural example.

9.3 Dalton's Atomic Theory

This section explains how the two laws of Section 9.1 and 9.2 provided the experimental foundation for Dalton's Atomic Theory, which offers a particle-level explanation: atoms are indivisible and merely rearrange during reactions rather than being created or destroyed, thus accounting for conservation of mass; and atoms of a given element are identical so they always combine in the same fixed ratios, thus accounting for constant proportions. John Dalton's postulates, presented in 1808, state that all matter is made of very tiny particles called atoms that participate in chemical reactions; atoms are indivisible and cannot be created or destroyed; atoms of a given element are identical in mass and chemical properties; atoms of different elements have different masses and chemical properties; atoms combine in simple whole-number ratios to form compounds; and the relative number and kinds of atoms are constant in a given compound. Examples used to illustrate the postulates include hydrogen and oxygen combining to form water without the atoms being destroyed, and magnesium burning in air to form magnesium oxide. (What if...) Students consider how the substances around us would change if atoms could combine in any ratio rather than fixed whole-number ratios, reflecting on the significance of Dalton's postulates.

9.4 How Atoms Combine?

Atoms of an element can combine with each other to form molecules of that element, while atoms of different elements combine to form molecules of compounds. A molecule is defined as an electrically neutral entity consisting of more than one atom that is capable of independent existence and shows all the properties of that substance; some elements like helium exist only as atoms because they are already stable. The section recalls from Chapter 8 that atoms with eight electrons in the valence shell (or two electrons if the K-shell is outermost) are stable, and atoms with fewer valence electrons may share, gain, or lose electrons to attain stability. This combining happens in two broad ways: sharing of electrons (covalent bond) and transfer of electrons (ionic bond). When atoms combine, the total energy of the system becomes lower than the sum of individual atomic energies, making the arrangement more stable; the force holding atoms together is called a chemical bond.

9.4.1 Bonding by sharing of electrons — Covalent Bond

A. Molecules of elements

This sub-section explains covalent bond formation in molecules where both atoms are of the same element. For hydrogen (atomic number 1), the single electron in the K-shell means each atom needs one more electron; two hydrogen atoms therefore share one electron each to form H₂, a single bond depicted as H—H. For chlorine (atomic number 17), each atom has seven valence electrons and needs one more; two chlorine atoms share one electron each to form Cl₂, also a single bond (Cl—Cl). For oxygen (atomic number 8), each atom has six valence electrons and needs two more; two oxygen atoms share two electrons each to form O₂, resulting in a double bond depicted as O=O.

B. Molecules of compounds

When atoms of two different elements combine, they still share electrons to satisfy the octet rule. For hydrogen chloride (HCl): hydrogen needs one electron and chlorine needs one, so they share one electron each, forming a single covalent bond (H—Cl). For water (H₂O): oxygen needs two electrons while each hydrogen needs one, so two hydrogen atoms each share one electron with the same oxygen atom, forming two single bonds. The section also introduces the Bridging Science and Society content about atomic/nuclear energy, noting that nuclear energy plays a vital role in electricity generation, medicine, and research; India's contributions to nuclear energy include the work of Raja Ramanna, called the Father of the Indian Nuclear Programme.

C. Naming covalent compounds

Covalent compounds are named using a prefix system that indicates the number of atoms of each element. The first element retains its regular name while the second ends in -ide; prefixes include mono- (1), di- (2), tri- (3), tetra- (4), penta- (5), hexa- (6). Mono- is typically omitted for the first element but used for the second; vowel-dropping rules apply when a prefix ending in 'o' or 'a' meets an element starting with a vowel (e.g. monoxide), but prefixes ending in 'i' are retained (e.g. dioxide). Worked examples include CO (carbon monoxide), CO₂ (carbon dioxide), CS₂ (carbon disulfide), PCl₃ (phosphorus trichloride), SF₆ (sulfur hexafluoride), N₂O₄ (dinitrogen tetroxide), and N₂O₅ (dinitrogen pentoxide). A special rule applies to hydrogen as the first element: no prefix is added regardless of the number of hydrogen atoms, so H₂S is named hydrogen sulfide. Common names like water (H₂O) and ammonia (NH₃) are noted as exceptions that override systematic names.

9.4.2 Bonding by electron transfer — Ionic bond

This sub-section explains bond formation by electron transfer. Atoms whose valence shells have fewer than four electrons generally donate those electrons to achieve stability, forming positively charged cations; atoms with more than four valence electrons gain or share electrons to complete their octet, with the gainers forming anions. The formation of sodium chloride (NaCl) is the central example: sodium (atomic number 11) has one valence electron, loses it to become Na⁺ (11 protons, 10 electrons); chlorine (atomic number 17) has seven valence electrons, gains one to become Cl⁻ (17 protons, 18 electrons). Cations and anions are collectively called ions. The electrostatic force of attraction between oppositely charged ions is called an ionic bond. The section also notes through a Threads of Curiosity inset that ionic compounds typically form three-dimensional crystals rather than individual molecules: in NaCl, each Na⁺ is surrounded by six Cl⁻ and vice versa, arranged in a repeating 3-D crystal lattice structure.

A. Naming ionic compounds

Ionic compounds are named by writing the cation name first followed by the anion name; simple anions end in -ide. Metals generally form cations and non-metals form anions. Polyatomic ions—formed from two or more elements—generally do not end in -ide. Table 9.1 lists common monoatomic ions (Na⁺, Li⁺, K⁺, Ag⁺, Ca²⁺, Ba²⁺, Fe²⁺, Fe³⁺, Cu⁺, Cu²⁺, Mg²⁺, Zn²⁺, Al³⁺, F⁻, Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻, O²⁻, S²⁻) and common polyatomic ions (OH⁻, NO₃⁻, HCO₃⁻, CO₃²⁻, SO₄²⁻, NH₄⁺) with their formulae and valencies.

9.5 Writing Chemical Formulae

This section introduces the criss-cross method as a faster way to write formulae without working through electron configurations.

9.5.1 Writing chemical formulae of covalent compounds

The steps are: write the element symbols, write their valencies below each, and criss-cross the valencies as subscripts. If the valency after criss-crossing is one, it is not written. Worked examples include HCl (valencies 1 and 1 → HCl), H₂S (valencies 1 and 2 → H₂S), and CCl₄ (valencies 4 and 1 → CCl₄).

9.5.2 Writing chemical formulae of ionic compounds

The steps are: write the cation symbol first, then the anion; write the charges (not as superscripts but beneath the symbols); criss-cross only the numbers; divide subscripts by any common factor to get the simplest ratio. Worked examples: CaCl₂ (Ca²⁺ and Cl⁻), Al₂O₃ (Al³⁺ and O²⁻), MgO (Mg²⁺ and O²⁻—equal valencies simplify to 1:1), CaCO₃ (Ca²⁺ and CO₃²⁻), Mg(OH)₂ (Mg²⁺ and OH⁻—brackets used for polyatomic ions appearing more than once), Al(OH)₃, and Al₂(SO₄)₃. The rule that charges on ions are not shown in the formula of the compound is noted.

9.6 Properties of the Ionic and the Covalent Compounds

Activity 9.4 is a two-part class experiment comparing solubility and electrical conductivity of camphor, sodium chloride, copper sulfate, sugar, and naphthalene in water, kerosene, and petrol, and in solid and dissolved states. Students group compounds by similar behaviour and draw conclusions. Ionic compounds (NaCl, CuSO₄) are generally soluble in water but insoluble in kerosene and petrol; covalent compounds (camphor, naphthalene) are generally insoluble in water but dissolve in organic solvents. Ionic compounds do not conduct electricity in the solid state because ions are held in fixed positions, but they do conduct when dissolved in water because ions are free to move. Covalent compounds do not provide ions in solution (e.g. sugar dissolves in water but does not conduct), and non-polar covalent compounds like camphor and naphthalene neither dissolve in water nor conduct. Ionic compounds have high melting and boiling points due to strong inter-ionic attractions; covalent compounds generally have low melting and boiling points. (Activity 9.4) Students set up a conductivity circuit with electrodes, a 9 V battery, and a bulb, test each of the five compounds in solid form and in aqueous solution, and record all observations in Table 9.2 before grouping compounds by shared properties.

9.7 Molecular Mass of Covalent Compounds

Molecular mass is the total mass of a molecule, calculated by adding the atomic masses of all atoms in the molecular formula. Worked Example 9.4 calculates the molecular mass of water: H = 1 u, O = 16 u, so H₂O = (1 × 2) + (16 × 1) = 18 u. Worked Example 9.5 calculates the molecular mass of carbon dioxide: C = 12 u, O = 16 u, so CO₂ = (12 × 1) + (16 × 2) = 44 u. The section notes that ionic compounds do not form molecules because their ions arrange in 3-D crystals; molecular mass therefore applies only to covalent compounds.

9.8 Formula Unit Mass of Ionic Compounds

Since ionic compounds do not form molecules, the relevant mass unit is the formula unit mass, which is the mass of one formula unit — the simplest whole-number ratio of ions. Formula unit mass is calculated by adding the atomic masses of all atoms in the formula unit. Worked Example 9.6 calculates the formula unit mass of sodium oxide: Na = 23 u, O = 16 u, so Na₂O = (23 × 2) + (16 × 1) = 62 u. Worked Example 9.7 calculates the formula unit mass of calcium nitrate, Ca(NO₃)₂: Ca = 40 u, N = 14 u, O = 16 u, giving (40 × 1) + {(14 × 1) + (16 × 3)} × 2 = 164 u.
