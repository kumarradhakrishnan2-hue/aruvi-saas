Chapter 3: Tissues in Action

The chapter opens by framing multicellular life as a hierarchy — cells of similar type performing similar functions cluster to form tissues, tissues combine into organs, and organs integrate into organ systems. A tissue is formally defined as a group of structurally similar cells working together to perform a specific function. The formation of specialised tissues enables division of labour, increasing the efficiency and complexity of life processes in both plants and animals.

3.1 Why are Plant and Animal Tissues Different?

This section explains the structural and functional divergence between plant and animal tissues by tracing it to two root differences: lifestyle and nutrition. Plants are largely immobile and require rigid support, which cell walls provide; animals lack rigid walls, giving cells the flexibility needed for locomotion. The mode of nutrition also diverges: animals digest externally obtained food, while plants synthesise food through photosynthesis using solar energy. Both kingdoms have distinct conducting tissues for transporting food and water, and their growth patterns differ because the tissues responsible for growth are structurally and functionally distinct.

3.2 Tissues for Growth in Plants

Growth in plants occurs in three ways — increase in length (height of stem and depth of roots), increase in girth (thickness of stem), and regrowth after cutting or grazing. All these require actively dividing cells, which together constitute meristematic tissue. The section introduces the three kinds of meristematic tissue and then transitions to permanent tissues formed by differentiation.

3.2.1 Apical meristem — How do plants grow in length?

The apical meristem is the actively dividing tissue located at the tips of roots and shoots, responsible for increasing plant length. The section establishes through experimental reasoning that roots grow only from their tips: when tips are removed, growth stops, confirming that tip cells divide continuously (connecting to the mitosis observed in onion root tips in Chapter 2). Similarly, shoot tips contain actively dividing cells. (Activity 3.1: Students set up two jars with onion bulbs, measure root growth over several days, cut the root tips of one bulb on day 3, and observe that tip-removed roots cease elongation while intact roots continue — inferring the role of apical meristem in length growth.)

3.2.2 Lateral meristem — How do plants grow in girth?

The lateral meristem consists of actively dividing cells arranged in a ring within the stem; these cells divide to produce new cells concentrically inward and outward, increasing stem diameter. Annual growth rings visible on a cut trunk cross-section are the product of lateral meristem activity — wide rings indicate favourable growth conditions and narrow rings unfavourable ones. Scientists use the count of annual rings to estimate tree age and reconstruct past climatic conditions.

3.2.3 Intercalary meristem — How do plants grow after being cut?

When the tip of a stem is removed, length growth stops but new branches arise from the nodes. The intercalary meristem is located at the base of the internode or just above the node; it enables regeneration after cutting or grazing. This is why hedges regrow bushily after trimming and grass reappears after mowing or grazing. The three meristematic types together account for all plant growth: apical for length, lateral for girth, intercalary for regrowth and branching. Meristematic cells share characteristic features — small size, thin walls, large prominent nucleus, dense cytoplasm, many organelles, absence of vacuoles, and tight packing with little or no intercellular space — all of which support continuous and rapid division.

3.2.4 Permanent tissues

When meristematic cells lose the ability to divide, they undergo structural and functional changes and become permanent tissues through a process called differentiation. Permanent tissues are specialised for specific functions such as protection, support, or transport, and may be simple (one cell type) or complex (more than one cell type). The section prompts students to examine transverse sections of stems and leaves under a microscope, observing that the sunflower stem contains several distinct tissue types arranged in functional groups.

(i) Protective tissue — Epidermis

The epidermis forms the outermost single layer of tightly packed, flat and rectangular cells covering all plant parts. It is coated with a waxy cutin layer called the cuticle, which reduces water loss through transpiration and protects against mechanical injury and parasite invasion; plants in dry habitats have a thicker cuticle. In roots, epidermal projections called root hairs increase the surface area for water and mineral absorption. In leaves, the epidermis contains stomata, which facilitate gaseous exchange and transpiration; transpiration in turn creates the transpiration pull that drives water movement in xylem and aids in elimination of metabolic wastes.

(ii) Supporting tissue — Simple permanent tissues

Simple permanent tissues provide structural support and are of three types: parenchyma, collenchyma, and sclerenchyma, each differing in wall composition and the kind of support provided. (Pause and Ponder 1: Students are prompted to find out why coconut husk fibres are hard and brittle while coriander leaf stalks are soft and flexible, drawing on the structural differences between sclerenchyma and collenchyma.)

a. Parenchyma

Parenchyma consists of living cells with thin walls and intercellular spaces; cells are loosely packed. It is the primary storage tissue in plants and also performs photosynthesis in green regions. In aquatic plants, specialised parenchyma contains large air spaces (aerenchyma) that provide buoyancy.

b. Collenchyma

Collenchyma is composed of living cells with unevenly thickened corners due to pectin deposition. This unevenness gives the tissue both support and flexibility, allowing plant parts such as stems and tendrils to bend without breaking.

c. Sclerenchyma

Sclerenchyma cells have uniformly thick walls impregnated with lignin, making them hard, strong, and almost always dead at maturity. This tissue is found in stems, leaf veins, and the hard outer coverings of seeds and nuts (e.g., coconut husk, walnut shell), where mechanical strength rather than flexibility is needed.

(iii) Conducting tissues — Complex permanent tissues

Complex permanent tissues are composed of more than one cell type working together; xylem and phloem are the two conducting tissues in plants. Xylem transports water and minerals from roots upward to all plant parts and also provides mechanical strength; it consists of tracheids, vessels (both tubular, thick-walled, and primarily dead), xylem parenchyma (the only living xylem component), and xylem fibres (sclerenchymatous). Phloem is predominantly composed of living cells and transports food (assimilates) from leaves to other plant parts; it consists of sieve tubes (long tubular cells joined by perforated end walls), companion cells (specialised parenchyma that regulate loading and unloading of sugars in sieve tubes), phloem parenchyma (which stores food materials, resins, tannins, and latex), and phloem fibres (sclerenchymatous, providing strength). Plant tissues are not isolated but are organised into three tissue systems: the dermal tissue system (outer covering, protection, water loss reduction), the ground tissue system (main plant body between dermal and vascular tissues, comprising parenchyma, collenchyma, and sclerenchyma), and the vascular tissue system (xylem and phloem). (Pause and Ponder 2–4: Students are asked why a thick cuticle is advantageous in deserts but disadvantageous underwater, how dead xylem cells collaborate with living leaf cells to keep water moving against gravity, and what would happen to a plant if stomata were absent from stems or leaves.)

3.3 Animal Tissues

Animal cells also group into specialised tissues that perform distinct functions. The section introduces the four major animal tissue types — epithelial, connective, muscular, and nervous — framing each through everyday bodily experiences such as blinking, clenching a fist, breathing, and sensing temperature.

3.3.1 Epithelial tissues — Structure and functions

Epithelial tissue forms the outer body covering (skin) and lines internal organs such as the mouth, lungs, blood vessels, and intestine. Cells are closely packed with minimal intercellular space, preventing pathogen entry, reducing water loss, and facilitating absorption, secretion, and substance movement. Different structural variants are adapted for different functions: a single layer of thin, flat cells suits rapid gas/liquid exchange (lining blood vessels and lungs); multiple layers of flat tightly packed cells provide mechanical protection against friction and microbes (skin, mouth, oesophagus); cuboidal or columnar secretory cells produce mucus, enzymes, hormones, and sweat (salivary glands, stomach lining); specialised receptor cells with hair-like cilia support sensory functions of smell, taste, sound, and balance (nostrils, taste buds, inner ear); and a single layer of tall pillar-like cells with hair-like projections maximises nutrient and water absorption (lining of small intestine).

3.3.2 How are various parts connected in our body?

Connective tissue connects and supports other tissues and organs. Blood and bone are both connective tissues but differ in matrix composition: blood has a fluid, watery matrix (plasma) carrying red blood cells (RBCs, containing haemoglobin for oxygen transport and living about 4 months), white blood cells (WBCs, which collect at infection sites and cause inflammation and pus), and platelets (which initiate blood clotting). Bone has a rigid matrix of calcium and phosphorus compounds providing strength and rigidity; cartilage has a soft, jelly-like matrix offering flexibility and cushioning at bone ends. Tendons connect muscles to bones and transmit the contractile force that produces movement; ligaments connect bones to bones, provide joint stability, limit excessive movement, and help prevent dislocation. (Activity 3.2: Students recall everyday experiences — a skin cut, a skin infection, exercise — and connect them to the components and functions of blood.) (Activity 3.3: Students perform physical actions — touching elbow, pressing ear, wiggling fingers, moving leg at knee — to experientially identify bone, cartilage, tendon, and ligament respectively, then compare with the table.)

3.3.3 Can we control movement in our body?

Voluntary movements (running, writing, lifting) are under conscious control and are carried out by skeletal muscles attached to the skeleton. Skeletal muscle cells are long, cylindrical, unbranched, multinucleate (many nuclei per cell), and striated (showing light and dark bands). Involuntary movements (food movement in the intestine, heartbeat) occur without conscious control. Smooth muscles, found in organs like the stomach and intestines, have spindle-shaped, uninucleate, non-striated cells and produce slow, continuous movements such as digestion. Cardiac muscles, found only in the heart, have cylindrical, branched, uninucleate fibres with faint striations; they work rhythmically and tirelessly throughout life without fatigue.

3.3.4 How does the body sense, communicate and respond?

Nervous tissue forms the body's control and coordination network, enabling rapid responses (such as withdrawing a hand from heat) and complex functions (such as memory). The brain is the control centre, coordinating activities across the body; all muscles — voluntary and involuntary — receive instructions from nervous tissue. The functional unit of nervous tissue is the neuron (nerve cell), specialised to receive, process, and transmit messages. Each neuron has three main parts: the cell body (contains the nucleus and controls cell activities), dendrites (branch-like projections that receive signals from other neurons), and an axon (a long fibre that carries messages away from the cell body to axon terminals, which transmit signals to other cells).

3.4 The Musculoskeletal System

The musculoskeletal system integrates bones, muscles, joints, cartilage, tendons, and ligaments into a single functional unit that enables upright posture, movement, balance, and protection of delicate internal organs. It operates under the control of the nervous system: muscles receive neural signals, contract, pull on bones via tendons, and produce movement at joints. The adult human skeleton constitutes approximately 12–15% of body weight, varying with age, gender, and body composition. (Activity 3.4: Students measure their own body weight, use reference data for average bone and muscle mass percentages for their age, gender, and body type, calculate estimated bone and muscle weights, and compare values with classmates to discuss individual variation in body composition.)

3.4.1 The musculoskeletal system in action

The range and direction of movement at a joint depends on the type of joint present. Different body parts (elbow, shoulder, knee, neck, fingers, toes, wrist) allow different combinations of complete rotation, partial rotation, bending, and other movements. A joint is the junction between two or more bones; joints permit movement but cannot cause it — muscles contracting and pulling on bones produce actual movement. (Activity 3.5: Students move different body parts and record whether each allows complete rotation, partial rotation, bending, or other movements, compiling observations in a table to motivate the classification of joint types.)

3.5 Types of Joints

The chapter identifies four types of joints based on structural form and range of movement permitted.

3.5.1 Ball and socket joint

The ball and socket joint is exemplified by the shoulder, where the rounded head of the upper arm bone fits into a shallow hollow of the shoulder bone. Together with the collarbone forming the shoulder girdle, this joint allows movement in all directions — forward, backward, sideways, and circular. It is the most mobile joint type.

3.5.2 Hinge joint

The hinge joint allows movement in a single plane, like a door hinge. The elbow is the primary example: it bends and straightens in one direction only. A similar hinge joint at the knee is protected by the kneecap (patella).

3.5.3 Pivot joint

The pivot joint connects the skull to the backbone, allowing the head to rotate side to side. It functions like a doorknob turning in its socket and is responsible for the "no" head-shaking motion.

3.5.4 Fixed joints

Fixed joints, such as those joining the flat bones of the skull, permit no movement. The skull's immobile joints protect the brain, eyes, and ears from injury even during body movement. (Pause and Ponder 5: Students examine images of classical and folk dance poses of India and identify which joints are involved in each pose and what type of movement each joint allows.) 3.6 Skeletal System

The skeletal system is a framework of bones that provides strength, maintains upright posture, and protects delicate internal organs. It includes three major structures: the skull (flat bones joined by fixed joints, protecting the brain, eyes, and ears), the vertebral column or backbone (a flexible column of small bones called vertebrae extending from the base of the skull, with cartilage discs between each pair of vertebrae acting as cushions and allowing bending and twisting without spinal cord injury), and the rib cage (12 pairs of ribs attached to the spine posteriorly and to the sternum anteriorly via flexible cartilage, which allows the cage to expand and contract during breathing and thereby enables air movement in and out of the lungs; rib injury makes breathing painful).

The chapter also connects this science to Indian traditional practice and society through two Bridging Science and Society passages. The first discusses Yoga — described in ancient Indian texts as combining physical postures, breathing, and meditation — noting research evidence that it improves flexibility, posture, and breathing, reduces stress, and helps prevent lifestyle diseases; International Yoga Day is observed on 21 June each year. Maintaining correct posture, proper nutrition, regular exercise, and yoga together sustain bone strength, muscle fitness, and joint flexibility. The second passage describes crown gall disease in plants, caused by the bacterium Agrobacterium tumefaciens, which triggers tumour-like swellings through uncontrolled cell division; scientists studying how this bacterium transfers its genetic material into plant cells later leveraged this mechanism for plant tissue culture and genetic engineering, and Agrobacterium is now used as a vector to introduce useful genes into plants for improved crops and disease-resistant varieties.
