Chapter 11: Reproduction: How Life Continues

11.1 Asexual Reproduction
Asexual reproduction is found in many unicellular organisms such as bacteria, amoeba, and yeast, as well as in simple multicellular organisms like hydra and sponge, and in many plants. In plants, new individuals arise from vegetative parts without seeds — for example, potato and ginger sprout from fleshy underground stems, money plant and sugarcane grow from stem cuttings, and Bryophyllum leaves produce tiny plantlets. This mode is called vegetative propagation and involves only one parent, producing genetically identical offspring. The central cell-division process underlying all asexual reproduction is mitosis, which yields two daughter cells with the same chromosome number as the parent, making offspring genetically identical clones of the parent; this enables rapid population increase under favourable conditions.

11.1.1 How is vegetative propagation in plants helpful in agriculture?
Because asexual reproduction produces genetically identical individuals, scientists and horticulturists have adapted vegetative propagation into several practical techniques — cutting, grafting, layering, and tissue culture — that allow desirable plant varieties to be propagated efficiently at scale. These methods are widely used in agriculture and horticulture to cultivate high-quality crops. Tissue culture, which produces mass plantlets from shoot-tip meristems, is particularly powerful as it eliminates virus-infected plants and ensures uniform, high-yielding stock, with Krishi Vigyan Kendras (KVKs) under ICAR training farmers in modern grafting for high-yield fruit cultivation. (Activity 11.1) Students visit a school garden or farm to observe the techniques of cutting, grafting, and layering directly with gardeners or farmers, recording the procedure steps, node counts on cuttings, and outcomes in their notebooks.

11.2 Asexual Reproduction in Yeast and Fungi (within 11.1 scope — continued)
In yeast, a small round outgrowth called a bud forms on the parent cell, enlarges, and separates to live independently — a process called budding; in hydra, the same budding mechanism operates at the multicellular level, with multiple buds sometimes visible simultaneously on the parent body. Fungi such as Rhizopus and Aspergillus reproduce by spore formation: spores are produced in enormous numbers (millions per colony) inside sac-like structures or on swollen vesicles on fungal hyphae, are lightweight, usually single-celled, and float on air currents until moisture and nutrients allow them to germinate rapidly into new individuals. Experiments by Louis Pasteur established that new life always arises from pre-existing life, disproving spontaneous generation and supporting the germ theory of disease and the cell theory that all cells arise from pre-existing cells. (Activity 11.2) Students prepare a yeast-sugar solution, allow it to activate, and observe a drop under a compound microscope to identify and draw the budding outgrowths on parent yeast cells. (Activity 11.3) Students set up a moist chamber with bread or roti, keep it warm and dark for three days, and then observe and draw mould growth under a magnifying glass and microscope, comparing their diagrams with figures of Rhizopus and Aspergillus to understand spore-based reproduction.

11.2 Sexual Reproduction
Sexual reproduction involves two parents, each contributing genetic material to the offspring. A critical problem is that if each parent contributed a full set of chromosomes, the chromosome number would double every generation; this is solved by meiosis, a special cell division that halves the chromosome number to produce haploid gametes. In animals, male gametes are called sperm and female gametes are eggs; in plants, pollen grains carry male gametes and deliver them to the ovule which contains the egg (female gamete).

11.2.1 How does meiosis help create variations in sexual reproduction?
Each species has a fixed chromosome number; humans have 23 pairs (46 total), and during meiosis each gamete receives only one chromosome from each pair, resulting in 23 chromosomes per gamete. When two gametes fuse at fertilisation the diploid number is restored. The random segregation of chromosomes during meiosis means each gamete carries a unique combination of genetic information, creating enormous variation among offspring — with 23 chromosome pairs, the possible combinations are astronomically large. This variation is key to species survival, enabling individuals to adapt better to changing environments and contributing over time to evolution. (Activity 11.4) Students use three pairs of differently coloured beads to simulate how meiosis segregates characters, randomly picking one bead from each pair to form combinations; with three pairs, eight combinations are possible, and students are asked to extrapolate to the scale of 23 chromosome pairs.

11.2.2 Sexual reproduction in flowering plants
Flowering plants (angiosperms) are the most diverse plant group; their flowers serve as reproductive organs. A complete flower has four whorls: sepals (outermost, protective), petals (coloured, attracting pollinators), stamens (male part — filament + anther producing pollen grains with male gametes), and a pistil (female part — stigma, style, and ovary containing ovules each with an egg cell). The stamen is the male reproductive part and the pistil is the female part; the stigma tip may be flat or sticky to receive pollen, and the style is a tube connecting stigma to ovary. (Activity 11.5) Students collect different flower types, observe each whorl from outer to inner, record the presence of sepals, petals, stamens, and pistil in a table, cut transverse and longitudinal sections of the ovary under a dissecting microscope, and draw what they observe to infer the function of each part. (Activity 11.6) Students investigate pollination in pea plants by removing stamens from select flower buds and flowers, covering different combinations with muslin bags, and observing fruit development to infer that pollen transfer from anther to stigma is essential for fruit formation.

11.2.3 How does the process of pollination occur in flowers?
Pollination is the transfer of pollen grains from the anther to the stigma of a flower. When pollen is transferred to the stigma of the same flower or another flower on the same plant, it is called self-pollination; when pollen moves from a flower on one plant to the stigma of a flower on a different plant of the same species, it is cross-pollination. Pollination is essential for fruit and seed formation; without it, the ovule cannot be fertilised and fruits do not develop.

11.2.4 Pollination strategies and reproductive success
Pollination depends on external agents called pollinators — wind, water, insects, or birds. Wind-pollinated plants (wheat, maize, rice) produce pollen in large quantities that is light and small, and have long feathery stigmas to trap it. Water-pollinated aquatic plants (Vallisneria, Hydrilla) rely on water currents. Insect-pollinated plants (sunflower, hibiscus, marigold) have brightly coloured, fragrant flowers producing nectar, with large, sticky or spiny pollen and sticky stigmas; bird-pollinated flowers (coral tree, hibiscus) attract sunbirds and Indian white-eye. (Activity 11.7) Students analyse a data table showing pollen production per flower and estimated seeds formed for wind-pollinated grasses (500,000–1,000,000 pollen grains; 50–200 seeds) versus insect-pollinated plants (20,000–40,000 pollen grains; 800–1,000 seeds), comparing the pollen-to-seed ratio and discussing why producing vast quantities of pollen can still be an effective strategy despite its apparent inefficiency.

11.2.5 Fertilisation and seed formation
Once pollen lands on a compatible stigma, pollen grains germinate and produce pollen tubes that grow down through the style into the ovary. The male gamete travels through the tube to the ovule and fuses with the egg cell — this fusion is fertilisation, and the fertilised egg is a zygote, which develops into an embryo. Simultaneously, the ovary enlarges and develops into a fruit while the ovules develop into seeds containing the embryo. Seeds are dispersed by wind, water, or animals; when conditions of water, air, and temperature are favourable, the seed germinates and grows into a new plant.

11.3 Sexual Reproduction in Animals
Animals reproduce sexually and sometimes asexually. In many aquatic animals (frogs, most fish), fertilisation occurs outside the body — external fertilisation — where the female releases eggs into water and the male releases sperm over them; large numbers of eggs are laid but many are lost to water currents or predators. In reptiles, birds, and mammals, internal fertilisation takes place inside the female body, providing greater protection to the fertilised egg or embryo and generally improving survival chances for offspring.

11.4 Variations in Reproduction in Animals
Animals show wide variation in reproductive strategy but all face the same core challenge: ensuring gametes meet and offspring survive long enough to reproduce. Fish (external, water) produce hundreds to thousands of eggs at a time with low offspring survival; frogs (external, water/land) produce 5,000–50,000 eggs per laying; lizards (internal, land) produce 2–20 eggs with moderate survival; birds (internal, water/land) produce 1–15 eggs with moderate to high survival. In fish, amphibians, and insects, the egg yolk is sufficient only to produce a larva, which then feeds on organic matter and grows through a transformation (metamorphosis) before becoming an adult — as seen in butterflies and frogs. In contrast, reptile and bird eggs carry enough yolk to nourish the developing embryo until hatching. In mammals, the zygote develops inside the female body, and young are typically cared for and fed breast milk after birth.

11.5 Reproduction in Human Beings

11.5.1 Reproductive maturity
As a child grows into an adult, reproductive organs mature and begin producing gametes — sperm in males and eggs in females. When a sperm meets an egg inside the female body, a zygote forms, develops into an embryo, and eventually into a foetus in the uterus. This section traces how gametes form and meet, and how new life develops in humans.

11.5.2 What are the parts of the male reproductive system?
The male reproductive system produces sperm and delivers them to the female body. Sperm are produced in two oval-shaped testes located in a skin pouch called the scrotum, which keeps them slightly cooler than body temperature — a necessary condition for sperm formation. The testes also produce hormones that regulate sperm production and cause puberty changes in boys. Sperm travel through the vas deferens, which opens into the urethra — a common passage for urine and sperm. Glands including the seminal vesicles and prostate add nutrient fluids to nourish the sperm and keep them motile. Each sperm has a head containing genetic material and a long tail for swimming.

11.5.3 What are the parts of the female reproductive system?
The female reproductive system consists of a pair of ovaries, oviducts (fallopian tubes), a uterus, and a vagina. The ovaries produce eggs (female gametes) and release hormones that bring about puberty changes and regulate the reproductive cycle. The oviducts connect each ovary to the uterus. The uterus is a bag-like structure where the foetus develops; it opens into the vagina through a narrow passage called the cervix.

11.5.4 How are reproductive cells made?
The formation of gametes is called gametogenesis, which occurs in the testes and ovaries through meiosis, halving the chromosome number. Human body cells have 46 chromosomes; sperm and eggs have 23 each, so that fertilisation restores the species number of 46. In males, gametogenesis produces numerous tiny, actively motile sperm; in females, it produces a single large, nutrient-rich, non-motile egg each cycle. This asymmetry in size, number, stored nutrients, and motility between male and female gametes is characteristic across most animals.

11.5.5 What happens when a sperm meets an egg?
From puberty onwards, usually one mature egg is released from an ovary each month — a process called ovulation (typically around day 14 of the cycle). Before ovulation, the uterine lining thickens in preparation. The egg travels to the oviduct; during sexual intercourse, millions of sperm enter through the vagina and swim up the reproductive tract. If a sperm reaches the egg and fuses with it in the oviduct, a zygote is formed. The zygote undergoes mitotic divisions as it travels to the uterus and implants into the thickened uterine lining — this implantation marks the beginning of pregnancy.

11.5.6 What happens when an egg is not fertilised?
If no fertilisation occurs, the egg degenerates within about a day. The thickened uterine lining — no longer needed — sheds along with blood through the vagina in a process called menstruation (a period), lasting 3–7 days. The menstrual cycle typically repeats every 21–35 days (often ~28 days), beginning at puberty (ages 10–14) and continuing until menopause (~age 50). The chapter presents a diagram of the key stages: menstruation (days 1–5), uterine lining rebuilding (days 6–14), ovulation (day 14), uterine lining thickening (days 15–28), followed by breakdown and repeat if no fertilisation occurs.

11.5.7 Pregnancy and childbirth
Human pregnancy lasts about nine months, divided into three trimesters. In the first trimester, the fertilised egg develops into an embryo (first two months) with major organs forming; from the ninth week it is called a foetus. In the second trimester, the foetus grows bigger and stronger and the mother feels its movements. In the third trimester, the baby grows rapidly and prepares for life outside the womb. During childbirth, uterine muscle contractions push the foetus out through the birth canal; medical or surgical delivery methods are used when a normal vaginal birth is not possible. After birth, breastfeeding is essential as mother's milk provides complete nutrition and disease protection; newborns need warmth, vaccination, and gentle care.

11.5.8 Mother's health during pregnancy
A pregnant woman's health directly affects the baby's growth. She requires a balanced diet rich in proteins, vitamins, and minerals; regular medical check-ups; appropriate light exercise and rest; and emotional support from family. She should avoid smoking, alcohol, and unprescribed medicines. Emotional well-being is as important as physical health during pregnancy.

11.5.9 What does it mean to be sexually mature?
Sexual maturation during adolescence means the body is becoming capable of reproduction — sperm production begins in boys and menstrual cycles begin in girls. This physical capacity develops gradually and does not equate to readiness for adult responsibilities. Emotional maturity — the ability to handle feelings, communicate clearly, and make thoughtful decisions — takes considerably longer to develop than physical sexual maturity.

11.5.10 How can unwanted pregnancies and infections be prevented?
Since sexual activity involves close physical contact, infections called Sexually Transmitted Infections (STIs) — including gonorrhoea, herpes, syphilis, genital warts, and HIV (which can lead to AIDS) — can be transmitted between individuals; some STIs are not yet curable. Using condoms prevents both STI transmission and pregnancy. Other contraceptive methods include oral contraceptive pills (which alter hormone levels to prevent ovulation, with possible side effects) and Intra-Uterine Devices (IUDs) such as Copper-T placed in the uterus. Surgical methods include blocking the vas deferens (males) or fallopian tubes (females) to prevent gamete meeting. Abortion, a surgical removal of an unwanted pregnancy generally within the first trimester, is legal but selective abortion based on foetal sex is strictly prohibited by Indian law to protect the societal sex ratio.

The chapter connects reproduction to several societal applications. Tissue culture has revolutionised banana farming by allowing mass production of virus-free, high-yielding plantlets from apical meristems. Sexual reproduction in plants underpins plant breeding through selective breeding, artificial hybridisation, and genetic engineering, enabling development of high-yielding and disease-resistant crop varieties. Indian scientists at the Central Drug Research Institute, Lucknow, developed the world's first non-steroidal, non-hormonal weekly oral contraceptive pill, now distributed free through the National Family Planning Programme. More than 10 lakh ASHA workers across India promote hygiene, immunisation, family planning, and maternal care under the National Health Mission, particularly in rural communities.
