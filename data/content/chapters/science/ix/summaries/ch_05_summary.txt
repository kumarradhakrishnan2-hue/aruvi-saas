Chapter 5: Exploring Mixtures and their Separation

5.1 How Can We Classify Mixtures?

Mixtures are classified on the basis of their uniformity into two broad categories: homogeneous and heterogeneous. A homogeneous mixture, also called a solution, has a uniform composition throughout — a well-stirred sugar–water mixture tastes equally sweet from first sip to last. Examples include vinegar (acetic acid in water) and aerated drinks (carbon dioxide in water). A heterogeneous mixture, by contrast, is non-uniform; sand particles are easily visible in water and settle over time. The chapter asks students to decide whether oil and water is homogeneous or heterogeneous, establishing that appearance and settling behaviour are key distinguishing criteria. (Think It Over) Students consider why suspended particles settle in muddy water over time but not in milk, and why sunlight appears as bright rays through gaps in dense foliage — priming inquiry into particle size and light scattering before the formal classification begins. (Activity 5.1) Student groups prepare salt-in-water, chalk powder-in-water, and milk-in-water mixtures; pass a laser beam through each; record visibility and Tyndall-effect observations from the side; predict settling behaviour; and then filter each mixture to check for residue on filter paper — establishing empirically that these are different types of mixtures requiring further exploration.

5.2 Solutions

Solutions are homogeneous mixtures formed when a solute dissolves in a solvent. In a sugar–water solution, sugar is the solute and water is the solvent. This section opens the quantitative question of how much solute is present relative to solvent, and whether that proportion can be expressed numerically — setting up the sub-sections on concentration.

5.2.1 Concentration of a solution

Concentration is defined as the amount of solute dissolved in a given amount of solvent or solution. The section establishes why the right proportion always matters: Oral Rehydration Solution (ORS) must contain specified amounts of salt and sugar in a fixed volume of water — altering either quantity produces a salt–sugar solution but not clinically valid ORS. The pesticide-spraying example reinforces the point: too little pesticide fails to protect crops; too much damages crops, soil, and the environment. Understanding concentration is essential across medicine, agriculture, food, cosmetics, and everyday cooking. 5.2.2 How do we express concentration?

The chapter presents three percentage-based methods for expressing concentration, each suited to different practical contexts.

A. Mass by mass percentage (% m/m or % w/w) expresses the grams of solute per 100 g of total solution: % m/m = (mass of solute / mass of solution) × 100. It is used for homogeneous mixtures and for labelling packaged foods (milk powder, spice mixtures), showing how much salt, sugar, or protein is present. A worked example shows that dissolving 10 g of salt in 90 g of water gives 10% m/m.

B. Mass by volume percentage (% m/v or % w/v) expresses grams of solute per 100 mL of solution: % m/v = (mass of solute / volume of solution) × 100. It is preferred when measuring liquid volume is easier than weighing — common in medicines (5% glucose intravenous solution). Dissolving 5 g of glucose in water to make 100 mL gives 5% m/v.

C. Volume by volume percentage (% v/v) expresses millilitres of solute per 100 mL of solution: % v/v = (volume of solute / volume of solution) × 100. It applies when two miscible liquids are mixed, as in perfumes, cosmetics, and vinegar (5% v/v acetic acid). Mixing 1 mL of liquid pesticide to make 100 mL of spray yields 1% v/v. The section notes that industries often use % w/w, numerically equal to % m/m. (Threads of Curiosity) Hospital saline drips are 0.9% m/v sodium chloride — 0.9 g of salt in 100 mL of solution — because this concentration is isotonic with blood and safely replaces lost fluids. (Pause and Ponder) Students calculate zinc oxide content in talcum powder at 4% m/m, determine the % v/v of orange juice concentrate when two 15 mL tablespoons are mixed into 150 mL of juice, and reason through how to make vinegar from 100% glacial acetic acid at 5% v/v.

5.2.3 Solubility of substances

Solubility is the maximum amount of solute that dissolves in a fixed quantity of solvent (100 mL or 100 g) at a given temperature; a solution that cannot dissolve any more solute at that temperature is saturated. The solubility of solid solutes in liquid solvents generally increases with temperature, while the solubility of gases in liquids generally decreases with rising temperature — a distinction important for understanding separation techniques. Solubility differences at different temperatures are exploited to separate substances from mixtures. (Activity 5.2) Students read solubility curves for two hypothetical compounds A and B (plotted as grams per 100 g of water against temperature from 10 °C to 80 °C), predict which dissolves more at a given temperature, fill in comparison statements about how solubility changes from 20 °C to 60 °C for each compound, and then predict what happens when a saturated solution prepared at high temperature is cooled slowly — leading directly into crystallization.

5.3 Methods of Separation of Homogeneous Mixtures

5.3.1 Crystallization

Crystallization is the process of forming crystals from a saturated solution and is used to separate or purify solids from homogeneous mixtures. When a saturated solution prepared at a higher temperature is cooled, the reduced solubility at the lower temperature forces excess solute to separate as pure crystals — solids whose particles are arranged in a regular geometric pattern. The method works particularly well when two solids are both soluble in the same solvent and one is present in small quantity; differential solubility at different temperatures allows the desired compound to crystallise out while impurities remain in solution. Familiar crystals include rock salt, candy sugar (mishri), snowflakes, and frost. (Activity 5.3) Students prepare a saturated copper sulfate solution in water with a drop of dilute sulfuric acid (added by the teacher), filter the hot solution to remove insoluble impurities, allow the filtrate to cool slowly undisturbed, then filter, rinse, and dry the resulting blue crystals on a watch glass — observing that slow cooling produces larger, well-shaped crystals. (Think as a Scientist) Students design an experiment comparing crystal size when a hot saturated copper sulfate solution is cooled rapidly in ice-cold water versus slowly at room temperature, using a controlled split of one batch into two equal parts. (Pause and Ponder) Students use the solubility curves from Activity 5.2 to predict which compound deposits more solid when equal masses of hot saturated solutions are cooled from 80 °C to 60 °C, and reason about whether increasing or decreasing evaporation rate changes the size of common salt crystals. (Activity 5.4) Students observe a diagram showing seawater → concentrated saturated solution → salt crystals and describe in their own words the salt manufacturing process depicted.

5.3.2 Distillation

Distillation separates a homogeneous mixture of two miscible liquids by heating the mixture until the lower-boiling liquid vaporises; the vapour passes through a condenser where it is cooled — usually by circulating water — and condenses as a pure liquid collected in a separate vessel, while the higher-boiling component (or dissolved solid) remains in the distillation flask. This technique requires a boiling point difference of at least about 25 °C between the two components and can also be used to recover a solvent from a solution containing dissolved solids. Acetone (b.p. 56 °C) and water (b.p. 100 °C) are readily separated by distillation because their boiling points differ sufficiently; acetone vaporises long before significant water vapour forms. 5.3.3 Paper Chromatography

Paper chromatography separates the components of a mixture by exploiting differences in how strongly each component interacts with both the solvent (mobile phase) and the paper (stationary phase). The solvent rises through the paper by capillary action, carrying the dissolved components with it at different rates; compounds that interact more with the paper travel more slowly than those that interact more with the solvent, causing them to separate into distinct spots or bands. The technique is used to separate pigments in plant extracts, dyes in inks, and coloured components in food colours. Different solvents — water, alcohol, or solvent mixtures — may be needed depending on the substances involved. The word "chromatography" derives from the Greek chroma (colour) and graphein (to write), reflecting its origin in separating coloured substances. (Activity 5.5) Students draw a pencil baseline 2 cm from the bottom of a chromatography paper strip, mark a black sketch-pen spot at the centre, place the strip in a shallow layer of water so that the water level is below the spot, and observe as the ink separates into different colour bands as the solvent rises — inferring that black ink contains multiple coloured components. An alternative uses green food colour with a 2% m/v salt solution as solvent; green spinach extracts or flower petal pigments may also be tried. (Pause and Ponder) Students assess four True/False statements: whether salt can be separated from solution by evaporation or distillation; whether distillation works for liquids with the same boiling point; whether the solvent level should be above the sample spot at the start; and whether evaporation and crystallization are the same process.

5.4 How Can We Separate the Components of Heterogeneous Mixtures?

5.4.1 Separation of two immiscible liquids

Immiscible liquids do not mix and form separate layers when combined; oil and water, sand and water, and iron filings and sulfur are all examples of heterogeneous mixtures. Two immiscible liquids of different densities can be separated using a separating funnel: the denser liquid settles to the bottom and is drained off through the stopcock first, a small mixed fraction is discarded, and the lighter liquid is then collected separately. In a mustard oil–water system, the yellow mustard oil (less dense) forms the upper layer and water (denser) forms the lower layer. Most gas–gas mixtures are homogeneous; smoke, fog, and airborne dust are heterogeneous mixtures involving gas. Solid–solid heterogeneous mixtures use physical property differences — such as magnetism — for separation. (Activity 5.6) Students pour 5 mL of mustard oil and 20 mL of water into a 50 mL separating funnel, allow the layers to form, drain the lower water layer slowly through the stopcock, discard a small mixed fraction, and collect the oil layer separately. (What if…) Students reason about how layers would form if two immiscible liquids of identical density were placed in a separating funnel, questioning the density-based separation principle.

5.4.2 Sublimation

Sublimation is the direct transition of a solid to the vapour state below its melting point, without passing through the liquid state. On cooling, the vapour reverts directly to solid without becoming liquid — a process called deposition. Camphor and naphthalene are common sublimable substances; solid carbon dioxide (dry ice, used for ice cream storage) also sublimes. Substances that do not sublime (such as sand) can be separated from those that do by gentle heating: the sublimable component vaporises, deposits on a cooler surface, and is thus removed from the non-subliming residue. (Activity 5.7) Students place a camphor–sand mixture in a china dish, invert a cotton-plugged glass funnel over it, heat gently, and observe white camphor deposits forming on the inner funnel wall while sand remains in the dish — directly demonstrating sublimation and deposition. This section also introduces alloys: homogeneous mixtures of two or more metals (or a metal and a non-metal) formed by mixing molten metals. Alloys cannot be separated by physical methods because they solidify into a single-appearing material. Examples include brass (~80% copper, 20% zinc), bronze (~80% copper, 20% tin), and stainless steel (iron with carbon 0.03–0.8%, chromium 16–18%, nickel 10–14%, molybdenum 2–3%), all prepared to improve strength, rigidity, or corrosion resistance. (Pause and Ponder) Students justify whether immiscible liquids form separate layers in a separating funnel, and whether sublimation differs from evaporation.

5.4.3 Suspensions

Suspensions are heterogeneous mixtures in which undissolved solid particles remain dispersed throughout a liquid medium without dissolving. Unlike solutions, the particles in a suspension are large enough to be visible to the naked eye (greater than 1000 nm in diameter); they do not dissolve and settle over time when the mixture is left undisturbed. Examples include sand in water, sawdust in water, and tea leaves in water. Simple filtration removes larger particles but often leaves water still cloudy with very fine particles, necessitating additional techniques such as centrifugation and coagulation.

A. Centrifugation spins a mixture at high speed in tubes; as the tubes become horizontal, centrifugal force drives heavier particles outward to settle at the bottom of the tube while the lighter liquid remains at the top. The technique is widely used in laboratories to separate blood components (red blood cells and plasma) and in chemical industries. (Activity 5.8) Students make their own model centrifuge using a cardboard disc and thick thread, observing how the heavier particles move outwards, and decide which mixture they would like to separate with it.

B. Coagulation involves adding a coagulant (such as powdered alum, fitkari) to a suspension; the coagulant causes fine suspended particles to clump together into larger aggregates that then settle by sedimentation. The settled clumps can be removed by decantation or filtration. The formation of paneer from milk is a familiar food example: acid (lemon juice or vinegar) causes coagulation of milk proteins, producing cheese.

5.4.4 Colloids

Colloids are a distinct category of mixture — neither true solutions nor true suspensions — in which dispersed particles range from 1 to 1000 nm in diameter. Unlike suspensions, colloid particles do not settle over time; they remain uniformly dispersed throughout the dispersion medium as in a solution. Unlike solutions, colloid particles are large enough to scatter light. Blood, milk, tomato sauce, and ice cream are all colloids. The component particles form the dispersed phase; the medium in which they are dispersed is the dispersion medium. Emulsions are colloids in which both the dispersed phase and the dispersion medium are liquids: oil-in-water emulsions (milk, vanishing cream) and water-in-oil emulsions (butter, body lotion, cold cream). Emulsifying agents such as milk proteins stabilise emulsions. Some medicines are formulated as emulsions to aid dispersal in water and reduce the greasy feel. (Threads of Curiosity) This section notes the role of emulsifying agents in stabilising emulsions, and that simple emulsions can be made by shaking cooking oil with water containing a few drops of soap solution. (Pause and Ponder) Students reason about whether clouds — tiny water droplets or ice crystals in air — are solutions, suspensions, or colloids; and explain why cities with heavy smoke and dust appear hazy.

5.5 Tyndall Effect

The Tyndall effect is the scattering of light by particles in a colloid or suspension, making the path of a light beam visible from the side. It does not occur when light passes through a transparent solution because solute particles are too small (less than 1 nm) to scatter light. Colloid particles (1–1000 nm) and suspension particles (greater than 1000 nm) are large enough to scatter light, producing a visible beam. The phenomenon is named after the scientist John Tyndall. Everyday observations include sunlight streaming through gaps in leaves, floodlights in sports stadiums, and the illuminated beam visible when light enters a dark room through a small hole — all caused by scattering from dust, smoke, or water droplets in air. (Activity 5.9) Students complete a comparison table (Table 5.1) filling in the nature, particle size, visibility, separation by filtration, settling behaviour, and Tyndall effect for solutions, suspensions, and colloids — consolidating the three-way classification introduced in sections 5.1 through 5.5.

The chapter also connects this science to India's heritage and to modern social challenges. Ancient Indian coastal communities produced two distinct salt types by crystallization: panga salt, obtained by boiling concentrated sea brine, and karkatch salt, produced by solar evaporation of seawater — yielding crystals of different sizes. The traditional Deg-Bhapka distillation method, practiced in Kannauj, Uttar Pradesh (India's perfume capital), captures the post-rain petrichor fragrance from soil and transforms it into Mitti ka Ittar; the Fragrance and Flavour Development Centre in Kannauj supports farmers and entrepreneurs working with fragrance-producing plants. A Bridging Science and Society feature on the paperfuge — a hand-powered, electricity-free centrifuge made from cardboard and string — shows how the principle of centrifugation can be applied in a low-cost device to separate blood components for malaria and anaemia detection in remote areas.
