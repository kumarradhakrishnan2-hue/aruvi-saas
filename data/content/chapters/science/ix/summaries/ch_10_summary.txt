Chapter 10: Sound Waves: Characteristics and Applications

10.1 Production of Sound
This section establishes that sound is produced by the vibration of objects. It begins by recalling prior learning about sonority and the role of vibration in producing sound in musical instruments. The key concept introduced is vibration — defined as the periodic to-and-fro (oscillatory) motion of an object — and students learn that sound ceases as soon as vibration stops. The section also notes that sound can be produced by vibrating strings, membranes, air columns, and other vibrating bodies, and that the object producing sound is called the source. In humans, sound is produced by the vibration of vocal cords inside the larynx; the tongue, lips, mouth, and nasal cavity shape this into speech.
(Activity 10.1) Students stretch a rubber band across an open cardboard box, pluck it to observe that sound is produced only while the band vibrates, change tension to observe how the sound changes, and then remove the band from the box to compare loudness — concluding that sound is produced by vibrations.

10.1.1 Tuning fork
A tuning fork is a U-shaped metal bar (steel or aluminium) with a stem; its prongs or tines are struck against a rubber pad to produce vibration. The section explains why a soft pad is used (to avoid damage) and establishes that a vibrating tuning fork confirms the vibration-sound link through two observations: hearing the sound and watching ripples on a water surface when the prong touches it.
(Activity 10.2) Students strike a tuning fork against a rubber pad, hold it near the ear in different orientations to verify sound production, and touch the vibrating prong to a water surface to see waves form — reinforcing that sound arises from vibrating objects.

10.2 Propagation of Sound
This section addresses how sound travels from a vibrating source to a listener and whether it can travel through all states of matter. The material through which sound propagates is defined as the medium. Two successive investigations demonstrate that sound travels through solids and through liquids, extending the earlier understanding that it travels through gases (air).
(Activity 10.3) One student knocks on a desk while the other listens first through the air and then with ear pressed to the desk surface, establishing that sound travels through solids.
(Activity 10.4) Two metal spoons are tapped together first in air, then submerged in a water-filled tub, demonstrating that sound travels through liquids.

10.2.1 Sound needs a medium to propagate
The section uses the vacuum bell jar experiment to demonstrate that sound cannot travel without a medium. An electric bell inside a sealed jar becomes progressively fainter as air is pumped out and is almost inaudible at near-vacuum; sound returns when air is let back in. The section applies this to outer space, explaining why spacewalking astronauts cannot hear each other directly and must use electronic communication devices built into their suits. The conclusion is that sound is a mechanical disturbance requiring a solid, liquid, or gaseous medium to propagate.

10.3 Sound Waves
This section explains how sound propagates mechanically through a medium using two analogies: a slinky and an oscillating piston in a tube. The slinky analogy shows that a disturbance travels along the spring as regions of closely spaced and widely spaced turns (compressions and rarefactions), while the marked coil itself only oscillates back and forth about its position — it does not travel with the wave. Applying this to air: a forward-moving piston compresses nearby air particles, creating a region of higher-than-average density called a compression (C); a backward-moving piston creates a lower-density region called a rarefaction (R). Alternate compressions and rarefactions propagate through the air as a sound wave. The disturbance (not the particles) travels outward. In an open medium, sound spreads in all directions as spherical waves from a point source. Sound waves are longitudinal waves — particles of the medium vibrate parallel to the direction of wave propagation. Since they require a material medium, sound waves are classified as mechanical waves.
(Activity 10.5) Students lay a marked slinky on a table and push-pull one end rhythmically while a partner holds the other end fixed, observing that compressions and rarefactions travel along the slinky while the marked coil only oscillates about its position — modelling how sound propagates through a medium.

10.4 Energy of Sound Waves
This section establishes that sound is a form of energy by showing that it can do mechanical work on matter at a distance. When a loud sound is produced near a taut membrane stretched over a container with grains sprinkled on it, the grains jump even though the source is not touching the membrane. The explanation is that sound waves transfer energy through the medium by particle vibration and particle collisions; it is the energy, not the medium particles themselves, that is transported. The section introduces microphones (converting sound energy to electrical energy via a vibrating diaphragm) and speakers (doing the reverse) as practical examples of sound energy conversion.
(Activity 10.6) Students stretch a cellophane or rubber sheet over a wide-mouthed container, sprinkle grains evenly on it, and produce loud sounds nearby without touching the container — observing grains move or jump as evidence that sound carries energy.

10.5 Graphical Representation of a Sound Wave
This section introduces the graph of a sound wave as a plot of the density of the medium against distance from the source at a given instant of time. Regions of compression appear as crests (density above average) and rarefactions appear as troughs (density below average). The highest point on the graph is called the crest and the lowest the trough. The section establishes that this graphical form makes it possible to read off wave properties such as wavelength and amplitude visually, preparing students for the quantitative section that follows.

10.6 Characteristics of a Sound Wave

10.6.1 Wavelength, frequency and time period
Three fundamental wave quantities are defined here. Wavelength (λ, SI unit: metre) is the distance between two consecutive crests or two consecutive troughs. Frequency (ν, SI unit: hertz, Hz) is the number of complete density oscillations at a fixed point per unit time; it is what distinguishes musical notes — each note has a distinct frequency. Time period (T, SI unit: second) is the time taken for one complete density oscillation at a fixed point; frequency and time period are inversely related by ν = 1/T. The section notes that everyday sounds contain a mixture of frequencies, while nearly pure single-frequency sounds can be produced by a tuning fork or by whistling. A worked example calculates frequency and time period from oscillation count and duration.
(Activity 10.7 — teacher demonstration) Using the Phyphox app's Audio Spectrum mode, the teacher facilitates students singing musical notes Sa through Ni or using a tone-generating app, observing how frequency changes with each note and recording approximate frequency values — revealing that each note has a distinct frequency and that Sa is the lowest.

10.6.2 Amplitude and intensity of the sound waves
The amplitude of a sound wave is defined as the maximum change in the density of the medium in a compression (or rarefaction) compared to average density; a larger density change corresponds to larger amplitude. A wave with larger amplitude carries more energy than one with smaller amplitude. The intensity of sound is defined as the amount of sound energy passing through a unit area perpendicular to the direction of propagation per unit time. As sound travels away from its source it spreads over a larger area, so the same amount of energy covers more area and intensity decreases with distance. Sounds with larger initial amplitude carry more energy and can travel farther before intensity drops to zero.

10.6.3 Speed of Sound
The speed of sound is defined as the distance a point on a wave (such as a crest) travels per unit time. For a wave of given frequency, one wavelength is covered in one time period, giving the key relation v = λν. The speed of sound depends on the medium: it is fastest in solids, slower in liquids, and slowest in gases — roughly 15–20 times faster in solids and 4–5 times faster in water than in air. Speed in air also increases with temperature and humidity; for example, it is approximately 331 m s⁻¹ in dry air at 0°C and 344 m s⁻¹ at 22°C. In most media, speed depends only on the medium, not on the source or frequency — so if frequency changes, wavelength adjusts while speed stays constant. Several worked examples apply v = λν to calculate wavelengths for the audible range extremes, estimate lightning distance from thunder delay, and find frequency and time period from a graphical representation.
(What if… box) Students are prompted to reason what would happen to the quality of music if the speed of sound in air depended on its frequency — this is a body-text thought prompt classified as a reasoning task.

10.6.4 Human perception of sound
This section distinguishes measurable physical properties from subjective perception. Pitch is how humans perceive frequency: high-frequency sounds are perceived as shrill (high pitch) and low-frequency sounds as deep (low pitch), though the exact mathematical relation is complex. The human audible range is 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz (20 kHz); sounds below 20 Hz are infrasonic waves and those above 20 kHz are ultrasonic waves. Some animals can detect frequencies outside this range — dogs, cats, bats, and dolphins detect ultrasound; elephants detect infrasound. Loudness is how humans perceive amplitude: larger-amplitude sounds are heard as louder; loudness decreases with distance. Intensity is a physical measurement while loudness is subjective and depends on the listener's hearing ability. Sound loudness is measured in decibels (dB). Unwanted or harmful sound is noise; prolonged exposure above safe limits can cause hearing loss, detectable by audiograms, and may be addressed with hearing aids (microphone + amplifier + speaker).
(Activity 10.8 — teacher demonstration) Using a mobile app to generate tones, the teacher leads students from 100 Hz to 1000 Hz in 100 Hz steps and then down toward 20 Hz, having students listen and describe how sound changes with frequency — establishing that higher frequency sounds are perceived as shriller.

10.7 Reflection of Sound
Sound waves bounce off obstacles (solids or liquids) in a manner analogous to light reflection: the incident and reflected sound make equal angles with the normal to the reflecting surface, and all three lie in the same plane. This section frames the laws of reflection for sound and introduces the two most common acoustic consequences — echo and reverberation — that follow from this principle.

10.7.1 Echo
An echo is a reflected sound that reaches the listener after a perceptible time delay. It is produced when sound reflects off a hard, smooth, distant surface such as a mountain, cliff, or long corridor wall. The minimum time interval the human brain requires to perceive two sounds as separate is 0.1 s; if the reflected sound arrives within less than 0.1 s it merges with the original and no distinct echo is heard. Taking the speed of sound as 340 m s⁻¹, the minimum echo distance from a reflecting surface is 17 m (sound must travel to the surface and back, totalling 34 m in 0.1 s). Hard, smooth surfaces produce stronger echoes; soft surfaces absorb and rough surfaces scatter sound, reducing echo clarity. A worked example applies the echo formula to calculate distance from a wall given the delay time.

10.7.2 Reverberation
Reverberation occurs in large enclosed spaces (halls, auditoriums) where sound undergoes multiple reflections from walls and other surfaces. When reflected sounds arrive with time differences of less than 0.05 s, they cannot be distinguished from the original sound and the sound appears to persist after the source has stopped. Reverberation is architecturally managed in modern auditoriums through sound-absorbing panels, upholstered seats, curtains, and porous materials, which reduce unwanted multiple reflections and prevent garbling. A well-designed auditorium achieves controlled reverberation so the audience at all positions hears clear, undistorted sound.

10.8 Ultrasonic and Infrasonic Waves, and their Applications
This section surveys the practical uses of sound at frequencies outside the human audible range. Infrasonic waves (below 20 Hz) are used to detect earthquakes, volcanic eruptions, and severe storms because they travel long distances through both air and the Earth. Ultrasonic waves (above 20 kHz) have a wide range of applications: imaging internal organs without surgery (ultrasonography), breaking kidney stones into smaller fragments that can pass naturally, ultrasonic welding and cleaning of delicate machine parts, non-destructive testing to detect defects inside metal blocks, and locating objects via reflection.

10.8.1 Echolocation
Echolocation is the biological and technological application of reflected sound waves to locate objects. Bats emit short bursts of ultrasonic waves and use the returning echoes to determine the position of obstacles and prey even in complete darkness. Other animals — dolphins, whales, and certain birds — also use echolocation for navigation and hunting. Humans have adapted this principle in sonar (Sound Navigation and Ranging): ultrasonic pulses are transmitted into water and the reflected signals are analysed to determine the distance, direction, and speed of underwater objects such as submarines or shipwrecks. A worked example uses the sonar equation (distance = v × t/2) to calculate depth from echo return time and wave speed.

The chapter connects sound to several real-world applications. Microphones convert sound energy into electrical signals via a vibrating diaphragm, while speakers reverse the process — both illustrating the transformability of sound energy. Noise pollution, measured in decibels, poses significant health risks including hearing loss; hearing aids (comprising a microphone, amplifier, and speaker) help those affected. Audio surveillance using sensitive sound sensors detects drones and aircraft by their characteristic low-frequency signatures, enabling airspace monitoring for safety and security.
