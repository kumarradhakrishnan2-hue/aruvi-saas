Chapter 4: Describing Motion Around Us

4.1 Motion in a Straight Line
This section introduces linear motion as the simplest kind of motion, defined as movement along a straight line. The chapter situates it within a broader landscape of motion types — linear, circular, and oscillatory — and focuses on linear and uniform circular motion. Students learn that to study any motion, they must first describe an object's position at various instants and track how that position changes over time.

4.1.1 Describing position
The section establishes that describing an object's position requires specifying a reference point, from which the object's distance and direction are measured. The reference point is called the origin and is represented as 'O' on a number line. Positions to the right of O are taken as positive and to the left as negative. An object is said to be in motion if its position with respect to the reference point changes with time, and at rest if it does not.

4.1.2 Distance travelled and displacement
This section distinguishes between two different measures of how an object moves. Distance travelled is the total length of the path covered by the object regardless of direction; it is a scalar quantity requiring only a numerical value. Displacement is the net change in position between two given instants of time — it has both magnitude (the straight-line distance between start and end positions) and direction (from initial to final position), making it a vector-like quantity. The SI unit for both is the metre. A key insight demonstrated with an athlete running back and forth is that the total distance travelled can exceed the magnitude of displacement, and the two are equal only when the object moves in a single direction without turning back. (Activity 4.1) Students analyse the vertical motion of a ball thrown upward and returned to the starting point, filling a table of positions, total distances, and displacements to determine the relationship between the magnitude of displacement and distance travelled.

4.1.3 Average speed and average velocity
Average speed is defined as total distance travelled divided by time interval (Eq. 4.1) and has only a numerical value — no direction. The section distinguishes uniform motion in a straight line (equal distances in equal time intervals, constant speed) from non-uniform motion (unequal distances in equal time intervals). Average velocity is then defined as displacement divided by time interval (Eq. 4.2a–b), giving both magnitude and direction; for straight-line motion, direction is indicated by a positive or negative sign matching that of the displacement. The SI unit for both average speed and average velocity is m s⁻¹. Average velocity is described as the average rate of change of position with respect to time.

4.1.4 Average acceleration
This section introduces average acceleration as the change in velocity divided by the time interval (Eq. 4.3a–c). Like velocity, acceleration has both magnitude and direction; for straight-line motion it is in the direction of velocity when the magnitude of velocity is increasing, and opposite to velocity when the magnitude is decreasing. The SI unit is m s⁻². The section illustrates with worked examples (Example 4.3 — a bus accelerating then braking; Example 4.4 — an object in free fall) that acceleration can be constant across successive time intervals, as in the case of gravitational acceleration g = 9.8 m s⁻². (Activity 4.2) Students look up 0–100 km h⁻¹ acceleration times for various cars on the internet and calculate the magnitude of average acceleration for each, recording results in a table.

4.2 Graphical Representation of Motion
This section introduces graphical methods as a way to visually represent how position, velocity, and acceleration change with time. Graphs allow comparison of two objects' motions, identification of whether motion is uniform or non-uniform, and calculation of physical quantities such as velocity and acceleration. The section restricts discussion to motion in a straight line in one direction, where distance and magnitude of displacement are equal and speed and magnitude of velocity are equal.

4.2.1 Plotting graph
The mechanics of graph-plotting are taught step by step using position-time data for a vehicle (Table 4.3). 4.2.2 Position-time graphs
This section teaches how to read and interpret position-time graphs. A straight-line position-time graph indicates constant velocity; a curved graph indicates changing velocity and thus accelerated motion. A line parallel to the time axis indicates an object at rest. The slope of the position-time graph — the ratio of change in position (BC) to change in time (CA) — equals the average velocity of the object over that interval. Comparing slopes of two lines for different objects shows which object has higher velocity. (Activity 4.4) Students take a segment AB on the plotted position-time graph, draw parallel construction lines to form triangle ABC, and calculate the magnitude of average velocity as BC/CA, confirming the slope interpretation.

4.2.3 Velocity-time graphs
This section extends graphical representation to velocity-time graphs. A straight line parallel to the time axis indicates constant velocity and zero acceleration. A straight line with positive slope indicates constant acceleration in the direction of velocity; a negative slope indicates constant acceleration opposite to velocity. The slope of the velocity-time graph equals the average acceleration (computed as change in velocity divided by change in time, using the triangle method from Figs. 4.17d and 4.17e). The area enclosed between the velocity-time graph and the time axis over a given interval equals the displacement during that interval — demonstrated for both rectangles (constant velocity, Fig. 4.18a) and trapezoids (changing velocity, Fig. 4.18b), with worked numerical examples.

4.3 Kinematic Equations for Motion in a Straight Line with Constant Acceleration
This section derives three kinematic equations that mathematically describe motion with constant acceleration. Starting from the definition of average acceleration and the velocity-time graph (Fig. 4.19), the primary equations are derived: v = u + at (Eq. 4.4a) and s = ut + ½at² (Eq. 4.4b), where u is initial velocity, v is final velocity, t is time, s is displacement, and a is constant acceleration. A third equation, v² = u² + 2as (Eq. 4.4c), is derived by eliminating t from the primary pair. These equations relate the five physical quantities — s, t, u, v, and a — for straight-line motion with constant acceleration; they are valid only under that condition. Example 4.8 applies Eq. 4.4c to compute braking distances for a car decelerating at –4 m s⁻², showing that stopping distance quadruples when initial speed doubles.

4.4 Motion in a Plane
This section broadens the scope from one-dimensional to two-dimensional motion, defining motion in a plane as movement in two dimensions — examples include a vehicle overtaking another, the path of a kicked ball, or a satellite in orbit. The section transitions to circular motion as the main example of planar motion covered at this stage.

4.4.1 Uniform circular motion
This section defines circular motion as motion along a circular path and introduces uniform circular motion as the special case where speed is constant throughout. For one complete revolution, the distance travelled is the circumference 2πR while displacement is zero. Average speed for one revolution is given by v_av = 2πR/T (Eq. 4.5) where T is the time period, while average velocity over one revolution is zero. Although speed is constant, the direction of velocity continuously changes at every point — it is always along the tangent to the circle at that point. Because velocity direction changes continuously, uniform circular motion is accelerated motion even though speed is constant; this distinguishes everyday use of "acceleration" (change in speed magnitude) from the physics definition (any change in velocity, including direction). In the real world, true uniform circular motion is an idealised model underpinning more complex situations such as planetary orbits and vehicle turns. (Activity 4.5) Students place a marble inside a ring on a smooth surface, spin it so it moves along the inner boundary, then lift the ring mid-motion and observe that the marble moves in a straight line after release — demonstrating that the direction of velocity at the instant of release determines subsequent motion.

The chapter connects its core physics to India's historical and contemporary contributions. The concept of speed as distance divided by time is traced to the ancient Indian treatise Aryabhatiya (5th century CE), and a problem from the Ganitakaumudi (14th century CE) is solved using this principle.
