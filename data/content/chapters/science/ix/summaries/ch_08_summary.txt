Chapter 08: Journey Inside the Atom

8.1 Rediscovering the Roots of Atomic Theory

This section traces the philosophical and scientific origins of atomic thinking across ancient India and ancient Greece. Acharya Kanada, in his Sanskrit text Vaisesika Sutras, proposed that matter (dravya) divided repeatedly would yield the smallest, indivisible particles he called parmanus, which combine in dyads and triads to form all material substances. Independently, the Greek philosophers Leucippus and Democritus proposed identical particles they named atomos, meaning indivisible. The section emphasises that this early concept of the atom arose as a philosophical idea, not from experimental observation. In 1808, John Dalton gave the first scientific treatment, proposing that all matter is made of indivisible atoms — the fundamental building blocks — which became the starting point for the modern understanding of atomic structure.

8.2 A Short Historical Journey Through Atomic Models

This section introduces the historical progression of atomic models, explaining that scientists proposed simple pictures of the atom and revised them as new experimental evidence emerged. It establishes the key turning point: until the late 19th century, atoms were regarded as indivisible, but the discovery that certain elements emit radiation (radioactivity) showed atoms must contain smaller particles. In 1897, J. J. Thomson studied the conduction of electric current through low-pressure gases in a cathode ray tube and identified cathode rays as streams of negatively charged particles — electrons — with mass far smaller than atoms. Because cathode rays were independent of the cathode material, Thomson concluded electrons are a universal subatomic component present in every element; the charge of an electron is taken as –1 by convention.

8.2.1 Thomson's model of an atom

Thomson's discovery of the electron raised the question of where the positive charge resides, since atoms are neutral overall. He proposed the plum pudding model: the atom is a uniform sphere of positive charge with electrons distributed throughout it, analogous to a watermelon whose red pulp is the positive matter and whose seeds are the electrons. This was the first genuine attempt to describe how positive and negative charges coexist in a balanced, neutral atom. Though later superseded, it was a foundational step in structural atomic theory.

(Pause and Ponder) Students reason about what would happen if the positive charge in Thomson's clay model were less than the total negative charge of the beads, and evaluate whether an orange or lemon is a better analogy than a watermelon, identifying where each comparison falls short.

8.2.2 Testing Thomson's model: The gold foil experiment

In 1911, Geiger and Marsden, working under Rutherford, fired a narrow beam of positively charged alpha particles at an extremely thin gold foil to test Thomson's model. Thomson's model predicted that the diffuse positive charge would cause only slight deflections; instead, while most alpha particles passed through undeflected, some were deflected at large angles and a few bounced directly back — a result Thomson's model could not explain. This deflection phenomenon is called scattering, giving the experiment its alternate name, the alpha-ray scattering experiment. The surprising backscattering of a few particles pointed decisively to a concentration of positive charge in a very small region, demolishing the plum pudding picture.

(Think as a Scientist) Students observe the gold foil diagram and predict, with a drawn diagram, how the observations would change if the gold foil were made thicker, reasoning about how increased thickness raises the probability of alpha particles encountering a nucleus.

(Pause and Ponder) Students reason about what would happen if negatively charged particles replaced alpha particles in Rutherford's experiment, and explain how a single result — the sharp backscattering of a few alpha particles — is sufficient to rule out Thomson's model entirely.

A. Rutherford's model of an atom

From the gold foil experiment, Rutherford drew three key conclusions that together constitute his planetary model: most of an atom is empty space (explaining why most alpha particles pass through undeflected); all the positive charge and most of the mass are concentrated in an extremely small, dense region called the nucleus; and electrons revolve around the nucleus in orbits, analogous to planets around the Sun. Rutherford calculated the atom's diameter at approximately 10⁻¹⁰ m and the nucleus at approximately 10⁻¹⁵ m — meaning the nucleus is about 10⁵ times smaller than the atom, comparable to a pepper grain at the centre of a cricket ground. This model explained the scattering results but introduced a new puzzle regarding atomic stability.

B. Limitations of Rutherford's model

Rutherford's planetary model could not account for why atoms are stable. Classical physics requires that a charged particle moving in a circular orbit — and therefore continuously accelerating — must radiate energy; an orbiting electron should thus lose energy, spiral inward, and collapse into the nucleus. Since matter clearly does not collapse, Rutherford's model was incomplete, and a new explanation for electron stability was required. Before addressing this, the section introduces a related discovery: the proton.

C. Discovery of the proton

Rutherford established that the nucleus carries positive charge from particles called protons, which are much heavier than electrons and carry a charge equal in magnitude but opposite in sign. For an atom to be electrically neutral, its number of protons must equal its number of electrons — helium (2 protons, 2 electrons) and sodium (11 protons, 11 electrons) illustrate this. The identity and charge balance of every element therefore rest on this equality of proton and electron counts.

(Pause and Ponder) Students work an assertion-reason question evaluating whether Rutherford's conclusion about nuclear mass concentration is correctly explained by Thomson's model of uniformly distributed positive charge.

8.2.3 Bohr's model of the atom

Niels Bohr resolved the stability problem in 1913 by proposing that electrons occupy fixed circular paths called stationary states, orbits, or shells, each characterised by a definite, constant energy — hence the term energy levels. The shells are labelled K, L, M, N … (or n = 1, 2, 3, 4 …), with K being closest to the nucleus and having the lowest energy; energy increases with distance from the nucleus. Electrons may move between shells only by absorbing or emitting energy exactly equal to the difference between the two levels, and each shell can hold only a limited number of electrons. Crucially, in a stationary state an electron does not lose energy despite its motion, resolving the collapse problem that undermined Rutherford's model.

(Threads of Curiosity) Students learn why Bohr's shells are labelled K, L, M, N rather than A, B, C, D: the notation originated with X-ray physicist Charles Barkla, who reserved lower letters in case an earlier series were discovered (none was), and Bohr adopted the same convention.

8.3 What Components Contribute to the Mass of an Atom?

This section opens the puzzle of nuclear mass: Rutherford's model established that most atomic mass is in the nucleus, and electrons contribute negligibly. Yet helium (2 protons) has a mass about four times that of hydrogen (1 proton), not double — a discrepancy that suggested something else in the nucleus was adding mass without adding charge. The section sets up this unresolved problem before introducing Chadwick's solution.

(What if …) Students consider how the size of everyday objects would be affected if atoms had no empty space.

8.3.1 Discovery of the Neutron

In 1932, James Chadwick (a student of Rutherford) resolved the mass discrepancy by discovering a new subatomic particle with mass nearly equal to a proton but with no electrical charge — the neutron, symbol n⁰. Neutrons are found in the nucleus of all atoms except ordinary hydrogen; together with protons (the nucleons), they account for virtually all atomic mass. A table of the three subatomic particles lists electron (e⁻, charge –1), proton (p⁺, charge +1), and neutron (n⁰, charge 0). The section also explains the role of neutrons in nuclear stability: because protons repel each other electrostatically, heavier nuclei need progressively more neutrons both to dilute the repulsion by spacing protons apart and to strengthen the nuclear force that binds nucleons together — which is why uranium has 146 neutrons to its 92 protons.

(Threads of Curiosity) Students learn that lighter atoms (e.g., carbon, oxygen) have roughly equal numbers of protons and neutrons, while heavier atoms have many more neutrons, with uranium carrying 92 protons and 146 neutrons.

8.4 Symbols of Elements

Dalton introduced the first pictorial symbols for elements in 1803, and in 1813 Berzelius proposed alphabetic symbols derived from Latin names, giving rise to the current system approved by IUPAC. The norms are: symbols use the first letter or first two letters of the element name; the first letter is always capitalised and the second (if present) is lowercase; some symbols use a non-adjacent letter (e.g., Cl for chlorine, Zn for zinc); and some symbols derive from Latin, Greek, or German names (Fe from ferrum, Hg from hydrargyros, W from wolfram). A reference table lists symbols for 27 common elements. Standardised symbols enable international communication in chemistry regardless of language.

(Pause and Ponder) Students invent a name and symbol for a hypothetical newly discovered element and verify it against IUPAC rules, then reflect on the confusion that would arise if different scientists used different symbols for the same element.

8.5 Atomic Number

The atomic number (symbol Z) is defined as the number of protons in the nucleus of an atom and uniquely identifies every element. Because atoms are electrically neutral, the number of protons always equals the number of orbiting electrons. Hydrogen (Z = 1) has one proton and one electron; helium (Z = 2) has two of each; lithium (Z = 3) has three of each. The atomic number is therefore both an identity label and a count of electrons, which governs all of the atom's chemical behaviour.

8.6 Mass Number

The mass number (symbol A) equals the total number of nucleons (protons + neutrons) in the nucleus. Because the electron mass is negligible, A accounts for virtually all atomic mass. Examples: hydrogen (1 proton, 0 neutrons, A = 1), helium (2 protons, 2 neutrons, A = 4), lithium (3 protons, 4 neutrons, A = 7). The standard notation places A as a superscript and Z as a subscript to the left of the element symbol (e.g., ¹²₆C for carbon-12). The number of neutrons is therefore A – Z.

(Pause and Ponder) Students solve numerical problems: finding electrons, protons, and neutrons given atomic number and nucleon count; finding neutrons given atomic number and mass number; and finding the mass number of an atom given neutrons and atomic number.

8.7 How Are Electrons Distributed in Different Energy Levels?

The Bohr–Bury rules for filling electron shells state: the maximum number of electrons in a shell is given by 2n², so K holds 2, L holds 8, and M holds 18; the outermost shell can hold no more than 8 electrons (K is exceptional at 2 maximum); and electrons fill shells in order from the innermost outward (K before L, L before M, etc.). Starting from hydrogen (Z = 1) with its one electron in K, each additional element is built by adding one electron to the appropriate shell, yielding the electronic configuration for each element.

8.8 Combining Capacity of an Atom: Valency

The valence shell is the outermost shell, and the electrons in it are valence electrons. Atoms with a complete octet (8 valence electrons), or 2 electrons in the case of helium, are stable and largely unreactive. Atoms with incomplete valence shells react by losing, gaining, or sharing electrons to achieve an octet — the octet rule. Valency is defined as the number of electrons an atom loses, gains, or shares to complete its outermost shell: sodium (config. 2, 8, 1) loses one electron, giving valency 1; oxygen (config. 2, 6) gains two electrons, giving valency 2; carbon (config. 2, 4) shares four electrons, giving valency 4. Elements with a complete octet already (noble gases) have valency 0. The section closes by noting that some compounds violate simple valency rules, to be addressed in higher grades, and prompts students to extend Table 8.4 with a valency column.

8.9 A Deeper Look into Atomic Structure

This section transitions from the basic nuclear model to two further structural features — isotopes and isobars — that reveal the atom's internal complexity beyond Dalton's original picture of identical, indivisible atoms.

8.9.1 Isotopes

Isotopes are atoms of the same element that have the same atomic number (Z) but different mass numbers (A), owing to different neutron counts. Hydrogen illustrates this with three naturally occurring isotopes: protium (¹₁H, ~99.98%, no neutrons), deuterium (²₁H, ~0.015%, one neutron), and tritium (³₁H, traces, two neutrons). Carbon has three isotopes: ¹²₆C (most abundant), ¹³₆C, and ¹⁴₆C — all with 6 protons and 6 electrons but differing neutron counts. Isotopes share the same chemical properties because chemical behaviour depends on electron configuration, which is the same across isotopes; they differ in physical properties such as boiling and melting points.

(Pause and Ponder) Students reason about two atoms of sodium with 11 protons each but 12 or 13 neutrons — comparing atomic numbers, mass numbers, and identifying them as isotopes — and calculate the average atomic mass of bromine from its two isotopes at given natural abundances.

A. Average atomic mass

Because isotopes of an element occur in nature in unequal proportions, a simple arithmetic mean of isotopic masses does not accurately represent atomic mass as found in nature. The correct value is the weighted average atomic mass, calculated by multiplying each isotope's mass by its fractional natural abundance and summing the products. Chlorine illustrates this: its two isotopes ³⁵Cl (75%) and ³⁷Cl (25%) give a simple average of 36 u but a weighted average of (35 × 75/100) + (37 × 25/100) = 35.5 u. This fractional value does not mean any single chlorine atom has mass 35.5 u; it means that across a large population of chlorine atoms the weighted average is 35.5 u, accurately reflecting the isotopic mixture as it occurs in nature.

8.9.2 Isobars

Isobars are atoms of different elements that have the same mass number but different atomic numbers. The example given is calcium (Z = 20), potassium (Z = 19), and argon (Z = 18), all with mass number 40. Despite being distinct elements, each has the same total number of nucleons, distinguishing isobars (same A, different Z) from isotopes (same Z, different A). The section closes by noting that even Bohr's model is incomplete — electrons do not follow well-defined orbits but exist as probability clouds — and that the quantum mechanical model, to be studied in higher grades, carries the story forward.
