Chapter 6: How Forces Affect Motion

6.1 The Concept of Force

This section establishes the concept of force as a physical quantity that requires both magnitude and direction to be specified — making it a vector quantity, consistent with position, displacement, velocity, and acceleration introduced in Chapter 4. A force can set a stationary object in motion, change the speed or direction of a moving object, and alter the shape of an object; familiar examples include kicking a ball, a cricket bat striking a ball, and fingers squeezing a lemon. The SI unit of force is the newton, written with a lowercase 'n' and symbolised as N. If either the magnitude or the direction of a force changes, the effect of the force also changes.

(Think It Over) Students consider why a canoe moves forward when the canoeist pushes water backwards, and in which of two canoes — one empty, one carrying a passenger — the same paddle force produces greater speed.

6.1.1 Measuring the magnitude of a force

This subsection explains how the magnitude of a force is measured using a spring balance, which students have used earlier to measure the weight of objects. The weight of an object is itself the gravitational force with which the Earth pulls the object; a spring balance measures this and, more generally, any force applied to it. The section notes that in everyday life the smallest forces humans can directly feel are of the order of millinewtons, while specialised scientific experiments can detect forces as small as yoctonewtons (10⁻²⁴ N).

6.2 Balanced and Unbalanced Forces

This section introduces the idea that in real situations multiple forces typically act on an object simultaneously, and what matters is the net force. When two forces of equal magnitude act in opposite directions on an object — as in a tug-of-war with equally matched teams — they are called balanced forces and the object does not change its state of motion; when forces are unequal, a net force acts in the direction of the larger force and the object accelerates. When forces act in the same direction their magnitudes add to give the net force; when they act in opposite directions the net force equals the difference and acts in the direction of the larger force. The section illustrates this with worked Example 6.1, in which 10 N and 6 N forces act on a block in three configurations to yield net forces of 16 N, 4 N, and 4 N respectively.

(Pause and Ponder) Students identify two forces on a barbell held steady by a weightlifter and decide whether they are balanced; they also determine, from an arm-wrestling scenario where arms tilt toward one player, which player exerted the larger force.

6.3 The Force of Friction: Often Overlooked but Always Present

This section deepens understanding of friction, building on prior knowledge from Grade 8. When an object rests on a surface and a push is applied, the object may not move because the force of friction between the contact surfaces acts opposite to the direction of applied force; the object moves only when the applied force exceeds friction so a net force acts. Other forces acting on a pushed object include gravity (downward) and the normal force from the surface (upward), but these two are balanced and do not affect horizontal motion. Once a force is removed from a moving object, friction decelerates it to rest — which is why one must continuously apply force to maintain motion against friction.

The section includes Activity 6.1 (Let us investigate), a student-executed physical activity in which a rubber band launches a stack of four coins across surfaces of different materials (wooden table, laminated table, polished marble/tile floor) and the coin's travel distance is compared across surfaces to conclude that friction is smaller on smoother surfaces. Activity 6.2 (Let us measure) is a student-executed activity using a spring balance and wooden block to directly measure friction on each surface, confirming that the spring balance reading when the block just begins to move approximates the friction force, and that smoother surfaces give smaller readings consistent with longer travel distances in Activity 6.1.

(What if...) Students consider what would happen if friction disappeared from the world and how the motion of objects would be affected.

(Think as a Scientist) Students conduct a thought experiment imagining a surface with zero friction: would the coins' velocity decrease? Would the object ever stop or continue moving forever? This bridges toward Newton's first law.

6.4 Newton's First Law of Motion

This section states Newton's first law: an object at rest remains at rest, and an object in motion continues to move with a constant velocity, unless a net force acts upon it. In the absence of a net force, acceleration is zero — an object cannot begin to move or change its velocity. The section clarifies that "constant velocity" means no change in magnitude or direction; if non-zero, the object moves in a straight line at unchanged speed. Two worked examples apply the law: Example 6.2 shows that a moving box on which the applied force exactly equals friction continues with constant velocity (net force zero); Example 6.3 asks students to draw position-time and velocity-time graphs for an object with zero net force, yielding a horizontal position-time line (at rest) or a sloped straight line (constant velocity) and in each case a flat velocity-time graph.

(Pause and Ponder) Students determine whether a net force acts on an object moving at constant velocity; identify which of three listed situations (rest, constant velocity motion, constant acceleration) are possible when net force is zero; and explain with an example how additional applied forces can produce zero net force in a real-world situation where forces always exist.

6.5 Newton's Second Law of Motion

This section establishes the quantitative relationship between net force, mass, and acceleration. A net force produces acceleration; the section first asks students to reason from experience toward the hypothesis that larger force produces larger acceleration for the same object, and that the same force produces smaller acceleration for a larger mass.

(Think as a Scientist) Students frame the hypothesis that for a fixed object a larger force results in larger acceleration, and devise how to test it using weights of different magnitudes as a force source.

Activity 6.3 (Let us experiment — Demonstration activity) is a teacher-facilitated classroom demonstration in which a cart made from a cardboard box and wheels is pulled by a hanging cup via a thread-and-pulley system; the cup's descending weight applies a constant force and the cart's travel time is recorded by slow-motion video; doubling the cup's mass (force) and comparing the ratio of times yields the ratio of accelerations, confirming that acceleration increases with force for fixed mass.

(Think as a Scientist) Students frame the second hypothesis that for the same force a smaller mass produces larger acceleration, and consider how to test it by doubling the cart's mass.

Activity 6.4 (Let us experiment — Demonstration activity) is a teacher-facilitated classroom demonstration repeating Activity 6.3 with the cup mass held constant while the cart mass is doubled; comparing times confirms that doubling mass halves acceleration for fixed force.

Newton's second law is stated: when a net force acts on an object, the object accelerates in the direction of the net force; the magnitude of acceleration is proportional to the net force and inversely proportional to the mass. Mathematically: a = F/m, or F = ma. This gives the SI definition of one newton as the force that produces 1 m s⁻² acceleration on a 1 kg mass. The section also derives the gravitational force formula F = mg, where g = 9.8 m s⁻² (approximately 10 m s⁻² for quick estimates). Applications include: a fielder pulling hands backward while catching a ball to increase impact time, reduce acceleration, and thus reduce force on the hands; worked Examples 6.4 (weightlifter holding 30 kg barbell, applying 294 N upward), 6.5 (block on floor pushed with 50 N vs 55 N versus 50 N friction — rest in one case, 0.4 m displacement in 2 s in the other), and 6.6 (1500 kg sports car velocity-time graph yielding 3000 N, zero, and −3000 N forces in three time intervals).

(Pause and Ponder) Students determine the net force on a toy car moving at constant velocity; decide which of two children of different masses on identical swings requires a larger force for the same acceleration; and explain why bubble wrap or hay protects fragile glass items during transport.

6.6 Newton's Third Law of Motion

This section presents Newton's third law: whenever one object exerts a force on a second object, the second simultaneously exerts an equal and opposite force on the first. Forces always occur in pairs but act on two different objects, so they do not cancel each other. Activity 6.5 (Let us explore) is a student-executed activity in which a student on a wheeled chair pushes and then pulls a heavy table; the chair moves in the opposite direction each time, demonstrating that the table exerts an equal and opposite force back on the student. Activity 6.6 (Let us verify) is a student-executed activity in which two identical spring balances are connected hook-to-hook on a table and pulled; their readings are equal every time regardless of the magnitude of applied force, confirming equal magnitudes of Newton's third law force pairs. Activity 6.7 (Let us understand) is a student-executed activity in which an inflated balloon is attached to a straw threaded on a taut string; releasing the balloon's neck causes the balloon to accelerate in the direction opposite to escaping air, modelling rocket propulsion. The section explains that the Vikram lander of Chandrayaan-3 used reverse engine thrust to slow down for a soft landing near the Moon's south pole. Examples include: walking (foot pushes ground backward, friction pushes person forward), rowing a canoe (paddle pushes water backward, water pushes paddle forward), climbing a coconut tree (legs push trunk down, trunk friction pushes person up), and rockets (exhaust expelled downward, equal reaction force pushes rocket upward). The law applies equally to contact and non-contact forces — illustrated with magnets repelling and similarly charged balloons. Worked Examples 6.7 (Earth and fruit exert equal gravitational forces; Earth barely accelerates due to enormous mass) and 6.8 (0.1 kg bullet and 5 kg gun with 2 N force: bullet acceleration 20 m s⁻², gun acceleration 0.4 m s⁻²) are included.

(Pause and Ponder) Students explain why a firefighter struggles holding a water-issuing pipe, and suggest how a spacecraft in negligible gravity can change its velocity.

6.7 Forces Acting on a System of Objects

This section extends Newton's laws from a single object to two or more connected objects treated as a system. Two boxes of masses m₁ and m₂ on a frictionless surface are connected by a string and pulled by external force F. Instead of analysing each box separately (distinguishing internal tension T from external force F), the section introduces the system approach: internal forces within the system — here, the tension between the boxes — need not be considered; only the external force F matters. Applying Newton's second law to the system gives acceleration a = F/(m₁ + m₂), exactly as if the system were a single object of total mass. The section notes that the same result follows from individual analysis but the system approach is simpler, illustrating the broad power of Newton's laws for complex, multi-body situations.

The chapter also connects its physics to real-world and societal contexts. The airbag in a vehicle works on Newton's second law: in a collision the airbag inflates and increases the time over which the passenger decelerates, reducing the force experienced and lowering injury risk, particularly when combined with seat belts. Walking and running depend on Newton's third law: feet push the ground backward and friction (a third-law reaction) drives the person forward; grooves on footwear soles and treads on tyres increase friction for safer movement, while wet or icy surfaces reduce friction and make movement hazardous.
