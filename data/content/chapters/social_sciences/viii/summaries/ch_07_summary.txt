Chapter 7: Factors of Production

Introduction

This chapter introduces the concept of factors of production — the resources or inputs used in producing goods and services. It opens with the example of Ratna, who runs a small restaurant called Pause Point on the city outskirts, illustrating how any business must combine multiple inputs: choosing a location, organising money, hiring staff, buying ingredients, and planning. The chapter establishes that businesses combine various inputs to create goods and services while also generating opportunities for economic activity. A student activity asks learners to survey economic activities in their locality and classify the inputs required across different types of shops and services.

Factors of Production

The chapter classifies the inputs used in a production process into four types: land, labour, capital, and entrepreneurship. Technology is introduced as a facilitator and crucial enabling factor that allows businesses to produce more goods with the same or fewer inputs. Each factor is explored in detail in subsequent sections.

Land (natural resources)

In economics, the word 'land' encompasses not only geographical land but also all natural resources — soil, forests, water, air, sunlight, minerals, oil, and natural gas. The section connects to the earlier chapter on Natural Resources and Their Use. Businesses either purchase land outright or pay rent to use it for a period of time. A student activity invites learners to revisit their locality report and identify which items can be classified as 'land'.

Labour (human resources)

Labour refers to the physical and mental effort used in production. The section explains that carpenters, farmers, construction workers, teachers, and doctors all contribute differently through their work, using varying levels of physical strength, knowledge, and skill.

People as a resource

Human beings play a key role in economic activities by applying their knowledge, skills, and decision-making abilities to create goods and services. The section distinguishes between labour — basic physical and mental effort — and human capital, which refers to the specialised skills, knowledge, abilities, and expertise required to perform that labour effectively. Human capital thus reflects not merely the effort of labour but the quality and efficiency of that labour.

Facilitators of human capital

The chapter identifies three key facilitators that develop and strengthen human capital.

Education and training: Education helps individuals gain knowledge starting from basic literacy and extending to expertise in specific fields. The section uses the example of a civil engineering student learning design principles and applying them to build infrastructure. Training — such as observing construction sites and hands-on application — complements formal education to prepare individuals for careers.

Healthcare: Good health supports cognitive development, enabling children to attend school regularly and learn better. Healthy workers are more productive, more creative, and less likely to be absent from work. The section poses a reflective question about how healthcare infrastructure contributes to developing human capital.

Social and cultural influences: A culture of hard work, continuous improvement, and dedication helps countries advance. The section introduces the Japanese concept of kaizen, meaning 'continuous improvement', applied in Japan since the mid-1940s, which has contributed to higher standards of living. The German work ethic — characterised by punctuality, attention to detail, and a focus on quality — is cited as another example of how cultural values contribute to human capital and economic success. A student activity asks learners to survey workplace culture among adults in their community.

Challenges to human capital

The section notes that India has made progress in human capital development since independence. Literacy is highlighted as an important characteristic that enhances the skills and productivity of the population. As of 2023, the Adult Literacy Rate in India stands at 85 per cent for males and 70 per cent for females, as per World Bank estimates. The chapter notes that despite progress, India faces ongoing challenges in developing human capital. The section introduces the concept of demographic dividend — the economic benefit a country gains when it has a large, young, working population with fewer dependants. According to the Economic Survey of India 2024, 65 per cent of Indians are below the age of 35. To realise this demographic dividend, individuals must have access to quality education, health, training, and skilling. A Think About It activity asks students to consider how loss of schooling affects a child's future prospects.

India's ancient skill heritage

This section explores India's long tradition of skill-based knowledge. For ancient Indians, work was an offering requiring devotion and a blend of kalā (art) and vidyā (knowledge). Tools were worshipped, a tradition that continues as Vishwakarma Puja or Ayudha Puja. Knowledge was passed from generation to generation through texts such as the shilpa shastras, which contain detailed design guidelines for sculptures, paintings, buildings, wooden items, and jewellery. The section highlights stitched shipbuilding as a specific example — a technique over 2,000 years old in which wooden planks were stitched together using cords instead of nails, making ships flexible and suited to navigating the Indian Ocean. This technique supported maritime trade and cultural exchanges. A student activity explores why indigenous techniques have declined and invites learners to document traditional techniques from their own regions.

Capital

Capital comprises monetary resources and durable assets such as machinery, tools, equipment, vehicles, computers, shops, factories, and office buildings used for day-to-day business operations. The section defines capital as money plus human-made resources used to produce goods and services. Returning to Ratna's example, capital would include funds for leasing the space, purchasing furniture, and buying kitchen equipment. The section explains the sources of capital: personal savings, family, and friends are typically the first recourse for entrepreneurs; bank loans are used when personal funds are insufficient; and large companies raise money from the public through the stock market by selling shares and offering dividends. A student activity asks learners to identify a local factory and estimate its capital investment.

Entrepreneurship

An entrepreneur is someone who identifies a problem, develops an innovative solution, combines various factors of production, takes risks by investing money and time, makes key decisions about business operations, and contributes to the welfare of society. Entrepreneurship brings innovative products and services to market, creates job opportunities, and supports livelihoods. The section profiles J.R.D. Tata — born in 1904 — as an exemplary Indian entrepreneur who led the Tata Group, started India's first airline (Tata Airlines, 1932, later Air India), expanded into steel, cars, power, and chemicals, and was known for caring about workers' welfare. He received the Bharat Ratna in 1992. A startup is defined as an entrepreneurial venture with limited resources aiming at rapid growth through technology. A Think About It activity explores what motivates entrepreneurs beyond profit.

Technology: An Enabler of Production

Technology is defined as the application of scientific knowledge to production-related activities. The section notes that some early forms of technology — such as pulleys and wheelbarrows — have existed since ancient times and are still in use. Modern technological developments, such as UPI payments, GPS for logistics, and drones for crop management, make production and daily life easier.

Technology paving the way for accessing knowledge, skills, and job opportunities

The section highlights specific government platforms that use technology to democratise access to education and employment. SWAYAM (Study Webs of Active Learning for Young Aspiring Minds) offers free Massive Open Online Courses for learners from Grade 9 upwards in subjects such as robotics, aquaculture, and textile printing. The National Career Service portal helps people find job opportunities across sectors. Technology has eliminated geographical barriers to knowledge, skill development, and employment in India and abroad. A student activity invites learners to identify technological advancements that have impacted their communities.

How are the Factors Connected?

The five factors — land, labour, capital, entrepreneurship, and technology — are combined in varying proportions to produce goods and services. Labour-intensive production, such as agriculture, construction, and handicrafts, relies more on human effort, while capital-intensive production, such as semiconductor chips or satellites, requires more machinery and specialised equipment. These factors complement each other; if any is missing or misused, production becomes inefficient or halts. However, new technologies can alter these proportions — for example, mechanisation in agriculture reduces dependence on labour, while 3-D printing can help revive dying textile art forms. The section introduces the supply chain as the network of individuals, organisations, resources, activities, and technology involved in producing and selling goods. It notes that over-reliance on distant sources can disrupt the supply chain, as demonstrated during the COVID-19 pandemic. A case study of mobile phone assembly in India — described as the world's second-largest mobile phone manufacturer as of 2025 — illustrates how all factors work together at every stage of production.

Responsibilities towards Factors of Production

Since production uses natural resources that are limited and can be harmed, producers must use them responsibly — reducing waste, avoiding pollution, and protecting the environment so that future generations can meet their needs. The section gives examples of leather factories in Tamil Nadu that pollute rivers and soil, and improperly recycled smartphones releasing harmful substances. Producers are also responsible towards their workers in three key areas: fair compensation and working conditions; skill development and training; and upholding workplace rights and protections against discrimination. Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) is introduced as the mechanism through which businesses address social and environmental concerns. India was the first country in the world to enact a CSR law, in 2014, mandating companies to spend 2 per cent of their average three-year profits on CSR activities.
