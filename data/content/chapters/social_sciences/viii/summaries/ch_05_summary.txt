Chapter 5: Universal Franchise and India's Electoral System

Universal Adult Franchise

The chapter establishes that India's Constitution makers decided from the outset that universal adult franchise would be the cornerstone of Indian democracy. Every citizen aged 18 and above has the right to vote — one person, one vote of equal value — regardless of caste, creed, race, religion, gender, education, or income. The minimum voting age was lowered from 21 to 18 in 1988. This right, enshrined in Article 326, applies to Lok Sabha elections, state and union territory legislative assembly elections, and all local body elections. To vote, a citizen must be registered in their constituency; those convicted of certain serious crimes are disqualified.

Bridging Barriers, Enabling Participation: Ensuring Universal Franchise

India's scale — about 980 million eligible voters in 543 Lok Sabha constituencies during the 2024 general elections — and its geographic complexity make organising elections uniquely challenging. The ECI has introduced accessibility measures to ensure no voter is excluded: electoral officials travel to remote locations, the 2024 elections saw home voting for elderly citizens and persons with disabilities for the first time, and postal voting was available to a specific category of voters. Assistive technologies such as braille-enabled voter cards and app-based requests for wheelchair assistance were also deployed. The chapter notes India has 3.1 million elected representatives in over 250,000 local government bodies, all chosen through universal adult franchise. The Role of the Election Commission of India (ECI)

This section frames the ECI's responsibilities and the types of elections it manages. At the national level, citizens elect representatives to the Lok Sabha; at the state level, to legislative assemblies. Local body elections (gram panchayats and urban local bodies) are managed by State Election Commissions in consultation with the ECI.

Election Commission of India — A brief introduction

The ECI is an independent constitutional body established in 1950. It conducts elections to the Lok Sabha, Rajya Sabha, State Legislative Assemblies, and the offices of the President and Vice President. India's first general elections were held in 1951–52. The ECI performs four key functions: setting election dates, registering political parties, enforcing the Model Code of Conduct, and overseeing the entire electoral process. Its structure spans three levels: nationally through the Chief Election Commissioner and two Election Commissioners; at the state/UT level through the Chief Electoral Officer; and at the district level through the District Election Officer, who oversees the Returning Officer (conducting elections) and the Electoral Registration Officer (managing voter lists).

Managing the electoral process

The 2024 Lok Sabha elections illustrate the scale of India's electoral management: approximately 980 million voters, 543 parliamentary constituencies, and over one million polling stations. The voting procedure involves four sequential steps — verifying identity, inking the finger and signing, checking the finger a second time, and finally pressing a button on the Electronic Voting Machine (EVM). The Voter Verifiable Paper Audit Trail (VVPAT) provides a printed slip voters can verify. The NOTA (None Of The Above) option lets voters reject all candidates. Of the 543 Lok Sabha seats, 84 are reserved for Scheduled Castes and 47 for Scheduled Tribes. India's EVM and VVPAT technology has also been adopted in countries such as Namibia and Bhutan. Model Code of Conduct (MCC)

The Model Code of Conduct governs the behaviour of parties, candidates, and the ruling government during elections to ensure free and fair polling. The ruling party cannot announce new schemes once the MCC is in force; candidates must campaign peacefully; and vote-buying through gifts is punishable. The MCC originated in Kerala in 1960, was circulated by the ECI during the 1962 elections, and has been proactively enforced by the ECI since 1991. Former Chief Election Commissioner T.N. Seshan (1990) is credited with strengthening electoral integrity through stricter enforcement of campaigning rules, introduction of voter ID cards, and vigilance on candidate spending.

Understanding Elections in India — A Very Brief Overview

This section introduces a brief survey of India's several types of elections, spanning direct elections to the Lok Sabha and state assemblies, indirect elections to the Rajya Sabha, and electoral college processes for the President and Vice President.

Election to the Lok Sabha and State Legislative Assemblies

India uses the First-Past-the-Post system: the candidate with the most votes in a constituency wins, even without reaching 50 per cent. Lok Sabha members are called Members of Parliament (MPs); state assembly members are Members of Legislative Assembly (MLAs). After elections, the party or coalition with a majority of Lok Sabha seats forms the national government, with its leader becoming the Prime Minister. In states, the majority party's leader becomes the Chief Minister. The chapter draws a clear distinction between MPs (national focus) and MLAs (regional focus).

Election to the Rajya Sabha

Rajya Sabha elections are indirect. Of its 245 members, 233 are elected by state MLAs through a single transferable vote system that ranks candidates by preference, while the President nominates 12. Seat allocation is proportional to state population. The Rajya Sabha is the Permanent House — never dissolved — with members serving six-year terms and one-third retiring every two years to maintain continuity.

Election of the President of India

The President is elected by an Electoral College comprising elected MPs from both Parliament houses and MLAs from all states and the union territories of Delhi and Puducherry, using the single transferable vote system. Excluded are nominated MPs, nominated MLAs, members of Legislative Councils, and nominated UT members — ensuring only directly elected representatives participate, maintaining democratic legitimacy. Election of the Vice President of India

The Vice President is elected by an Electoral College consisting of both elected and nominated members of both Houses of Parliament, using the single transferable vote system. The Vice President chairs the Rajya Sabha and assumes presidential duties whenever the President is unable to function.

Challenges and The Road Ahead

The chapter closes by acknowledging challenges facing India's electoral system: the growing influence of money, a notable proportion of candidates with criminal records, and voter apathy especially in urban areas. The path forward, the chapter argues, lies in voter empowerment through education, media, and awareness campaigns — particularly for youth — so that citizens make thoughtful and responsible choices. An informed and vigilant voter is presented as the strongest safeguard of democracy.
