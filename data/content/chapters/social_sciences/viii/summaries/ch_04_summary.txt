Chapter 4: The Colonial Era in India

The Age of Colonialism

The chapter opens by defining colonialism as the practice in which one country takes control of another region, establishing settlements and imposing its political, economic, and cultural systems. Though colonialism is not a recent phenomenon — it can be traced to great empires of the first millennium BCE and to the spread of Christianity and Islam — the 'Age of Colonialism' conventionally refers to Europe's territorial expansion from the 15th century onward. European powers — Spain, Portugal, Britain, France, and the Netherlands — established colonies across Africa, Asia, the Americas, Australia, and the Pacific islands through military conquest that often involved massacre or enslavement of native populations. The motivations included political competition between European states, economic gain through access to resources and markets, religious conversion of indigenous peoples to Christianity, and scientific inquiry into geography and natural history. Colonisers justified conquest through a claimed 'civilising mission', portraying colonised peoples as 'savage' or 'primitive', while the reality was loss of independence, exploitation of resources, destruction of traditional ways of life, and imposition of foreign cultural values.

Europeans in India

Before European arrival, India had been a vibrant economic and cultural powerhouse for millennia, trading with Greeks and Romans in commodities such as spices, cotton, ivory, gems, sandalwood, teakwood, and wootz steel. Historical estimates by economist Angus Maddison suggest India contributed at least one-fourth of world GDP throughout the first millennium CE, making it one of the two largest economies alongside China. European travellers from the 16th century onward described India as 'flourishing', noting her manufacturing capabilities, diverse agricultural output, and extensive internal and external trading networks. This very prosperity made India an attractive target for European colonial ambitions.

The Portuguese: commerce and atrocities

The Portuguese explorer Vasco da Gama arrived at Kappad near Kozhikode in May 1498, marking the beginning of European colonisation in India. Despite a cordial initial reception, his aggressive conduct — which during his second voyage included seizing, torturing, and killing Indian merchants and bombarding Calicut — failed to build friendly relations. The Portuguese captured strategic ports, including Goa (1510), which became the capital of their Indian colony, as well as several trading posts along the Malabar and Coromandel coasts. They imposed a naval licensing system called cartaz, requiring all ships in the Arabian Sea to purchase Portuguese navigation permits; ships without permits were seized. This dominance allowed them to monopolise the spice trade between India and Europe for nearly a century. Alongside commercial exploitation, the Portuguese establishment of the Inquisition at Goa in 1560 brought severe religious persecution of Hindus, Muslims, Jews, and Christian converts suspected of practising their original faiths, accompanied by forced conversions and the destruction of Hindu temples. The Goa Inquisition was only abolished in 1812. The chapter also notes the resistance of Rani Abbakka I of Ullal, who formed alliances with neighbouring kingdoms to repel repeated Portuguese attacks; her successor Rani Abbakka II reportedly set Portuguese ships on fire, and both figures are remembered today through the Yakshagana dance-drama tradition.

The Dutch: commerce and competition

The Dutch arrived in India in the early 17th century focused primarily on commercial dominance, especially in the spice trade. They established a Dutch East India Company and set up trading posts at Surat, Bharuch, Cochin, Nagapattinam, and Masulipatnam, with their most significant presence in the Malabar region of Kerala, where they displaced the Portuguese. Their influence in India was ultimately curtailed after their decisive defeat at the Battle of Colachel in 1741, where the forces of King Marthanda Varma of Travancore defeated the Dutch both on land and at sea — a rare instance of an Asian power successfully repelling a European colonial force.

The French: colonial ambitions

The French entered India later, establishing a trading post at Surat in 1668 and subsequently at Pondicherry in 1674, where they set up their East India Company (Compagnie des Indes Orientales). Governor-General Dupleix (1742–1754) pioneered strategies later adopted by the British: training Indian soldiers in European military techniques to create disciplined sepoys, and ruling indirectly through puppet Indian rulers installed through interventions in succession disputes. The French colonial ambitions were ultimately checked during the Carnatic Wars (1746–1763) against the British; despite capturing Madras in 1746, the French lost ground and their colony was reduced to Pondicherry and a few enclaves. Unlike the Portuguese, the French generally did not interfere in Indians' social and religious life, though Dupleix ordered the destruction of Pondicherry's large Vedapurishwaran temple in 1748 under pressure from Jesuit priests.

Enter the British

The chapter introduces Britain's nearly two-century domination of the Indian subcontinent as requiring examination of its main mechanisms, moving from the earlier European precedents into a focused study of British colonial methods and impacts.

From traders to rulers

The British conquest of India exemplifies how a trading company transformed into an imperial power through gradual and calculated means. The English East India Company, granted a royal charter by Queen Elizabeth I with powers including raising a private army, initially maintained the pretence of being mere traders. In the 17th century this allowed them to establish coastal footholds — Surat, Madras, Bombay, and Calcutta — with minimal resistance, as local rulers welcomed foreign trade. These modest beginnings concealed the Company's long-term ambitions. The strategy of 'divide and rule'

The Company's agents cultivated political relationships with local rulers, offering military support to some against their rivals and inserting themselves into Indian political conflicts as power brokers. They exploited rivalries between regional rulers, succession disputes within ruling houses, and religious tensions within Indian society — a systematic 'divide and rule' policy. The Battle of Plassey (1757) exemplifies this: Robert Clive conspired with Mir Jafar, the Nawab of Bengal's military commander, promising him the nawabship in exchange for betrayal. Mir Jafar's forces stood aside during the battle, ensuring a British victory over the Nawab Siraj-ud-daulah despite the Company's smaller numbers; 'Mir Jafar' remains a synonym for 'traitor' in India. The East India Company then gradually consolidated control. In the 19th century it introduced the Doctrine of Lapse, by which any princely state whose ruler died without a natural male heir would be annexed — deliberately disregarding the Hindu tradition of adoption as legitimate succession. This led to the annexation of numerous princely states, fuelling widespread resentment. Another stratagem, the 'subsidiary alliance', installed a British Resident in Indian courts ostensibly to provide protection; in exchange, the Indian ruler had to maintain British troops at his own expense and conduct foreign affairs only through the British. The Nizam of Hyderabad entered such an alliance in 1798, followed by several others. This system transferred real power to the British while burdening Indian rulers with the costs of their own subjugation.

From Paradise to Hell? / Devastating famines

After their victory at Plassey, the East India Company secured the right to collect revenue in Bengal, Bihar, and Odisha. Robert Clive had described Bengal as 'the paradise of the earth', yet the Company extracted maximum revenue while investing minimally in governance or development. In 1770–1772, coming on top of two years of crop failure, the Company's harsh revenue collection — requiring farmers to pay cash taxes regardless of harvest conditions, and even increasing land tax during the famine — caused a catastrophic famine that killed an estimated 10 million people, nearly one-third of Bengal's population. Contemporary British official W.W. Hunter documented the horror in his writings. Throughout British rule, such famines recurred; during the Great Famine of 1876–1878, up to 8 million Indians perished, mostly in the Deccan plateau. During the famine, the British administration continued to export grain to Britain — about one million tonnes of rice per year — while Viceroy Lord Lytton ordered no government interference with food prices, simultaneously organising an extravagant durbar in Delhi. The total number of famine deaths during British rule has been estimated at between 50 and 100 million, comparable to World War II casualties. Historians note that while famines had occurred in India throughout history, they had never reached such scale; colonial policies deepened India's rural poverty irreversibly.

The drain of India's wealth

Economic exploitation formed the foundation of British colonial policy. U.S. historian Brooks Adams (1895) argued that the Bengal plunder flowing to London after Plassey directly financed Britain's Industrial Revolution — 'stolen wealth from India', in the words of historian Will Durant. Building on this analysis, Dadabhai Naoroji's 1901 work Poverty and Un-British Rule in India and Romesh Chunder Dutt's Economic History of India compiled British sources to document the wealth drained from India. A more recent estimate by economist Utsa Patnaik puts the total for 1765–1938 at 45 trillion US dollars in today's value — about 13 times Britain's GDP in 2023 — extracted not only through taxes but by charging Indians for the colonial power's own expenditures on railways, the telegraph network, and wars. Changing Landscapes / Decline of India's indigenous industries

Before the 18th century, India was renowned for its manufacturing, especially in textiles — cotton, silk, wool, jute, hemp, and coir — with Indian cotton textiles in high demand worldwide for their intricate designs, bright colours, and textures ranging from ultra-thin muslins to embossed fabrics. The section contains a student activity on textile terminology. British policy imposed heavy duties on Indian textiles imported into Britain while forcing India to accept British manufactured goods with minimal tariffs; Britain also controlled sea trade and exchange rates. The result was the ruin of India's textile industry: exports fell sharply while British imports grew. Communities of skilled artisans were reduced to poverty and forced back to subsistence agriculture on overtaxed land. Governor-General William Bentinck noted in 1834, "The bones of the cotton weavers are bleaching the plains of India." Similar destruction unfolded for iron, steel, paper and other manufactures. India's share of world GDP fell to barely 5 per cent at Independence — from one of the world's richest lands to one of its poorest in less than two centuries.

Dismantling traditional governance structures

Before British colonisation, India had well-organised systems of local self-governance: village councils that managed community affairs, resolved disputes, and organised public works such as irrigation. Acting Governor-General Charles Metcalfe in the 1830s described Indian village communities as 'little republics' that survived every political upheaval. The British systematically dismantled these indigenous structures, replacing them with a centralised bureaucracy designed primarily for tax collection and maintaining order rather than promoting public welfare, destroying centuries-old mechanisms of community decision-making. The introduction of British codes of law disregarded customary laws and practices, creating courts that were expensive, time-consuming, and conducted in a foreign language.

Transforming Indian education: creating 'brown Englishmen'

India had diverse pre-colonial educational traditions — pāthaśhālās, madrasās, vihāras, and apprenticeship learning — transmitting practical knowledge, cultural values, and traditions. British reports noted hundreds of thousands of village schools across India even in the early 19th century. The turning point was Thomas B. Macaulay's 1835 'Minute on Indian Education', in which he declared European knowledge vastly superior to India's and set as the educational objective the creation of a class "Indian in blood and colour, but English in taste, in opinions, in morals, and in intellect." Despite opposition from British Orientalists who argued for teaching in Indian languages, Macaulay's policy prevailed. India's traditional schools disappeared; English became a language of prestige associated with colonial masters, creating lasting divisions between English-educated elites and the masses. The new education system served colonial objectives by producing Indian clerks and minor officials for the lower ranks of colonial administration at a fraction of the cost of British personnel, while sidelining traditional knowledge and authority. The section contains a student activity prompting critical reflection on Macaulay's statements and their relationship to the 'civilising mission'.

Reshaping economic structures to serve imperial needs

The British transformed India's economy from a self-sufficient agricultural system supplemented by craft and manufacture into a supplier of raw materials for British industry and a captive market for British goods. The railway network, often cited as a colonial benefit, was designed primarily to move raw materials from the interior to ports for export and to distribute British manufactured goods throughout India; it largely ignored existing trade patterns and also served to move armies quickly to suppress rebellions. Crucially, India's railway construction was paid for mostly by Indian tax revenue, meaning Indians funded infrastructure that served British strategic and commercial interests. The same applied to the telegraph network. Even the administrative costs of colonialism — the colonial bureaucracy, military installations, and lavish lifestyles of British officials — were financed by Indian taxation. The chapter summarises this with the phrase "Indians funded their own subjugation."

Early Resistance Movements: Challenging Colonial Authority

Despite British claims that India would remain 'the jewel in the crown' of the British Empire forever, resistance movements arose almost from the beginning of the conquest. The chapter surveys several such movements against the background of the 1909 map of the British Indian Empire, distinguishing territories under direct British administration from the princely states.

The 'Sannyasi-Fakir rebellion'

One of the earliest organised resistance movements, the 'Sannyasi-Fakir Rebellion', began in Bengal after the famine of 1770. Groups of sannyasis (Hindu ascetics) and fakirs (Muslim ascetics), whose traditional free movement for pilgrimage and charity was restricted by new East India Company land and taxation policies, attacked British treasuries and tax collectors over roughly three decades. The British suppressed the rebellion, executing some participants and labelling them 'bandits'. The rebellion later inspired Bengali writer Bankim Chandra Chattopadhyay's novel Anandamath (1882), which contained the song 'Vande Mātaram', later adopted as India's national song.

Tribal uprisings

India's tribal communities faced particular threats as the British expanded into forests and hills: restriction of access to forests and forest produce, acquisition of tribal land, imposition of cash taxes, debt traps, replacement of traditional tribal councils with the British legal system, missionary activity encouraging conversion, and a colonial law categorising hundreds of tribal communities as 'criminal tribes'. The Kol Uprising (1831–1832) in Chota Nagpur (present-day Jharkhand) arose when the British introduced land policies favouring outsiders over original tribal inhabitants; the Kol tribes temporarily established control over significant territory before British forces defeated them. The Santhal Rebellion of 1855–1856, led by brothers Sidhu and Kanhu Murmu across parts of present-day Jharkhand, Bihar, and West Bengal, arose against moneylenders and landlords taking ancestral lands with British support; the Santhals declared their own government before the British crushed the uprising brutally, burning villages and killing thousands. The section contains a student activity analysing a contemporary British sketch depicting the Santhal rebels.

Peasant uprisings against economic exploitation

Peasants across India suffered under unfair British revenue collection, frequently losing their lands to moneylenders or new landlords. The Indigo Revolt (1859–1862) illustrates this: European planters forced peasants in northern Bengal to grow indigo instead of food crops; the peasants were so poorly paid they fell into debt slavery, and resistance led to imprisonment, torture, and destruction of property. Their uprising directed at the planters was supported by educated Bengalis and the Bengali press, eventually forcing the British authorities to restrict the worst abuses.

The Great Rebellion of 1857

What the British called the 'Sepoy Mutiny' — later rejected by post-Independence historians as a term — is referred to in the chapter as 'The Great Rebellion of 1857'. Earlier discontent among sepoys surfaced in the 'Vellore Mutiny' of 1806, triggered by uniform regulations violating Hindu and Muslim religious practices. In 1857, rumours that rifle cartridges were greased with cow and pig fat — offending both Hindus and Muslims — ignited widespread revolt. At Barrackpore, sepoy Mangal Pandey attacked British officers and was executed. In Meerut, sepoys killed their officers and marched to Delhi, where they proclaimed the elderly Mughal emperor Bahadur Shah Zafar as their leader. The revolt spread across northern and central India, with sepoys capturing Kanpur, Lucknow, and Jhansi. Notable leaders included Rani Lakshmibai of Jhansi — who escaped a besieged Jhansi, seized the Gwalior fort, and died on the battlefield on 18 June 1858 — and Begum Hazrat Mahal of Awadh, who led the defence of Lucknow and refused to surrender. The British response was systematic and brutal: house-to-house massacres at Delhi, mass executions at Kanpur, and a punitive campaign of village-burning and crop destruction causing vastly more deaths than those inflicted by the rebels. The rebellion failed partly due to lack of unified command among the sepoys. In 1858, the British Crown took direct control of India from the East India Company, beginning the period of the British Raj and shifting policy from territorial expansion to consolidation of control.

The Legacy of European Colonialism in India

The chapter concludes that the European, predominantly British, conquest of India was not a 'civilising mission' but a systematic process of subjugation and exploitation, with brutal repression of resistance. At the same time, colonial rule had some largely unintended consequences: it opened India to the wider world and facilitated the documentation of India's geography, ethnic groups, monuments, and archaeological heritage — though this process also involved the large-scale theft of statues, paintings, jewels, and manuscripts sent to European museums and private collections. British and other European scholars' publication of Sanskrit texts in European languages had an enormous cultural impact: the German philosopher Georg Hegel described the spread of Sanskrit studies as like 'the discovery of a new continent', and Indian texts influenced European and American philosophers, writers, and artists throughout the 19th century — a reminder that while political domination flowed in one direction, cultural influence could flow in the opposite direction.
