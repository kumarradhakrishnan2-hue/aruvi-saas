Chapter 3: The Rise of the Marathas

Who are the Marathas?

The Marathas are a group of people native to the Deccan plateau, specifically from the region of present-day Maharashtra, identified by their language, Marathi, which has a rich literary history dating to the 12th century. During the 13th century, Maharashtra was ruled by the Yadava dynasty with Devagiri (present-day Daulatabad) as capital, until the Khilji Sultanate from Delhi overcame them in the early 14th century. Amidst these political changes, cultural traditions rooted in bhakti — devotion to a deity — continued to flourish, with saints like Dnyaneshwar, Namdev, Tukaram, and Ramdas composing devotional poetry in Marathi and translating key texts such as the Upanishads and the Bhagavad Gita, making their philosophy widely accessible. This cultural foundation, combined with a growing political awareness similar to that fostered by the Sikh gurus, laid the groundwork for the Marathas to organise themselves into a political power.

Foundation of Maratha Power and the Rise of Chhatrapati Shivaji

Shivaji was born in 1630 in the Bhonsle clan to Shahji and Jijabai; growing up in the jagir at Pune under Jijabai's guidance while his father served the Deccan sultanates, he witnessed firsthand the hardship caused by constant infighting among the Deccan sultans. At just 16, he launched military campaigns, beginning by capturing neglected forts in the Pune region and fortifying them as the basis of his vision of Swarājya — a sovereign kingdom extending across political, economic, and cultural dimensions. His kingdom soon expanded to India's west coast, leading him to found a full-time Maratha Navy in 1657, a revolutionary step since neither the Bijapur Sultanate nor the Mughal Empire maintained a comparable permanent naval force. To counter more powerful enemies, Shivaji employed guerrilla warfare — using small, fast forces with terrain knowledge — and famously defeated the Bijapur general Afzal Khan at Pratapgad Fort through a daring stratagem. When the Mughal nobleman Shaista Khan invaded with a large army and occupied Shivaji's territories for three years, Shivaji conducted a night raid on the Khan's camp, forcing him to flee Maharashtra. In retaliation, Shivaji sacked the wealthy Mughal port of Surat twice, acquiring vast treasure; these events were reported even in the London Gazette, signalling his international renown. After being forced to sign the Treaty of Purandar following a Mughal campaign under Jai Singh, Shivaji visited Aurangzeb's court at Agra, was insulted, placed under house arrest, and executed a famous escape by hiding in fruit baskets. In 1674, he was crowned Chhatrapati at the Raigad fortress with full Vedic rites, formally inaugurating the Maratha Empire and starting his own era, the Rājyābhisheka shaka. He subsequently conducted the dakshina-digvijaya — a southern conquest extending into present-day northern Tamil Nadu and Karnataka — providing the Marathas strategic depth against Mughal pressure. Shivaji died of fever at fifty, but his exploits had already become legendary; Europeans compared him to Alexander, and he inspired contemporaries such as the Bundela prince Chhatrasal and the Hindi poet Bhushan.

The Marathas after Chhatrapati Shivaji

After Shivaji's death, his son Sambhaji became Chhatrapati and faced Aurangzeb's full-scale invasion of the Deccan, which defeated the Bijapur and Golconda sultanates; Sambhaji was captured, tortured, and executed, and Raigad fell to the Mughals. His brother Rajaram then took the title and fled to Gingee in present-day Tamil Nadu, spreading the Mughal-Maratha conflict into south India, where Maratha forces successfully held their forts and maintained the upper hand in most engagements. Aurangzeb died in the Deccan without subduing the Marathas, and Tarabai — Rajaram's queen — then organised large Maratha armies that made sweeping inroads into Mughal territories in the north, becoming the architect of the northward Maratha expansion. During this rapid expansion, the Marathas underwent a structural transformation from the centralised state of Shivaji's era to a more decentralised confederacy still nominally headed by the Chhatrapati, but with regional chiefs and the Peshwa wielding significant power; Peshwa Bajirao I and his son Nanasaheb Peshwa were instrumental in the pan-Indian expansion. The Marathas briefly controlled Lahore, Attock, and even Peshawar, but suffered a severe defeat at the Battle of Panipat in 1761 against the Afghans; they recovered under Peshwa Madhavrao I and, led by Mahadji Shinde, recaptured Delhi in 1771. Three Anglo-Maratha Wars fought between 1775 and 1818 ultimately ended Maratha power, as British organisational and technological superiority combined with growing internal Maratha disunity; in effect, the British took India more from the Marathas than from any other power.

Maratha Administration

Civilian administration

Chhatrapati Shivaji instituted a centralised administration that abolished hereditary posts and land assignments, replacing them with salaried government officials paid from the state treasury, with periodic transfers to prevent any official from accumulating excessive power. He provided pensions to widows of soldiers killed in battle and offered their sons military positions, demonstrating care for military families. He also maintained an ashta pradhana mandala — a council of eight ministers — comprising the Pradhan (prime minister), Amatya (finance), Sachiv (land revenue), Sumant (foreign affairs), Senapati (commander-in-chief), Mantri (intelligence), Nyayadhish (chief justice), and Panditrāo (religious affairs). The Marathas levied taxes called chauth (25%) and sardeshmukhi (an additional 10%) from provinces not directly under their rule, offering in return protection and non-interference in internal administration — an arrangement eventually formalised through Mughal treaties.

Military administration

The Maratha armed forces were divided into infantry, cavalry, and navy; the cavalry comprised bargirs (state-equipped soldiers) and shiledars (self-equipped soldiers). The Marathas also recognised the superiority of European-style disciplined troops and artillery in the 18th century, with Mahadji Shinde maintaining a large European-style army. Swords and lances were preferred weapons, but guns and rockets — including metal tube rockets by 1770 — were also employed. Forts were the core of Maratha military strategy, used to control key routes, shelter armies during guerrilla campaigns, and sustain prolonged resistance against invasion, as articulated by finance minister Ramachandrapant Amatya in the Ādnyāpatra.

Maritime supremacy

Chhatrapati Shivaji established a navy to protect the west coast, and in the 18th century Kanhoji Angre led the Marathas to many naval victories through superior geography knowledge and battle tactics, even against technologically superior European vessels. Europeans of the time controlled Indian maritime trade by compelling Indian ships to purchase naval trade passes (cartaz); the Marathas challenged this practice by reversing the demand and requiring passes from the Europeans — earning Kanhoji Angre the label of "pirate" from frustrated European traders. The Maratha navy's strength was demonstrated when even the ruler of Muscat, Oman retreated rather than engage Shivaji's fleet of over a hundred ships.

Judicial system

The Marathas maintained an efficient judicial system notable for its restrained use of capital punishment. The panchayat — a local gathering of officials and prominent men — served as the primary body for dispensing justice, with appeals possible to a Maratha chief. In major towns such as Pune and Indore, a kotwāl (police) maintained law and order within the city.

Trade networks

Chhatrapati Shivaji actively encouraged maritime foreign trade, with Maratha ships regularly visiting ports as far as Mocha in Yemen, Muscat in Oman, and Malacca in Malaysia, carrying gold, textiles, and other cargo. Roads were constructed and maintained; in 18th-century Odisha, a network of ferries supported riverine transport, and bridges were built over rivers and streams.

Cultural Revival

Chhatrapati Shivaji's vision of Swarājya extended to culture: his royal seal bore a Sanskrit inscription rather than the prevalent Persian, signalling a deliberate assertion of Indian cultural identity. He commissioned the Rājya-Vyavahāra-Kosha, a treatise providing Sanskrit equivalents for Persian diplomatic terms, significantly reducing the proportion of foreign loanwords in Maratha administration. A devout Hindu who respected other faiths, he promoted Sanskrit and Marathi literature, rebuilt desecrated temples, supported religious institutions, and adopted a saffron flag as the Maratha emblem. His successors continued this cultural patronage; the Bhonsles of Nagpur revived the worship of Jagannath at Puri, which had been disrupted during Mughal rule.

The mighty Maratha women

Tarabai, widow of Rajaram, was the principal architect of the northward Maratha expansion after Aurangzeb's invasion of the Deccan; recognising that north India was undefended while Aurangzeb concentrated in the Deccan, she organised large armies to invade Mughal territories and preserved Maratha independence through exceptional military strategy and tenacity. Ahilyabai Holkar, of the Holkar dynasty that ruled central India around present-day Indore, governed her state wisely for thirty years after losing her husband and son, caring for ordinary people while building and restoring hundreds of temples, ghats, wells, and roads across India from Kedarnath to Rameswaram; she also rebuilt the Kashi Vishwanath temple at Varanasi and the Somnath temple in Gujarat, and promoted the Maheshwar handloom weaving tradition in Madhya Pradesh. In focus: Thanjavur

Ekoji, the half-brother of Chhatrapati Shivaji, conquered the Thanjavur region of present-day Tamil Nadu in the late 17th century, initiating Maratha rule there. The Thanjavur Marathas fostered a rich syncretic culture that blended local Tamil traditions, the Telugu culture of former rulers, and Marathi culture, becoming great patrons of the arts with several rulers themselves being poets and dramatists. Serfoji II was the most notable of the Thanjavur Maratha rulers: multilingual in Indian and European languages, he patronised musicians during whose time modern Carnatic music took shape and Bharatanatyam developed its early classical form; he established the Dhanwantari Mahal — a medical centre offering free treatment using both Indian and Western medicine — started the first printing press by a native Indian ruler, and had the history of the Bhonsle family inscribed on the walls of the Brihadishwara temple at Thanjavur, one of the largest inscriptions in India.

The Maratha legacy

The Maratha Empire challenged Mughal dominance and became the largest Indian empire before the British takeover, controlling much of central and northern India. The Marathas established an efficient administration, revived local Hindu traditions without religious discrimination, and inspired cultural confidence across Indian regions. Their pursuit of Swarājya — sovereignty driven by resistance to oppressive rule — later inspired many Indians with the belief that they could govern themselves, planting early seeds for India's freedom movement.
