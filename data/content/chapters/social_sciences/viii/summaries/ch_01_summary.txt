Chapter 1: Natural Resources and Their Use

When does Nature become a Resource?

The chapter distinguishes between Nature and natural resources. Nature comprises life and non-life forms in our environment not created by humans; elements of Nature become 'resources' when humans use them for sustenance or to create goods — a tree becomes a resource when its wood is made into furniture. For any entity to qualify as a resource, three conditions must be met: it must be technologically accessible, economically feasible to exploit, and culturally acceptable. Examples such as inaccessible deep-sea petroleum and trees in sacred groves illustrate that these conditions are not always satisfied. The word 'exploitation' is used in the technical sense of extraction, utilisation and consumption of natural resources.

Categories of Natural Resources

The chapter introduces categorisation as a tool for clearer communication, grouping natural resources by use and by renewability.

Resources essential for life

This category covers resources life cannot exist without — air, water, and food — drawn from the atmosphere, rivers, ponds, and cultivated soil. These cannot be manufactured by humans.

Resources for materials

This category encompasses resources from which humans create physical objects for utility or beauty — wood into furniture, marble into sculpture, coal and gold from India's geographically diverse landscape.

Resources for energy

This category covers natural sources powering modern life: coal, water, petroleum, natural gas, sunlight, and wind that generate electricity for buildings, transportation, and production.

Renewable and non-renewable resources

A second axis of categorisation is based on renewability. The chapter explains Nature's principle of restoration and regeneration: restoration returns a degraded system to health, while regeneration creates new life. A fallen tree decomposing and enriching soil from which new trees grow exemplifies this cycle.

Renewable resources

Renewable resources — sunlight, river water, forests, soil — naturally replenish themselves. Solar, wind, hydroelectric energy, and timber are renewable as long as managed sustainably. However, a critical condition applies: the natural rhythm of restoration must not be disturbed. If timber is harvested faster than forests regenerate, depletion results. The chapter cites evidence that fossil fuel-driven industrialisation and deforestation have disrupted Nature's cycles, causing Himalayan glaciers to melt faster than precipitation can replace them, threatening water security in the plains. Industrial waste discharged into rivers further breaks Nature's regenerative cycle. A student activity asks students to identify such actions in their surroundings.

Non-renewable resources

Non-renewable resources are formed over long geological periods and cannot be replenished at the rate of use. Fossil fuels — coal and petroleum — and minerals and metals such as iron, copper and gold are examples. India's coal reserves are highlighted: estimated to last approximately 50 more years, they are being mined to meet growing energy demand. The chapter calls for judicious use of these resources until more sustainable alternatives become widely available.

Distribution of Natural Resources and its Implications

Natural resources are distributed unevenly across the planet and within countries. This uneven distribution shapes human settlements, trade patterns, international relations, and conflicts — many wars have been fought to control natural resources. Industries that locate near resource deposits create local employment, expand townships, and improve quality of life, but these benefits often come with costs: displacement of people, threats to sacred places, and environmental damage. A map of India showing the distribution of coal, oil, iron ore, and bauxite illustrates the concentration of resources in specific regions. The chapter also notes that national and international trade depend on the location of resources, and that combined with human knowledge and skills, resources generate distinctive products such as Wootz steel. Water resources crossing political boundaries generate inter-state and international tensions, illustrated by the example of the Kaveri River shared among Karnataka, Tamil Nadu, Kerala and Puducherry.

The 'Natural Resource Curse'

The chapter introduces the 'natural resource curse' or 'paradox of plenty' — regions rich in natural resources sometimes experience slower economic growth because they fail to develop industries that convert raw resources into higher-value products. India has generally avoided this curse through investment in industrial development, but balancing extraction with sustainability remains a challenge. Human knowledge, good governance, and strategic planning determine whether resources become lasting benefits or temporary windfalls.

Responsible and Wise Use of Natural Resources: Stewardship

The chapter frames sustainable resource use under the concept of stewardship — acting as responsible custodians of Nature. It warns that irresponsible treatment of natural resources has already caused pollution, biodiversity loss, and climate change.

Restoration and regeneration of renewable resources

Two examples illustrate over-exploitation of renewables. For groundwater, farmers across India extract water for irrigation faster than natural replenishment, causing falling water tables, higher extraction costs, and eventual unavailability. Water harvesting, pond rejuvenation, and reduced consumption are cited as remedies. For soil, improper use of chemical fertilisers and pesticides has caused degradation. The chapter contrasts this with traditional practices — cow dung, natural fertilisers, mulching, multi-cropping — that sustained soil health, and calls for learning from them.

Overexploitation of groundwater: a caselet from Punjab

The Punjab case is examined in depth. Punjab's Green Revolution in the 1960s shifted farmers to high-yielding varieties of wheat and paddy that demanded more water than traditional seeds. Free electricity supply enabled over-pumping of groundwater. Chemical pesticides and fertilisers compounded the problem. Today, almost 80 per cent of Punjab's area is classified as 'overexploited', with groundwater inaccessible above depths of about 30 metres in many districts, and chemical contamination posing health hazards. The chapter concludes that short-term food security was achieved at the cost of long-term sustainability.

The case of cement

Cement production is identified as one of the most polluting industries: it releases fine dust harmful to human and animal lungs, reduces plant yields by settling on leaves, and causes soil and water pollution. The Central Pollution Control Board has issued guidelines to minimise this. The chapter highlights moves toward alternative materials — traditional stone and mud, plant-based materials, and recycled plastics. It also discusses Vrikshaayurveda, an ancient Indian botanical science formalised around the 10th century CE, which offers sustainable practices for seed management, soil-appropriate planting, irrigation, pest control through natural repellents, crop rotation, and ploughing methods that retain soil moisture. The caselet of Pema's farm in Sikkim illustrates how Sikkim transitioned to 100 per cent organic farming by 2016, with biodiversity flourishing, farmer incomes rising by 20 per cent on average, and the state becoming a global model for sustainable agriculture.

Responsible and judicious use of resources

For non-renewable resources, the imperative is to extend their availability long enough for sustainable alternatives to emerge — primarily by switching to renewable energy sources wherever possible.

The International Solar Alliance — India's leadership in renewable energy

India and France co-launched the International Solar Alliance (IASE) in 2015, a coalition of sunshine-rich countries committed to solar power. India has channelled billions into solar projects across developing nations; the Bhadla Solar Park exemplifies India's renewable ambitions. The chapter closes by noting that access to basic resources like water and clean air is inequitably distributed, with the poor and pollution-exposed bearing disproportionate burdens. It invokes the Bhagavad Gita's concept of lokasangraha — acting for the wellbeing of all — as a guiding philosophy for stewardship of natural resources.
