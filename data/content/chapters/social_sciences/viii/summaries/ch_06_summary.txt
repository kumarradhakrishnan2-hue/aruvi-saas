Chapter 6: The Parliamentary System: Legislature and Executive

Introduction

India's independence marked the beginning of self-governance, and the Constitution laid foundational principles including Universal Adult Franchise — guaranteeing all adult citizens the right to vote irrespective of social or economic background, gender, race, or religion. The people of India directly elect their representatives to the Lok Sabha; a majority group from among them forms the government. The Parliament is the supreme legislative body that controls and guides the work of the government. There have been 17 Lok Sabhas since 1952, with the 18th constituted in June 2024.

Composition of the Parliament of India

The Indian Parliament is composed of the President and two houses: the Lok Sabha (House of the People / Lower House) and the Rajya Sabha (Council of States / Upper House) — a 'bicameral' system. Lok Sabha members are chosen through direct election based on universal adult suffrage, with a constitutional maximum of 550 members; Rajya Sabha members are elected indirectly by an electoral college. Representation in both houses is based on state population. Extensive debate during Constitution-making concluded that a single elected house would be inadequate; in the spirit of federalism a Council of States was needed. India practices federalism, distributing power between the centre, states, and local governments to balance national unity and local interests.

Function

Each House of Parliament has a presiding officer. The Lok Sabha Speaker conducts sessions, maintains discipline, and ensures rules are followed; the Rajya Sabha is presided over by the Vice President. The Union Executive — comprising the President, Vice President, and the Council of Ministers headed by the Prime Minister — gives life to Parliament's legislative functions. The Council of Ministers is chosen from MPs of both houses and is collectively responsible to the Lok Sabha. Translation services are provided in 18 Indian languages to make Parliamentary discussions accessible to all.

Legislative Functions of the Parliament

The Constitution classifies Parliament's key functions under four heads: Constitutional functions, Lawmaking, Executive accountability, and Financial accountability.

1) Constitutional function

Parliament is directly entrusted with upholding the core values of the Indian Constitution, including enabling parliamentary democracy through universal adult suffrage, following the separation of powers between the legislature, executive, and judiciary, ensuring federalism, and upholding Fundamental Rights and Directive Principles of State Policy. The election of the President and Vice President, and amendments to the Constitution, are among Parliament's vital constitutional functions.

2) Lawmaking

Among the primary responsibilities of the legislature is making laws. A bill — a draft of a proposed law — goes through a rigorous process: introduction in either House, reading, referral to a Standing Committee, clause-by-clause discussion and amendments, a vote, repetition in the other House, Presidential assent, and Gazette notification. The chapter illustrates this through the Right of Children to Free and Compulsory Education Act, 2009 (RTE), rooted in the Directive Principles of State Policy. After the 86th Constitutional Amendment Act, 2002 mandated free and compulsory education for children aged 6–14 (Article 21A), RTE was tabled in the Rajya Sabha, studied by a committee, and passed in the Lok Sabha in August 2009. Money Bills — concerned with taxation and government borrowing — can be introduced only in the Lok Sabha and require prior Presidential recommendation.

3) Executive accountability

The Prime Minister and the Council of Ministers are answerable to the Lok Sabha through mechanisms like the Question Hour — typically the first hour of a Parliamentary session — where MPs can ask ministers questions about government policies and activities. Special committees, which may include MPs from different political parties, hold ministries accountable by requiring them to explain and justify their policies and actions.

4) Financial accountability

Parliament approves and monitors government expenditure through the annual budget, examining the distribution of funds to different ministries. The government is expected to provide Parliament with necessary information in a timely and accurate manner.

Executive Functions of Parliament

The President is the Head of State and the nominal head of the Executive; key functions include appointing the Prime Minister and other ministers, summoning Parliament, and giving assent to bills. The President can exercise discretionary powers during political crises, such as when no party holds a clear majority in the Lok Sabha. The Prime Minister is the de facto executive authority, appointed by the President as the leader of the majority party or coalition in the Lok Sabha. The Prime Minister leads the Council of Ministers, advises the President, coordinates different ministries, and shapes national policies. The Prime Minister and Council of Ministers are collectively responsible to the Lok Sabha. Civil servants — permanent officials also called administrators or bureaucrats — implement laws and policies under the direction of ministers.

Differences Between the Legislature and the Executive

The Legislature (Parliament — President, Lok Sabha, and Rajya Sabha) makes laws and oversees the work of the Executive. The Executive (President, Vice President, and Council of Ministers headed by the Prime Minister) enforces the laws made by the Legislature. The Legislature keeps a check on the Executive through questions and committee scrutiny; the Executive prepares and implements the budget, provides information to Parliament, and can function independently in day-to-day matters.

The Judiciary — Role of Checks and Balances

The Judiciary is the branch of government responsible for interpreting and applying the laws of the land and resolving disputes. Functioning through courts, the Judiciary acts as custodian of the Constitution, ensuring all branches of government operate within constitutional principles and safeguarding fundamental rights. The Judiciary checks that laws passed by Parliament have not violated the Constitutional framework, and can intervene if the Executive violates the Constitution in implementing laws. Together, the Legislature, Executive, and Judiciary form a system of separation of powers and checks and balances, ensuring no single organ becomes too powerful.

Legislative and Executive Functions at the State Level

Each state has its own legislature and executive. Members of Legislative Assembly (MLAs) formulate laws on subjects in the State List and Concurrent List. The Constitution specifies three legislative lists: the Union List (exclusively legislated by the Union), the State List (exclusively by states), and the Concurrent List (both can legislate, but Union law prevails in conflict). The RTE Act applies nationwide despite education being in the Concurrent List.

Structure of State Legislatures

State legislatures can be unicameral (one house — Vidhan Sabha) or bicameral (Vidhan Sabha and Vidhan Parishad). States with a bicameral system are Andhra Pradesh, Bihar, Karnataka, Maharashtra, Telangana, and Uttar Pradesh; all other states are unicameral.

Parallel Structure of Union and State Governments in India

The state-level structure mirrors the union-level structure. The constitutional head at the union level is the President; at the state level it is the Governor (appointed by the President). The de facto executive authority at the union is the Prime Minister, and at the state is the Chief Minister — both leaders of the majority party or coalition in their respective lower houses. Both councils of ministers are collectively responsible to their respective lower houses. The union legislature is bicameral (Lok Sabha and Rajya Sabha); state legislatures can be unicameral or bicameral. Money bills originate only in the Lok Sabha at the union level and only in the Vidhan Sabha at the state level.

Challenges to the Effective Functioning of the Legislatures

The Indian legislature has the critical role of enacting laws, overseeing administration, approving budgets, discussing national policies, and addressing public grievances. However, it faces challenges including regular absenteeism, non-cooperative conduct, reduced quality of debates, and disruptions of the Question Hour. Parliament meets three times a year in Budget, Monsoon, and Winter Sessions, with sittings of about 6 hours a day. Data shows a declining trend in sittings — from 677 in the 1st Lok Sabha to 356 in the 13th — and concerns about parliamentary productivity. The chapter emphasises that informed, engaged citizens, active democratic participation, and the growing presence of young and diverse voices in public life are key to strengthening the legislature and ensuring it truly serves all people.
