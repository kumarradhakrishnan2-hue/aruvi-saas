Chapter 2: Reshaping India's Political Map

A new era in India's journey began in the early 11th century as invasions from beyond the Hindu Kush mountains reshaped India's political map. Largely Turkic and Afghan in origin, these invaders were drawn by India's reputed riches, territorial ambitions, and religious motivations. The chapter traces India's political landscape from the 13th century onward, covering major dynasties, resistance movements, administrative systems, and the lives of common people during this turbulent medieval period (defined in the chapter as the 11th to 17th centuries).

Rise and Fall of the Delhi Sultanate

The Delhi Sultanate was formed after the defeat of King Prithviraj Chauhan in 1192 and saw five successive Turkic-Afghan dynasties: the Mamluks, Khiljis, Tughlaqs, Sayyids, and Lodis. While the Sultanate controlled parts of northern India, neighbouring kingdoms such as the Eastern Gangas in the east and the Hoysalas in the south resisted its advance and became thriving cultural centres. The Sultanate period was marked by political instability, violent successions (almost two sultans in three seized power by eliminating their predecessors), and military campaigns that plundered cities and destroyed temples. Ala-ud-din Khilji campaigned across north and central India, repelled Mongol invasions, and extended the Sultanate southward through his general Malik Kafur. Muhammad bin Tughlaq expanded the empire further but made poorly executed decisions including shifting the capital from Delhi to Daulatabad and introducing a token copper currency that disrupted trade and caused economic decline. Some sultans imposed the jizya, a discriminatory tax on non-Muslims. At the end of the 14th century, Timur's devastating raid on Delhi caused mass destruction. The subsequent Lodi dynasty saw the Sultanate's territory shrink considerably in the face of growing internal resistance.

Resistance to the Delhi Sultanate

Throughout its existence, the Delhi Sultanate faced strong resistance from multiple kingdoms. The Eastern Ganga kingdom of Kalinga (present-day Odisha and parts of Bengal and Andhra Pradesh) successfully repelled the Sultanate; its ruler Narasimhadeva I in the mid-13th century defeated the Sultanate's Bengal governor and built the Surya temple at Konark to commemorate his victories. During the Tughlaq period, the Musunuri Nayakas — Telugu chieftains — formed a confederacy of over 75 chieftains that expelled Muhammad bin Tughlaq's forces from Warangal around 1330–1336. In the south, the Hoysalas fended off multiple Sultanate attacks but eventually weakened and were absorbed into the rising Vijayanagara Empire. The Bahmani Sultanate emerged in the mid-14th century and held much of the Deccan. Rajput kingdoms, particularly Mewar, also remained beyond the Sultanate's reach; in the 15th century, Rana Kumbha of Mewar successfully repelled invasions from the Delhi Sultanate and subsequent regional sultanates.

The Vijayanagara Empire

In the 14th century, two brothers — Harihara and Bukka — broke away from Muhammad bin Tughlaq's authority and established an independent kingdom that grew into the Vijayanagara Empire in the south. The Bahmani Sultanate to the north was a major rival, which eventually fragmented into five Deccan Sultanates (Bijapur, Golconda, Berar, Ahmednagar, and Bidar). The Vijayanagara rulers also contended with the Gajapati rulers of Odisha. The empire became a significant political and cultural force in southern India.

Krishnadevaraya

The Vijayanagara Empire reached its peak under Krishnadevaraya in the 16th century, who expanded its dominance over the Deccan and achieved both military power and a cultural renaissance. He patronised poets and scholars in Sanskrit, Telugu, and Kannada, composed the Telugu epic Amuktamalyada, and provided grants to major temples including Tirupati and the Vitthala temple at his capital. Foreign travellers, including the Portuguese Domingo Paes, recorded the city as remarkably prosperous and well-populated. After Krishnadevaraya's death in 1529, the empire declined; in 1565 a coalition of Deccan Sultanates defeated the Vijayanagara forces at the Battle of Talikota, sacking and destroying the capital city. The empire fragmented into regions under Nayakas (former military governors) and ended in the mid-17th century.

The Mughals

Babur, a Turkic-Mongol ruler and descendant of Timur, was driven from Samarkand and turned to India. In 1526, he defeated Ibrahim Lodi at the First Battle of Panipat using gunpowder and field artillery, ending the Delhi Sultanate and founding the Mughal Empire. He left a notable autobiography, the Baburnama, revealing his complex character as both a cultured intellectual and a ruthless conqueror. After Babur's death in 1530, his son Humayun struggled to consolidate the empire; the Afghan leader Sher Shah Suri temporarily seized large parts of north India before Humayun reconquered the territory. A brief interlude saw Himu (Hemchandra Vikramaditya) capture Delhi, but he was defeated by Akbar at the Second Battle of Panipat and executed.

Akbar

Declared emperor at 13 after Humayun's accidental death, Akbar pursued a blend of military conquest and political strategy to expand and stabilise the Mughal Empire. His early campaigns were brutal — notably the siege of Chittorgarh where he ordered the massacre of some 30,000 civilians. Over time, he shifted towards greater political tolerance: he abolished the jizya, entered into marriage alliances with Rajput kingdoms, appointed Hindu officials, promoted the doctrine of sulh-i-kul ('peace with all'), and engaged in interfaith dialogues. Despite being illiterate, he established a 'house of translation' at Fatehpur Sikri where major Sanskrit texts including the Mahabharata, Ramayana, Bhagavad Gita, and Panchatantra were translated into Persian. His long reign of nearly 50 years (1556–1605) saw the empire grow significantly. His son Jahangir continued the love of art and attempted Deccan expansion; his grandson Shah Jahan is remembered for building the Taj Mahal, and for a cultural peak that included Humayun's Tomb, the Red Forts at Delhi and Agra, and flourishing calligraphy and miniature painting.

Aurangzeb

Aurangzeb seized power by defeating and executing his eldest brother Dara Shikoh, imprisoning his ailing father Shah Jahan in Agra Fort, and removing his other brothers. He crowned himself emperor in 1658 and ruled for nearly 49 years. His military campaigns brought the Mughal empire to its greatest territorial extent but constant rebellions and 25 years of costly Deccan warfare depleted the treasury and strained the administration, contributing significantly to the empire's rapid decline after his death in 1707. A devout Sunni Muslim, Aurangzeb banned music and dance at court, reimposed the jizya and a pilgrimage tax on Hindus, and in 1669 ordered the demolition of Hindu and Jain temples and Sikh gurudwaras across the empire, including those at Varanasi, Mathura, and Somnath.

Resistance to the Mughals

The Mughal Empire faced persistent resistance from diverse groups. Jat peasant communities in the 17th century rose up against oppressive Mughal administration. Many tribal groups — the Bhils, Gonds, Santhals, and Kochs — fought attempts to annex their territories or impose taxes, with those in forested and hilly regions maintaining varying degrees of independence. Rani Durgavati, queen of the Garha kingdom (a Gond kingdom in central India), ruled wisely, maintained a substantial army, and fell fighting Akbar's forces in 1564 rather than surrender, becoming a symbol of regional resistance.

Surge of the Rajputs

Rooted in proud martial traditions and located in northwest India, the Rajputs frequently battled invading forces. Two major Rajput clans emerged from the Mewar and Marwar regions. Rana Sanga unified several Rajput clans in the early 16th century but was defeated by Babur at the Battle of Khanwa. Maharana Pratap of Mewar refused Mughal suzerainty and fought at the Battle of Haldighati (1576), escaping to wage years of guerrilla warfare from the Aravalli hills with support from the Bhil community. During Aurangzeb's reign, Rajput nobles including Durga Das Rathore of Marwar rebelled, keeping Mughal authority limited in Rajasthan. Some Rajput states did eventually ally with the Mughals through diplomacy and marriage alliances.

The Ahoms

In the 13th century, the Ahom ethnic group migrated from present-day Myanmar to the Brahmaputra Valley, forming the Ahom kingdom. They resisted both Sultanate and Mughal expansion using their unique paik system — requiring every able-bodied man to provide labour or military service in exchange for land rights — enabling the state to maintain infrastructure and a large force without a permanent standing army. The Ahoms assimilated local culture, promoted agriculture, and contributed to Assamese traditions. In the 17th century, when Aurangzeb's forces briefly captured the Ahom capital Garhgaon, the Ahoms used knowledge of their terrain — forests, hills, and rivers — and guerrilla tactics to repulse the invaders. The Battle of Saraighat (1671) on the Brahmaputra, led by commander Lachit Borphukan, saw 10,000 Ahom soldiers defeat a Mughal force of 30,000, preserving Ahom independence. The Rise of the Sikhs

Guru Nanak founded Sikhism in 15th-century Punjab, spreading a message of equality, compassion, and belief in the oneness of God. As Mughal intolerance grew, the Sikh community was drawn into conflict. Emperor Jahangir tortured Guru Arjan to death, prompting his successor Guru Hargobind to introduce martial training and form a Sikh army. In 1675, Guru Tegh Bahadur stood against religious persecution on behalf of Kashmiri Pandits and was publicly beheaded in Delhi on Aurangzeb's orders. His son Guru Gobind Singh responded by founding the Khalsa in 1699 — a martial brotherhood committed to justice, equality, and defence of faith — which frequently clashed with Mughal forces. As the Mughal Empire declined, Sikh confederacies in Punjab were unified by Maharaja Ranjit Singh at the turn of the 19th century, establishing a strong Sikh Empire that resisted both Mughal remnants and British expansion until the mid-19th century.

Administering India

Administration under the Delhi Sultanate centred on the sultan as absolute political and military authority, assisted by a council of ministers. The iqta system assigned territories to nobles (iqtadars) to collect taxes for the sultan's treasury — particularly to fund the army — creating a network of local administrators whose posts were not hereditary. Tax burdens fell most heavily on the peasantry. The Mughal administrative framework, reorganised by Akbar, included specialised ministers: the Diwan for finances, Mir Bakhshi for military matters, Khan-i-Saman for public works and agriculture, and Sadr for justice and education. These were replicated across twelve provinces (subahs) with subdivisions. Akbar also introduced the mansabdari system, in which officers (mansabdars) maintained specified numbers of troops and animals according to their rank, enabling rapid army assembly without a permanent centralised force; they were paid through land grants (jagirs) and known as jagirdars. Akbar's finance minister Todar Mal introduced systematic land surveys to improve revenue collection. The chapter notes that non-Muslim officials remained a minority in senior administrative posts throughout Akbar's rule.

People's Lives

Despite shifting political powers between the 13th and 17th centuries, India maintained vibrant economic activity through agrarian foundations, artisanal industries, community-based and temple-based economies, and extensive trade networks. Institutions such as shrenis (guilds), jatis (professional communities), and the hundi system (a financial instrument enabling cross-border fund transfers without physical currency) sustained economic life. Agriculture was the economic mainstay, with rulers typically extracting one-fifth of produce as land revenue, though some sultans raised it to half. Expansion of irrigation systems increased productivity. Indian goods — textiles, weapons, utensils, ship-built goods — were exported from ports like Calicut, Surat, Mangalore, and Hooghly, with India importing mostly luxury goods, silk, and horses. Temples functioned not only as religious and cultural centres but also as economic hubs — holding donated land and wealth, developing community infrastructure, providing merchants with credit, and funding maritime trade. By the late 17th century, economic stress increased for common people: peasants were often left with little after taxes, craftspeople faced harsh conditions, and repeated warfare caused population displacement. Despite this, and despite periodic clashes over desecrated sacred sites, people of different faiths largely coexisted and were economically interdependent. The chapter concludes noting that India's resilience across this period was expressed both through military resistance and through continuing creativity in art, literature, architecture, music, and spirituality.
