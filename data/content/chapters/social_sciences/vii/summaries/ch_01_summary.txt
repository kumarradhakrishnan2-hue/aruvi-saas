Geographical Diversity of India

## Introduction: India as a Subcontinent

India is the seventh-largest country in the world and forms part of Asia. Along with its neighbours — Pakistan, Bangladesh, Nepal, Bhutan, Sri Lanka, and Myanmar — it constitutes the Indian Subcontinent. The Himalayan Mountain range stands as a natural barrier in the north, the Thar Desert and Arabian Sea mark its western limits, the Indian Ocean lies to the south, and the Bay of Bengal to the east. These geographical boundaries have played a crucial role in shaping India's climate, culture, and history. For the purpose of study, India's geography is broadly divided into five regions: the great mountain zone, the plains of the Ganga and Indus, the desert region, the southern peninsula, and the islands.

## The Himalayas

The Himalayan range stretches approximately 2500 km and forms a massive natural wall along India's northern border. The range spans six countries — India, Nepal, Bhutan, China, Pakistan, and Afghanistan — and contains some of the world's highest peaks, those exceeding 8000 metres being collectively called the 'Eight Thousanders'. The word 'Himalaya' derives from the Sanskrit words *hima* (snow) and *ālaya* (abode), meaning 'abode of snow.'

### Formation of the Himalayas

India was once part of a supercontinent called Gondwana, where it neighboured Africa. Around 50 million years ago, the Indian landmass broke away and drifted northward, eventually colliding with Eurasia. This collision caused the land between the two masses to crumple and fold upward, forming the Himalayas — much like a carpet wrinkling when pushed. India continues to push into Asia at approximately five centimetres per year, meaning the Himalayas are still growing by about five millimetres annually.

### Three Main Ranges of the Himalayas

The Himalayas are broadly categorised into three ranges. The **Himadri** (Greater Himalayas) is the highest and most rugged section, permanently snow-covered and home to peaks like Mount Everest and Kanchenjunga, with very few human settlements. The **Himachal** (Lower Himalayas) lies south of the Greater Himalayas, has a more moderate climate, rich biodiversity, and hosts popular hill stations such as Shimla, Nainital, Darjeeling, and Mussoorie. The **Shivalik Hills** (Outer Himalayas) form the outermost and lowest range, consisting of rolling hills and dense forests that serve as a transition zone between the mountains and the Gangetic Plains.

### The Himalayas as a Water Tower

In summer, melting snow from the Himalayas feeds major rivers including the Ganga, Indus, and Brahmaputra, earning the range the title 'Water Tower of Asia.' These rivers and their tributaries supply water for drinking, farming, and industry to hundreds of millions of people. The Bhagirathi River, a major tributary of the Ganga, originates at Gaumukh (Cow's Mouth) in Uttarakhand from the Gangotri Glacier, one of the largest glaciers in the Indian Himalayas.

### Traditional Architecture and Biodiversity

The traditional house-building style of the western Himalayas, known as 'kath-kuni' or 'dhajji-dewari,' uses locally available stone and wood, keeping homes warm and earthquake-resistant. The Great Himalayan National Park in Himachal Pradesh, declared a UNESCO World Heritage Site, supports exceptional biodiversity including snow leopards, Himalayan monals, and rhododendrons, preserved jointly by the government and local communities.

## The Cold Desert of India — Ladakh

Ladakh is India's cold desert, where winter temperatures drop below –30°C and rainfall is extremely scarce. The terrain is rugged and rocky with deep valleys and saltwater lakes such as Pangong Tso; its moon-like appearance has led to it being called 'moonland.' This landscape was formed when the folded rocks of an ancient ocean floor were uplifted during the collision of the Indian and Eurasian plates, and have since been shaped by wind and water erosion. Despite harsh conditions, Ladakh is home to unique wildlife — snow leopards, ibex, and Tibetan antelopes — and is known for its ancient monasteries, yak herding, and vibrant festivals like Losar and the Hemis Festival.

## The Gangetic Plains

Moving southward from the Himalayas, the vast and fertile Gangetic Plains form one of the most important regions in India's history and civilisation. The plains are enriched by rivers such as the Ganga, Indus, and Brahmaputra, whose mineral-laden waters make the soil highly fertile and ideal for agriculture, supporting a large proportion of India's population. The flat terrain has enabled the development of an elaborate road and railway network, facilitating trade and movement across long distances. The plains also host important wildlife, including the Bengal tiger (protected under Project Tiger) and the endangered Indian gharial.

## The Great Indian Desert — Thar Desert

The Thar Desert is a vast arid region in northwestern India, spanning the states of Rajasthan, Gujarat, Punjab, and Haryana. Sand dunes — formed when wind shifts and shapes sand into hill-like formations — can rise as high as 150 metres. The desert acts as a natural barrier due to extreme daytime heat, cold nights, and lack of water, deterring movement of people and animals. People living in the Thar have adapted their food, clothing, and lifestyle to these conditions; water is scarce, and traditional conservation methods such as the *taanka* or *kund* rainwater harvesting systems are used. The city of Jaisalmer, known as the 'Golden City,' and its UNESCO World Heritage fort are located in this desert.

## The Aravalli Hills

The Aravalli Hills are among the oldest mountain ranges in the world, approximately 2.5 billion years old, stretching across Delhi, Rajasthan, Haryana, and Gujarat. Their highest peak, Mount Abu, stands at over 1700 metres, though most of the range lies between 300 and 900 metres. The Aravallis serve a crucial ecological function as a natural barrier, preventing the Thar Desert from expanding further eastward. The range is rich in minerals including marble, granite, zinc, and copper; ancient mines at Zawar provide evidence that Indians were the first in the world, over eight centuries ago, to master the extraction of zinc. Historic forts such as Chittorgarh, Kumbhalgarh, and Ranthambore are located within the Aravallis.

## The Peninsular Plateau

The Peninsular Plateau is a triangular, ancient landform in central and southern India, surrounded on three sides by the Arabian Sea, the Bay of Bengal, and the Indian Ocean. It is bordered by the Western Ghats — a tall, continuous mountain range along the western coast declared a UNESCO World Heritage Site, also known as the Sahyadri Hills — and the Eastern Ghats, which are lower and broken into smaller hills along the eastern coast. Between these ranges lies the Deccan Plateau, a vast highland. The plateau tilts slightly eastward, causing rivers like the Godavari, Krishna, Kaveri, and Mahanadi to flow toward the Bay of Bengal, while the Narmada and Tapti flow westward into the Arabian Sea. The plateau is rich in minerals, coal, and forests and is home to tribal communities including the Santhal, Gond, Baiga, Bhil, and Korku, who maintain distinct languages and traditions closely connected to nature.

## India's Amazing Coastlines

India's coastline extends over 7500 km and features beaches, rocky cliffs, lush forests, coral reefs, and river deltas. The West Coast stretches from Gujarat to Kerala and is characterised by short, swift rivers originating in the Western Ghats that form estuaries; the largest are the Narmada and Tapti estuaries. The East Coast lies between the Eastern Ghats and the Bay of Bengal, featuring wide plains, major fertile deltas of the Godavari, Krishna, Kaveri, and Mahanadi rivers, and important water bodies like Chilika Lake and Pulicat Lake lagoon.

## Indian Islands

India has two major island groups: **Lakshadweep** in the Arabian Sea and the **Andaman and Nicobar Islands** in the Bay of Bengal. Lakshadweep is an archipelago of 36 coral islands near the Malabar coast of Kerala. The Andaman and Nicobar Islands comprise over 500 volcanic islands and serve as strategic outposts of India in the ocean; Barren Island, located here, is India's only active volcano. The Andaman Islands are also historically significant as the site of the Cellular Jail, where freedom fighters were imprisoned under severe conditions during the colonial era.

## The Delta in West Bengal and the Sundarbans

The Sundarbans lie in the delta formed by the Ganga, Brahmaputra, and their tributaries, with roughly half the delta in India and the rest in Bangladesh. This region is a UNESCO Heritage Site and features a unique combination of river, sea, and land, covered in mangrove forests. The Sundarbans are home to many species, most notably the Royal Bengal Tiger.

## The Hills of the Northeast

The hills of northeastern India, including the Garo, Khasi, and Jaintia Hills of the Meghalaya Plateau, are known for lush greenery and among the highest rainfall in the world. This region is rich in forests, unique wildlife, and fertile land, and is home to culturally vibrant communities such as the Khasi people, who celebrate festivals like Shad Suk Mynsiem as an expression of gratitude toward nature. Mawlynnong Village in Meghalaya's East Khasi Hills is renowned as the 'cleanest village in Asia,' famous for its eco-friendly practices and remarkable living root bridges created by weaving tree roots over many years.
