Understanding Markets

## Introduction

Markets are the mechanisms through which goods and services resulting from primary, secondary, and tertiary economic activities reach people. The chapter opens with a quotation from 18th-century economist Adam Smith, who observed that prosperity emanates from markets that develop when people need goods and services they cannot create themselves. The chapter explores how markets function, their role in people's lives, the government's regulatory function, and how consumers can assess the quality of goods and services.

## What is a Market?

A market is a place where people buy and sell goods and services, known by different names in different regions — bazaar, haat (Hindi), or mārukatté (Kannada) — and can exist either as a physical location or online. Markets fulfil both needs (things essential for survival such as food, water, clothing, and shelter) and wants (things desired but not essential for survival). In addition to facilitating economic transactions, markets connect people, traditions, and ideas.

### The Glorious Hampi Bazaar, Karnataka

The Hampi Bazaar in Karnataka was one of the most prosperous markets of the Vijayanagara Empire, located opposite the Virupaksha temple and renowned as a centre of flourishing trade. Portuguese traveller Domingos Paes described Hampi as "the best-provided city in the world," noting the extraordinary variety of goods traded there, including grains, silk, animals, and precious stones. Another Portuguese traveller, Fernao Nuniz, wrote of craftsmen working in the streets producing golden jewellery, rubies, diamonds, and pearls, and marvelled at the abundance of everything in what appeared to be a barren land.

## Prices and Markets

Every market requires a buyer and a seller who must agree on a price for a transaction to take place. Price is defined as the amount at which a buyer is willing to buy and a seller is willing to sell particular goods or services. Buyers and sellers often negotiate and bargain to arrive at an acceptable price; if no mutually agreeable price is reached, the transaction may not occur.

The chapter illustrates price determination through three scenarios involving the sale of guavas. When the seller fixes the price too high, buyers are unwilling to purchase; when the price is too low, the seller cannot make a profit; over time, a price emerges that is just right — high enough to satisfy the seller and low enough to attract buyers. Through repeated interactions, the quantity of goods offered by sellers and the quantity demanded by buyers together determine a price that balances the interests of both parties.

## Markets Around Us

### Physical and Online Markets

A physical market is where buyers meet sellers in person and purchase goods or services in exchange for money; it includes weekly markets and haats, local bazaars, and large multi-storey malls. Online markets allow buyers and sellers to transact from any location using shopping applications or websites, enabling the purchase of a wide range of goods — from books and clothes to electronics — with home delivery. Manufacturers also use online platforms to procure inputs for production, and services such as online classes can be availed without leaving home.

### Domestic and International Markets

A domestic market is one where goods and services are bought and sold within the geographical boundaries of a country, such as the procurement of paper from Indian paper mills for printing books. International markets exist beyond a nation's boundaries, where sellers in one country export their products and buyers import goods produced in another country, thereby enabling trade across borders. India, for example, exports software services to North America, pharmaceuticals to Africa, and engineering goods to Europe, while importing crude petroleum from West Asia, diamonds from Africa, and vegetable oils from South East Asia.

## Wholesale and Retail Markets

Several participants are involved in the smooth flow of goods from producers to consumers. Wholesalers buy goods in large quantities directly from producers or manufacturers and store them in warehouses called godowns; for perishables, cold storage facilities are used, and the produce is then brought to markets called mandīs. Wholesale markets exist for a wide range of commodities including grains, vegetables, chemicals, electronic items, construction materials, and automotive parts, with well-known examples including the Khari Baoli spice market in Old Delhi and Asia's oldest textile market in Surat, Gujarat.

Wholesalers supply goods to retailers — shopkeepers who sell in smaller quantities to final consumers for personal consumption rather than resale. Retail stores exist not only for goods but also for services such as salons, movie theatres, and restaurants, helping increase the availability of goods and services to households. In cases where wholesalers find it difficult to reach a large number of retailers due to distances and terrains, distributors bridge this gap.

In online markets, the distribution channel differs: manufacturers send bulk quantities to the warehouse of a business called an aggregator, which operates a website or mobile application that organises and combines offers from multiple sellers. The aggregator packs and delivers products directly to online buyers. The Surat textile market illustrates the physical supply chain in detail, showing how raw cotton arrives from nearby states, is transformed through weaving, dyeing, and finishing, and then flows through wholesale markets to small shopkeepers and large retail stores across India and internationally.

## The Role of Markets in People's Lives

Markets play a fundamental role in economic life by facilitating transactions between producers and consumers and enabling individuals, households, and businesses to access goods and services they cannot produce themselves. Markets also signal to producers what consumers want — for example, when consumers demand energy-efficient refrigerators, producers respond by manufacturing such products, benefiting society. Beyond economic functions, markets have non-economic significance: long-lasting trusted relationships develop between buyers and sellers, sometimes continuing across generations, and markets serve as social spaces where people from different communities exchange ideas and traditions.

The Ima Keithal (Mother's Market) in Imphal, Manipur, is a unique example where approximately 3,000 women own and run all the shops, selling vegetables, traditional Manipuri attire, handloom and handicrafts, and daily essentials. This market provides employment and income for thousands of families while also serving as a meeting point for different communities and cultures. Cultural traditions in markets extend beyond commerce, as illustrated by the practice in south India where sellers of haldi and kumkum give a small quantity free of charge as a mark of auspiciousness and good wishes.

## Government's Role in the Market

### Controlling Prices for Protecting Buyers and Sellers

The government controls the prices of certain goods to protect both buyers and sellers. It sets maximum prices — for instance, lifesaving drugs have an upper limit on their prices — and minimum prices, such as the Minimum Support Price for essential agricultural products like wheat, paddy, and maize, ensuring farmers do not incur losses. The government also sets minimum wages to ensure fair payment to employees, though it must calibrate these price controls carefully, as prices set too low remove producers' motivation to produce, while prices set too high disadvantage consumers.

### Ensuring Quality and Safety Standards

The government ensures the welfare of consumers by requiring manufacturers to follow quality and safety standards while producing goods and delivering services. For instance, pharmaceutical companies must obtain government approvals and submit to sample testing to confirm that medicines meet required quality standards, protecting consumers' health. The government also monitors the weights and measures of packaged products to verify net quantity.

### Mitigating the External Effects of Markets

Markets sometimes have significant effects beyond the buying and selling transaction itself, such as environmental pollution caused by factories producing certain goods. When manufacturing activities — like the production of single-use plastics — pollute the environment and pose health risks, the government intervenes by implementing strict regulations to mitigate these negative effects. This regulatory role ensures that markets do not impose undue costs on society.

### Providing Public Goods

Some goods and services, such as public parks, roads, and policing, are not expected to generate profit for private producers and are therefore provided by the government. These are called public goods — services or commodities accessible to all members of society whose present use does not diminish their availability for future use. The government's provision of these goods ensures that essential services are available to all citizens, not just those who can pay for them.

The historical basis for government regulation of markets is noted through a reference to Kauṭilya's Arthaśhāstra, which included instructions for traders of clarified butter, specifying that buyers should be given a small additional measure to compensate for the ghee sticking to the measuring can — demonstrating that consumer protection is an ancient principle.

## How Can Consumers Assess the Quality of Products and Services?

Consumers need to evaluate the quality of products before purchasing, taking into account factors such as price, physical properties, and safety. Product labels carry important information including net quantity, date of manufacture, batch number, best-before date, maximum retail price (MRP), name and address of the manufacturer, nutrition facts, ingredients list, and allergen declaration. Government agencies provide certification marks that confirm products fulfil minimum quality standards.

Key certification marks in India include: the FSSAI (Food Safety and Standards Authority of India) mark on food products, confirming they are safe to consume; the ISI (Indian Standards Institution) mark issued by the Bureau of Indian Standards (BIS), found on electrical appliances, construction materials, and automotive tyres; the AGMARK certification for agricultural products such as vegetables, fruits, cereals, pulses, spices, and honey; and the BEE (Bureau of Energy Efficiency) Star Rating on electronic appliances such as TVs, air conditioners, and laptops, where a higher star rating indicates greater energy efficiency, benefiting both the consumer through lower electricity bills and the environment. Beyond official certifications, consumers are also guided by word-of-mouth recommendations from family and friends, as well as online reviews and feedback from other consumers when shopping digitally.
