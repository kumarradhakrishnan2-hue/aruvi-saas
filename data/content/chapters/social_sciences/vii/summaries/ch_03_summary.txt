Climates of India

## Weather, Seasons and the Climate

Weather refers to what we experience from day to day — rain, sunshine, wind, heat — and it keeps changing. Climate, by contrast, is the long-term pattern of weather that a region experiences over several decades, and this pattern varies from region to region. Seasons occur as the Earth revolves around the Sun, recurring every year, and the weather of a place differs in each season.

India recognises a distinct rainy season (monsoon) in addition to the four seasons common worldwide. Traditionally, many parts of India divide the year into six seasons or ṛitus: vasanta (spring), grīṣhma (summer), varṣhā (rainy season), śharad (autumn), hemanta (pre-winter), and śhiśhir (winter). Specific rituals and festivals are associated with these ṛitus, such as Vasanta Pañchamī and Śharad Pūrṇima. Human, plant and animal life are in rhythm with the seasons — crops grown, food eaten, and clothes worn all change accordingly. Scientists have recorded changes in global climates in recent decades, many caused by human actions.

## Types of Climates in India

India's climate is highly diverse due to its varied geography. The Himalayan mountains have an alpine climate with cold, snowy winters and cool summers, while lower Himalayan and hilly areas have a temperate climate with moderately cold winters and mild summers — locations where many hill stations are found. The northern plains have a subtropical climate with very hot summers and cold winters, suitable for wheat cultivation.

The Thar Desert in the west has an arid climate with extremely hot days, cool nights and very little rainfall, requiring unique water conservation practices. The western coastal strip experiences a tropical wet climate from heavy monsoon rainfall, ideal for rice and spices. The central Deccan Plateau has a semi-arid climate with hot summers, mild winters and moderate monsoon rainfall, while eastern India and the southern peninsula experience a tropical climate with mild winters and distinct wet and dry periods governed by monsoon winds.

## Factors Determining the Climate

### Latitude

Latitude measures distance from the Equator. Places at low latitudes near the Equator receive the sun's rays nearly perpendicularly, concentrating heat over a smaller area, making them warmer. At higher latitudes near the poles, the sun's rays are more oblique and pass through more atmosphere, distributing less energy over a larger surface and resulting in colder conditions. This is evident in India: Kanniyakumari and the Nicobar Islands near the Equator remain warm throughout the year, while northern places like Srinagar are much cooler.

### Altitude

Temperature decreases as altitude increases, which is why hill stations like Shimla, Ooty, Darjeeling and Munnar are cooler than surrounding plains. This happens because atmospheric pressure and air density decrease with altitude, making the air less dense and cooler, and because the sun heats the Earth's surface first — so higher altitudes farther from the surface are less warm. The Himalayas are so high that many peaks remain permanently below freezing and stay snow-covered.

### Proximity to the Sea

The sea acts as a temperature moderator for coastal areas: summers are not too hot and winters are not too cold. This is because the sea absorbs and releases heat more slowly than land. Moving inland from the coast, temperatures become more extreme — hotter summers and colder winters. Mumbai (coastal) has summers around 32°C and winters around 18°C, giving a temperature range of about 14°C, while inland Nagpur reaches 44°C in summer and 10°C in winter — a range of 34°C.

### Winds

Winds move masses of warmer or cooler air and influence temperature, humidity and precipitation. States like Punjab, Haryana, Rajasthan and Madhya Pradesh receive dry, hot winds from the deserts of Arabia and Afghanistan in summer, causing heat waves, while cold winds from across the Himalayas bring cold waves in winter. In contrast, winds blowing from the sea carry moisture that can result in rainfall — as seen with the monsoon winds.

### Topography

The physical features of a region significantly shape its climate. The Himalayas and Karakoram ranges protect the Indian subcontinent from the cold desert winds of Central Asia. The flat Thar Desert has no such protection from hot dry winds. The Western Ghats act as a barrier to the southwest monsoon, causing heavy rainfall on their western slopes while leaving the Deccan Plateau in a rain shadow.

A microclimate is a localised climate differing from the surrounding region, with its own temperature, humidity and precipitation patterns. Enclosed valleys, some forests and urban heat islands — cities with dense concrete structures and little vegetation that trap heat — are examples of microclimates that can influence local flora, fauna, agriculture and human health.

## The Monsoons

The monsoon season is central to life in India. The word 'monsoon' comes from the Arabic mausim meaning 'season', and strictly refers to seasonal winds over a large area of the Indian Ocean and surrounding regions including South Asia, Africa and Australia. The mechanism is based on the principle that land heats and cools faster than the ocean, creating pressure differences that drive seasonal wind reversals.

In summer, the Asian landmass heats up, creating a low-pressure system. Cooler, moisture-laden ocean winds are drawn inland and their moisture condenses and falls as heavy monsoon rains. In winter, the land cools faster than the ocean; now the land has high pressure while the ocean remains at relatively lower pressure, causing dry winds to blow from land to ocean. The summer monsoon advances from southern India in early June northward until it covers the subcontinent by mid-July and is called the southwest monsoon. The Western Ghats act as a barrier, giving their western slopes heavy rain while the Deccan Plateau to the east receives less. The northeast monsoon in winter brings dry cold weather to much of south India, though winds passing over the Bay of Bengal collect moisture and deliver rainfall to parts of east and south India. Mawsynram in Meghalaya receives the world's highest average annual rainfall of about 11,000 mm. The monsoons have also inspired classical music, including ragās such as Meghamalhār and Amruthavarshini.

## Climate and our Lives

India's climate deeply shapes its culture and economy. Many festivals are linked to seasons and agriculture — Bihu, Baisakhi, Pongal, Onam, Lohri, Chhath Puja, Makar Sankranti, Gudi Padwa, the Hemis and Losoong festivals — reflecting the intimate relationship between seasonal rhythms and cultural life. Traditional ecological knowledge, developed through close observation of nature, has also helped communities predict the monsoon.

Monsoon failure — poor rainfall during the monsoon season — severely affects agriculture, raises food prices, causes water scarcity and triggers migration of agricultural labourers to cities. Industrial activity also depends on predictable weather and water availability, demonstrating the broad socioeconomic consequences of climate.

## Climates and Disasters

### Cyclones

Cyclones form when an intense low-pressure system develops over the sea, drawing in moist ocean winds that rotate inward and form clouds. The cloudless centre is called the eye of the cyclone. India's eastern coastline is especially vulnerable to cyclones every year, causing loss of life, property damage, soil erosion and infrastructure destruction. The India Meteorological Department (IMD) tracks cyclones, and the National Disaster Response Force (NDRF) — with 12 battalions across India — carries out rescue and evacuation operations.

### Floods

Floods occur when water overflows onto normally dry land due to heavy rainfall, river bank breaches or glacial bursts. States such as Uttar Pradesh, Bihar, Kerala, Andhra Pradesh and Assam are particularly flood-prone. In Himalayan regions, glacial lakes can burst when glaciers melt rapidly or excessive rain builds pressure — as in the 2013 Uttarakhand disaster that destroyed the Kedarnath area and killed about 6,000 people. Urban flooding is worsened by poor drainage, encroachment on waterways and impermeable concrete or asphalt surfaces.

### Landslides

Landslides are the sudden collapse of rock, soil or debris, commonly triggered by heavy rain, earthquakes or volcanic activity. They are frequent in Himachal Pradesh, Uttarakhand, Sikkim, Arunachal Pradesh and the Western Ghats, especially during the monsoon. Human activities such as deforestation, improper construction and blocking natural water flow have increased landslide risks.

### Forest Fires

Forest fires are uncontrolled fires fuelled by dry conditions, drought, high winds or human carelessness. They are common in Uttarakhand, Himachal Pradesh, Madhya Pradesh, Chhattisgarh and the Western Ghats. Beyond destroying forests, fires harm wildlife, degrade ecosystems, pollute the air and displace communities, with both environmental and economic consequences.

## Climate Change

Climate change refers to significant long-term shifts in temperature, precipitation and weather events, affecting the planet or specific regions. While natural processes drove climate change in past millennia, since the 19th century it has been largely driven by the burning of fossil fuels, deforestation, harmful industrial practices and patterns of excessive consumption. Fossil fuels — coal, petroleum and natural gas — formed from buried plant and animal remains over millions of years.

Burning fossil fuels and other human activities release large quantities of greenhouse gases such as carbon dioxide, enhancing the natural greenhouse effect and causing rapid global warming that disrupts the climate patterns to which plants, animals and human societies have adapted. In India, rising temperatures are already evident — early 2025 saw average temperatures 1–3°C above normal, shortening and warming the winter and affecting agriculture and industry. Governments worldwide, including India's, promote climate mitigation measures such as reducing greenhouse gas emissions, planting trees, expanding renewable energy, improving energy efficiency and encouraging sustainable lifestyles, though these often conflict with goals of economic growth.
