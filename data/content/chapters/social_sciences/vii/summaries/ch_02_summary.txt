Understanding the Weather

## Weather and its Elements

Weather is defined as the state of the Earth's atmosphere at a particular time and place. The atmosphere is the layer of gases surrounding the Earth, commonly referred to as air, and it is structured in multiple layers like a cake. The layer closest to the Earth's surface is called the troposphere, which extends from 6 to 18 kilometres above the ground — thinner at the poles where cold air contracts, and thicker in the tropical zone where warmer air expands. Almost all weather phenomena occur within the troposphere, and it is where all land-based life exists.

The elements of weather describe the different ways in which the atmosphere behaves and is experienced. These elements are: temperature (how hot or cold the atmosphere is), precipitation (any form of water such as rain, snow, sleet, or hail that falls from the sky), atmospheric pressure (the weight of air above us felt on the Earth's surface), wind (the movement of air including its speed and direction), and humidity (the amount of water vapour present in the air). Together, these five elements define the weather at any given place and time.

Because different people in different places experience weather differently, a commonly agreed system of measurement is necessary to communicate weather conditions precisely. Without standardised measurement, a description like "chilly" would mean different things to someone in Chennai compared to someone in Kashmir. Measuring weather precisely also helps in planning activities and preparing for extreme conditions.

From early times, humans observed natural signs to forecast weather. The behaviour of animals and natural phenomena — such as ants carrying their eggs to higher ground, frogs croaking loudly, squirrels gathering nuts, birds flying low, and the opening and closing of pine cones — provided valuable clues about coming rain or storms. This traditional knowledge has been passed down through generations, and even today in many parts of India, people use such methods to predict the weather, especially the arrival of the monsoon.

Meteorology is the systematic study of weather and its evolution, and it forms the basis for modern weather forecasting. Over the last few centuries, scientists have developed precise instruments to measure the elements of weather, and meteorologists use the data collected to predict weather behaviour in a region hours, days, or even weeks in advance.

## Weather Instruments

### a) Temperature

Temperature is measured using thermometers, and there are several types — some measure the ambient (immediate surroundings) temperature, while others record the maximum and minimum temperatures during a day. Thermometers often use a coloured liquid that expands when temperature rises, though digital thermometers are increasingly preferred for their precision and greater data-recording ability. Temperature scales used include the Celsius scale and the Fahrenheit scale; for example, 15 degrees Celsius is the same as 59 degrees Fahrenheit.

Useful statistics can be derived from temperature recordings. The range of temperature is the maximum temperature minus the minimum temperature during a given period, usually 24 hours. The mean daily temperature is calculated by adding the maximum and minimum temperatures of the day and dividing by two.

The India Meteorological Department (IMD) was established in 1875 and has the motto ādityāt jāyate vriṣhti, meaning "From the sun arises rain," a phrase derived from the ancient text Manusmṛiti. The complete sentence from the Manusmṛiti reads: "From the sun arises rain, from rain comes food, and from food, living beings originate," which reflects the ancient understanding of the sun's role in the water cycle.

### b) Precipitation

Precipitation refers to any form of water — rain, snow, sleet, or hail — that falls from the sky. Rainfall is measured using an instrument called a rain gauge, which consists of a collecting funnel, a measuring cylinder, and an attached scale. When it rains, water falls into the funnel and is collected in the cylinder; the height of the water collected is read from the scale, so if the water level is at 5 mm, the area is said to have received 5 mm of rainfall.

### c) Atmospheric Pressure

Atmospheric pressure is the pressure exerted by the weight of the air above and around us. It is higher near the sea coast and decreases as altitude increases; at greater heights the air becomes thinner, meaning there is less oxygen available, which can cause breathlessness, dizziness, and fatigue — a condition that requires the body to acclimatise (adjust to the new conditions). The atmospheric pressure at Khardung La in Ladakh, which is over 5,600 metres above sea level and where Indian army personnel are stationed, is generally about 650 millibars.

The instrument used to measure atmospheric pressure is called a barometer, and it displays readings in units called millibars (mb). The normal atmospheric pressure at sea level is around 1013 mb; when pressure drops below 1000 mb, it indicates a depression or low-pressure system, which can develop into a storm or cyclone. Meteorologists closely monitor atmospheric pressure because sudden drops can signal dangerous weather events.

### d) Wind

Wind is the movement of air from areas of high pressure to areas of low pressure, and it is characterised by its speed and direction. Wind plays a critical role in weather forecasting, and it is important for pilots, sailors, and farmers, who use wind data to predict weather, navigate safely, and anticipate rainfall patterns. Greater wind speed also causes soil to dry faster, which is of relevance to agriculture.

A wind vane (or weather vane) is used to measure the direction of the wind; it has a rotating arm with a pointer at one end and a tail at the other, so that when wind blows, the pointer turns in the wind's direction. A wind sock, which is a type of wind vane used on airport tarmacs, gives pilots an indication of wind direction during take-off and landing. The anemometer is the instrument used to measure both wind direction and speed; it has three or four metal cups mounted on a vertical shaft that rotate when wind blows, and a meter counts the rotations over a period of time to calculate speed in kilometres per hour (km/h).

### e) Humidity

Humidity refers to the amount of water vapour present in the air, and it also depends on factors like temperature, wind, pressure, and location. Humidity is measured as relative humidity: air with no water vapour at all would be rated at 0%, while air fully saturated with water vapour is rated at 100%. In practical terms, dry weather has a relative humidity between 20% and 40%, while humid weather typically falls between 60% and 80%.

The instrument used to measure humidity is called a hygrometer, and various types exist based on different operating principles. When humidity is high, water evaporates slowly, which means wet clothes take longer to dry and the body sweats more without effective cooling. The measurement of humidity is important not only in weather forecasting but also in industrial processes such as food processing, and in museums where maintaining a dry environment is essential to preserving exhibits.

## Weather Stations

A weather station brings together all the instruments needed to measure and track the weather at a particular place and time. Readings of temperature, humidity, precipitation, wind speed and direction, and atmospheric pressure are taken at regular intervals, which enables the mapping and forecasting of weather patterns.

An Automated Weather Station (AWS) is a self-operating system that uses various sensors to measure and record weather data without the need for human intervention. AWS units are widely used in agriculture, aviation, navigation, and environmental monitoring, providing accurate and timely weather information. In 2023, the National Disaster Management Authority set up an AWS at a glacial lake in Sikkim at an altitude of more than 4,800 metres above sea level, providing early information about upcoming weather conditions in that vulnerable region.

## Predicting the Weather

Meteorologists collect data from weather instruments over long periods of time and use scientific methods to predict how the weather will behave in a given region. Such predictions have become increasingly important as climate change makes extreme weather events — including droughts, floods, and cyclones — more frequent. Accurate weather predictions allow people, communities, and governments to prepare for and respond to dangerous conditions.

Weather predictions issued by the India Meteorological Department include a colour-coded warning system that categorises weather conditions such as fog, heat waves, heavy rain, cold waves, extremely heavy rain, ground frost, strong surface winds, hailstorms, thunder and lightning, and dust storms. The warning levels range from no warning to watch (be updated), alert (be prepared), and warning (take action), helping people and authorities respond appropriately. For example, when stormy weather is expected at sea, fishermen are warned against venturing out, and coastal areas may be evacuated if a cyclone is approaching.

The five elements of weather — temperature, humidity, precipitation, wind, and atmospheric pressure — together define the weather at any place and time, and each element is measured using specific instruments whose data contribute to monitoring and prediction. In different situations, one element may be dominant: for instance, rainfall in July, temperature in May and December, atmospheric pressure during a cyclone, and wind during a loo (the strong, hot, and dusty wind that blows across north India in summer) or during the spread of forest fires. Weather is also closely linked to climate, which distinguishes it as a concept from the longer-term patterns studied in climatology.
