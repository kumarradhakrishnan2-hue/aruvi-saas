The Constitution of India - An Introduction

## Introduction

Republic Day, celebrated on 26 January each year, marks the coming into effect of the Indian Constitution on 26 January 1950. The Constitution of India is carefully preserved in a helium-filled glass case in Parliament, as helium is a gas that does not react with paper or ink, thereby helping to preserve the document over time. This chapter explores what a constitution is, why it is needed, how the Indian Constitution was written, what shaped it, and what its key features are.

## What Is a Constitution?

A constitution is a document that spells out a nation's basic principles and laws. It lays out the framework of the three organs of government — legislature, executive, and judiciary — along with their roles, responsibilities, and the checks and balances among them. It also defines the rights and duties of citizens and outlines the long-term goals and aspirations of the nation. The Constitution of India is the document that important officials of the country pledge to uphold; the President takes an oath to preserve, protect, and defend it, while the Prime Minister, Council of Ministers, and judges promise to follow its provisions.

## Why Do We Need a Constitution?

A constitution functions as a rulebook for a country, much like the official rulebook in a sporting competition. Without such a document, disputes between parties — whether in sport or governance — cannot be resolved fairly. The basic rules and principles in a constitution determine what kind of government will exist, how it will be formed, and how it will function, including how laws are made and implemented, who elects the executive, and how the judiciary is constituted. Many constitutions, including India's, also state the values and ideals the country is committed to — such as equality, justice, fraternity, pluralism, and freedom — which form the basis for all laws and principles. The Constitution of India has 25 parts and 12 schedules and is recognised as the world's largest written constitution; when it came into effect in 1950, it had 22 Parts and 8 Schedules, and the numbers have grown through subsequent amendments.

## Writing the Constitution of India

While India was struggling for independence from British rule, it became necessary to plan how the nation would govern itself. Questions arose about what type of government should be established, what rules and principles should guide the nation, who should have the right to vote, and how disputes should be resolved in a large, culturally diverse, and complex country like India. To address these questions, a Constituent Assembly was formed in 1946, initially with 389 members, which was later reduced to 299 after the Partition of India; of these, 15 were women. The members of the Constituent Assembly represented India's diverse regions, professions, and social groups.

### How Was Our Constitution Developed?

The Constituent Assembly was formed on 9 December 1946, with its members elected by the legislative assemblies of the provinces. Dr. Rajendra Prasad served as the Chairman of the Constituent Assembly and later became the first President of India. The initial text of the Constitution was prepared by a Drafting Committee chaired by Dr. B.R. Ambedkar, an eminent social reformer and the first Law and Justice Minister of independent India. The work was completed on 26 November 1949, and the Constitution was adopted on 26 January 1950, which is why that date is celebrated annually as Republic Day. The Indian Constitution was thus developed and written over a period of almost three years.

## What Shaped and Influenced the Indian Constitution?

The Indian Constitution had many influences: the experience, ideas, and ideals of the Indian freedom movement; India's civilisational heritage and culture; and useful learnings from the constitutions of other countries.

### Influence of the Indian Freedom Movement

Many key ideals and values of the Indian freedom struggle were enshrined in the Constitution. A number of leaders of India's freedom movement were members of the Constituent Assembly and therefore carried their experience and ideas directly into the Constitution-making process. Core values such as equality of all, justice for all, freedom, fraternity, and the preservation of India's cultural heritage — as well as the idea of using the Constitution as a tool for achieving these values — emerged from the freedom struggle. The experience of the freedom movement also provided practical answers to questions about universal adult franchise, separation of powers, protection of fundamental rights, the process of amending the Constitution, and the relationship between central and state governments.

### India's Civilisational Heritage and History

Some fundamental principles embedded in India's culture found their way into the Constitution, even if expressed differently. These include acceptance of diverse points of view, the sanctity of nature, the pursuit of learning and knowledge, respect for women, and concepts such as vasudhaiva kutumbakam (the world is one family) and sarve bhavantu sukhinah (an invocation for the well-being of all creatures). Earlier governance traditions explored in India — from the janapadas and sanghas to Kauṭilya's concept of saptānga and the principle of rājadharma — emphasised the duties and role of the people in governance, which is why Fundamental Duties became part of the Constitution. The idea of India being one country, embracing its diversity, is also embedded in the Constitution.

### Learnings from Across the World

In keeping with the Indian tradition of ā no bhadrāh kratavo yantu viśhwatah ("Let noble thoughts come to me from every side"), the Constitution makers studied the constitutions of France, USA, UK, Ireland, Australia, and other democratic countries. The ideals of liberty, equality, and fraternity were adopted from France's constitution, which enshrined them from the French Revolution of 1789. The concept of Directive Principles of State Policy was inspired by the Irish Constitution. The American Constitution helped shape the concept of an independent judiciary. This global learning was integrated with India's own heritage to create a unique constitutional framework.

## Key Features of the Constitution of India

The Constitution of India comprehensively addresses the structure and functioning of government. It clearly defines the roles, functions, responsibilities, and accountability measures for each of the three organs of government — the legislature, the executive, and the judiciary. The electoral system is laid down to ensure that every eligible citizen can vote. India follows a three-tier system of government: central, state, and local (the Panchayati Raj system), with certain functions reserved for the central government and others assigned to state governments.

### Fundamental Rights

Fundamental Rights are constitutionally guaranteed rights that every citizen can demand and enforce through the courts. Examples include the Right to Equality (Article 14), the Right to Freedom (Article 21), the Right against Exploitation, and the Right to Education (Article 21-A). If someone is treated unfairly on account of who they are, they can approach a court and receive remedy, making Fundamental Rights binding promises rather than mere guidelines.

### Directive Principles of State Policy (DPSP)

The Directive Principles of State Policy represent the essence of the social and economic vision that the framers of the Constitution had for India. They are goals that the government should try to achieve, covering areas such as welfare (Article 41), a Uniform Civil Code (Article 44), social, political and economic justice (Article 38), environment and wildlife protection (Article 48-A), protection of monuments and places of national importance (Article 49), and public health and standard of living (Article 47). Unlike Fundamental Rights, the DPSP are guidelines rather than strict rules; a citizen cannot go to court if the government fails to pursue them, though they set the direction for governance and policy.

### Fundamental Duties

Fundamental Duties describe what citizens owe to the nation and to one another. They include abiding by the Constitution and respecting its ideals, the National Flag, and the National Anthem; defending the country; valuing and preserving the rich heritage of India's composite culture; protecting and improving the natural environment and having compassion for living creatures; striving towards excellence in all spheres of individual and collective activity; and ensuring educational opportunities for children between the ages of six and fourteen. Part IV-A, which contains Fundamental Duties, was added to the Constitution in 1976.

## The Constitution Is a Living Document

The Constitution makers understood that new laws and rules would need to emerge over time, so they built in an amendment process. Any changes (called amendments) to the Constitution are rigorously debated in Parliament before acceptance; some require debate in legislative assemblies as well, and public opinion is often sought. For example, the Panchayati Raj System, studied in Grade 6, was integrated into the Constitution in 1992 through the 73rd Constitutional Amendment Act. In 2004, following a citizen's legal challenge, the Supreme Court ruled that flying the national flag at one's home is part of the Fundamental Right to Freedom of Expression, illustrating how citizen action can also drive constitutional evolution. The Constitution is therefore not a static document but a living one that can be improved according to the country's needs. The Indian Constitution is also a work of art: calligrapher Prem Behari Narain Raizada wrote its text by hand, while Nandalal Bose and his team illustrated its pages with scenes from Indian history ranging from Mohenjo-daro to the freedom movement.

## Understanding the Preamble: The Guiding Values of the Constitution of India

The Preamble is a brief introductory statement at the beginning of the Constitution that encapsulates its core values and guiding principles. The Preamble declares that the Constitution has been drawn up and enacted by the people of India through their representatives, not handed down by a king or any outside power. The key terms in the Preamble are: Sovereign (the people have the supreme right to make decisions on internal and external matters, with no external power able to dictate the government); Socialist (wealth generated socially should be shared, and ownership of land and industry should be regulated to reduce socio-economic inequalities); Secular (citizens have complete freedom to follow any religion, but there is no official religion, and the government treats all religious beliefs and practices with equal respect); Democratic (a form of government where people enjoy equal political rights, elect their rulers, and hold them accountable, run according to basic rules); Republic (the head of state is an elected person, not a hereditary one); Justice (citizens cannot be discriminated against on grounds of caste, religion, or gender, social inequalities must be reduced, and the government must work for the welfare of all); Liberty (there are no unreasonable restrictions on citizens in what they think, how they express their thoughts, or how they act on them); Equality (all are equal before the law, social inequalities must be ended, and the government must ensure equal opportunity for all); and Fraternity (all citizens should behave as members of the same family and no one should treat a fellow citizen as inferior). The terms 'Socialist' and 'Secular' were added to the Preamble through the 42nd Constitutional Amendment in 1976. These guiding values are present across the entire Constitution but are most concisely expressed in the Preamble.
