From Barter to Money

## Introduction

This chapter explores the evolution of exchange systems from the earliest barter arrangements to modern digital money. It is framed around the observation, attributed to economist John Maynard Keynes, that money is important because it links the present to the future. The chapter traces how human societies moved from exchanging goods directly for other goods, through the use of commodity money such as cowrie shells and livestock, to metallic coinage, paper currency, and finally digital payment systems.

## Barter System

The barter system is defined as a way of exchanging goods and services without using money. In this system, two parties exchange items they possess for items they need — for example, trading an extra eraser for a pencil. The barter system was the earliest form of exchange and there is widespread historical evidence of it from around the world, with people using commodities such as cowrie shells, salt, tea, tobacco, cloth, cattle, and seeds as media of exchange. Unique examples of commodity money include the giant Rai stone discs of Yap Island in Micronesia, the Aztec copper Tajadero (chopping knife) of Central Mexico and Central America, and Tevau (red feather coils made from birds' feathers) used on the Solomon Islands.

## Why Do We Need Money?

The barter system, while functional in simple communities, presented significant practical challenges as the scale and complexity of trade grew. The central problem was the requirement for a double coincidence of wants — a situation where two people must each possess exactly what the other wants and be willing to exchange at the same time. Finding such a perfect match was rare and difficult, making everyday transactions cumbersome.

### Problems of the Barter System

Several specific limitations of the barter system are identified through the illustration of a farmer who owns an ox but needs shoes, a sweater, and medicines. First, the farmer would struggle to find someone who both needs an ox and can offer all the required items. Second, a direct exchange like an ox for a pair of shoes would not be a fair or proportional exchange, highlighting the absence of a common standard measure of value — an agreed-upon worth for a transaction that helps determine the comparative value of different goods and services. Third, the problem of divisibility arises because a large and indivisible asset like an ox cannot be split into smaller portions to purchase multiple smaller items. Fourth, carrying a large animal or heavy bags of grain from one place to another creates the problem of portability. Finally, goods like wheat or grain that might be used as an intermediate medium of exchange cannot be stored indefinitely because they rot or get eaten by pests, illustrating the problem of durability — the inability of these commodities to be preserved over a longer period.

### Continuing Barter Practices

Although money has replaced barter systems across most of the world, some barter practices persist. The Junbeel Mela is a notable example — a three-day annual socio-cultural fair held at Junbeel in Morigaon district of Assam. Dating back to the 15th century, the fair originally brought together the chiefs of the Tiwa, Karbi, Khasi, and Jaintia tribal communities of Assam and Meghalaya to discuss political matters and maintain friendly relations. Over time it evolved into a trading fair where communities from the hills exchange local products such as roots, vegetables, fruits, herbs, spices, and handmade natural artifacts with people from the plains for rice cakes and other foods unavailable in hilly regions. The fair begins with Agni Puja (worship of fire), a prayer for universal wellbeing. Other contemporary barter examples include book exchange clubs where old books are swapped for new ones, and the practice of vendors visiting homes to offer new utensils in exchange for old clothes or fabrics — a system that benefits both parties by allowing households to dispose of unwanted items while vendors obtain materials for resale or recycling.

## Basic Functions of Money

The limitations of barter made it necessary to develop a common medium of exchange. As the variety and volume of goods being exchanged grew, and trade extended over longer distances, money emerged as a practical solution. Money serves several fundamental functions in an economy. First, it acts as a medium of exchange — a widely accepted tool that facilitates buying and selling of goods and services. Second, money acts as a store of value, meaning it can be saved and used at a later time without deteriorating, unlike perishable commodities such as wheat. Third, money provides a common denomination or unit of account that measures and compares the value of different goods and services through prices, enabling straightforward transactions between buyers and sellers. For example, shopkeepers receive money from customers, use it to pay workers' salaries, and workers in turn use it to purchase essentials and pay school fees. Fourth, money functions as a standard of deferred payment, meaning it is accepted as a means of paying obligations at a future date — for instance, when a buyer cannot pay the full price immediately and requests to pay the remainder later.

## The Journey of Money

The evolution of money in India can be traced along a broad timeline that moves from barter (around 6000 BCE) through cowrie shells, metal coinage (around 1000–600 BCE), official paper money (introduced in 1861), debit and credit cards (from around 1980), and digital money including UPI (from 2016).

### Coinage

Coins were among the earliest forms of money. In ancient India, rulers issued coins made from precious metals — gold, silver, and copper — or their alloys. These coins were called kārṣhāpaṇas or paṇas, and had symbols punched onto them called rūpas. The word paṇa is a linguistic ancestor of words still used for money in Indian languages today: paṇaṁ in Tamil, Telugu, and Malayalam, and haṇa in Kannada. The process of producing coins is called minting, and a mint is the manufacturing facility where coins are made. The minting and issue of coins was controlled by rulers, and the coins of powerful rulers came to be accepted across multiple kingdoms, facilitating inter-regional trade. The two sides of ancient coins — the obverse (head) and the reverse (tail) — carried various symbols and motifs such as animals, trees, hills, royal emblems, kings, queens, and deities. For example, coins of the Chalukyas of Kalyani featured an image of Varaha (an avatar of Vishnu) on one side and a decorated three-tiered royal parasol on the other, while silver coins of the Cholas bore the tiger emblem. Roman gold coins excavated in Pudukkottai, Tamil Nadu, indicate that India engaged in maritime trade with Rome, and scholars conclude that this trade was in India's favour. The alloys used in ancient coins consisted of silver and copper; modern Indian coins are alloys largely of iron combined with chromium, silicon, and carbon in precise proportions. In pre-independence India, the anna was a unit of currency equal to one-sixteenth of a rupee; by 1947, one anna could purchase a dozen bananas. Today, Indian coins come in multiple denominations with both Hindi and English inscriptions, and special coins are minted to commemorate important national events. The ₹ symbol was adopted by the Government of India in 2010, designed by Udaya Kumar of IIT Bombay; it combines the Devanagari letter 'Ra' and the Roman 'R' with two parallel horizontal lines at the top representing both the national flag and the 'equal to' sign.

### Paper Money

As coinage spread and was used for an ever wider range of transactions — from buying vegetables to purchasing land — carrying and storing large quantities of coins became impractical. This led to the development of paper money, also called currency. Paper money was first used in China and was introduced in India in the late 18th century, with institutions such as the Bank of Bengal and the Bank of Bombay issuing early currency notes. While coins are used for smaller denominations, paper currency serves higher denominations. The denominations of Indian currency today include coins of 50 paisa, ₹1, ₹2, ₹5, and ₹10, and paper notes of ₹10, ₹20, ₹50, ₹100, ₹200, ₹500, and ₹1000. Indian currency notes feature motifs depicting the country's cultural heritage on the reverse side, and include special tactile features to assist visually impaired persons in identifying denominations. In ancient times coins were issued by individual rulers; today the Reserve Bank of India (RBI) is the sole central authority legally empowered to issue currency — it is illegal for any other entity to issue currency in India.

## New Forms of Money

Advances in technology have given rise to new, intangible forms of money. Digital money exists in electronic form and cannot be physically touched or felt. Examples include debit cards, credit cards, net banking, and UPI (Unified Payments Interface). These methods directly transfer funds from one bank account to another, removing the need for physical currency in a transaction. QR codes — collections of black and white squares readable by smartphones and QR scanners that contain information about the receiver's bank account — are widely used for digital payments. For instance, street vendors such as fruit sellers now display QR codes on their carts, allowing customers to scan and pay digitally so that the payment goes directly into the vendor's bank account. The evolution of money thus continues, with society continually developing new and more convenient methods for making and receiving payments.
