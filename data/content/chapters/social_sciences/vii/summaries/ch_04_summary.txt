New Beginnings - Cities and States

## New Beginnings: Cities and States

This chapter explores the emergence of India's Second Urbanisation, beginning in the 1st millennium BCE after a long gap following the decline of the Indus/Harappan civilisation. It traces the rise of janapadas and mahājanapadas, their governance systems, technological innovations, social organisation, and the spread of urban culture across the Subcontinent.

## From First to Second Urbanisation

In the early 2nd millennium BCE, the Indus/Harappan/Sindhu-Sarasvatī civilisation — India's 'First Urbanisation' — disintegrated. Its cities were abandoned or reverted to rural lifestyles, and for nearly a millennium, urban life was largely absent from India, as all hallmarks of Harappan urban order disappeared: elaborate structures, busy markets, specialised occupations, writing systems, sanitation, and state administration. Then, in the 1st millennium BCE, a vibrant new phase of urbanisation began in the Ganga plains, parts of the Indus basin, and neighbouring regions, gradually spreading across the Subcontinent. This new phase is known as India's 'Second Urbanisation', and it has continued right up to the present day. Evidence for it comes from two main sources: archaeological excavations confirming the existence of ancient urban centres, and ancient literature — late Vedic, Buddhist, and Jain texts — full of references to these new cities.

## Janapadas and Mahājanapadas

Towards the end of the 2nd millennium BCE, regional cultures reorganised in north India as people formed clans sharing common languages and customs, each associated with a territory called a janapada, led by a rājā or ruler. The Sanskrit word 'janapada' means 'where the people (jana) have set foot (pada)', indicating settled communities. As trade networks expanded, some early states merged, giving rise by the 8th or 7th centuries BCE to larger units called mahājanapadas. Ancient texts list 16 mahājanapadas, extending from Gandhāra in the northwest to Anga in the east and Aśhmaka in central India near the Godavari River, with many concentrated in the fertile Ganga plains due to rich agriculture, availability of iron ore, and expanding trade networks.

### Capitals of the Mahājanapadas

The mahājanapadas each had capital cities that were fairly large and well-fortified, surrounded by moats — deep, wide ditches filled with water — as additional defence. Gateways through the rampart walls were deliberately kept narrow to allow guards to control the movement of people and goods. Remarkably, most of those ancient capitals continue to exist as living cities today, making them urban centres often 2,500 years old. The most powerful mahājanapadas included Magadha (in present-day Bihar), Kosala (in present-day Uttar Pradesh), Vatsa, and Avanti (in present-day Madhya Pradesh).

### Pushkalavati and the Northwestern Mahājanapadas

The mahājanapadas spread across a wide geographical area, including Gandhāra in the northwest with its capital at Takṣhaśhilā, and Kamboja further northwest with its city of Pushkalavati. These northwestern territories were important nodes connecting the Ganga plains to Central Asia and beyond, contributing to the broad trade and cultural networks of the period. The geographical spread of the mahājanapadas, from the northwest to central and eastern India, underlines how widespread this phase of state formation and urbanisation truly was.

## Early Democratic Traditions

Each janapada had an assembly or council — called sabhā or samiti — where matters concerning the clan were discussed, with members likely being elders. The rājā was not expected to rule independently or arbitrarily; a good ruler was supposed to take advice from these assemblies, ministers, and administrators, and according to some texts an incompetent ruler could even be removed by the assembly. Most mahājanapadas functioned as monarchies where the rājā held ultimate authority, with the position being hereditary; the king collected taxes, maintained law and order, built fortifications, and commanded an army. However, at least two mahājanapadas — Vajji (or Vṛijji) and Malla — were different: known as gaṇas or sanghas, they gave the sabhā or samiti more power, including the selection of the rājā through discussion and vote, leading scholars to call them 'early republics', among the earliest such systems in the world.

## More Innovations

The age of janapadas and mahājanapadas was a period of profound change across technology, culture, and economy. A major technological shift involved iron metallurgy: techniques of extracting and shaping iron were perfected from the early 2nd millennium BCE, and by the late 2nd millennium BCE iron tools had become widespread, facilitating larger-scale agriculture and producing better, lighter, and sharper weapons — swords, spears, arrows, and shields — than bronze. This period also saw the emergence of new schools of thought — late Vedic, Buddhist, and Jain — whose teachings were spread by travelling scholars, monks, nuns, and pilgrims, and Indian art underwent a renewal that would blossom further in the age of empires.

### Coins and Trade

Another major innovation of this period was the first use of coins in India, made necessary by growing trade. The earliest Indian coins were made of silver, a soft metal into which symbols could be punched, giving them the name 'punch-marked coins'; later, coins of copper, gold, and other metals were also produced. Generally each mahājanapada issued its own coins, but coins from neighbouring regions circulated widely and were exchanged in trade across different parts of India and even beyond.

## The Varṇa–Jāti System

As societies grew more complex with urbanisation, Indian society organised itself through a two-fold system of jāti and varṇa. Jāti referred to a community of people defined by a specific professional occupation closely tied to their livelihood, with skills transmitted from generation to generation; jātis were often further subdivided into sub-jātis with their own customs regarding marriage, rituals, and food. Varṇa was a concept from Vedic texts comprising four groups: Brahmins (preservation and spread of knowledge, performance of rituals), Kshatriyas (defence and warfare), Vaishyas (trade, business, agriculture), and Shudras (artisans, craftspeople, workers, servants). Early historical evidence shows the system was more flexible in earlier periods, with individuals and communities changing occupations when circumstances required, but the system became more rigid over time, especially during British rule, and eventually led to inequalities and discrimination. The word 'caste', derived from the Portuguese 'casta', is used by scholars to refer either to varṇas, jātis, or the entire varṇa–jāti system.

## Developments Elsewhere in India

In the 1st millennium BCE, important communication routes opened across the Subcontinent. The Uttarapatha connected the northwest to the Ganga plains and eastern India, while the Dakṣhiṇapatha ran from Kauśhāmbī southward across the Vindhya Range. In the eastern region, major cities emerged, such as Śhiśhupalgarh (present-day Sisupalgarh near Bhubaneswar), the capital of the Kalinga region, built on a strict square plan with imposing fortifications, a water-filled moat, and broad streets. In the southern regions, cities began emerging from around 400 BCE, and three kingdoms arose — the Cholas, Cheras, and Pāṇḍyas — whose history is recorded in the most ancient Tamil literature. The southern regions, rich in precious stones, gold, and spices, traded profitably both within India and with overseas kingdoms and empires. By 300–200 BCE, almost the entire Subcontinent was an interconnected land where goods and culture travelled across regions and beyond India to Central and Southeast Asia, after which the mahājanapadas ceased to exist, giving way to the next phase of India's history.
