From the Rulers to the Ruled - Types of Governments

## What is Government? What are its Functions?

Government plays a central role in the lives of citizens by maintaining law and order, ensuring peace, stability, and security, managing relationships with other countries, taking care of national defence, delivering essential goods and services such as education and healthcare, managing the economy, and working for the welfare of the people. India has a democratic form of government, and while many countries share this form, others have different types of governments. Even among democratic governments, there are important differences in structure and functioning.

## What is Democracy?

Democracy is best understood as 'rule of the people', meaning that the source of power and authority in a democracy rests with the people of the country. This concept is illustrated through the analogy of a school student committee, where students discuss three possible methods for forming a committee: all students participate directly, the head teacher selects members, or students elect representatives by voting. The third method — electing representatives — is identified as the democratic approach, as it ensures every group has a voice through chosen representatives.

### Functions of Government

The three core functions of government correspond to the roles of creating, implementing, and enforcing rules. The creation of rules is called the legislative function; implementation and administration according to those rules is the executive function; and ensuring that rules are followed is the judicial function. These three functions form the structural framework through which any government operates.

### Abraham Lincoln's Definition of Democracy

Abraham Lincoln, a U.S. president in the late 19th century, famously described democracy as 'government of the people, by the people, for the people', a definition that remains widely used today. This phrase encapsulates the core idea that in a democracy, governmental authority originates from, operates through, and serves the people.

## What Makes Governments Different?

Governments across the world differ from one another along four key dimensions. First, who decides that 'this is the government' — that is, who or what grants the government its authority and power; in a democracy it is the people, while in a theocracy it is religious authority. Second, how the government is formed — through elections in a democracy, or through hereditary succession in a monarchy. Third, what the different parts of the government are and what they do — whether the legislative, executive, and judicial functions are performed by independent bodies or by the same body, and whether the rules for government are written in a constitution or determined by a monarch. Fourth, what goals the government is working towards — some governments aim for equality and prosperity for all, while others may serve only certain families or groups.

## Democratic Governments around the World

### Fundamental Principles of Democracy

All democratic governments share a set of fundamental principles. Equality means every person has the right to be treated equally, with equal access to education, health, and equal standing before the law. Freedom means citizens have the right to make their own choices and express their opinions. Representative participation means every citizen has the right to elect representatives through elections, with elected representatives forming the legislature. Universal adult franchise grants every citizen above a certain age the right to vote. Fundamental rights such as the right to equality, freedom of speech and expression, and right against exploitation are protected in a democracy. An independent judiciary ensures that citizens' fundamental rights are upheld and that laws are followed by all, including the government itself. It is important to note that these principles have evolved over time; for example, Switzerland granted women the right to vote only in 1971, and effective universal adult suffrage in the UK came in 1928 and in the USA by 1965.

### Different Forms of Democratic Governments

#### Direct Democracy

Direct democracy is a form where all citizens are directly responsible for all rules, regulations, and functions of governance, participating in decisions without intermediaries. This form is practised in limited ways in some countries like Switzerland. Its main limitation is the difficulty of practical implementation in larger countries, as having every citizen involved in all decisions makes governance unwieldy.

#### Representative Democracy

Representative democracy is the most common form today, in which people elect representatives through universal adult franchise, as in India. The government is always accountable to the people who elected it, and elections are held at regular, fixed intervals — every five years in India and every four years in the USA. Representative democracy takes two main sub-forms:

##### Parliamentary Democracy

In a parliamentary democracy, the members of the executive are also part of the legislature. In India, the prime minister and the council of ministers are also members of parliament. The council of ministers is accountable to the legislature and continues in office only as long as it retains the confidence of the lower house (the Lok Sabha in India). The people elect the legislature, and selected members of that legislature become ministers.

##### Presidential Democracy

In a presidential democracy, the executive works independently of the legislature. The president is elected directly by the people and does not require the confidence of the legislature to hold office. The USA is an example of a presidential democracy, where the Upper House (Senate) and Lower House (House of Representatives) have equal power, and the judiciary is independent from both the executive and the legislature.

### Key Terms: Executive, Legislature, Upper and Lower Houses, Separation of Powers

The executive is the organ of government responsible for implementing the law. The legislature is the body responsible for making laws, known as Parliament in India, Congress in the USA, and by other names elsewhere; in most democracies it has two houses. The Lower House is elected by direct voting and is generally more powerful; in India it is the Lok Sabha, and in the USA it is the House of Representatives. The Upper House is formed by election or nomination; in India it is the Rajya Sabha, and in the USA it is the Senate. Separation of powers means that the legislature, executive, and judiciary work independently and do not interfere in each other's functions.

## A Peek into History: Early Republics

A republic is a form of government in which the head of state is elected and is not a hereditary monarch. Ancient India had early examples of republican governance: in the Vajji (or Vṛiji) mahājanapada, the Lichchhavi clan practised collective decision-making, chose leaders based on merit rather than birth, and held elections for key positions, with representatives from various clans meeting regularly to address issues and ensure people's welfare. A remarkable historical example from the Chola period is found in the Uttaramerur inscriptions from the 10th century CE in Tamil Nadu, which detail the election of members to the village sabhā, including sealed ballot boxes, qualifications and duties of members, and conditions for their dismissal, including immediate removal for corruption. Republics also existed in ancient Greece and Rome in the 5th and 4th centuries BCE, though voting rights were restricted, excluding women, workers, and slaves in many regions.

## Other Forms of Government

### 1. Monarchy

A monarchy is a form of government ruled by a monarch — a king or queen — who exercises sovereign power. In Indian history, mahājanapadas were headed by kings guided by the sabhā or samiti, and kings were expected to govern within the limits of rājadharma — ruling according to dharma and ensuring the welfare of all people. In some parts of the world, kings claimed divine power and held absolute authority over law, enforcement, and punishment. The Shānti Parva of the Mahābhārata contains Bhīṣhma's teachings to Yudhiṣṭhira on good governance, emphasising that a raja must prioritise the welfare of the people, apply law without bias, avoid attachment to power, seek wise counsel, and understand that his authority is bound by dharma.

#### Absolute Monarchy

In an absolute monarchy, the monarch has complete control over making laws, enforcing them, and adjudicating when they are broken. Saudi Arabia is an example: the king holds all power and governs according to Islamic law, though he appoints a council to advise him, he is not bound by its advice.

#### Constitutional Monarchy

In a constitutional monarchy, the monarch is the head of state but holds only nominal power. Real executive power is exercised by the prime minister, and legislative power by an elected parliament. The United Kingdom is an example: although Britain has a king, it functions as a parliamentary democracy in practice, with all laws made by the elected parliament and the Council of Ministers led by the prime minister exercising executive power.

### 2. Theocracy

A theocracy is a form of government where the country is ruled by the principles of religion and by religious leaders. Iran is an example of a unique system combining theocratic and democratic elements: its constitution is based on Islamic principles, the Supreme Leader holds ultimate authority over the legislature, executive, and judiciary and is selected by Islamic clerics for life, while an elected president and parliament handle everyday governance. Other current theocracies include Afghanistan and Vatican City.

### 3. Dictatorship

A dictatorship is a form of government in which one person or a small group holds absolute power, unconstrained by any constitution or law. Adolf Hitler, appointed Chancellor of Germany in 1933, passed laws giving himself full power and eliminating opposition, and went on to be responsible for the Holocaust — the killing of at least six million Jews — as well as hundreds of thousands of Roma and others, and caused World War II (1939–1945), which resulted in immense loss of life and property. Idi Amin was a military dictator in Uganda directly responsible for the merciless killing of thousands of people, and he forced many people of Indian descent who had settled in Uganda to flee the country. The story of Shane, a fictional resident of North Korea, illustrates how in a dictatorship citizens have no freedom in daily life — from haircut styles to internet access — and are subject to constant surveillance and compulsory military service.

### 4. Oligarchy

Oligarchy, from the Greek words olígos (few) and árkhō (to rule), is a type of government where a small, powerful group — usually wealthy families or influential individuals — makes all important decisions. In ancient Greece, aristocratic families ruled as oligarchies in some city-states. In modern times, political commentators have observed that even some democracies can show signs of oligarchy when a small group of politicians and wealthy businesspeople hold disproportionate influence over governance.

## Why Democracy Matters

Democracy is not the only form of government, but it is widely regarded as a better form than others because it guarantees universal adult franchise, equality amongst citizens, freedom of speech, separation of powers, and the wellbeing and prosperity of all citizens — features absent in absolute monarchy, dictatorship, and oligarchy. In a genuine democracy, people can lead their lives freely — choosing what to speak, wear, believe, and express — so long as they do not harm others' rights. The government is accountable to the people who elected it, and if it fails in its functions, citizens can change their representatives through elections, providing a constant check on government conduct. More than half of the countries in the world have adopted democratic forms of government. However, democracies also face serious challenges including corruption, wealth disparity, excessive control by a few over democratic institutions, erosion of judicial independence, and manipulation of information channels, all of which can hinder the achievement of democratic ideals and require citizens to remain vigilant.
