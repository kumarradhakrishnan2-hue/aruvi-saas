The Gupta Era - An Age of Tireless Creativity

## A New Power Emerges

By the 3rd century CE, the Kuṣhāṇa Empire began to weaken, creating space for new kingdoms, including the Gupta dynasty, which is believed to have originated near present-day Uttar Pradesh as regional rulers. The Gupta period is considered remarkable in Indian history for significant developments in art, architecture, literature, and science, flourishing especially during the reign of Chandragupta II. The Iron Pillar of Delhi, erected during Chandragupta II's reign and still rust-free after 1,600 years, stands as a testament to the advanced metallurgical skills of this era; scientists attribute its preservation to a unique protective layer formed by the special iron and atmospheric oxygen.

### Chandragupta I

Chandragupta I played a crucial role in the early expansion of the Gupta Empire through strategic alliances and is remembered for his coins, including a gold coin featuring him with his queen Kumāradevī. He laid the foundation for a strong empire that his successors would expand greatly.

### The Warrior King: Samudragupta

The prayāga praśhasti, a pillar inscription in Prayagraj composed by the court poet Harisena, praises Samudragupta's ambition to be 'dharaṇi-bandha' — to unify the Earth — through military campaigns that defeated many kings. Many defeated rulers were reinstated as tributaries, while others submitted without contest; Samudragupta is also depicted as a veena player on coins, reflecting his patronage of art and learning. Harisena's inscription records how the king supported art, learning, and trade, making his kingdom prosperous.

### Chandragupta II (Vikramāditya)

Chandragupta II, also known as Vikramāditya, was a devoted follower of Viṣhṇu, and the Garuḍa symbol appears on many of his inscriptions and coins. He extended the empire and surrounded himself with learned men, poets, and artists, greatly enriching his court. The Viṣhṇu Purāṇa specifies key regions of the empire — Anugaṅga, Prayāga, Sāketa, and Magadha — though at its peak the empire covered most of present-day north and west India with parts of central and east India.

## A Traveller's Account of Indian Society in the Gupta Age

Chinese traveller Faxian visited India in the early 5th century CE on a pilgrimage to sacred Buddhist sites, and his travelogue records observations of a prosperous, well-governed society in the Gangetic plains. He described happy and numerous people, wealthy Vaishya merchants establishing charitable houses for the poor and sick, well-ordered city lanes, and a king whose guards received salaries. However, Faxian also noted the harsh treatment of chanḍālas, who were considered outcastes and lived outside city limits, reminding readers that historical accounts reflect only one perspective and must be corroborated with multiple sources.

## Glimpses of the Gupta Empire

### Governance and Administration

Gupta rulers used military conquests, diplomacy, and matrimonial alliances to expand and consolidate their empire, adopting grand titles such as 'mahārājadhirāja', 'samrāṭ', and 'chakravartin' to assert supreme authority. The empire was divided into provinces, with land granted to local rulers, priests, and chieftains; these grants were recorded on copper plates, many of which have been discovered by archaeologists. A notable example of matrimonial alliance was Prabhāvatī Gupta, daughter of Chandragupta II, who married a Vākāṭaka prince and, after his early death, served as regent ruler, maintaining strong ties between the two kingdoms and commissioning temples dedicated to Viṣhṇu.

### Thriving Trade

The primary revenue source for the Guptas was land tax, supplemented by fines, taxes on mines, irrigation, trade, and crafts; this revenue funded administration, the army, temples, infrastructure, and patronage of scholars and artists. In the Gupta era, India traded extensively with the Mediterranean world, Southeast Asia, and China, exporting textiles, spices, ivory, and gemstones through a vast Indian Ocean trade network. Socotra Island in the Arabian Sea served as a significant waypoint, with archaeological evidence of Indian traders there alongside those from Egypt, Arabia, Rome, and Greece.

## New Ideas and Wonders: The Classical Age

Gupta rulers were primarily devotees of Viṣhṇu but also patronised Buddhist institutions, including the renowned Nālandā university and several vihāras, reflecting an inclusive and open approach. The prolonged peace and stability of the Gupta period promoted notable achievements across many fields — Sanskrit literature, mathematics, astronomy, medicine, and metallurgy — leading historians to label this era the 'classical age' of India. Knowledge from previous eras was consolidated into numerous texts, including major Purāṇas, while Āyurveda was codified through texts like the Charaka Saṃhitā and Suśhruta Saṃhitā.

### Āryabhaṭa

Āryabhaṭa lived in Kusumapura around 500 CE and authored the Āryabhaṭīya, a treatise on mathematics and astronomy. He proposed that the Earth spins on its axis to explain day and night, calculated the length of a year as 365 days 6 hours 12 minutes and 30 seconds (very close to the modern value), and provided correct explanations for solar and lunar eclipses. His mathematical techniques for calculation and equation-solving became the foundation for further scientific advances in India and beyond.

### Varāhamihira

Varāhamihira was a mathematician, astronomer, and astrologer who lived in Ujjayinī, a city famous for its tradition of learning. His encyclopedic work, Bṛihat Samhitā, covered astronomy, astrology, weather forecasting, architecture, town planning, and farming. His ability to combine observation, logical reasoning, and traditional knowledge made him a pioneer in science.

### Kālidāsa

Kālidāsa is renowned for his exquisitely refined contributions to Sanskrit literature and poetry. One of his celebrated works, Meghadūtam ('The Cloud Messenger'), tells the story of a yakṣha who, after being banished, sends a message to his beloved through a passing cloud, while vividly describing the landscapes and weather of north India.

## The Quest for Beauty

Gupta rulers created a supportive environment for creativity, producing iconic works of art across key centres including Sārnāth (known for exquisite Buddha sculptures), the Ajanta caves, and Udayagiri's rock-cut caves and deity carvings. The Ajanta caves were carved with the support of both the Guptas and Vākāṭakas, featuring elaborate cave temples with central stūpas and renowned paintings such as Bodhisattva Padmapāṇi. 'Gupta art' set high aesthetic standards that left a lasting impact, seen in terracotta sculptures of sacred rivers Ganga and Yamuna and temple carvings depicting Viṣhṇu and scenes from the Mahābhārata.

## The Decline of the Guptas

By the 6th century CE, the Gupta Empire began declining due to repeated invasions by the Hūṇa tribe from central Asia, which weakened control over north India. Simultaneously, the rise of powerful regional rulers led to internal conflicts, fragmenting the empire's authority.

## Meanwhile in the South and Northeast

While the Guptas ruled in the north, the Pallavas emerged as a powerful dynasty in parts of present-day Tamil Nadu, Karnataka, Telangana, and Andhra Pradesh, likely gaining power as the Sātavāhanas declined. The Pallavas were great patrons of art and architecture, devotees of Śhiva, and credited with constructing magnificent temples and rock-cut caves; their capital Kānchipuram became a major centre of learning, with ghaṭikās — centres of learning — fostering intellectual growth.

In the northeastern region, the Kāmarūpa kingdom, ruled by the Varman dynasty, extended over the Brahmaputra valley and parts of present-day Bengal and Bangladesh. The ancient name for this region, Prāgjyotiṣha, is mentioned in the Rāmāyaṇa and Mahābhārata, and the kingdom was a prominent cultural and political centre where temples and monasteries flourished. Samudragupta defeated both a Pallava ruler and the ruler of Kāmarūpa during his campaigns but allowed local kings to retain their thrones as long as they accepted his rule and paid tribute, maintaining peaceful relations across the subcontinent.
