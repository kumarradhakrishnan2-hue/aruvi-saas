How the Land Becomes Sacred

## What is 'Sacredness'?
Sacredness refers to finding something of deep religious or spiritual significance — worthy of respect, reverence, and regarded as holy or divine. In the context of this chapter, sacredness applies to special locations, shrines, pilgrimage journeys, and the very land itself. Sacredness is therefore connected not just with religion and spirituality, but also with geography, traditions, and culture.

## Sacred Places Across Religions
Almost every religion in India has its own sacred places. Religions that originated outside India — Islam, Christianity, Judaism, and Zoroastrianism — have revered sites such as the Dargah Sharif of Ajmer and the Velankanni Church in Tamil Nadu, visited by followers of multiple faiths. For Buddhism, sacred sites include places the Buddha visited or where his relics are kept, such as the Great Stupa at Sanchi and the Mahabodhi Stupa at Bodh Gaya, where the Buddha attained enlightenment — a site receiving over four million visitors annually.

In Sikhism, takhts are seats of spiritual authority associated with important Gurus, such as the Akal Takht at Amritsar and Takht Sri Patna Sahib; Sikhs aspire to visit these in their lifetimes. Sikh tradition also records pilgrimages by Guru Nanak to places like Haridwar, Varanasi, and Puri. In the Jain tradition, tīrthas are associated with the Tīrthankaras — supreme preachers of dharma — and the places where they attained liberation or meditated, such as Mount Abu and Girnar in Gujarat.

## Pilgrimages
Many Indians undertake tīrthayātrās (pilgrimages) to sacred sites called tīrthas, a term that literally means a crossing place — symbolically, a transition from ordinary worldly life to a higher spiritual life. This tradition is at least 3,000 years old and involves not just a physical journey but also an inner journey requiring a specified code of conduct. Without modern transport, Indians crisscrossed the Subcontinent for millennia, resulting in the entire geography being considered sacred.

Pilgrimages are undertaken for spiritual growth and individual development but also serve social and economic purposes. Jawaharlal Nehru observed that pilgrimage traditions — from Badrinath and Amarnath in the Himalayas to Kanyakumari in the south — fostered a feeling of one country and one culture. The Sabarimala temple in Kerala, dedicated to Ayyappa, draws over ten million devotees annually; its difficult hilltop trek symbolises the challenges of the inner spiritual path. The Pandharpur Wārī in Maharashtra is an 800-year-old annual pilgrimage tradition where pilgrims walk for 21 days to the Vithoba temple.

## Becoming Aware of Sacred Geography
Sacred sites are spread across India but are interconnected through networks that cover the Subcontinent. The chār dhām yātrā comprises four sites deliberately located at the southern, northern, eastern and western corners of India. The 12 jyotirlingas — sacred shrines dedicated to Shiva — and the 51 Shakti pīthas also span the entire map of India, including parts of present-day Bangladesh and Pakistan.

The story of the 51 Shakti pīthas explains that these are the places where the body parts of the divine mother Sati fell after Vishnu used his chakra to cut her body — symbolising that the whole land becomes the body of the divine mother. These sacred networks crisscross India's length and breadth, creating a sacred geography in which the land itself becomes sacred. Pilgrims travelling these routes encountered diverse languages, customs, and foods while also noticing deep commonalities, making pilgrimage a major factor in the cultural integration of the Indian Subcontinent.

People travelled for various reasons — religious devotion, trade, debate, and learning from teachers in different parts of the country. Their routes often converged, enabling exchange of goods, ideas, and experiences that enriched communities and led to new ideas emerging and old ones being adapted.

## Sacred Ecology
Tīrthas are typically located on riverbanks, near lakes, in forests, or on mountains, so the natural landscape itself is perceived as sacred space or puṇyakṣhetra. This perception has historically helped protect and preserve Nature, as humans see themselves as part of it rather than separate from it. Geography, culture, and spirituality fuse together in these kṣhetras.

### Rivers and Sangams
Rivers have been worshipped in India since Vedic times. The nadīstuti sūkta of the Rigveda is a hymn praising 19 major rivers of ancient northwest India. Seven rivers — Ganga, Yamuna, Godavari, Sarasvati, Narmada, Sindhu, and Kaveri — are invoked in water rituals even today, and in local languages rivers are referred to with respect, such as 'Ganga ji'. River confluences (sangams) are particularly sacred; Prayagraj, the confluence of Ganga, Yamuna, and the invisible Sarasvati, hosts the Kumbh Mela, which UNESCO has listed as an intangible heritage of the world.

The Kumbh Mela originates in the legend of amṛita manthana, in which the devas and asuras jointly churned the cosmic ocean to extract amṛita, the divine nectar of immortality. Vishnu in the form of Mohini snatched the kumbha (pitcher) of amṛita, and drops fell on four places — Haridwar, Prayagraj, Nashik, and Ujjain — where the Kumbh Mela is now held. An estimated 660 million people participated in the 2025 Kumbh Mela.

### Mountains and Forests
Mountains are symbolically seen as gateways from earth to heaven, which is why many tīrthas and temples are located on hilltops. The physical journey to mountain peaks is seen as a symbolic journey toward the divine, testing pilgrims' physical and mental strength. Sacred mountain shrines include Mount Kailash, the Vaishno Devi temple at Katra, Tiruvannamalai in Tamil Nadu, and the Tirumala hills.

### Trees, Forests and Sacred Groves
The peepul tree (Ficus religiosa) is sacred to Hinduism, Buddhism, Sikhism, and Jainism; the tree at the Mahabodhi Temple in Bodh Gaya is regarded as a descendant of the original tree under which the Buddha attained enlightenment. The peepul tree's importance in Indian cultural geography goes back millennia, as evidenced by its depiction on seals from Mohenjo-daro. The two great epics, the Mahābhārata and Rāmāyaṇa, contain vivid descriptions of sacred rivers, forests, and pilgrimages; countless local and tribal traditions across India claim the epic heroes passed through their localities, with shrines marking those passages.

Sacred groves are natural forests protected by rural and tribal communities because they are seen as the abodes of deities — for example, Ryngkew or Basa in Meghalaya. Known by different names across India (kāvu in Malayalam, devare kādu in Kannada, devarāī in Marathi, khlaw kyntang in Khasi, oraṇ in Rajasthan, and others), these groves shelter great biodiversity and often contain water bodies that support conservation. Locally, sacred groves create harmonious relationships between deity, ecosystem, and humans — for instance, in Thanjavur, grove deities protect fruit bats that are considered sacred and play a critical ecological role in pollination and seed dispersal.

## From Pilgrimage to Trade
Pilgrimage routes and trade routes often overlapped in ancient India, as pilgrims required goods that traders could supply, and some traders doubled as pilgrims. Major ancient trade routes such as the Uttarapatha (connecting northwestern and eastern India) and the Dakṣhinapātha (from Kaushambi through Ujjain to Paithan) carried goods including precious stones, coins, gold, diamonds, cotton, spices, and sandalwood along routes that intersected with sacred tīrthas.

## Sacred Geography Beyond India
The concept of sacred geography is not unique to India. Ancient Greece had sacred mountains and groves; Native Americans viewed Nature as sacred; the Maoris of New Zealand regard the Taranaki Maunga mountain as their ancestor, and recent legislation has granted it the legal rights of a human being, reflecting their worldview.

## More Sacred Sites
In Hindu, folk, and tribal belief systems, elements of Nature — mountains, rivers, trees, animals, stones — are regarded as sacred. The Dongria Khond tribe considers the Niyam Dongar hill in Jharkhand the abode of their supreme deity and prohibits tree-cutting there. The Todas of the Nilgiris regard mountain peaks, Shola forests, wetlands, and specific trees as sacred, connecting them to their rituals. Sikkim has identified and protected sacred mountains, caves, lakes, rocks, and hot springs. Ultimately, the whole of planet Earth is considered sacred — she is Mother Earth or Bhūdevī.

## Restoring and Conserving the Sacred
The harmonious relationship between people and sacred geography sustained Indian civilisation over millennia and created shared values across the Subcontinent, but this relationship is today under great strain. Sacred rivers like the Yamuna, Mahanadi, and Kaveri have become polluted, and sacred groves are being encroached upon for agriculture and industry. At a time when sustainability is a global concern, a worldview that embeds sacred geography has a significant contribution to make, and people have spoken up to protect their environment, deities, and values when sacred places are threatened.
