The Age of Reorganisation

## Introduction: The Age of Reorganisation
After the last Maurya emperor was assassinated around 185 BCE by his commander-in-chief Puṣhyamitra Śhunga, the Maurya Empire broke up and many new kingdoms emerged across the subcontinent. This period is called the 'Age of Reorganisation' by some scholars because existing regions were being reorganised into new kingdoms that constantly competed to become powerful. The period spans roughly from the 2nd century BCE to the 3rd century CE and witnessed political rivalries, foreign invasions, cultural exchanges, and flourishing trade, art, and literature.

## Surge of the Śhungas
Puṣhyamitra Śhunga founded the Śhunga dynasty, ruling over parts of north and central India, and performed the aśhvamedha yajña — a Vedic ritual in which a horse was left to wander freely and any unchallenged territory it crossed became part of the king's empire — to establish his supreme authority. The Śhunga period witnessed a revival of Vedic rituals and Sanskrit as a preferred language for philosophical and literary works; Patañjali compiled the Yoga Sūtras during this era. The Śhungas patronised art and architecture, notably adding beautifully carved railings and reliefs depicting scenes from the Buddha's life to the Bharhut Stūpa in present-day Madhya Pradesh, considered among the earliest examples of Buddhist art.

## The Sātavāhanas
The Sātavāhanas ruled large parts of the Deccan from the 2nd century BCE onward, comprising present-day Andhra Pradesh, Telangana, and Maharashtra, with famous capitals at Amrāvatī and Pratiṣhṭhāna. Trade and commerce flourished significantly; coins depicting ships have been found across India, indicating advanced shipbuilding, navigation, and active maritime trade reaching as far as the Roman Empire, with exports including spices, textiles, sandalwood, ivory, and gold-plated pearls, while glass and perfumed ointments were imported. Agriculture thrived in the Krishna-Godavari river system, and the Naneghat Caves near Pune served as toll-collection points and resting places on major trade routes.

### Life under the Sātavāhanas
In the Sātavāhana tradition, princes were named after their mothers — for example, Gautamīputra Sātakarṇi was named after his mother Gautamī Balaśhrī, a powerful queen who donated land to Buddhist monks and had an important inscription carved at Nāśhik. Inscriptions from the Naneghat caves record a widow queen who performed Vedic rituals including the aśhvamedha yajña and made generous donations of land, animals, and coins to priests, scholars, and monks. These inscriptions, written in Brahmi script, also contain numerals that resemble modern number shapes, providing evidence that modern numerals ultimately originated in India. The Sātavāhanas were devoted followers of Vāsudeva (Kṛiṣhṇa) but also patronised Vedic scholars, Jaina monks, and Buddhist monks by granting tax-free land, as illustrated by the Karla caves and Pitalkhora caves in Maharashtra. The empire fragmented in the 3rd century CE due to weak central control and economic decline.

## Coming of the Chedis
After the decline of the Maurya Empire, Kalinga rose to prominence under the kings of the Chedi dynasty. King Khāravela, a devoted follower of Jain teachings known as bhikṣhu-rāja or 'monk-king', developed the Udayagiri-Khandagiri caves near Bhubaneswar as notable examples of rock-cut architecture created for Jain monks, featuring intricate panels, statues, and spacious carved chambers. The Hāthīgumphā inscription in Brahmi script, found in one of these caves, records Khāravela's military campaigns and welfare works year by year, and his pride in protecting all schools of thought — a principle described as fundamental to the 'Indian ethos'.

## Kingdoms and Life in the South
Between the 2nd–3rd century BCE and the 3rd century CE, three powerful kingdoms — the Cheras, the Cholas, and the Pānḍyas — rose to prominence in south India, competing with each other while contributing to regional trade and culture. These kingdoms had remained independent even during the height of Mauryan power, as noted in Aśhoka's own edicts. The period also saw the flourishing of Sangam literature — the oldest literature of south India — consisting of anthologies of poems expressing personal emotions and societal values, composed at assemblies (sangams) of poets.

### The Cholas
The Cholas were a powerful dynasty ruling parts of south India from the 3rd century BCE to the 13th century CE, with king Karikāla defeating a combined force of Cheras and Pānḍyas to establish supremacy. Karikāla built the Kallaṇai or Grand Anicut, a water diversion system on the Kāveri River that enabled more land to be brought under cultivation, earning the delta the name 'rice bowl of the South'; it is still in use today. The famous Tamil epic Silappadikāram, set across the Chola, Pānḍya, and Chera kingdoms, centres on principles of justice and a ruler's dharma, following the story of Kaṇṇagi and her husband Kovalan.

### The Cheras
The Cheras, also known as the Keralaputra, ruled over the western parts of Tamil Nadu and Kerala with their capital at Vanji (present-day Karur), and were known patrons of Tamil literature and Sangam poets. They maintained extensive trade connections with the Roman Empire and West Asia, exporting spices, timber, ivory, and pearls, making the kingdom a major hub for international commerce.

### The Pānḍyas
The Pānḍyas ruled parts of Tamil Nadu with their capital at present-day Madurai and were mentioned by Megasthenes in his work Indika as a prosperous kingdom with a strong administration and active trade with Greeks and Romans. They were an important naval power, and their kings left inscriptions asserting concern for subjects' welfare and encouragement of all schools of thought.

## Invasions of the Indo-Greeks
After Alexander's retreat from the Indus plains, the satraps he left behind established independent domains and came to be known as the Indo-Greeks, who took control of the northwestern regions (roughly present-day Pakistan and Afghanistan) following the decline of the Mauryas. Though they arrived as conquerors, they were deeply influenced by Indian culture, leading to a blend of Greek and Indian elements in governance, art, language, and daily life. The Heliodorus pillar near Vidisha, erected by an Indo-Greek ambassador who praised Vāsudeva as 'god of gods', exemplifies this cultural confluence; Indo-Greek coins depicted both Greek deities and Indian deities like Vāsudeva-Kṛiṣhṇa and Lakṣhmī. The Śhakas (Indo-Scythians) ended Indo-Greek rule; they developed the Śhaka Samvat calendar, which was adopted as the Indian National Calendar in 1957.

## The Emergence of the Kuṣhāṇas
The Kuṣhāṇas, originally from central Asia, entered India probably in the 2nd century CE, and at their peak their empire extended from central Asia to large parts of northern India, controlling significant sections of the Silk Route that connected India with Asia and the West. King Kaṇiṣhka, their most powerful ruler, featured both Buddha and Śhiva on his coins, reflecting the peaceful coexistence of various schools of thought and his patronage of art and culture. Kuṣhāṇa art and architecture are celebrated for the Gāndhāra school — which blended Greco-Roman and Indian features in grey-black schist stone sculptures, particularly fine Buddha images — and the Mathurā school, which used red sandstone in a distinctly Indian style to depict deities including Kubera, Lakṣhmī, Śhiva, and Buddha with fuller figures. This era also saw the composition of major Sanskrit texts including the Mahābhārata and the Rāmāyaṇa, and marked a dynamic process of cultural interaction and assimilation with ultimately a dominance of Indian themes across art, architecture, and literature.
