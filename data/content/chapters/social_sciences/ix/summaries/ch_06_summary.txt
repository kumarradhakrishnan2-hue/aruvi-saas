Chapter 06: Democracy

Tracing Democratic Traditions from Early Times
This section traces the deep historical roots of democratic ideas in India, arguing that the country's democratic ethos evolved through a long historical process rather than emerging suddenly. It recalls the Vedic-period assemblies of collective decision-making—the Sabha, Samiti, and Vidhata—and the early republican states (gaṇas or saṁghas), noting that the king was never an independent ruler but worked in consultation with assemblies, ministers, and officers. A verse from the Aikyamatya Sūktam of the Ṛig Veda (Fig. 6.1, cited as Ṛig Veda 10.191.3) is used to show that values of consultation, consensus, and shared responsibility have ancient roots, and the functioning of the Bauddha Saṁghas—monastic communities established by Gautama Buddha that debated, chose leaders, and decided by voting—is offered as further evidence. The section then explains how foreign invasions and British colonisation (by the 19th century) disrupted political structures and limited participation, while the freedom struggle revived democratic ideas, leading to the Constituent Assembly formed in 1946, which took 2 years, 11 months, and 18 days to draft the world's longest written Constitution—adopted on 26 November 1949 and in force on 26 January 1950—shaped by both indigenous traditions and the global spread of democratic values, and made flexible through amendment under Article 368.

Principles of Democracy
This section introduces the non-negotiable principles on which a thriving democracy rests, all upheld in India through provisions of the Constitution, and then treats each principle in turn under its own named sub-heading.

Popular Sovereignty
This sub-section explains that in a democracy the ultimate source of power lies with the people, so the state derives its authority from them. It links the principle to free and fair elections, to a government that formulates policies and implements laws, and to the Universal Adult Franchise—the right of every citizen aged 18 and above to vote by secret ballot—while pointing forward to the next chapter on elections. (THINK ABOUT IT) Students discuss why it is important that the ultimate source of power rests with the people and how participation through voting strengthens popular sovereignty.

Rule of Law
This sub-section presents the Rule of Law as a fundamental principle ensuring equality before the law, equal protection of the law, and the idea that no one is above the law. It explains that disputes are resolved in courts through procedures established by law rather than by force or personal influence, that no citizen can be punished without due process, and that everyone is given a fair opportunity to defend their rights against arbitrary action, so that the rule of law promotes justice, accountability, and trust in the democratic system.

Fundamental Rights
This sub-section describes how the Fundamental Rights enshrined in the Constitution safeguard citizens' rights, freedom, and dignity. It lists the six Fundamental Rights with their articles—the Right to Equality (Articles 14–18), the Right to Freedom (Articles 19–22), the Right Against Exploitation (Articles 23–24), the Right to Freedom of Religion (Articles 25–28), Cultural and Educational Rights (Articles 29–30), and the Right to Constitutional Remedies (Article 32)—and explains that these rights are enforceable in courts, indivisible and inalienable, subject to reasonable restrictions, with remedies available under Articles 32 and 226. (LET'S EXPLORE) Students discuss the significance of and differences among Fundamental Rights, Fundamental Duties, and the Directive Principles of State Policy, and discuss the significance of the Right to Education (Article 21A), added in 2009 to ensure free and compulsory education for children aged 6 to 14.

Separation of Powers
This sub-section explains the separation of powers among the legislature (which makes laws), the executive (which implements them), and the judiciary (which interprets them), each with distinct responsibilities that prevent the concentration of power and maintain checks and balances. It illustrates the principle with the judiciary's power to review constitutional amendments and to declare an inconsistent law unconstitutional, and it highlights the judiciary as an impartial, independent institution that upholds the Constitution and has used initiatives such as Public Interest Litigation (PIL) to ensure access to justice for all. (THINK ABOUT IT) Students discuss what would happen if the same organ of government made, implemented, and enforced laws.

Accountability and Transparency
This sub-section explains that democratic governments are accountable to citizens, who can evaluate government actions through elections, public debates, and engagement with civil society, thereby strengthening democracy and building trust. It notes that several institutional and policy frameworks ensure accountability and transparency in India, giving the Right to Information (RTI) Act, 2005, as an example that allows citizens to seek information from government agencies.

Multi-Party System
This sub-section describes a multi-party system in which several political parties contest elections and represent diverse voices, giving people the power to choose those they consider most suitable. It explains that each party is guided by particular ideas, values, and principles; that the party or coalition securing more than 50 per cent of seats forms the government while others form the opposition; and that parties reflect the socio-cultural, ideological, and regional diversity of Indian society, functioning within the framework of the Representation of the People Act, 1951.

Safeguarding the Rights of Vulnerable Groups
This sub-section states the government's duty to protect all communities regardless of caste, gender, religion, or regional background, and to distribute resources equitably to the most vulnerable sections in light of historical inequalities. It cites Article 46 of the Constitution, which directs the State to promote with special care the educational and economic interests of the weaker sections, particularly the Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes, and to protect them from social injustice and exploitation. A supporting table (introduced in a "Don't Miss Out" note) maps democratic processes—Legislative, Electoral, Judicial, Civil Society, Accountability, Decentralisation, and Checks and Balances—to their institutions (such as the Legislature, the Election Commission of India, Courts, the CAG, CIC, Lokpal, and CVC, and local bodies) and the democratic values each promotes. (LET'S EXPLORE) Students fill a table matching key features of Indian democracy—popular sovereignty, rule of law, separation of powers, fundamental rights, accountability and transparency, and the multi-party system—with examples from their own state, town, or village.

Role of Media in Democracy
This section explains that the media has become integral to daily life, keeping people informed about world developments and serving as a voice of the masses. It notes that newspapers, news channels, and social media platforms raise public issues and help resolve them through appropriate mechanisms, and that because of this critical role in safeguarding people's voices and upholding democratic principles, the media is often called the 'fourth pillar of democracy'.

Types of Democracy
This section classifies democracy into direct and representative (indirect) forms (Fig. 6.4). In a direct democracy citizens participate directly in most decision-making, which is difficult in large countries, with Switzerland given as an example; in a representative democracy people elect representatives, do not govern directly, hold periodic elections, and keep the government accountable, with India as the example. It further distinguishes parliamentary democracy—where members of the executive are also part of the legislature and the executive is accountable to the legislature (as in India and Canada)—from presidential democracy, where the executive is independent of the legislature and the President is elected by and accountable to the people (as in the United States of America). A comparative table sets out the type of democracy, system of government, head of state and head of government, and key features for India, Canada, the United Kingdom, Switzerland, and the United States, introducing terms such as federalism and sovereignty. (LET'S EXPLORE) Students select their ideal students'-council model from the Team A–D examples in Fig. 6.5 and give reasons for their choice. (LET'S EXPLORE) Working in pairs or groups, students choose two of India's neighbouring countries and find out whether they hold regular elections, who chooses the head of government, whether more than one party exists, and how much freedom citizens have, then judge the system as democratic, partially democratic, or non-democratic.

Democracy in Practice: India's Living Democracy
This section presents India as the world's largest democracy, supported by figures such as over 96.8 crore registered voters in 2024, an average Member of Parliament representing about 25 lakh people (2019), elections conducted in 22 scheduled languages with election symbols so non-readers can vote, more than one million polling stations reaching remote areas (sometimes for a single voter), and over 2,800 registered political parties. It argues that India's democracy is "living" because of its scale, diversity, and citizens' active engagement, and introduces participatory democracy—greater citizen involvement beyond elections through local councils, public consultations, and community initiatives. It describes India's three-tier framework of Union, State, and Local governments and notes constitutional provisions protecting tribal heritage, including Autonomous District Councils (ADCs) in specific north-eastern regions and the PESA Act (Panchayats Extension to Scheduled Areas) of 1996, which empowers the Gram Sabha in tribal areas.

Case Study: Democracy at the Grassroots in India
This named case study shows how democracy functions beyond elections through local self-government, using two award-winning examples. The Jethipura Gram Panchayat (Block Idar, Sabarkantha, Gujarat) received the Nanaji Deshmukh Rashtriya Gaurav Gram Sabha Puraskar in 2019 for effective Gram Sabhas, proper record-keeping, Special Gram Sabhas and Mahila Sabhas, and inclusive participation of Scheduled Castes, Scheduled Tribes, and women in matters of education, sanitation, and welfare. The South Manubankul Gram Panchayat (Rupaichari Block, Tripura Tribal Areas Autonomous District Council) is recognised as a "women-friendly panchayat" for promoting women's participation, supporting self-help groups, and delivering health, sanitation, and livelihood facilities, illustrating that active participation in grassroots and Panchayati Raj institutions makes democracy more responsive and inclusive. The section closes by noting that the democratic spirit extends into everyday social life through civil society organisations, socio-religious and community institutions, cooperatives, and trade unions. (LET'S EXPLORE) Students identify examples from the case study that reflect participation, fairness, limiting the concentration of power, and the peaceful resolution of disagreements.

Women and the Right to Vote: The Indian Experience
This section contrasts the long struggles women faced elsewhere—full voting rights in Britain only in 1928 and in the United States in 1920—with India, where Universal Adult Franchise was extended to all citizens without discrimination when the Constitution came into force in 1950, so Indian women did not have to fight prolonged battles for the vote. It cautions, however, that the right to vote does not automatically ensure equal participation, as social prejudices continue to limit women's representation in politics.

Women's Reservation in Local Bodies
This sub-section explains constitutional provisions for women's participation in decision-making bodies, citing Article 243(d), which reserves not less than one-third of directly elected Panchayat seats for women (with 21 States and 2 Union Territories providing up to 50%), and Article 243(t), which reserves not less than one-third of directly elected Municipality seats for women (with 17 States and 1 Union Territory providing 50% as of 2023). It then notes, via a "Don't Miss Out" account, how the Right to Information (RTI) Act, 2005, was shaped through democratic processes—raised by citizens, journalists, and civil society, examined by parliamentary committees and expert groups, debated and refined in Parliament, and enacted to empower citizens to seek information from public authorities—and how civil society and community institutions act as bridges between citizens and the State through PIL, campaigns, and community organisations. (LET'S EXPLORE) Students discuss the advantages of increased representation of women in the legislature. (THINK ABOUT IT) Students explore and discuss the contributions of eminent Indian leaders, such as Chaudhary Charan Singh and Babu Jagjivan Ram, who emerged from rural backgrounds and worked to strengthen grassroots democracy.

Challenges to Democratic Practices in India
This section argues that democracy requires constant care, awareness, and responsible exercise of rights, and that actions such as damaging public property, spreading misinformation, violating public rules, or indifference to public issues weaken democratic values. It identifies ongoing challenges—illiteracy, misinformation, inequality, poverty, regionalism, gender inequality, social discrimination, and gaps in the implementation of laws and policies—and highlights the spread of fake news through social media and digital platforms as a major contemporary concern that can distort public opinion and even lead to conflict. (LET'S EXPLORE) In small groups, students identify examples of possibly misleading or false information on social media, discuss what made them question its reliability, and reflect on why access to accurate information matters in a democracy.

Emergency
This named content-block treats the Emergency of 1975–77 as a major challenge to Indian democracy. It explains that rising unemployment, inflation, allegations of misgovernance, and public dissatisfaction with the government led by Indira Gandhi culminated in a National Emergency imposed in June 1975 on grounds of internal disturbance, during which a majority of Fundamental Rights were suspended, the press was censored, and political leaders and activists were arrested. It describes the mass movements led by Jayaprakash Narayan (Lok Nayak), especially in Bihar and Gujarat, and notes that the Emergency was lifted in 1977 and general elections followed, with the ruling government's defeat demonstrating the strength of Indian democracy and the importance of constitutional safeguards, civil liberties, and active citizen participation.

Democracy and You
This closing section addresses the young citizen's role, stressing that being well-informed—through newspapers, news programmes, and responsible internet use—enables people to form informed opinions, understand how democracy operates, and contribute positively to society. It highlights programmes such as the National Service Scheme (NSS) and the National Cadet Corps (NCC) that instil civic responsibility, social awareness, and respect for democratic ideals through community service and teamwork, and (via Fig. 6.9) presents ways youth can participate, including understanding the Constitution, following fundamental duties, being media literate, taking leadership in school and community, and joining nation-building activities. (LET'S EXPLORE) Students observe Fig. 6.9 and explore other digital and non-digital ways of participating in a democracy. (THINK, DISCUSS, ACT) Students reflect on how their actions as students contribute to democracy, discuss a community issue they care about, and choose one civic activity to carry out during the month.
