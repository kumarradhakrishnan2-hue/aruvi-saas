Chapter 7: Elections

Why do Elections Matter?
This opening section establishes that regular and periodic elections lie at the core of democracy and are one of the most important processes for exercising democratic rights. It explains that representatives to public offices can be chosen through either direct or indirect elections: in India, members of the Lok Sabha (central level), the Vidhan Sabha or Legislative Assembly (state level), and local bodies such as Panchayats and municipal corporations are chosen through direct elections every five years, while the President, Vice President, and members of the Rajya Sabha are chosen through indirect elections. Using the Grade 8 example of Gurmat as a class representative, the section argues that no representative or government can continue beyond its term without seeking the people's mandate again, and that a genuine choice requires more than one contesting party. It introduces the key idea that elected representatives are accountable to the people, and that the right to elect representatives and hold them accountable is exercised principally through the vote. The margin defines Direct Elections (citizens vote directly to choose representatives or leaders) and Indirect Elections (citizens vote for representatives who then elect the leaders). Inside India's Election Machinery
This section frames the rest of the chapter by stating that for democracy to be realised, elections must be free, fair, and transparent. It stresses the scale of the task in a vast and diverse country where millions vote across thousands of constituencies, requiring laws, a coordinated system at the national, state, and local levels, and a robust machinery to manage it. It names the principal stakeholders whose roles the chapter will examine: the Election Commission of India (ECI), political parties, civil society, media, and voters, and notes that the Constitution and various parliamentary laws provide for their functions and powers.

The Electoral System
This section explains that the first step in conducting elections is deciding the method of converting votes cast into seats in the legislature, which is what distinguishes one electoral system from another. It records that the framers of the Constitution chose the plurality system, also called the First-Past-The-Post (FPTP) system, for the Lok Sabha and the Vidhan Sabha, while the Rajya Sabha and the elections of the President and Vice President use proportional representation. It describes the Vidhan Sabhas as directly elected law-making bodies and explains that six states—Andhra Pradesh, Bihar, Karnataka, Maharashtra, Telangana, and Uttar Pradesh—have a bicameral legislature with an Upper House (Vidhan Parishad or Legislative Council) and a Lower House (Vidhan Sabha), the Parishad being filled partly by members elected by the Vidhan Sabha, local bodies, graduates, and teachers, and partly by the Governor's nominees from fields such as art, science, social service, literature, and co-operatives, through proportional representation using a single transferable vote. The Electoral Systems comparison table contrasts three imaginary countries—Haritbhumi (FPTP), Ratnadweep (Majority System), and Swarnalok (Proportional Representation)—to show how each converts votes into government, including how FPTP can produce a winner with less than 50% of votes, how the Majority System uses a second round when no candidate crosses 50%, and how PR distributes seats in proportion to each party's vote share. The Laws
This section briefly examines the major legislation governing elections in India: the Representation of the People Act, 1950, the Representation of the People Act, 1951, and the Presidential and Vice-Presidential Act, 1952. It explains that the 1950 Act deals mainly with the allocation of seats, delimitation of constituencies, preparation and revision of electoral rolls, and the right of every citizen above 18 to vote without discrimination, while the 1951 Act covers the conduct of elections and post-election disputes, laying down rules for nomination of candidates, election campaigns, voting procedures, and dispute resolution. Together the Acts are presented as ensuring the integrity, transparency, and accountability of the electoral process. The section also lists examples of electoral offences and corrupt practices under the 1951 Act: any gift, offer, or promise by a candidate to induce a person to contest, withdraw, vote, or refrain from voting; appealing for votes on the basis of religion, caste, race, community, or language; and taking the assistance of government-related personnel such as gazetted officers, judges and magistrates, armed forces, police, and excise officers.

Delimitation Commission
This section explains that the boundaries of parliamentary and assembly constituencies are not permanently fixed but change over time with shifts in population density, so that without delimitation one MP might represent far more people than another and defeat the objective of equal representation. It defines delimitation as the process of determining the number of seats and fixing the territorial boundaries in each state for Lok Sabha and Vidhan Sabha elections, aiming to keep the ratio of seats to population as close to equal as possible. The accompanying note records that Article 82 of the Constitution mandates the establishment of a Delimitation Commission and that India has had four such Commissions—in 1952, 1963, 1973, and 2002—inviting students to find out the reason for the long gap between 1973 and 2002.

Election Commission of India (ECI)
This section establishes the ECI as the body in which the Constitution vests the superintendence, direction, and control of the entire process of conducting elections. It describes the ECI as an autonomous, permanent constitutional body established on 25 January 1950 that conducts elections to the Lok Sabha, Rajya Sabha, Vidhan Sabha, Vidhan Parishad, President, and Vice President, with Articles 324 to 329 providing for its establishment and defining its powers and duties. At the state level, election work is supervised by the Chief Electoral Officer, appointed by the ECI from among civil servants, while officers such as District Magistrates, Sub-Divisional Magistrates, and Tahsildars are given election roles as District Election Officers, Returning Officers, and Electoral Registration Officers. The section then details the ECI's functions. First, the ECI creates the electoral roll by sending enumerators to every household to collect data on eligible electors, preparing rolls constituency-wise and polling-station-wise so that only those on the roll may vote, and conducting a Special Intensive Revision (SIR) to update, verify, and correct the rolls—adding eligible citizens, especially young voters who have just turned 18, and deleting names on grounds of death, change of residence, duplicate enrollment, or being permanently untraceable, with time given to raise claims and objections before the final roll is published. (THINK ABOUT IT) Students find out the procedure and documents required for voter registration and discuss the experience with someone who has just registered on turning 18. (LET'S EXPLORE) Students learn that ETPBS stands for Electronically Transmitted Postal Ballot System, which lets service voters cast a postal ballot electronically from outside their constituency, and find out who are classified as service voters and who can vote using ETPBS. Second, the ECI decides the schedule and date of elections when a Legislature's five-year term ends or it is dissolved earlier, weighing factors such as weather, agricultural cycles, examination schedules, and festivals. Third, the ECI registers political parties and allocates symbols: only registered parties (and independent candidates) may contest, the ECI insists on periodic organisational elections to ensure inner-party democracy, classifies parties as recognised national, recognised state/regional, or registered-unrecognised, allots symbols to national and state parties, and acts as a quasi-judicial body in disputes over party recognition and symbols. Fourth, the ECI is responsible for ensuring free and fair elections, since unfairness—candidates influenced by personal favours, inaccurate counting, or accessibility barriers—reduces the democratic value of an election even when it is held, so the process must be inclusive, impartial, and trustworthy; the ECI has taken initiatives for infrastructure accessibility and technology-assisted inclusion for Persons with Disabilities, senior citizens, transgender persons, and Particularly Vulnerable Tribal Groups, including home voting for those above 85 and PwDs with 40% benchmark disability, extended nationwide for the first time in the 2024 General Elections. Fig. 7.6 presents the ECI's digital ecosystem of apps and portals—Suvidha (for candidates and parties), ETPBS (service voters), Voter Helpline App (registration and information), Saksham App (PwD services), cVIGIL App (reporting Model Code of Conduct violations), Sugam (managing requisitioned vehicles), and ERONET (electoral roll and form processing). The section also notes the annual observance of 25 January as National Voters' Day and the Voters' Pledge (Fig. 7.5).

No Voter to Be Left Behind
This section highlights the unparalleled scale of India's electoral exercise, with over 96.8 crore eligible voters spread across diverse regions and terrains, managed autonomously by the ECI. It explains that to carry out functions such as updating electoral rolls, candidate nominations, regulating campaigns, law enforcement, coordination among states, security arrangements, counting of votes, declaration of results, and dispute resolution, the ECI makes extensive use of information and communication technologies and e-governance, alongside inclusion measures such as EVMs with Braille (Fig. 7.7) and home voting for senior citizens (Fig. 7.8). A note records the International Election Visitors' Programme (IEVP) as the ECI's flagship programme for international cooperation, under which it has signed MoUs with 28 Election Management Bodies and three international organisations—IFES, International IDEA, and the United Nations.

Political Parties
This section explains the role of political parties as the primary means by which voters learn about candidates, their policies, their vision for development, and their positions on social, economic, cultural, and political issues. It notes that most candidates belong to a party though some contest independently, and that parties support democracy by providing governance, accountability, representation, and public engagement, organising public opinion, campaigning on issues, and offering voters meaningful, informed choices (Fig. 7.9 and Fig. 7.11). It describes India's multi-party system as a reflection of the country's diversity of languages, cultures, religions, and regions, and introduces defection—abandoning or switching from the party under whose banner a candidate was elected—which can be seen either as political opportunism or as an act of conscience; the associated note explains the Anti-Defection Law passed in 1985 through the 52nd Constitutional Amendment Act, under which a member who voluntarily gives up party membership or defies the party whip may be disqualified by the Speaker or Chairman. The section then sets out the ECI's criteria for party recognition (Fig. 7.12): the conditions for National Party recognition (such as securing 6% of valid votes in four or more states plus four Lok Sabha seats, or 2% of Lok Sabha seats from at least three states, or state-party status in four states), the conditions for State Party recognition (vote-share and seat thresholds in the Legislative Assembly or Lok Sabha from the concerned state), and the definition of Registered Unrecognised Political Parties (RUPP) as parties that have not secured enough votes or have never contested since registration. Challenges to Free and Fair Elections
This closing section acknowledges that conducting elections for over 96.8 crore voters, with thousands of polling stations and hundreds of parties across diverse regions and socio-economic realities, is a challenging task. It identifies challenges such as misinformation, fake news, and intimidation, and explains that the ECI addresses them through the Representation of the People Acts of 1950 and 1951, the Model Code of Conduct, EVMs, the Voter Verifiable Paper Audit Trail (VVPAT), voters' awareness campaigns, and other measures, concluding that constant vigilance and active citizen participation can make elections more representative and democracy more robust.
