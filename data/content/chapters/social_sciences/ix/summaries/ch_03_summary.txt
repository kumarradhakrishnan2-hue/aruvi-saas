Chapter 03: Atmosphere and Climate

The chapter opens by introducing the atmosphere as the blanket of air held around the Earth by gravity, a mixture of gases in varying proportions that is vital for the survival of all living beings. It explains that the atmosphere shields the planet from harmful solar radiation, including ultraviolet radiation, regulates temperature by trapping some of the Sun's energy and preventing it from escaping to space, and is a key component of the Earth's weather and climate systems, influencing temperature, humidity, and air pressure. (THINK ABOUT IT) Students imagine and discuss with friends and teachers what would happen if there were no atmosphere.

Composition of the Atmosphere: This section explains that the atmosphere is a mixture of gases dominated by nitrogen (about 78 per cent) and oxygen (about 21 per cent), the two most abundant gases essential to life, with carbon dioxide, argon, helium, neon, krypton, xenon, ozone, and hydrogen present in much smaller quantities, alongside water vapour and tiny dust particles. It notes that water vapour, which generally ranges from about 0.1 to 0.4 per cent, plays a significant role in cloud formation and precipitation, and that the composition of the atmosphere varies with altitude. A pie diagram (Fig. 3.2) presents these proportions. (LET'S RECALL) Students recall from their Grade 8 Science textbook how nitrogen is useful for plants.

Structure of the Atmosphere: The atmosphere has a layered structure, with layers defined by changes in temperature and density as altitude increases, air density being highest near the surface and decreasing upward (Fig. 3.3). The troposphere, averaging about 12 kilometres, holds the air we breathe, most water vapour and clouds, and nearly all weather phenomena such as rainfall, fog, and hail, with temperature falling with height up to the boundary called the tropopause; above it the stratosphere extends to about 50 kilometres, is cloud-free and ideal for aircraft, and contains the ozone layer that filters the Sun's harmful ultraviolet radiation, ending at the stratopause. The mesosphere reaches about 80 kilometres, again cooling with altitude and burning up most meteorites, while the thermosphere (from 80 to 700 kilometres) heats rapidly with height as its gases absorb X-rays and short-wave ultraviolet radiation, aids radio transmission, contains the ionosphere, and hosts the auroras (Aurora Borealis and Aurora Australis). The outermost exosphere has very thin air from which light gases like helium and hydrogen escape into space, and together all these layers shape the Earth's atmospheric processes, weather, and climate.

Weather and Climate: This section distinguishes weather, the hour-to-hour and day-to-day condition of the atmosphere that can vary significantly from one day to the next, from climate, the average of weather conditions and variations over a large area for an extended period, usually thirty years or more. It uses everyday questions about whether it will rain or be sunny to make the idea of changeable weather concrete.

Elements of Weather and Climate: The major elements that shape weather and climate and affect human life are temperature, precipitation, humidity, wind, and atmospheric pressure. Temperature varies between day and night and across seasons and is governed largely by insolation, the incoming solar energy intercepted by the Earth, which decreases from the equator towards the poles so that temperature also falls poleward (Fig. 3.6 shows the Earth's torrid, temperate, and frigid temperature zones); humidity is the presence of water vapour in the air, rising as warmer air holds more moisture; and precipitation occurs when saturated air condenses and falls as drizzle, rain, snow, sleet, or hail, affected by prevailing winds, mountains, and seasons. Atmospheric pressure is the weight of air on the surface, highest at sea level and falling with altitude, producing low-pressure areas where warm air rises (cloudy, wet weather) and high-pressure areas where cold air sinks (clear, sunny skies), with air always moving from high to low pressure. Wind is this movement of air from high to low pressure, its direction influenced by the Earth's rotation (Fig. 3.7), and a table (Table 3.1) classifies wind speeds such as calm, light breeze, strong breeze, and storm and their common effects; local winds are also covered, with the land breeze blowing from land to sea at night and the sea breeze from sea to land by day, both moderating coastal climates (Fig. 3.8). (THINK ABOUT IT) Students reflect on other occasions when strong winds have caused problems for them. (LET'S EXPLORE) Students find out other categories of wind based on speed and their common effects and share their findings in class.

Seasons in India: Because atmospheric conditions settle into common patterns over weeks and months, the year is divided into seasons, and India's climate is broadly classified as tropical monsoon. The Indian Meteorological Department (IMD) recognises four distinct seasons: winter (December to early April), summer or pre-monsoon (April to June, or to July in the north-west), the monsoon or rainy season of the advancing south-west monsoon (June to September), and the post-monsoon or retreating monsoon (October to December), while the more temperate Himalayan states also experience autumn and spring. The section also notes the traditional Indian scheme of six roughly two-month seasons (ṛtu) reflected in the Indian calendar (Table 3.2). (LET'S ANALYSE) Students note the weather report from a local newspaper for two weeks and observe the changes in the weather. (THINK ABOUT IT) Students find out, with the help of elders and teachers, which Hindustani classical rāgas are connected to each season.

Monsoon: This section explains that India's climate is strongly influenced by monsoon winds, first noticed by ancient sailors and named by Arab traders from the Arabic word mausim, meaning season, to describe the seasonal reversal of the wind system. The south-west or summer monsoon blows from sea to land across the Indian Ocean, Arabian Sea, and Bay of Bengal between June and September, caused by the Indian landmass heating faster than the oceans and forming a low-pressure area that draws in moist, rain-bearing winds, accounting for most of the country's rainfall; the north-east or winter monsoon (October to February) reverses this as the land cools faster than the seas and cold, dry winds blow seaward, bringing rain mainly to the south-eastern coast (Tamil Nadu, Andhra Pradesh, and parts of Karnataka) after gathering moisture over the Bay of Bengal. Maps show the normal dates of the advancing (Fig. 3.10) and retreating (Fig. 3.11) monsoon, and the text stresses that the monsoon is vital to Indian agriculture, water supply, daily life, festivals, and livelihoods, though excessive rain can cause floods and weak monsoons droughts; boxes note ancient rainfall-prediction methods (Kṛiṣhiparāśhara, the Bṛihatsaṁhitā, Kālidāsa's Meghadūtam) and modern efforts such as the National Monsoon Mission and Mission Mausam. (LET'S EXPLORE) Students describe in their own words how the monsoon affects the lives of the people around them.

Climate Change: Climate change is presented as one of the most urgent challenges facing the Earth, referring to long-term changes in temperature, rainfall, and wind driven mainly by human activities such as the burning of fossil fuels, deforestation, and industrial pollution, which increase greenhouse gases like carbon dioxide, methane, nitrous oxide, and water vapour and trap heat, raising global temperatures. Its consequences include more frequent floods and droughts, melting glaciers, rising sea levels, and loss of biodiversity, and it threatens ecosystems, human health, agriculture, and livelihoods, affecting women and children especially. The section argues that tackling it requires collective effort, including reducing carbon footprints, using renewable energy, protecting forests, and adopting sustainable lifestyles. (LET'S EXPLORE) In the "My Carbon Footprint" activity, students tick habits across transport, electricity use, water use, and waste and plastics, score their impact, and write down a two-point climate action pledge.

Punjab Floods 2025: A Case Study: The chapter closes with a case study of the severe 2025 Punjab floods, caused by heavy monsoon rains and the successive swelling of the Satluj, Beas, and Ravi rivers, which damaged villages, agricultural fields, houses, and infrastructure such as roads and bridges and caused heavy economic, social, and environmental losses. It separates the natural causes, including very heavy monsoon rain intensified by western disturbances and rainfall across Himachal Pradesh and Jammu & Kashmir feeding already-high rivers (the Satluj, Beas, Ravi, and Ghaggar), from the human-made causes, including weak and old embankments (dhūsī bāndh), houses and farms built too close to rivers, silt reducing the capacity of rivers and dams, and late or poorly communicated flood warnings. The listed effects include loss of life, displacement of thousands to relief camps, submerged farmland and ruined paddy crops, damaged poultry and dairy farms with animal deaths, damaged roads, bridges, and public buildings, and health problems from standing water. (CLASSROOM DISCUSSION) Students discuss how far natural factors versus human activities caused the floods, whether better planning could have reduced the damage, the National Disaster Management Authority (NDMA) flood-management guidelines, and the role students and youth can play in disaster preparedness.
