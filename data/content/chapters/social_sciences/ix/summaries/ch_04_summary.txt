Chapter 4: Early Humans and Beginning of Civilisation

This chapter traces humankind's story from its earliest ancestors to the first civilisations, framed by four Big Questions about how humans lived before civilisation, how they communicated before writing, how archaeology helps us understand the past, and how early civilisations interacted. It opens by noting that the history of humanity reaches far beyond the kingdoms and the Sindhu–Sarasvatī civilisation studied in earlier grades, and that the period before writing is known mainly through archaeological evidence because written languages emerged at different times in different regions. Among the earliest writing systems is the pictographic Harappan script (also called Sindhu lipi), found on seals and pottery but still undeciphered, alongside the cuneiform script of the Sumerians in Mesopotamia and the hieroglyphic script of ancient Egypt, both of which have been deciphered and mark the beginning of the historical period about 5000 years ago. The chapter also notes the Brahmi script, used from about 400 BCE in southern India and the Ganga Valley and later formalised under Emperor Aśhoka in the 3rd century BCE. (LET'S EXPLORE) Students observe a Harappan seal, discuss why the script has not been deciphered, and find out what efforts have been made to decipher it.

The Invention of Writing: Before and After
This section presents a comparative table contrasting the period before writing with the period after it across four measures. In terms of the span of human history, more than 99 per cent of it (from about 3 million years ago to 5000 years ago) falls before writing, while less than 1 per cent falls in the last 5000 years including the present. Before writing, the main sources for reconstructing the past are tools, implements, and other material objects (artefacts), whereas after writing both material remains and written documents can be used. The section also contrasts how the lives, thoughts, and ideas of people are hard to know without writing but are revealed through literature afterwards, and how the measurement of time shifts from approximate dating to relatively accurate dating because documents record specific dates of events such as coronations and wars.

Why Should We Study Early Human History?
This section explains that studying early human history reveals the long process of humankind's biological and cultural evolution in response to changes in climate and environment. Biological evolution refers to the gradual physical and genetic changes by which early ancestors, the australopithecines (australis meaning southern and pithecus meaning primates), evolved into modern Homo sapiens, while cultural evolution explains how humans adapted during the Quaternary Period (the last 26 lakh years) by developing tools, techniques, and technology to use natural resources. Over time, ways of life shifted from hunting and gathering to agriculture and food production, and the ability to produce surplus food and goods laid the foundation for civilisation. The section establishes that our earliest ancestors evolved in Africa and began moving out around 2 million years ago, with Homo erectus—an upright hominin carrying hand axes and cleavers—being the first to exit Africa and disperse into Asia and Europe between 2 and 0.5 million years ago, followed by a later wave around 125,000 years ago associated with early Homo sapiens, who evolved in Africa around 300,000 years ago. (THINK ABOUT IT) Students discuss why early humans left Africa to migrate to other regions.

How do we know about our ancestors?
This content-block explains the work of archaeology in reconstructing early human life. Archaeologists explore early human sites to uncover clues such as tools, bones, and other objects left behind by early ancestors, using each clue to build a picture of how people lived long ago. To better understand these clues, they also experiment by making and using similar tools, which helps them learn how early humans created, used, and depended on these objects in daily life.

Who Were Our Human Ancestors?
This section describes the earliest human settlements of Africa, Asia, and Europe—together called the 'Old World'—where different kinds of ancestors co-existed, and identifies the making of the earliest stone tools around 3.3 million years ago as the beginning of distinctively 'human behaviour', marking humans as 'hominins' or tool makers whose tools acted as extra-corporal limbs. It explains how discarded tools and the buried remains of ancestors turned into fossils that archaeologists use as major evidence, and traces a sequence of ancestors: Homo habilis (handy man) in the Olduvai Gorge of Tanzania and Kenya who made chopper tools; Homo erectus, inventors of handaxes and cleavers who migrated out of Africa; Homo neanderthalensis of Europe and Southwest Asia who made Middle Palaeolithic flake tools until about 40,000 years ago; and Homo sapiens, the only surviving human species, who developed complex technologies. It also introduces the division of early human history into distinct periods based on technological progress—Palaeolithic, Mesolithic, Neolithic, Chalcolithic, Bronze Age, and Iron Age—as illustrated in the periods chart. (LET'S EXPLORE) Students observe the skulls in the evolutionary sequence and note changes in skull shape and the gradual straightening of the face across ancestors. (THINK ABOUT IT) Students discuss why the shift to farming during the Neolithic period is called a revolution rather than a simple change.

Palaeolithic Hunter–Gatherers
This section explains that the Stone Age is broadly divided into Palaeolithic, Mesolithic, and Neolithic, with palaeo meaning 'old' and lithic meaning 'stone', so the Palaeolithic is the Old Stone Age. In the Indian subcontinent the oldest human settlement dates to about 2 million years ago, with Attirampakkam in Tamil Nadu dated to about 1.5–1.7 million years ago and Isampur in Karnataka to about 1.2 million years ago, where animal fossils and large cutting tools such as handaxes, cleavers, scrapers, and choppers of quartzite and limestone were used to process animal meat, tubers, skin, and marrow. It then describes progress through smaller tools such as scrapers, borers, and points that improved hunting efficiency, and later the invention of the bow and arrow, parallel-sided blade and microblade tools, the hunting of small game, symbolic communication, cave and rock-shelter paintings, body decoration with pigments, and the first beads of stone, bone, and shell. It notes the burin or engraver used to engrave symbolic features on bones and shells such as ostrich eggs, and links these developments to Homo sapiens, who spread across the world including Australia and the Americas between 50,000 and 12,000 years ago.

Mesolithic Hunter–Gatherers
This section describes how, around 12,000 years ago, Earth's climate became warmer and forests and grasslands expanded into formerly ice-covered areas, offering a wider variety of resources such as small game, fish, and edible wild grains and triggering the first-ever population explosion in human history. It explains that a variety of microlithic tools enabled people to gather aquatic food, with fishing as the mainstay of the subsistence economy, and that art activity flourished while caves and rock shelters were frequently occupied. It highlights the World Heritage Site of Bhimbetka in Madhya Pradesh, which contains hundreds of painted rock shelters with Mesolithic and earlier human occupation.

The Neolithic Revolution
This section explains the gradual transition from hunting and gathering to a food-producing way of life, known as the Neolithic revolution, whose hallmark was the domestication of select animals and plants and the development of new breeds through cultivation and husbandry. It contrasts hunter–gatherers, who made tools for procuring food, with Neolithic farmers, who made tools for food production and processing, developed varied earthenware pottery, used many raw materials, and established the first village settlements that laid the foundations for the urban revolution. It uses a chart to show the gradual shift to agriculture across different world regions—West Africa, West Asia, India, the India Ganga Plains, North China, and South China—emphasising that this transition did not occur at the same time everywhere but at different periods in different areas. (THINK ABOUT IT) Students observe the chart, identify the animals that were domesticated and the types of human habitats and objects used, and consider whether any of these are still used today.

Neolithic Period in Indian Subcontinent
This section describes regional variation in the beginning of agricultural life in the Indian subcontinent, centring on Mehrgarh on the Bolan River (in present-day Pakistan), the oldest Neolithic site and earliest agricultural village dating to about 7000 BCE. It explains that Mehrgarh's people built handmade sun-dried brick houses and granaries, buried their dead in graves, made ornaments from semi-precious stones such as lapis lazuli, carnelian, and shells, cultivated wheat and barley, raised sheep, goats, and the zebu humped bull, and were the first to make copper objects—thus entering the metal age as Chalcolithic people by about 4000 BCE and laying the basis for the Bronze Age Sindhu–Sarasvatī civilisation around 3500 BCE. It adds that by 2500 BCE most of the subcontinent was occupied by Neolithic agricultural communities practising cattle, sheep, and goat herding and cultivating cereals, millets, and pulses, sometimes interacting with contemporary Chalcolithic cultures.

Sindhu–Sarasvatī Civilisation
This section traces how the Neolithic way of life that began at Mehrgarh around 7000 BCE spread through the Indus valley and eastward, with some settlements mastering copper extraction around 4000 BCE to become the earliest Chalcolithic sites and mark the beginning of the Bronze Age, leading to the emergence of the civilisation by 2600 BCE. It explains that copper tools in the fertile alluvial plains of the Indus and Ghaggar-Sarasvatī basins raised productivity and prosperity and supported large-scale pottery with diverse regional styles, and that radio-carbon dates from sites such as Bhirrana and Kunal place the Pre-Harappan phase between 7000 and 5500 BCE. It describes the Early Harappan stage of cultural continuity—seen in pottery traditions, semi-precious stone beads, shell bangles, terracotta objects, and copper working, with forerunners of perimeter walls, seals, and script—and lists crafts such as pottery, copper work, shell work, and bead production found at Harappa, Kunal, and Datrana, noting graffiti and early seals with geometric and animal motifs as forerunners to the Harappan script that led to the emergence of urban centres. (THINK ABOUT IT) Students consider how long-distance trade would have been affected had the Harappans not followed a standard binary and decimal system of cubical stone weights.

Bronze Age Civilisations Outside India
This section introduces the other major Bronze Age civilisations contemporary with the Harappans—Mesopotamian, Egyptian, and Chinese—and establishes that four early world civilisations emerged in river plains: the Harappan along the Sindhu and Sarasvatī, the Mesopotamian around the Euphrates and Tigris, the Egyptian along the Nile, and the Chinese in the Huang He basin. It explains that the geographical proximity of Mesopotamia and the Indus–Ghaggar-Sarasvatī valleys facilitated strong contacts and trade between the Mesopotamian and Harappan civilisations, whereas the Egyptian and Chinese civilisations show little tangible evidence of direct contact with the Sindhu–Sarasvatī civilisation. (THINK ABOUT IT) Students discuss why rivers were important in the growth of early civilisations.

Mesopotamian Civilisation
This section explains that although the four civilisations were contemporaneous, the earliest city-based civilisation emerged in Mesopotamia, the Greek name meaning the "land in between" the Euphrates and Tigris in West Asia, covering mainly modern Iraq and Kuwait with parts of Turkey and Iran, and bordered by the fertile crescent of the Zagros and Taurus foothills. It notes that farming began here 12,000 years before present and copper tools arrived around 4500 BCE, improving agriculture, craft, and trade, and that over 2000 years large cities became 'city-states' with the four major city-based civilisations—Sumerian, Akkadian, Assyrian, and Babylonian—flourishing from 3500 BCE onwards. (LET'S EXPLORE) Students find out which countries constitute West Asia in present times.

a. The Sumerians
This subsection describes the Sumerians as the earliest to evolve into a city-based civilisation at Ur and other cities in Sumer (present-day southern Iraq), the first to build dams and canals for irrigation and to use mud and burnt bricks in construction. It explains their worship of many gods believed to control natural forces, the building of a grand stepped temple called a ziggurat for each city's chief god around which the city grew and to which all economic activity was tied, and the restriction of temple entry to high priests and priestesses of the ruling class, reflecting a clear social hierarchy. It adds that cities were large settlements where kings lived in grand palaces while people lived in small brick houses and worked in farming and crafts such as metalworking, pottery, and textiles, alongside merchants and traders. It also notes, as a contemporary parallel, that while the Sumerians developed canal irrigation the Early Harappans built check dams called 'gabarbands', with the elaborate water-harvesting tanks at Dholavira in Kachchh and the burnt-brick dockyard at Lothal as marvels of Harappan water management. (LET'S EXPLORE) Students look for similarities between temples in India and Sumerian temples as centres of socio-cultural and economic activity.

The Beginning of Writing
This subsection explains that the Sumerians were the first to start writing around 3300 BCE with cuneiform, a script of wedge-shaped marks pressed with a stylus into damp clay tablets (from the Latin cuneus, 'wedge'), whose scribes enjoyed high status and which by 3000 BCE was used across Mesopotamia by different city-states speaking different languages. It notes that cuneiform tablets record myths, epics, hymns, "law codes", and educational treatises as well as farming and craft activities, and lists Sumerian achievements: the invention of the wheeled cart and sailboat, the use of calculations for building and measuring fields, and a number system based on 60 that gave us the 60-minute hour, 60-second minute, and 360-degree circle. (LET US RECALL) Students consider whether our understanding of a civilisation changes when its script is deciphered, comparing cuneiform with the still-undeciphered Harappan script.

b. The Akkadians
This subsection describes how Sumerian power was overshadowed in 2334 BCE by a new city-state centred on Akkad in central Mesopotamia, whose people spoke Akkadian but used the same cuneiform script, and who established the world's first dynastic empire and saw the evolution of creative literature. It explains that cuneiform tablets of the Akkadian king Sargon record Mesopotamian trade with the eastern territories of Dilmun, Magan, and Meluhha—Meluhha generally identified with the Sindhu–Sarasvatī civilisation and Dilmun and Magan with today's Bahrain and Oman—trading semi-precious stone beads, ivory, timber, gold dust, and probably copper with the Harappans. (LET'S EXPLORE) Students find out more about early stories like the Epic of Gilgamesh and share them in class.

c. The Assyrians
This subsection explains that the Akkadian empire lost supremacy around 2154 BCE to a new city-state, Assur, in northern Mesopotamia, and that the Assyrian civilisation which supplanted the Akkadians lasted until early 1700 BCE, spreading Assyrian dominance across Mesopotamia and neighbouring regions in the west and south in a short period.

d. The Babylonians
This subsection describes how Babylonia gained dominance in central Mesopotamia from 1900 BCE, rising to glory under Hammurabi from 1792 BCE, who expanded the city-state into a large empire and, most significantly, compiled the Code of Hammurabi—rules for civil and social conduct that became a foundational model for future legal systems. It explains that by the end of 1400 BCE the Babylonians lost prominence through repeated attacks by the Hittites (Indo-European people of Anatolia) and other rising powers who mastered new technologies, along with environmental degradation, pressure on agricultural land, and internal political and economic problems, allowing new powers to emerge in West Asia.

Egyptian Civilisation
This section presents Egyptian civilisation as one of the earliest, known for rich historical records and lasting influence, noted by Greek and Roman writers such as Herodotus in the 5th century BCE and the 19th-century collection de L'Egypte, and reconstructed from papyrus records that preserve stories and practical details. It explains papyrus manufacture and examples such as the Papyrus Ebers of medical cures, libraries from 2000 BCE storing labelled papyrus scrolls including early forms of 'Sindbad the Sailor', 'Aesop's Fables', and 'Cinderella', and the decipherment of the Egyptian script through the Rosetta Stone found by Pierre Bouchard in 1799 and read by Jean-François Champollion in 1822. It describes the Egyptian calendar of three seasons and 365 days based on the rising of Sirius, the emergence of city-states around 3000 BCE, the fertilising annual inundation of the Nile with its rich black mud 'kemet', the collective effort of digging ditches and dams that gave rise to local government and an administrative class, the rulers called Pharaohs whose burials evolved from the mastaba to the step pyramid at Saqqara, the belief in the ka and the practice of mummification, Egyptian leisure and festivals such as the Sed festival, and the relatively high rights of Egyptian women, exemplified by Cleopatra. (THINK ABOUT IT) Students consider why a scale in the Papyrus of Ani shows a heart on one side and a feather on the other, and what it reveals about early Egyptian beliefs. (LET'S MAP) Students locate the River Nile and reason why the northern part is called Lower Egypt and the southern part Upper Egypt. (LET'S EXPLORE) Students study the social hierarchy pyramid to identify Egypt's social classes, their occupations, and a typical daily routine of each class. (THINK ABOUT IT) Students observe images of daily items to infer Egyptian fashion, which social classes used the items, and what visual clues support their reasoning.

The Chinese Civilisation
This section describes the Chinese civilisation flourishing along the Huang He (Yellow River) and Yangtze, whose valleys held early Neolithic cultures around 7000 BCE, with copper/bronze metallurgy from around 2000 BCE and urban centres and the first Chinese Bronze Age territorial empire emerging around 1600 BCE. It outlines the organisation of Chinese history into dynasties—the Bronze Age Shang (1600–1046 BCE) and Zhou (1046–256 BCE), and the Iron Age from about 600 BCE including the unifying Qin (221–206 BCE), from which the name 'China' probably derives, and the Han (206 BCE–220 CE). It notes the Zhou rulers as kings and priests believed appointed by heaven yet dismissible, the careful examination of public officials in archery, horsemanship, calculations, writing, and music, and the use of oracle bones and tortoise shells as the earliest source of information about China. It further describes unique Chinese crafts such as ritual and prestige jade objects and figurines, carved marble ornaments, and mastered bronze metallurgy for weapons, tools, and ritual vessels; the Great Wall of China built from 680 BCE over some two thousand years as defence against nomadic raids; silk known from the Neolithic and becoming a major trade item under the Han that gave the Silk Route its name; a stratified society by 1500 BCE; and Chinese firsts including a money economy by the 5th century BCE, paper currency, and civil services through public examination. It closes by drawing the chapter's larger conclusion that the major Bronze Age civilisations arose independently in fertile river plains, harnessed agricultural productivity and new resources for economic and urban growth, developed social and administrative systems, and created writing systems that recorded transactions and later produced literary and creative texts, so that many of our cultural roots lie in the Bronze Age civilisations. (LET'S EXPLORE) Students explore Chinese logographic characters and note at least two whose shapes connect to their meanings. (THINK ABOUT IT) Students explore other reasons for building the Great Wall of China and share findings through a model or presentation. (LET'S RECALL) Students recall the Silk Route and India's connection with it, and China's contact with India through Buddhism from Grade 7.
