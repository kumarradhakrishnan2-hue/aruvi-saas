Chapter 2: Shaping of the Earth's Surface

The chapter opens by establishing that the Earth's surface is not fixed but is constantly transformed by powerful forces acting both from within the planet and on its surface, with the theory of plate tectonics offered as the central idea that explains these changes. It defines a landform as a natural feature on the Earth's surface produced by processes such as weathering, erosion, deposition, and the movement of the crust, giving mountains, valleys, plateaus, plains, deserts, and coastal features as examples. This opening frames the whole chapter, which moves from the internal forces that build major features to the external agents that wear them down and finally to the disasters associated with different landforms.

Plate Tectonics presents plate tectonics as a major theory in earth science, credited to W.J. Morgan, holding that the Earth's outermost layer is not one piece but is broken into several large and small tectonic plates that move slowly over the semi-molten layer beneath them and drive features such as mountains, earthquakes, and volcanoes. The section describes the Earth's three main layers — crust, mantle, and core — explaining that the crust and upper mantle together form the rigid lithosphere, which is broken into plates, while the semi-molten asthenosphere below allows them to move a few centimetres per year. It classifies plates as continental, oceanic, or mixed, names major plates (Pacific, Eurasian, African, North American, South American, Indo-Australian, and Antarctic), and attributes their movement to convection currents in the mantle, in which heat from the core makes molten material rise while cooler material sinks. It then explains the three types of plate boundaries — convergent (forming fold mountains like the Himalaya, or volcanic activity and earthquakes where an oceanic plate sinks below a continental plate), divergent (where rising magma forms new crust and features such as the Mid-Atlantic Ridge), and transform (where plates slide past each other and cause earthquakes, as along the San Andreas Fault) — and links most earthquakes and volcanoes to plate boundaries, especially the Pacific "Ring of Fire." (LET'S MAP) Students pick any two plates from the world map and complete a table listing the continents and ocean each carries. (LET'S EXPLORE) Students compare the plate map with the earthquake-and-volcano map to identify the correlation between them. (LET'S EXPLORE) Students use an atlas or globe to identify the continents and countries located around the Ring of Fire. (LET'S EXPLORE) Students investigate whether India is at risk of earthquakes, which regions are more vulnerable, and why human lives are at risk, prompted by the 2001 Gujarat earthquake. (LET'S EXPLORE) Students examine a photograph of volcanic deposition and reason about its cause, the grey powder, and what it reveals about the Earth's internal forces. (DON'T MISS OUT) The section notes that earthquakes were known in early India as 'bhūkampa' and that Varāhamihira's Bṛihatsaṁhitā linked their signs to wind, rain, clouds, animal behaviour, and planetary alignments, attributing them to four elemental forces — Vāyu, Agni, Indra, and Varuṇa. (DON'T MISS OUT) It also highlights the mud volcano at Baratang Island in the Andaman and Nicobar Islands as India's only mud volcano, where mud bubbles out due to underground gases and pressure.

Process of Weathering and Erosion introduces weathering and erosion as the paired processes that continuously break down and reshape the surface, wearing down mountains, carving valleys, forming plains, and creating caves, cliffs, and river deltas over long periods.

Weathering is defined as the breaking down of rocks into smaller pieces without movement of the broken material, and the section distinguishes its three types: physical weathering (rocks breaking due to temperature change, frost, or wind), chemical weathering (minerals changing through reactions with water, air, or acids), and biological weathering (caused by plants, animals, or micro-organisms, such as roots splitting rock). It notes that weathering both shapes the surface and helps form soil.

Erosion is defined as the wearing away and carrying of soil, rocks, and surface material by natural agents — water, wind, ice, or waves — and is distinguished from weathering by the fact that it involves movement of material. It names types of erosion (water, wind, glacial, and coastal) and then dwells on erosion's effect on human occupations, explaining how it strips fertile topsoil from farmland, washes away land and property near rivers and coasts, destabilises land for construction and mining, and damages tourism and fishing, showing that erosion directly affects human labour and livelihoods. (LET'S EXPLORE) Students observe the water- and wind-erosion photographs, note the erosion types, and discuss how farmers are affected. (DON'T MISS OUT) The section records that the Sindhu-Sarasvatī civilisation used contouring, bunding, terracing, dams, and canals for water management, documented in the Vedas, Kṛiṣhiparāśhara, Kauṭilya's Arthaśhāstra, and Vṛikṣhāyurveda, and that the Zabo system of Nagaland uses earthen bunds and check dams on hillslopes to conserve soil and water and recharge groundwater.

Agents of Gradation defines these as the natural forces — running water, glaciers, wind, waves, and groundwater — that wear down, transport, and deposit material to level the surface over time. It summarises how running water erodes rocks to form valleys and plains, glaciers carve U-shaped valleys, wind shapes deserts, sea waves form cliffs, beaches, and bays, and groundwater dissolves limestone to create caves and sinkholes, all continuously lowering high areas and filling low ones. (DON'T MISS OUT) The section observes that landforms shaped human history — river plains of the Ganga, Nile, Brahmaputra, and Indus gave rise to agricultural societies and early cities; mountains like the Himalayas acted as both barriers and corridors (through passes such as the Khyber); deserts such as the Thar encouraged trade routes like the Silk Route; and coasts and harbours supported trade and cultural contact.

Running Water explains how rivers shape land through erosion, transportation, and deposition along their course, forming V-shaped valleys, waterfalls, and rapids in the upper course, meanders, oxbow lakes, and floodplains in the middle course, and deltas, levees, and alluvial fans in the lower course, with these landforms mattering for agriculture, settlement, and ecosystems.

Waterfall describes a waterfall as a landform where a river drops over a steep cliff, forming in the upper course where hard rock resists erosion while softer rock below wears away. The section notes their value for tourism, hydroelectric power generation, recreation such as trekking and photography, and their cultural or religious significance.

Meander describes a meander as a winding bend in the middle or lower course formed by lateral erosion of the outer bank and deposition on the inner bank, gradually creating large loops. It explains that the fertile soil deposited along meanders supports agriculture and influences settlement, and that meandering rivers serve navigation, irrigation, and tourism, citing the Grand Anicut (Kallanai) in Tamil Nadu as an example of using rivers for irrigation.

Deltas defines a delta as a fan-shaped or triangular landform built from sediment deposited where a river meets a sea, ocean, or lake. It notes that deltas are highly fertile with rich alluvial soil ideal for crops such as rice and jute, important for fishing where fresh and saltwater mix, and centres of dense settlement, trade, and transport, though prone to flooding. (LET'S EXPLORE) Students explore the Sundarbans delta and find out why it is unique and popular with tourists.

Waves and Currents explains that waves and currents constantly reshape coastal land, creating beaches, sand bars, sea cliffs, sea caves, arches, and stacks. It describes a beach as a deposition landform of sand, pebbles, or rock along the shoreline, valued for tourism, recreation, fishing, and as a natural barrier against waves and coastal erosion. It then details coastal erosion landforms — cliffs, wave-cut platforms, caves, arches, and stacks — explaining how they form as waves undercut and erode headlands, and noting their importance for tourism and the need for coastal protection of settlements.

Glaciers explains glacial erosion as the carving of the landscape by slow-moving glaciers, producing U-shaped valleys, cirques, aretes, hanging valleys, and fjords, which serve tourism (trekking, skiing, mountaineering), harbours and fishing (fjords), and agriculture on fertile glacial soil, while glaciers act as vital freshwater sources feeding downstream rivers. It then describes moraines as depositional landforms of rock, soil, and debris (till) left by melting glaciers, classifying them as lateral, terminal, and medial moraines, and noting their fertile soil and their role in forming natural dams and lakes used for water supply, irrigation, and hydroelectric power. (THINK ABOUT IT) Students investigate the causes of the sudden February 2021 Chamoli flood in Uttarakhand that killed people and livestock and damaged buildings, roads, bridges, and hydel projects.

Wind explains wind erosion as strong winds lifting and carrying loose sand and soil to shape the land, creating yardangs (streamlined rock ridges), ventifacts (rocks polished by sandblasting), deflation hollows or blowouts (shallow depressions), and desert pavements (flat surfaces left after fine particles are blown away), which influence settlement and agriculture in arid regions and attract tourists and geologists. It then describes dunes as wind-formed hills or ridges of sand, classifying them as barchan, longitudinal, star, and parabolic dunes, and noting their role as natural barriers against desertification and wind erosion, their value for tourism and adventure sport, their protection of coastal settlements, and their use as a source of construction sand.

Underground Water explains that groundwater in limestone or soluble rock creates Karst topography through chemical weathering and erosion, forming caves, stalactites, stalagmites, sinkholes or dolines, and underground rivers. It notes that these landforms provide fresh water, tourism, and cultural or religious significance, and that stalactites and stalagmites attract geologists and adventurers for study and recreation. (LET'S EXPLORE) Students observe landforms around their school or home and identify which agent may have created them.

Landforms and Disasters introduces the disasters associated with different landforms, presenting four of them in turn. (LET'S EXPLORE) Students use newspapers, atlases, and books to list disaster-prone areas in India and the world and to note mitigation measures with recent examples.

Landslides explains that landslides result from natural and human factors that make slopes unstable — heavy continuous rainfall that adds weight and reduces friction, earthquakes and volcanic eruptions that shake slopes, steep slopes with loose or weathered rock, and human activities such as deforestation, mining, road and hillside construction, and poor drainage and land use.

Avalanches explains that avalanches are caused by sudden instability of snow on steep slopes — heavy snowfall adding weight over weak layers, a temperature rise reducing friction, wind piling snow unevenly, and triggers such as earthquakes, vibrations, or human activities like skiing, trekking, and construction.

GLOFs explains that Glacial Lake Outburst Floods are caused by the sudden release of water from glacial lakes when rapid glacier melting raises lake levels, heavy rain or snow adds water, and earthquakes, avalanches, or landslides weaken or collapse the natural ice or moraine dams, releasing destructive floods downstream.

Dust Storms explains that dust storms occur when strong winds lift loose, dry soil and sand, aggravated by prolonged drought and low rainfall, sparse vegetation from deforestation, overgrazing, or poor farming, and by climate change and extreme weather, and are common in desert and semi-arid regions.

The chapter closes by drawing the threads together: internal forces such as earthquakes, volcanic eruptions, folding, and faulting build mountains, valleys, and ocean basins, while external forces of weathering, erosion, and deposition wear them down, together producing the diverse landforms of the Earth, to which human life — its climate, resources, settlements, and cultures — is deeply connected, so that understanding the surface helps people prepare wisely for natural disasters and live sustainably with the planet.
