Chapter 05: State and Society up to 1000 CE

Understanding Early Indian State and Society
This opening section sets up the chapter's central distinction between a society and a state. A society is presented as a system of social relationships among people who share a common territory, culture, and sense of belonging, built from structural units such as families and households and institutions such as marriage, and regulated primarily by customs and practices rather than formal laws. A state, by contrast, is defined as an organised political system based on rules and laws, with defined rights and duties of rulers and subjects, mechanisms of governance, and institutions to enforce law and order. The section explains that such political systems developed gradually as societies grew larger and more complex, and that historians study early sources to trace how governance and political authority evolved, beginning with the Vedic period. The Beginnings…
This section establishes the Vedas as the earliest known corpus of Indian literature, especially important because the Harappan script of the Sindhu–Sarasvatī civilisation has not been deciphered. It notes that Vedic literature existed in oral form for several centuries before being written down, and that the date of composition of the Ṛig Veda remains debated among historians, with estimates ranging from the fifth to the second millennium BCE. It recalls that the Ṛig Veda was composed in the northwestern part of the subcontinent, where the Sindhu–Sarasvatī civilisation flourished, and that both were familiar with copper, using the Ṛig Veda manuscript in Devanagari script (Fig. 5.2) as an illustration.

State and Administration — The Vedic Period
This section uses Vedic texts as historical sources to reconstruct the times and geographies in which they were composed. It explains that the earliest sections of the Ṛig Veda were composed in the Sapta–Sindhu region—the Indus, its five tributaries, and the Sarasvatī—and that Vedic texts are studied to understand the lives, social structures, political systems, economic pursuits, and religious practices of the people. Because they are among the earliest texts and reflect a continuity of cultural practice, the Vedic texts are regarded as foundational to Indian culture and traditions.

The Four Vedas
This named content-block sets out the four Vedas and the four-part structure of each Vedic text. It describes the Ṛig Veda as the earliest, containing 1,028 hymns (sūktas); the Yajur Veda as elaborating the performative aspects of yajñas; the Sāma Veda as arranging Ṛig Vedic hymns for musical recitation, forming a foundation for the seven svaras of Indian music; and the Atharva Veda as containing hymns to ward off evil and treat physical and mental ailments. It also explains that each Vedic text has four parts—the Saṁhitās (hymns for invoking deities and offering oblations), the Brāhmaṇas (prose explanations of ritual), the Āraṇyakas (philosophical speculation of forest-dwelling sages), and the Upaniṣhads (inquiry into the Self, Ātman, and the universal being, Brāhman)—while distinguishing the concept Brāhman, the texts Brāhmaṇas, and the varṇa category of Brāhmaṇas.

Political Institutions in the Vedic Period
This section describes early Vedic society as organised into janas, or clans, bound by kinship ties, noting that the Ṛig Veda mentions about thirty janas, five of which—the Yadu, Turvaśha, Puru, Anu, and Druhyu—were collectively called the pañchajana. It explains that the rājā functioned primarily as a clan chief who led the group in warfare and protected its members, and introduces the three assemblies referred to in the Ṛig Veda and Atharva Veda: the sabhā, samiti, and vidhata. (LET'S RECALL) Students recall that the name 'Bharata' first appears in the Ṛig Veda in reference to the Bharata jana and reflect on what this reveals about how early communities identified themselves and their rulers.

Assemblies during Vedic Period
This section details the roles of the Vedic assemblies as a check on the rājā's authority. It presents the sabhā as a smaller judicial body composed of select elites, the samiti as a larger assembly focused on policy and political affairs that represented the broader population, and the vidhata as a popular gathering of community members that served as a forum for discussing warfare and other political matters. (THINK ABOUT IT) Students consider whether the roles of the rājā and the assemblies suggest a principle of people's participation in governance comparable to modern constitutions and parliaments.

Early Kingdoms and Republics
This section traces the transition from kinship-based to territory-based polity as janas evolved into janapadas—literally where a people first set foot—between roughly 1000 BCE and 600 BCE, and then into the sixteen mahājanapadas from about 600 BCE to 300 CE. It identifies Magadha in present-day Bihar as the most powerful, its strategic location and fertile plains enabling expansion that led to the Mauryan Empire, and notes the spread of Vedic culture into the Ganga plains alongside monarchical states (rājyas) and republican states (gaṇas or saṁghas). It also surveys southern polities—the Satavāhanas in the Deccan and the Cholas, Pandyas, Keralaputras (Cheras), and Satiyaputras of the far south—many mentioned in Aśhokan inscriptions, and the emblems and Sangam literature of the early Tamil kingdoms. (LET'S ANALYSE) Students compare maps of iron finds (Fig. 5.3) and settlement in the Ganga region, using PGW and NBPW pottery, iron implements, and expanding paddy cultivation as evidence to identify the factors behind this change. (LET'S RECALL) Students recall the royal emblems of the Cholas (tiger), Cheras (bow), and Pandyas (fish) and the role of Sangam literature in what is known about these three crowned kings, the Vendar of Tamilakam.

Duties and Ideals of the King
This section examines ideas of kingship drawn from early texts, noting the various royal titles—rājā, mahārāja, samrāṭ—used from the sixth century BCE onward. It cites Kauṭilya's Arthaśhāstra and the coronation oath in the Yajur Veda on the king's duty to judge impartially and protect the country, and the Śhānti Parva of the Mahābhārata on ethical conduct and justice, with the king responsible for protection from external threats and internal disorder and for administering justice in cases such as theft and adultery. It also explains the pan-Indian geopolitical ideal expressed through terms such as Jambudvīpa, Bhāratavarṣha, the aśhvamedha and rājasūya yajñas, Prithivi, and chakravarti kśhetra—the domain of a universal ruler—illustrated by Aśhoka's edicts, the Chera king Nedunjeral Adan, and the Chola Rajendra I's title Gangaikonda. (THINK ABOUT IT) Students discuss why it was important for rulers to protect people and ensure justice and what such descriptions reveal about the relationship between rulers and subjects.

Council of Ministers
This section explains that the king did not rule alone but governed through a multi-layered administrative system centred on a council of ministers (mantri-pariṣhad) of elder statesmen who advised him, generally including the treasurer, chief tax collector, chief legal advisor, and commander-in-chief. It presents Kauṭilya's theory of the state as an organic whole of seven constituents (Saptāṁga)—the king, ministers, territory, forts, treasury, army, and allies—that must remain integrated for the state to function. It cites an Aśhokan inscription showing that the council could take independent decisions in the emperor's absence, and the Junagadh Rock Inscription near Girnar, which uniquely preserves records of the Maurya, Western Satrap, and Gupta dynasties across some 700 years. (THINK ABOUT IT) Students discuss why different rulers chose to record their inscriptions on the same stone rather than a new one.

How Were Empires Administered?
This section describes the layered provincial administration of early Indian empires, illustrated by the Satavāhana āḥāras under amātyas, villages under a grāmika headman, district governors (pradeśhikas) who consulted local bankers, caravan leaders, artisans, and scribes, and city governors handling revenue and law and order. It explains the decentralisation between roughly 300 CE and 800 CE, with provinces called bhuktis in the north and manḍalas in the south, further subdivided down to villages, and gives the parallel northern and southern terms for each administrative tier. It compares the Guptas, who retained Kauṭilyan forms while adding a minister of peace and war (sāndhivigrahika) and kumārāmātyas and whose Damodarpur copper plates record a five-member district office; the Pallavas with their tax-free Brahmadeya villages and variyam committees; the Chālukyas with their agrahāram grants at Aihole and Badami; the tripartite struggle for Kannauj among the Gurjara–Pratihāras, Pālas, and Rāṣhṭrakūṭas; and the imperial Cholas with their manḍalams, valanāḍus, nāḍus, and self-reliant village assemblies, exemplified by the Uttaramerur inscription's kudavolai ballot-pot election system. (THINK ABOUT IT) Students discuss how dividing a kingdom into provinces, districts, and villages helped rulers manage empires and what similarities exist with governance in India today.

Ethics: The Foundation of Law and Life
This section explores the roots of ethical thought in early Indian texts such as the Vedas, epics, and the Bhagavad Gītā, which emphasise knowledge, faith, virtue, and an ethical way of life. It introduces the principle of samatva (sameness)—that all bodies are of the same matter and all consciousness a manifestation of one supreme consciousness—and ṛita, the all-pervading cosmic order of harmony and moral balance described in the Ṛig Veda. It explains dharma as duty, righteousness, and moral conduct (not religion), with its Buddhist equivalent dhamma, and derives the word from the Sanskrit root dhri, quoting the Mahābhārata that dharma is that which upholds all beings. It links politics and ethics from the Mauryas to the Cholas—Aśhoka's edicts on moral conduct and non-violence, the Uttaramerur inscription's requirement that candidates have honest earnings, and works such as Kāmandaka's Nītisāra and Bāṇabhaṭa's Kādambarī.

Social Structures and Everyday Life — Varṇa and Jāti
This section revisits the fourfold varṇa system—brāhmaṇas, kṣhatriyas, vaiśhyas, and śhūdras—and stresses that early Vedic texts do not indicate fixed social status by birth, with identity shaped by ethnicity, region, gotra, language, and especially occupation. It notes that the earliest reference to the four categories appears in the Puruṣhasūkta of the Ṛig Veda and that the varṇas were a functional division valuing knowledge, then political power, then wealth, citing the Buddhist Sutta Nipāta that a brāhmaṇa is such by deeds, not birth. It explains how jāti later emerged as a distinct structure through intermarriage, endogamy, and territorial difference, with the number of jātis growing without limit while the varṇas remained four. (LET'S EXPLORE) Students read the Ṛig Veda verse (9.112.3) describing a family of diverse occupations and, thinking like historians, examine the evidence, interpretation, and limits of the source before comparing their group's reading with another's.

Social Mobility
This section argues that varṇa and jāti were not always rigid, pointing to considerable social and occupational mobility. It notes that several ruling dynasties—the Nandas, Mauryas, Śhuṅgas, Satavāhanas, Vākāṭakas, Guptas, and Puṣhyabhūtis—came from diverse social backgrounds, and cites the Mandsaur Stone Inscription (473 CE) recording a guild of silk weavers skilled also in archery and astrology, and the Karitalai copper plates recording brāhmaṇas as land managers. It draws on Sangam texts such as the Tolkappiyam and Pattinappalai to show a society organised more by occupation and economic function—Arasar, Vanigar, Velar, Antanar, merchants, salt-makers, fishermen, artisans—than by rigid varṇa classification.

Family and Society
This section describes the kula (family) as the smallest social unit, connected through the grāma (village), the viśha headed by a viśhapati, and the jana protected by the rājā, showing the family's inseparability from the wider polity. It explains the concept of gotra as a patrilineal lineage traced to a Vedic ṛiṣhi that regulated marriage, and the framework of the four āśhramas—brahmacharya, gṛihastha, vānaprastha, and saṁnyāsa—each with its own dharma (Fig. 5.8). It also introduces the ṣhoḍaśha saṁskāras, the sixteen rites of passage marking life's key moments, and the four puruṣhārthas or goals of life—dharma, artha, kāma, and mokṣha—noting that artha should be guided by dharma.

Role of Women
This section presents the Vedic period as one in which women often held a respected position, participating in scholarly learning and rituals, chariot races, and the sabhā, with several Ṛig Vedic hymns attributed to women sages such as Apālā, Viśhvavārā, Ghoṣhā, and Lopāmudrā, and revered goddesses such as Uṣha and Aditi. It quotes the Manu-smṛiti (3.56) on honouring women, while acknowledging that women's position fluctuated and at times declined as conditions changed. It gives later examples of women's continuing roles—educated female characters in Gupta–Vākāṭaka literature, the regent Prabhāvatī Gupta who issued land grants, Sangam women in agrarian and economic life, poetesses such as Avvaiyar, and the Chola queen Sembiyan Mahādevī who patronised temples.

Religious Life and the Emergence of Bhakti
This section describes the non-hierarchical Vedic pantheon, in which different deities are praised as supreme and each is linked to an element of nature, invoked through yajñas offered to the sacred fire, and connects these practices to living festivals such as Chhath and Makar Sankranti and to an early ethic of respect for nature. It traces the rise of renunciants around the mid-first millennium BCE—parivrājakas, bhikṣhus, and śhramaṇas, including Gautama Buddha and Mahāvīra—and the evolution of worship toward personal deities such as Viṣhṇu, Śhiva, and Śhakti. It introduces Bhakti as a direct, ritual-free path accessible to all regardless of class or gender, gaining organised prominence in the sixth-century Tamil region through the twelve Āḻvārs (devotees of Viṣhṇu) and the sixty-three Nāyanmārs (devotees of Śhiva).

The Quest for Knowledge
This section portrays early Indian education as holistic, cultivating inner values such as truth, humility, self-control, and reverence for all beings, and grounded in dharma and the sacred guru-śhiṣhya paramparā, with the teacher's home as the gurukula and the student treated as family. It describes a broad curriculum—the Vedas, grammar, logic, philosophy, ethics, mathematics, science, medicine, astronomy, arts, crafts, music, and martial arts—alongside yoga and meditation, and surveys centres of higher learning such as Takṣhaśhilā, Nālandā, Vallabhī, and Vikramśhila (Figs. 5.10, 5.11) that attracted students from across and beyond India. The named content-block Literary Heritage of Early India lists key works, including Pāṇini's Aṣhṭādhyāyī and other foundational Sanskrit grammar texts, the Smṛiti literature on dharma and law, the medical Charaka and Suśhruta Saṁhitās, Kālidāsa's poetry, and the Tamil Tirukkural, Silappadikāram, Maṇimēkalai, and Sangam corpus. (LET'S EXPLORE) Students compare their own school life with that of a student in an early Indian gurukula—routine, subjects, teacher relationships, discipline, and living arrangements—and weigh the advantages and challenges of each.

Economy — Agriculture and Land Revenue
This section describes the elaborate Mauryan management of agriculture, with village land divided into individual holdings and common pastures and groves, state-encouraged forest clearing alongside protected forests, and a basic land tax generally fixed at one-sixth of produce. It notes Kauṭilya's classification of land types for revenue assessment and the wide range of crops—rice, pulses, wheat, linseed, mustard, saffron, sugarcane, vegetables, and fruits. It traces continuity into the post-Mauryan period through the Milindapañho's eight stages of agricultural operation, expanded cotton cultivation in the Deccan's black soil, the Amarakoṣha's chapter on crops, and Sangam accounts of southern fertility and produce such as jackfruit, pepper, turmeric, ragi, and sugarcane.

Irrigation
This section stresses agriculture's dependence on irrigation and the importance placed on reservoirs, canals, and dams. It returns to the Junagadh inscription of Rudradaman I to note that Puṣhyagupta, a governor of Chandragupta Maurya, built a dam on Sudarshana Lake near Girnar, highlighting the Mauryan state's role in irrigation as a source of revenue. (LET'S RECALL) Students recall the Grand Anicut (Kallanai) built by Karikala Chola, an irrigation structure repaired over many centuries and still in use today. (THINK ABOUT IT) Students discuss why irrigation structures such as dams and canals were considered important enough to be recorded in inscriptions.

Trade: Trade Routes and Ports
This section presents the expansion of trade and commerce as a major feature of the period, enabled by well-structured states, citing the Arthaśhāstra on Magadha's trade in textiles, gems, coral, pearls, metals, and state-regulated salt, and on state protection of trade routes and consumers. It describes the two great land routes—Dakṣhiṇāpatha and Uttarāpatha—emerging by about the sixth century BCE and linking inland regions to coastal ports, and the growth of ports such as Muziris, Kāveripaṭṭinam, Arikameḍu, and Masulipaṭnam (Fig. 5.12), with maritime trade across the Indian Ocean and expanding Roman trade in the early centuries CE. (LET'S RECALL) Students recall from the Silappadikāram the two Tamil routes linking Kanchipuram to Poompuhar and to Kanyakumari.

Guilds
This section explains guilds (śhreṇīs) as associations of traders, artisans, and merchants in the same occupation that grew in importance after the rise of the mahājanapadas and the introduction of silver punch-marked coins. It notes the Jātaka literature's reference to eighteen types of guilds, their regulation of quality and prices, and their guild courts enforcing professional discipline, as well as their functioning as banks, financiers, and trustees, illustrated by the Nāśhik cave inscription of Nahapāna and Uśhavadāta recording interest-bearing endowments to guilds. It closes the chapter's economic survey with the wide range of textiles—silk, cotton, wool, and linen—produced at centres such as Mathurā, Kāśhī, and Kāmarūpa, and the Sanchi ivory workers' guild that carved the Great Stupa's gateways, reflecting guilds' role in both craft and religious patronage.
