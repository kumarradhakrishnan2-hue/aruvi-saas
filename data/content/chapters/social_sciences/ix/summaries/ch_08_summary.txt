Chapter 8: Building Blocks in Economics: The Problem of Choice

The Big Questions
This opening section frames the chapter through everyday dilemmas—spending pocket money on snacks or saving for shoes, sharing five library copies among twenty students, a farmer choosing a crop, an enterprise choosing between more labour or capital, a government choosing between highways and hospitals—and argues that these are not random questions but examples of economic choices that individuals, enterprises, and governments must make. It distinguishes needs (essentials like food, water, and shelter) from wants (like gadgets, vacations, or luxury items), and stresses that human wants are unlimited and keep changing, as when people upgrade from a bicycle to a motorbike and then to a car (Fig. 8.1). The margin introduces the term market as a place, physical or virtual, where buying and selling of products and services takes place. (LET'S EXPLORE) Students list three things their parents bought this month, classify them into needs or wants, and discuss whether having too many wants may create problems.

Choices and Limited Resources
This section explains that resources—the factors used to produce goods and services, whether natural (like water and coal) or human-made (like capital and technology)—are limited in quantity yet can be put to many alternative uses, illustrated by the alternative uses of steel in medical equipment, aircraft manufacturing, and refrigerator manufacturing (Fig. 8.2). It introduces opportunity cost as the value of what is given up when one alternative is chosen over another, developed through a farmer choosing between growing barley and wheat on limited land. Using a table of five combinations (A to E) of barley and wheat, it derives the Production Possibility Curve (PPC), a downward-sloping curve showing the trade-off between the two crops and the maximum output achievable through efficient, waste-free use of resources, which aids better planning and decision-making by enterprises and governments (Fig. 8.3). (LET'S EXPLORE) Students ask their parents how they make everyday purchase choices and identify the opportunity cost of a decision, and consider whether time is a scarce resource and how they decide to spend it.

What does Economics Deal with?
This section traces the word economics to the Greek oikonomia—from oikos ('household') and nemein ('management')—so that economics originally meant household management, extended here to how families and nations plan the efficient use of limited resources. It defines the discipline of Economics as the study of how choices are made by optimising the use of limited resources to satisfy needs and wants, explaining how economic entities—consumers, producers, governments, and financial institutions—interact through wages, distribution of wealth, price determination, investment, and government policy and trade. It stresses that good decisions rely on data and analysis rather than guesswork: families allocate money across essential items, non-essential items, and savings; governments plan expenditure from tax revenue; and enterprises study market trends, while economists analyse alternatives, opportunity costs, and outcomes using sources such as economic surveys and company financial statements. The margins define economy, surveys, data, and economic entities, and Fig. 8.4 sets out the scope of economists' work across policymaking, business consulting, research and education, and finance.

Economic Survey: A Report on Indian Economy
This named content-block describes the Economic Survey of India as an important annual document prepared by the Ministry of Finance and presented in Parliament before the Union Budget. It reviews the country's economic performance over the past year and analyses sectors such as agriculture, industry, and services, along with areas like employment, inflation, education, health, and infrastructure, and discusses future challenges and opportunities. It explains that the Survey helps people understand how the Indian economy is performing, acts as a blueprint for the upcoming Union Budget by giving policymakers crucial insights, and presents economic data clearly for citizens, pointing readers to the latest edition at the India Budget website.

Key Questions in Economics
This section explains that any mismatch between unlimited wants and limited resources creates scarcity, which forces choices, giving rise to three key questions economics seeks to address—what to produce, how to produce, and for whom to produce (Fig. 8.5).

What to Produce and for Whom?
This sub-section explains that deciding what to produce concerns which goods and services, and in what quantities, should be produced over a given period, illustrated by the choice between water-intensive crops like sugarcane and paddy and drought-resistant crops like millets and pulses—where the opportunity cost of sugarcane's high profits is the forgone saving of water and improvement of soil health, reflecting a trade-off between short-term gains and long-term sustainability. The question of for whom to produce concerns the purpose of goods, how they are distributed, and who benefits, since consumers differ in needs, income, tastes, and lifestyles; this is shown through shoes made for different groups—simple durable school shoes for students, comfortable formal office-wear shoes for professionals, lightweight sports shoes for athletes, and affordable casual shoes or slippers for daily use—and through how the target consumer shapes the materials used, such as leather for high-income office-goers versus rubber or synthetic for affordable, durable footwear. (THINK ABOUT IT) Students discuss whether the government should allocate more funds to healthcare and education or to defence and space exploration, and why.

How to Produce?
This sub-section explains that after deciding what to produce, producers must decide how—which methods, resources, and technologies to use—by selecting the right mix of the factors of production: land, labour, capital, and technology (Fig. 8.6). It contrasts labour-intensive production (more workers, less machinery, typical of agriculture and handicrafts) with capital-intensive production (more machines and technology, typical of steel and automobile manufacturing). Using the example of a garment manufacturer, it shows that the choice of technique depends on the cost of capital, the level of technology available, the nature of the product (customised designer clothes need skilled labour while mass-produced garments suit machines), the relative cost and availability of labour, and government laws and regulations such as labour laws or incentives for machinery.

Economic Systems and How Choices are Made
This section explains that the answers to the three key questions depend on how the resources used to produce goods and services are organised and who controls decision-making about them, defining the economic system of a country as the system that governs the mechanisms for the production, consumption, and distribution of goods, services, and resources. It states that the three kinds of economic systems are explained in the pages that follow.

Planned Economy
This section describes a planned economy as one in which a central planning authority of the government, such as a planning commission, makes all major economic decisions—what and how much to produce, how, for whom, and at what prices—with the government owning most resources and sectors like land, factories, banks, and transport. It explains that limited private ownership means enterprises follow the central authority's targets rather than market demand and are heavily regulated through permits and licenses, which restricts competition and reduces the motivation to improve quality or innovate. Examples given are the former Soviet Union, North Korea, and Cuba, and the margin defines the planned economy as one where the allocation of resources and prices are determined by the government.

Market Economy
This section describes a market economy as one in which the questions of what, how, and how much to produce are settled mainly by the forces of demand and supply with little government intervention, the government acting like a referee in a football match that ensures safety and law and order without controlling prices or production. It explains that ownership of factories, shops, land, and other resources largely rests with individuals and private companies, and that many producers offering similar products encourages better quality, lower prices, and innovation, with examples including the United States of America, Japan, and Hong Kong—though governments still play an important role even in these economies (Fig. 8.7). The margin defines the market economy as one where resources and prices are determined primarily by market forces, with the government providing public goods and physical infrastructure. (LET'S EXPLORE) Students give their opinion on whether the government should completely stay out of enterprise decisions and think of an example where government action helped or harmed an industry or sector.

Mixed Economy
This section describes a mixed economy as one that combines features of both market and planned economies, in which private individuals, enterprises, and the government all play important roles, and large public sector companies operate alongside private ownership under some degree of government regulation (Fig. 8.8). It notes that most real economies are mixed, giving examples such as India (post-1991), China (post-1978), Germany, and Sweden, and observing that even market economies like the United States of America and Singapore have significant government involvement. The margins define the mixed economy and public goods (available to all without exclusion, such as parks, roads, police services, street lights, and basic education). A DON'T MISS OUT box traces how India's own system evolved: a state-led, planned-style approach after Independence, with the government controlling industries, allocating resources, and dominating key sectors like banking, transport, and heavy industry, until the serious economic difficulties of 1991 prompted major reforms that reduced regulation, encouraged private enterprise, opened the economy to global trade and investment, and shifted India towards a more market-oriented system while retaining an important role for the government. The chapter closes by drawing together its argument that economics is essentially about making choices in a world of scarce resources and unlimited wants, that these choices are reflected in the three key questions, and that different economic systems answer them differently while most modern economies combine market and planned elements.
