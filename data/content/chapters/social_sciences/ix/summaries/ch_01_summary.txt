Chapter 01: Understanding Social Science

As students move from Grades 6-8 into Grade 9, the chapter pauses to ask what 'Social Science' truly means and why it matters. It defines Social Science as the systematic study of human society, explaining not only what happened or where things are located but why events occur, how people live together, how environments influence life, how governments function, how economies operate, and how the past and present together shape the world. It contrasts Social Science with the natural sciences: while Physics, Chemistry, and Biology study the natural world, Social Science focuses on society, institutions, cultures, and human interactions, and on why things happen and how different aspects of life are interconnected.

Social Science in Everyday Life
This section grounds the discipline in a single ordinary day, showing how a house, food, roads, schooling, and electricity each depend on systems of governance, economic production, social cooperation, and the natural environment. It then widens the lens with a set of "why" and "how" questions—why some people live in crowded cities and others in scattered villages, why communities speak different languages, why regions depend on farming, industry, or trade, how governments make decisions affecting millions, and why some regions are more prone to floods or better suited to agriculture. The section argues that society does not function by chance but is shaped by history, geography, institutions, resources, and human choices, which Social Science explores through careful observation, evidence, and logical reasoning. (LET'S ANALYSE) Students identify a change in their locality over the past five years relating to transport, housing, education, technology, or the environment, discuss what changed and what caused it, and present a short report.

Understanding Society through Time and Traditions
This section traces how society has changed greatly over thousands of years, as early communities that depended directly on nature learnt to grow crops, domesticate animals, build settlements, develop tools, exchange goods, and organise governance, so that villages grew into towns and cities. It roots the spirit of inquiry in India's knowledge traditions of discussion, questioning, and logical reasoning, illustrating this with the concept of the Pañchamahābhūtas (the five great elements) that presents the natural world as an interconnected system, and with vasudhaiva kuṭumbakam ('the world is one family') expressing the interdependence of human societies. It links early Indian reflection on governance to public welfare and ethical responsibility through the Arthaśhāstra attributed to Kauṭilya, composed about 2,300 years ago, which examined administration, economic management, taxation, and the duties of rulers. The section concludes that the effort to understand society through observation and reflection has a long history that modern Social Science continues, with social scientists observing lives, conducting interviews and surveys, examining documents, and comparing times and places to build evidence-based explanations of what endures and what changes.

Social Science as a Study of Disciplines
This section argues that human society is too complex for any single field, using the example of a drought that simultaneously affects crops (environment), incomes (economy), relief measures (politics), migration (society), and coping traditions (culture). It establishes that Social Science is not one subject but a group of related disciplines, and that in Grades 9-10 it draws primarily from four core disciplines—Geography, History, Political Science, and Economics—while Sociology, Philosophy, Anthropology, and Psychology form part of the broader family to be developed in higher grades. It briefly characterises each of the four core disciplines in one line each, setting up the detailed treatments that follow.

Geography
This section defines Geography as the study of the location and distribution of places, objects, materials, and people, and of the relationships between human societies and their surroundings, examining both the physical features of the Earth's surface and the human communities that inhabit it. It presents Geography as viewing the world as a system of interdependencies, integrating spatial perspectives (location and its significance) with temporal perspectives (change over time), and drawing on both natural sciences and social sciences—for instance to explain why India has been a hub of global interaction and how its long coastline eased contact with Africa, Europe, and Southeast Asia. It lists the discipline's tools—maps, globes, atlases, Geographical Information System (GIS), and infographics—and previews the two-year study of Earth-shaping processes, landforms, atmosphere and climate, oceans, biomes, India's biosphere reserves, geospatial technologies, and regional life. (LET'S EXPLORE) Students use NCERT's School Bhuvan portal to access and map their own village or city.

History
This section defines History as the study of the human past through which societies understand people's experiences, values, and changes over time, noting multiple ways to interpret the past, including India's itihāsa-purāṇa tradition that transmits cultural memory and values through stories. It distinguishes historical traditions that emphasise moral and philosophical insight from those offering documentary verification, and describes how modern historiography increasingly relies on empirical evidence and tools such as human genetics, carbon-14 dating, and archaeology. The section surveys the range of sources historians use—literary and archaeological sources, and specifically epigraphic sources (inscriptions on stone, metal, or rock) and numismatic sources (coins and currency)—illustrated by figures of manuscripts, terracotta figurines, sculptures, Brahmi and Kannada inscriptions, and coins of Samudragupta and the Mughals. It previews the two-year journey through early human history, developments in India from early civilisation to the present, and world-history landmarks including the Greco-Roman world, the Reformation, Renaissance, Enlightenment, Industrial Revolution, and colonialism with its anti-colonial struggles.

Political Science
This section defines Political Science as the study of governance—how and why power is distributed, decisions are made, and policies implemented—covering constitutions, governments, State institutions, social movements, nation building, and foreign policy, and it uses India's Panchayati Raj system as an example of grassroots democracy. It shows that the study of politics in India began early and was linked to dharma (moral duty), artha (economic well-being), and rājadharma (the duties of the ruler), with texts such as the Vedas, Upaniṣads, Purāṇas, Mahābhārata, and Śhukranīti discussing justice, authority, and social order, and the Arthaśhāstra standing out as a foundational text on politics and administration. It emphasises that Indian political thought was holistic, linking politics with economics, social life, morality, and defence, and treating power as a responsibility rather than a privilege. The section previews the two-year study of democracy, elections, authority, civil society, governance and public policy, and national security.

Economics
This section defines Economics as the study of how individuals and societies decide to use limited resources to meet needs, examining how goods and services are produced, exchanged, and distributed, and how decisions are made by consumers, producers, and governments balancing goals such as growth and stability or efficiency and fairness. It presents economics as concerned not only with numbers and markets but with well-being, equity, and justice. It sketches India's economic history—once one of the world's leading economies and a centre of trade, industry, and maritime activity, disrupted by colonial rule into poverty and famine, then rebuilt after independence with progress in infrastructure, education, technology, poverty reduction, and life expectancy—while noting remaining challenges of raising incomes and spreading growth's benefits. It frames economic development as a central national goal and previews the study of the Indian economy, markets and prices, financial management and entrepreneurship, economic growth and GDP, international trade, and the Union Budget.

Why Should We Study Social Science?
This section answers how the knowledge helps in daily life, explaining that homes, water, roads, schools, markets, and digital spaces are all systems created and managed by society that Social Science helps us understand. It shows how the discipline explains social diversity—differences of language, custom, and occupation—as outcomes of geographical, historical, cultural, and economic factors, building respect and cooperation while recognising the underlying unity of Indian culture. It connects Social Science to active democratic citizenship, arguing that understanding how governments function prepares people to participate responsibly, and that the habit of careful questioning and reasoning helps address shared challenges such as environmental protection, public health, employment, and urban growth. It closes by stressing that Social Science connects past, present, and future, equipping citizens to make wiser choices.

The Future of Social Science
This section argues that as societies change rapidly—through new technologies, expanding cities, environmental concerns, migration, and global connections—the importance of Social Science grows, and that understanding these changes requires the same skills the chapter has introduced: observation, questioning, examining evidence, and recognising connections. It anticipates that Social Science will help address complex issues such as climate change, sustainable development, social harmony, and equitable use of resources, and guide the responsible use of new technologies. It concludes that Social Science prepares learners not only to understand the world but to help shape it as informed citizens rooted in cultural traditions, and frames the study as a journey from understanding how societies evolved, to how they function, to how they can improve.

Secondary-Stage Social Science: Your Two-Year Learning Journey (Grades 9-10)
This closing section presents Social Science as a window into the world rather than merely a school subject, previewing a two-year exploration of how historical events shaped the modern world, how geography influences life and economic activity, how political and democratic institutions function, and how economies organise production, distribution, and development. It highlights contemporary themes—environmental sustainability, social diversity, citizens' rights and responsibilities, and the impact of technology and global connections—and the skills of analysing evidence and comparing perspectives to see past, present, and future as linked. It explains that the Grade 9 textbook gives a broad and balanced introduction to the four disciplines, each offering a distinct perspective, and affirms that Social Science is about understanding people, places, society, culture, and power rather than memorising dates, maps, or definitions, encouraging learners to think independently and act as responsible citizens.
