Chapter 09: The Price Puzzle: What Drives the Market

The chapter opens with the four Big Questions that frame the enquiry: what factors influence the demand for and supply of goods and services, how prices are determined through demand–supply interactions, what market equilibrium is and whether it exists in the real world, and how and why governments intervene in markets. Using everyday puzzles — mangoes halving in price, vegetables costing more in the morning than the evening, a flight seat priced at ₹3,000 one day and ₹9,000 the next, seasonal discounts — it recalls the Grade 7 chapter 'Understanding Markets' and sets up the idea that prices are never random but are driven by two constant forces, demand and supply. The chapter promises to explore demand, supply, and price determination and to show the outcomes of their interplay in real situations.

Demand introduces demand as the quantity of a product people are willing and able to buy at a particular price, given their needs, preferences, season, trend, and income; it stresses that demand is willingness backed by ability, or purchasing power, not mere desire. The Law of Demand captures the inverse relationship between price and quantity demanded — as price rises quantity demanded falls, and as price falls it rises. This is illustrated through Srivalli, who buys 1 kg of mangoes at ₹150, 2 kg at ₹100, and 3 kg at ₹50, giving an individual demand schedule that, plotted graphically, becomes the downward-sloping individual demand curve (points A, B, C connected as line DD'). Market demand is then defined as the sum of all individual demands, shown by adding the schedules of Srivalli, Alex, and Israt (Table 9.1) to derive market demand QD and the flatter market demand curve DmDm' (Fig. 9.3); a note explains that the market curve is flatter because aggregating many consumers produces a larger total quantity response to the same price change.

Other Determinants of Demand explains that demand can shift even when price is unchanged, opening with the example of long queues for a newly launched smartphone. Price of related goods distinguishes two types: substitute goods (like tea and coffee, or mangoes and bananas), where a rise in one good's price increases demand for the other, and complementary goods (like smartphones and earphones, printers and cartridges, movie tickets and popcorn), which are used together so a change affecting one shifts demand for the other. Income of the consumer notes that higher household income lets people buy more or higher-quality products and raises confidence to spend, so quantity demanded rises even at constant prices. Taste and preference of the buyer explains that individual tastes shape demand (Srivalli prefers mangoes over cheaper oranges), and that the size and composition of a nation's population also matter — India's large population drives domestic demand and its age composition shapes demand for different goods such as sports, formal, or orthopaedic shoes. (THINK ABOUT IT) Students consider why eating successive mangoes gives less satisfaction, learning the principle of diminishing marginal utility — as additional utility from a good falls, willingness to pay and demand also fall. Seasonality shows that demand for goods such as school books, festive sweets, or winter clothing changes with weather, festivals, and cultural habits rather than price. Future price expectations explains that expected future prices affect current demand: if consumers expect prices to fall they postpone buying (as before festival sales), and if they expect prices to rise they buy immediately. (LET'S EXPLORE) Students build their own notebook demand schedule at different prices, identifying the price at which they would buy most and stop buying, and ask family members whether they postponed or advanced any purchase because of expected price changes.

Supply defines supply as the quantity of a product sellers are willing and able to offer at a particular price, and states the Law of Supply — the direct relationship where quantity supplied rises with price because higher prices raise profitability and attract new firms. Individual supply is shown through seller A's schedule (1 kg at ₹50, 2 kg at ₹100, 3 kg at ₹150), giving an upward-sloping supply curve (Fig. 9.4), and the mango-season example links low early-season supply to high prices and mid-season abundance to falling prices. Market supply is the sum of all individual supplies, derived by combining sellers A, B, and C (Table 9.2) to obtain QS and the market supply curve SmSm' (Fig. 9.5).

Other Determinants of Supply lists factors that shift supply apart from price. Price of related goods explains that a supplier's choice depends on the profitability of alternatives, illustrated by a farmer switching from low-priced wheat to high-priced chickpeas (Fig. 9.6). Number of sellers in the market notes that more sellers raise market supply and push prices down, while fewer sellers reduce supply and raise prices. Technology explains that improvements such as drip irrigation, weather sensors, and cold storage lower production costs and increase supply. Future expectations shows that suppliers produce more when they anticipate a demand boom and hold back or cut production when they expect lower demand, as with potato wholesalers withholding stock to sell later at higher prices. (LET'S EXPLORE) Students discuss, using diverse goods and services, what happens to supply when input costs change, an alternate input is discovered, resources are depleted, weather changes, or a disaster occurs.

Market Equilibrium establishes that prices are set by negotiation between what buyers will pay and what sellers will accept. Using Table 9.3, it shows excess demand at a low price (₹40) and excess supply at a high price (₹150), with equilibrium at ₹100 where quantity demanded equals quantity supplied (12 kg) and the market is 'cleared' with neither shortage nor surplus. (LET'S ANALYSE) Students plot the demand and supply curves at ₹40, ₹100, and ₹150 from Table 9.3, mark excess demand and supply, and consider how equilibrium could be reached.

Does Market Equilibrium Exist in the Real World? argues that although equilibrium is the intersection of demand and supply in theory (point E in Fig. 9.7), real markets are dynamic — technology, wages, interest rates, wars, political events, pandemics, weather, and disasters constantly shift demand and supply, so equilibrium is never stable and the market is always adjusting toward a new one. The COVID-19 face-mask example shows demand surging in 2020, supply lagging and prices rising, then prices falling as suppliers adjusted and demand later returned to pre-pandemic levels.

Tariffs by hotels: An example of dynamic markets illustrates dynamic pricing through a 100-room Goa hotel whose tariff ranges from ₹1,500 on an off-season weekday to ₹8,000 on a tourist-season weekend and ₹25,000 on New Year's Eve, and which may cut tariffs by 40 per cent overnight when a booking is cancelled. It explains that tariffs depend on booking speed, nearby hotels' rates, local events, weather forecasts, days before arrival, and past trends, and links sustainability concerns — fast fashion, overfishing, groundwater overuse — to future supply and equilibrium. (THINK ABOUT IT) Students give another real-life example where prices change frequently and explain why.

Role of Government in the Economy notes that India, now the fourth-largest economy, is a market-based but regulated economy where markets do not always work fairly because they allocate by willingness and ability to pay, which can make essentials like medicines inaccessible; hence government acts for equity and welfare. Regulation of Unfair Practices explains price ceilings (maximum prices for essentials like medicines), price floors (a minimum wage set above equilibrium), and the regulation of monopolies, where a single or few dominant sellers charge higher prices, restrict supply, and lower quality; it lists regulators including the RBI, the Central Consumer Protection Authority, TRAI, and SEBI, and gives the COVID-19 sanitiser case where hoarding and black-marketing led the government to cap prices under the Essential Commodities Act, 1955. (THINK ABOUT IT) Students share an example of the government fixing prices or wages — such as bus fares, medicines, or minimum wages — and why it was done. Provision of Public Goods explains that goods such as roads, bridges, parks, streetlighting, national defence, sanitation, and drainage are provided by government because they benefit all but generate no direct profit, illustrated by the neighbourhood park that stays unbuilt because families expect to free-ride on others' contributions. (LET'S EXPLORE) Students list two government-provided goods or services, then examine one — who benefits, why a private company would find it hard to provide, and what problems would arise if the government stopped providing it. Limitations of Government Intervention warns that excessive intervention can harm markets through price distortions and reduced producer incentives (a wheat price capped below market level cutting production and causing shortages), compliance burdens (licences, permits, and clearances that hamper ease of doing business and discourage small enterprises), and discouraged innovation and entrepreneurship (price controls reducing incentives to invest in better seeds, irrigation, or technology). (LET'S RECALL) Recalling the 'Democracy' chapter, students consider how a democratic government should decide when and how much to intervene in markets and whose voices — consumers, producers, workers, or others — it should weigh.

The chapter closes by tying these ideas together: understanding economic systems reveals the logic behind the prices people pay, the jobs they do, and the policies governments implement, and gives tools to think critically and make informed decisions. It reframes markets not as machines with fixed equilibria but as dynamic systems that adapt and respond, urging students, on noticing any price change, to ask whether supply is changing, demand is shifting, the market is moving toward or away from equilibrium, and whether government intervention is helping or hurting.
