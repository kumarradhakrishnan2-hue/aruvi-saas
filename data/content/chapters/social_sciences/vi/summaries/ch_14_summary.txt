Chapter 14: Economic Activities Around Us

Introduction

Chapter 14 builds on the distinction between economic and non-economic activities introduced in Chapter 13, focusing on how economic activities — those that create monetary value — are classified and what differentiates them. The chapter raises three guiding questions: how economic activities are classified, what differentiates activities grouped into sectors, and how the three sectors are interconnected. The introductory section notes that economic activities have grown vastly over time, from early occupations such as agriculture, livestock rearing, and pottery, to modern activities such as software development, manufacturing electronics, and providing financial services.

The Classification of Economic Activities into Economic Sectors

This section explains that economic activities sharing similar characteristics are grouped into broader categories called economic sectors. The chapter identifies three main types: the primary sector, the secondary sector, and the tertiary sector. An illustrated diagram maps out representative activities under each category — agriculture, mining, fishing, raising livestock, and forestry under the primary sector; construction, manufacturing, water supply, solar power, and electricity production under the secondary sector; and healthcare, trade and logistics, communication, banking, and transportation under the tertiary sector. The term economic sectors is defined as broad groups that include various activities contributing to a nation's economic prosperity.

A. Primary activities

Primary activities are those economic activities in which people are directly dependent on nature to produce goods. The section defines the primary sector as the group of activities involving extraction of raw materials directly from nature. Examples given include cultivation of grains and vegetables from agricultural farms, collecting wood from forests, extracting coal from mines, obtaining fish from fisheries, and collecting eggs from poultry farms. The most common primary activities identified are agriculture, mining, fishing, raising livestock, and forestry. The section contains a student activity ("Think About It") asking learners to recall primary activities they have observed and to identify the natural resources involved.

B. Secondary activities

Secondary activities are defined as economic activities in which people are dependent on outputs of the primary sector and transform them to produce goods. The secondary sector includes construction of buildings and roads, provision of utilities such as water, electricity, and gas, and the manufacturing of products in factories that process raw materials into forms that can be sold or consumed. Examples include processing grains into flour in mills, extracting oil from groundnut, processing tea leaves, converting wood into furniture and paper, making clothing from cotton, and producing automobiles from steel derived from iron ore. The section includes a "Don't Miss Out" data box showing automobile production statistics in India in 2022 — 45 lakh passenger vehicles, 10.3 lakh commercial vehicles, 8.6 lakh three-wheelers, and 2 crore two-wheelers — and a student activity ("Let's Explore") inviting learners to name two additional secondary sector activities.

C. Tertiary activities

Tertiary activities are defined as all those economic activities that provide support to people involved in primary and secondary activities. This sector, also called the service sector, covers services that are often invisible yet essential. Examples include truck drivers transporting farm produce, vendors selling produce to households, and professionals such as doctors, nurses, teachers, lawyers, and pilots providing specialist services. The section also mentions technicians who repair electronic items, mechanics who service vehicles, and electricians who maintain electricity supply. Communication services via mobile and internet, software development, and services at hotels, restaurants, banks, schools, hospitals, airports, shops, and warehouses are all cited as tertiary sector activities. Two key terms are defined: tertiary sector (the group of activities involving provision of services that complement both primary and secondary sectors) and warehouses (large buildings used to store products before sale).

Interdependence Among Sectors

This section explains that all three economic sectors work together in the process of converting natural raw materials into finished products for final consumption. It introduces the concept through two extended examples. The first is a case study of the AMUL dairy cooperative in Anand district, Gujarat, used to illustrate how primary, secondary, and tertiary activities interlock. The second example, presented through a diagram (Fig. 14.1), traces the journey of wood pulp through paper manufacturing to the production and sale of textbooks. The section emphasises that none of these end products would be possible without all three sectors working in concert.

Dairy cooperative: from farm to plate

This subsection narrates the history of Anand Milk Union Limited (AMUL) to make the interdependence of the three sectors concrete. In the early 1940s, farmers in Anand district sold milk to nearby villages, depending on middlemen who bought milk cheaply and resold it at a profit, leaving farmers exploited. On the advice of Sardar Vallabhbhai Patel, farmers formed a cooperative — a group of people who come together voluntarily to meet economic and social needs, own the organisation collectively, and make decisions as members. AMUL was set up in 1946 under the leadership of Tribhuvandas Patel and Dr. Varghese Kurien. The cooperative gave farmers, including women, control over production, pasteurisation (the process of preserving milk by heating to kill harmful bacteria), and sale of milk, eliminating the need for middlemen. As the cooperative grew, it established factories to produce butter, milk powder, ghee, cheese, and other products — a secondary sector activity. AMUL then transported and sold its products through lorries, trucks, railways, air, and shipping services and through retail stores across Gujarat and other states, and even exported products internationally — tertiary activities. The section notes other regional milk cooperatives such as Nandini (Karnataka), Mother Dairy (Delhi-NCR), Aavin (Tamil Nadu), Vijaya (Andhra Pradesh), Kevi (Nagaland), Sudha (Bihar), and Verka (Punjab). The subsection contains a student activity asking learners to list economic activities in their neighbourhood and classify them as primary, secondary, or tertiary, drawing arrows to show interdependence.
