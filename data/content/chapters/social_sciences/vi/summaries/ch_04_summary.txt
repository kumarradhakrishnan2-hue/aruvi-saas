Chapter 4: Timeline and Sources of History

How Do We Learn About the Past?

The chapter opens by situating history as the study of the human past and inviting students to reflect on their own earliest memories as a personal form of history. It establishes that the Earth has an enormously long history, of which Homo sapiens occupy only the most recent fraction. A visual timeline of life on Earth (Fig. 4.1) illustrates major stages from the formation of the planet through the appearance of the first cells, bacteria, plants, fish, reptiles, mammals and finally humans, expressed in billions, millions and years. The section introduces four types of scholars who study this past: geologists, who examine the physical features of the Earth such as soil, stones, hills and rivers; palaeontologists, who study fossil remains of plants, animals and humans from millions of years ago; anthropologists, who study human societies and cultures from the oldest times to the present; and archaeologists, who study the past by excavating objects that people, plants and animals left behind — tools, pots, beads, figurines, burnt grains and parts of structures. The term fossil is defined as impressions of footprints or parts of plants or animals preserved within layers of soil or rocks. The section contains a "Think About It" student activity.

How Is Time Measured in History?

This section explains that different societies have devised their own systems for measuring time, often anchored to significant events such as a ruler's birth or the start of a reign. It introduces the Gregorian calendar as the most widely used calendar globally, while noting that Hindu, Muslim, Jewish, Chinese and other calendars continue to be used alongside it for festivals and auspicious occasions. The section defines CE (Common Era) as the modern replacement for AD, both counting years forward from the conventional date of Jesus Christ's birth; and BCE (Before Common Era) as the replacement for BC, counting years backward from that date. It gives concrete examples: 1947 CE for India's independence and 560 BCE as the approximate birth year of Gautama Buddha. A "Let's Explore" student activity guides students to recognise that the Gregorian calendar has no year zero (1 BCE is immediately followed by 1 CE), and therefore the formula for calculating years between a BCE and a CE date is to add them and subtract 1. A second feature box, "Don't Miss Out", introduces the terms century (any period of 100 years, counted from 1 CE forward or 1 BCE backward) and millennium (any period of 1,000 years, counted in the same way). The section notes that the current year falls in the 21st century CE and the 3rd millennium CE. A further "Don't Miss Out" box describes Indian pañchāngas — traditional calendars based on the positions of the sun and moon that provide astronomical data, eclipse predictions, festival dates and weather forecasts, and remain widely used in India. A second "Let's Explore" activity asks students to construct a personal family timeline from 1900 CE to the present. A visual timeline (Fig. 4.3) spanning 300,000 BCE to the present marks key events including the Ice Age, first rock art, end of the last Ice Age, first settlements and agriculture, pottery, the Indus-Sarasvatī civilisation, world's first cities in Mesopotamia, beginning of copper metallurgy, birth of the Buddha and birth of Jesus.

What Are the Sources of History?

This section introduces the concept of a source of history, defined as a place, person, text or object from which we gather information about a past event or period. A "Let's Explore" activity asks students to construct a three-generation family tree using available sources such as photographs, diaries, ID cards and the memories of relatives, drawing a direct parallel between personal and historical investigation. The section explains that every object or structure tells a story and that historians assemble historical events through a variety of sources, while acknowledging that many pieces of the puzzle may remain missing. A detailed diagram presents the full taxonomy of historical sources: Archaeological Sources (structures such as monuments and mounds; excavations including human, animal and plant remains, tools and weapons, figurines and ornaments, pottery and toys, habitations and burials); Inscriptions (manuscripts, copper plates, coins); Oral Sources (genealogical accounts, folklore); Indian Literature (Vedas and Itihasas, poems and plays, historical texts, collections of stories, scientific and technological texts); Foreign Accounts (travelogues, historical chronicles); and Artistic Sources (paintings, sculptures, panels). The section describes how historians, archaeologists, epigraphists, anthropologists, linguists and scientists work together to reconstruct the past, and that when sources confirm each other the picture becomes clearer, while contradictory sources require scholars to judge which is more reliable. It notes the growing contribution of scientific methods — studies of ancient climates, chemical analysis of excavated materials and genetic studies of ancient peoples — as supplements to conventional sources. Newspapers and electronic media are mentioned as sources for more recent history. A second "Let's Explore" activity presents images of historical artefacts (a coin, a carved panel, and the Ashoka lion capital) and asks students to write what information each object conveys.

The Beginnings of Human History

This section provides a brief overview of human prehistory, beginning with the appearance of modern humans (Homo sapiens) approximately 300,000 years ago. It describes how early humans lived in bands or groups, constantly seeking shelter and food as hunters and gatherers who relied on hunting animals and collecting edible plants and fruits. They inhabited temporary camps, rock shelters and caves, communicated in languages now lost, used fire and crafted increasingly refined stone tools including axes, blades and arrowheads. The section notes that evidence of their lives survives in rock paintings found in hundreds of caves worldwide — some depicting simple figures or symbols, others showing detailed scenes of animals or humans. Early humans also made ornaments such as stone or shell beads and pendants from animal teeth, and sometimes exchanged these with other groups, suggesting early social and trade networks. The section contains a "Let's Explore" student activity based on an illustration of a rock shelter.

The First Crops

This section explains that over long ages, the Earth's climate underwent major changes. During Ice Ages, much of the Earth was covered in ice; the last Ice Age lasted from over 100,000 years ago to around 12,000 years ago. When the climate warmed and the ice partly melted, rivers swelled and eventually drained into the oceans. As conditions improved after the last Ice Age, humans in many parts of the world began settling down and cultivating cereals and grains, and domesticating animals such as cattle and goats. With greater food availability, these communities grew in size and number and settled near rivers, where water availability and fertile soil made agriculture easier. As communities expanded, social complexity increased: chieftains or leaders emerged to oversee community welfare, and land was collectively cultivated without individual ownership. Hamlets grew into villages that exchanged food, clothing and tools; villages eventually grew into small towns as networks of communication and trade developed. New technologies emerged at this stage — pottery for making clay objects, and metallurgy beginning with copper and later iron, enabling the production of durable tools, everyday objects and ornaments. The section closes by noting that this early progress prepared the ground for the emergence of civilisation (to be explored in Chapter 6), and reflects on the courage and resilience of early humans who overcame enormous challenges to ensure humanity's survival. A "Let's Explore" activity accompanies an illustration of an agricultural community from several millennia ago, and a "Think About It" activity invites students to reflect critically on the gender roles depicted in both the rock shelter and agricultural scenes.
