Chapter 13: The Value of Work

The chapter opens with a narrative involving two children, Anu and Kabir, who observe several people in their lives engaged in a wide variety of activities — a neighbour who is an Indian Air Force pilot receiving a salary for serving the country, a retired Border Security Force officer who voluntarily teaches geography to neighbourhood children and tends a vegetable garden, parents running a garment shop, a mother who volunteers with a community knitting group for women, and an elder brother who is a software engineer and also volunteers on weekends to teach computer skills at a youth development programme. This opening scene establishes the central idea that people around us perform many kinds of activities every day, and the chapter invites students to think carefully about the nature and purpose of those activities through a Let's Explore student activity that asks them to list the characters and classify their activities.

Types of Economic Activities

The chapter explains that all human activities can be divided into two broad categories: economic activities and non-economic activities. Economic activities are defined as those that involve money or are performed in exchange for money or money's worth for the parties involved. Several key vocabulary terms are introduced and defined: money's worth refers to the monetary value a person places on an object based on the benefit they derive from it; a market is a place where people engage in the exchange of goods and services, usually for money; a fee is a payment made for professional advice or services; a salary is a fixed regular payment generally paid monthly by an employer to an employee; a wage is a payment made for a specific period of time; and payment in kind is a non-cash payment received in exchange for work performed. Examples of economic activities include a business person selling school bags in a market, a farmer selling produce, a lawyer arguing a case and earning a fee, a truck driver transporting goods, and workers employed in a car manufacturing factory.

A second narrative follows Kavya, a child visiting her aunt's village of Kamlapur. Through Kavya's observations, the chapter illustrates different forms of economic activity and payment: her uncle works as a technician in a construction company operating a bulldozer and earns a monthly salary; her aunt is employed at the village post office for a monthly salary and additionally conducts paid online classes for a weekly fee; and a farm labourer named Sahil tills land using a tractor and earns a daily wage, receiving part of his payment in cash and the remainder as mangoes — an example of payment in kind. A student Think About It prompt asks students to recall economic activities they observed on their way to school and consider how those workers were paid.

The chapter then explains the concept of value addition using the example of Kavya's father, Rajesh, a carpenter who buys wood for ₹600 and sells the finished chair for ₹1,000. The remaining ₹400 represents the monetary value of Rajesh's skill, time, and effort — the process of transforming raw wood into furniture has added economic value. The chapter emphasises that economic activities add value at each stage of transforming something from one form into another, and this process is called value addition. A Let's Explore student activity invites students to assess which listed professions and activities create monetary value and to add their own examples.

The Importance of Non-Economic Activities

Non-economic activities are those that do not generate income or wealth but are done out of feelings such as gratitude, love, care, and respect. Examples include parents cooking for the family, helping children with schoolwork, youth taking care of grandparents, and family members assisting with home renovation. The chapter makes clear that the absence of monetary exchange does not diminish the importance of these activities — their value lies in their contribution to social welfare, personal wellbeing, and the overall quality of life.

*Sevā*: selfless service

The section introduces the concept of sevā, meaning selfless service, as a significant form of non-economic activity deeply embedded in Indian society. It is illustrated through the practice of langars (community kitchens) at gurudwaras, where food is served freely to every visitor, and through the distribution of prasād at temples. Such practices are described as fostering a sense of satisfaction and gratitude for what one has, and as a way of contributing to society without expecting anything in return. A Think About It activity asks students to reflect on the difference between Kabir's grandfather voluntarily teaching neighbourhood children and a paid school teacher, and to identify non-economic activities that are meaningful in their own families.

The strength of community participation

This section examines how non-economic activities gain additional significance through collective action. The Swachh Bharat Abhiyan is presented as a major example of community-wide non-economic participation, where citizens individually maintain their own homes and surroundings, but also come together to clean streets, roads, parks, and public spaces, with the collective effect of a clean home, neighbourhood, and nation. Van Mahotsav, the festival of forests, is cited as another example of organised community participation — an initiative that brings community members together for tree plantation drives to promote awareness about the value of trees and the conservation of forests. A Think About It student activity asks students to identify similar community practices from their own areas, to reflect on whether festival celebrations constitute non-economic activities, and to describe community programmes undertaken by their school or locality.
