Chapter 3: Landforms and Life

Introduction

The chapter opens by establishing that land takes many forms across the Earth's surface. A journey from Chhota Nagpur in Jharkhand to Prayagraj in Uttar Pradesh and on to Almora in Uttarakhand, for instance, passes through three distinctly different landscapes. A landform is defined as a physical feature on the Earth's surface, shaped over millions of years and deeply connected to the environment and life. The chapter organises these features into three broad categories: mountains, plateaus and plains. Each landform supports different climates, flora and fauna, and varying densities of human settlement. The section includes a student activity inviting groups to observe their surroundings and compare landscapes from journeys they have taken.

Mountains

Mountains are landforms that rise much higher than the surrounding landscape, identifiable by a broad base, steep slopes and a narrow summit. At lower altitudes, snow melts seasonally to feed rivers; at high altitudes, mountains may remain permanently snow-capped. Lower highlands with rounded tops are called hills. Key vocabulary introduced includes altitude (height above sea level) and precipitation (water reaching the ground as rain, snow or hail). Six examples illustrate mountains across the world: Mount Everest and Kanchenjunga (Himalayas), Mount Aconcagua (Andes), Mount Kilimanjaro (eastern Africa, isolated), Mont Blanc (Alps, western Europe) and Anamudi (south India, Kerala). Most mountains form ranges stretching thousands of kilometres. The chapter distinguishes young mountains with tall sharp peaks (like the Himalayas, still growing) from older, erosion-rounded mountains like the Aravallis.

Mountain environment

Mountain slopes are typically covered in montane forest, where conifer trees such as pines, firs, spruce and deodar dominate. These conifers are tall and cone-shaped with thin pointed leaves adapted to the cold. Above the treeline, vegetation gives way to grasses, mosses and lichens. The chapter describes the rich biodiversity of mountain habitats — deep forests, rivers, lakes, grasslands and caves — which shelter fauna such as the golden eagle, peregrine falcon, Canadian lynx, snow leopard, ibex, Himalayan tahr, mountain hare, yak, grey fox and black bear. A literary interlude presents two verses from Kālidāsa's Kumārasambhava invoking the Himalayas and mentioning the Ganga, deodar trees and mountain people, prompting students to connect poetry to geography. A "Don't Miss Out" box notes that the Ganga is nearly 2,500 km long, with tributaries such as the Yamuna and Ghagara originating in the Himalayas and the Son in the Vindhyas.

Life in the mountains

Mountain terrain is characteristically rugged with steep slopes, which restricts regular farming to valleys. On slopes, cultivation is made possible through terrace farming — cutting steps into hillsides to create level growing surfaces. In many mountainous regions, herding is preferred over agriculture. Tourism is highlighted as an important economic activity: the mountain environment attracts visitors for its crisp air and scenery, as well as for adventure sports (skiing, hiking, mountaineering, paragliding) and pilgrimages to sacred sites. The section cautions, however, that excessive visitor numbers can put the fragile mountain environment under pressure. A "Don't Miss Out" box profiles two inspirational Indian mountaineers: Bachendri Pal, the first Indian woman to summit Mount Everest in 1984, and Arunima Sinha, who climbed Everest in 2013 after losing a leg, and went on to climb the highest peak of every continent. A student activity (with accompanying images) asks students to discuss challenges of mountain life — avalanches, landslides, flash floods, cloudbursts, heavy snowfall, cold weather and uncontrolled tourism — and to reflect on why people still choose to live there. A "Don't Miss Out" box highlights the cultural significance of mountains: Mount Everest is called "Chomolungma" (Mother Goddess of the World) by Tibetans and "Sagarmatha" (Goddess of the Sky) by Nepalis; Mount Kailash is held sacred by followers of Hinduism, Buddhism, Jainism and the ancient Tibetan Bon religion.

Plateaus

A plateau is defined as a landform that rises above surrounding land with a more or less flat upper surface, often with steep sides. Like mountains, plateaus can be geologically young or old. Two major examples are introduced: the Tibetan Plateau (the largest and highest in the world, with an average altitude of 4,500 m, nicknamed the "Roof of the World") and the Deccan Plateau of central and south India (among the oldest plateaus, formed through volcanic activity). Plateaus are described as "storehouses of minerals," with mining as a major economic activity: the East African Plateau is noted for gold and diamond mining, while the Chhota Nagpur Plateau holds large reserves of iron, coal and manganese. The plateau environment varies widely: many have rocky, less fertile soils compared to plains, though lava plateaus (like parts of the Deccan) often have rich black soil suited to agriculture. Plateaus are also noted for spectacular waterfalls — including Victoria Falls (Zambezi River, southern Africa), Hundru Falls (Subarnarekha River, Chhota Nagpur Plateau), Jog Falls (Sharavati River, Western Ghats) and the Nohkalikai Falls (Cherrapunji Plateau, Meghalaya), which plunge 340 metres.

Plains

Plains are defined as landforms with an extensive flat or gently undulating surface, generally not more than 300 metres above sea level. The chapter focuses on floodplains: rivers originating in mountains carry sediments (rock, sand and silt) which are deposited in the plains, creating highly fertile soils ideal for diverse crops. Agriculture is the major economic occupation of plains, which also support rich biodiversity. A world map of major mountain ranges, plateaus and river plains is included, with a student activity asking students to apply the landform classification to named examples on it.

Life in the plains

The chapter notes that the first civilisations developed around rivers in fertile plains. Today, plains remain the most densely populated landforms. The Ganga plain (Gangetic plain) is the primary Indian example: approximately 40 crore people — over one-fourth of India's population — live there. Major occupations are river fishing and agriculture; food crops (rice, wheat, maize, barley, millets) and fibre crops (cotton, jute, hemp) are grown. Agriculture was traditionally rainfed but now relies heavily on irrigation via canals and groundwater; this has boosted production but also depleted groundwater, posing a future challenge alongside high population density and pollution. The section notes the cultural significance of rivers: sources and confluences are regarded as sacred by many communities, and in India numerous festivals and rituals are held at such locations. Rivers in plains also support navigation and trade, and a student activity encourages students to visit a nearby river and record its economic and cultural activities. The chapter then introduces deserts as an additional landform: large dry expanses with very little precipitation and unique flora and fauna. Examples include the hot Sahara and Thar deserts and the cold Gobi Desert. Despite harsh conditions, humans have adapted to desert life; communities of the Thar Desert hold rich cultural traditions including folk songs and legends. The chapter closes with the five tiṇais of ancient Tamil Sangam poetry — an alternative landscape classification associating Kuriñji (mountains) with hunting and gathering, Mullai (grasslands/forests) with cattle rearing, Marudam (fertile plains) with farming, Neydal (coastal regions) with fishing and seafaring, and Pālai (arid regions) with journeying and fighting. These reflect a deep cultural awareness of the human-environment relationship. The chapter's key takeaway is that each landform presents both challenges and opportunities, and humanity's ability to inhabit all of them is a testimony to our adaptability and resilience.
