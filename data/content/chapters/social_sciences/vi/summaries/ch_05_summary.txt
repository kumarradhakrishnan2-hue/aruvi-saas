Chapter 5: India, That Is Bharat

The chapter opens by situating India as an ancient land whose identity and boundaries have changed significantly over thousands of years. The region commonly called the 'Indian Subcontinent' has carried many different names across history, known to us through ancient texts, travellers' accounts, pilgrim records, and stone inscriptions. The chapter's organising inquiry is twofold: how did the people of India name their own land, and how did outsiders name it?

How Indians Named India

The Rig Veda, India's oldest text and several thousand years old, provides the earliest named reference to part of the Subcontinent. It calls the northwest region 'Sapta Sindhava', meaning the 'land of the seven rivers'. The term 'Sindhava' derives from 'Sindhu', the ancient name for the Indus River, which could also refer to rivers in general. This was a regional name, not one for the whole Subcontinent.

As time passed, names for other parts of India appeared in literature. The Mahabharata, one of India's most celebrated texts, lists numerous regions — Kashmira (roughly today's Kashmir), Kurukshetra (parts of Haryana), Vanga (parts of Bengal), Pragjyotisha (roughly today's Assam), Kaccha (today's Kutch), Kerala, and many others — showing that the Subcontinent's diverse regions were known and named. The chapter includes a map (Fig. 5.4) showing how these Mahabharata-era regions cover the entire geography of the Subcontinent. A student activity ("Let's Explore") asks students to identify which of these regions they recognise.

The Mahabharata also uses two terms for the Subcontinent as a whole: 'Bharatavarsha' and 'Jambudvipa'. Scholars place the composition of this text from a few centuries BCE onward. 'Bharatavarsha' means 'the country of the Bharatas'; 'Bharata' is a name that first appears in the Rig Veda as one of the main Vedic groups of people, and several kings named Bharata appear in later literature. 'Jambudvipa' means 'the island of the fruit of the jamun tree', a tree native to India, and this term came to denote the Indian Subcontinent. Emperor Ashoka (c. 250 BCE) used the term 'Jambudvipa' in one of his inscriptions to describe the whole of India, which at that time included what are today Bangladesh, Pakistan, and parts of Afghanistan.

A few centuries further on, 'Bharata' became the widely used name for the Subcontinent. The Vishnu Purana encapsulates this in a Sanskrit verse translated in the chapter: "The country that lies north of the ocean and south of the snowy mountains is called Bharata." A "Think About It" activity asks students to identify the 'snowy mountains' and evaluate this geographical description. The chapter notes that this definition was shared across regions: an ancient Tamil poem from about 2,000 years ago similarly describes a king's fame as extending from Cape Kumari in the south to the great mountain in the north, and from the eastern ocean to the western ocean, confirming that ancient Indians understood the Subcontinent's full extent. The name 'Bharat' persists today — written as 'Bharat' in north India and 'Bharatam' in south India — and appears in the very first line of the Indian Constitution: "India, that is Bharat, shall be a Union of States." A "Let's Explore" activity directs students to find this phrase in a reproduction of the Constitution's opening page.

How Foreigners Named India

The chapter then traces the names outsiders gave to India, most of which derive from the same root word: Sindhu. The Persians were the first foreigners to record India's name. In the 6th century BCE, a Persian emperor campaigned into the region of the Indus River — known to Indians as 'Sindhu' — and Persian records and inscriptions thereafter referred to India as 'Hind', 'Hidu', or 'Hindu'. The chapter notes explicitly that 'Hindu' in ancient Persian usage is a purely geographical term and does not refer to the Hindu religion. From Persian sources, the ancient Greeks derived 'Indoi' or 'Indike', dropping the initial 'h' because that sound did not exist in Greek. The chapter presents this etymology as a visual chain: Sindhu → Hindhu → Indoi/Indike.

The ancient Chinese also interacted with India and called it 'Yintu' or 'Yindu', words likewise traceable to 'Sindhu' via the chain: Sindhu → Hindhu → Indu → Yindu. Another Chinese term, 'Tianzhu', also derived from 'Sindhu', could additionally be read as 'heavenly master', reflecting the reverence the ancient Chinese held for India as the birthplace of the Buddha. The chapter includes a "Don't Miss Out" box on the Chinese pilgrim Xuanzang, who travelled to India in the 7th century CE, spent 17 years visiting scholars and collecting Buddhist texts, and returned to China to translate Sanskrit manuscripts into Chinese. The term 'Hindustan', familiar to modern readers, appears in a Persian inscription about 1,800 years ago and subsequently became the term most commonly used by invaders of India for the Subcontinent. A "Let's Explore" activity presents a table with rows for Persian, Greek, Latin, Chinese, Arabic and Persian, English, and French names for India, asking students to complete it.
