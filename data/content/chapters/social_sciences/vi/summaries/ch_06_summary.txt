Chapter 6: The Beginnings of Indian Civilisation

What Is a Civilisation?

This section introduces the concept of civilisation as an advanced stage of human societies. The chapter defines a civilisation as requiring at least seven key characteristics: some form of government and administration to manage complex society; urbanism including town-planning, city growth, and water and drainage management; a variety of crafts involving raw material management and production of finished goods; both internal and external trade to exchange goods; some form of writing for record-keeping and communication; cultural ideas about life expressed through art, architecture, literature, oral traditions or social customs; and productive agriculture sufficient to feed both villages and cities. The section also introduces the term metallurgy — the techniques of extracting, purifying and combining metals. A student activity (Think About It) invites learners to consider which characteristic is most fundamental to the development of the others.

From Village to City

This section introduces the geographical and historical context of the earliest Indian civilisation. The fertile plains of Punjab and Sindh, watered by the Indus River and its tributaries, and the region through which the now-seasonal Sarasvatī River once flowed, formed the cradle of early settlement. From about 3500 BCE, villages in this region grew into towns and then into cities, with the transition to full urbanisation occurring around 2600 BCE. Archaeologists have given this civilisation several names — Indus, Harappan, Indus-Sarasvatī, or Sindhu-Sarasvatī civilisation — and its inhabitants are called Harappans. The section explains that the city of Harappa in Pakistan's Punjab was the first site of this civilisation to be excavated, in 1920–21, which is why its people are called Harappans. This development is described as the "First Urbanisation of India." A student activity (Let's Explore) asks students to match major Harappan cities to their modern states.

The Sarasvatī River

This section focuses on the Sarasvatī River, which today is known as the Ghaggar in India and the Hakra in Pakistan. It now flows only seasonally. Many significant Harappan sites — including major cities like Rakhigarhi and Ganweriwala, and smaller settlements like Farmana, Kalibangan, Bhirrana, and Banawali — were located along this river's course. The section notes that the Sarasvatī River is first mentioned in the Rig Veda, an ancient collection of prayers, where it is revered as both a goddess and a river flowing "from the mountain to the sea." Later texts describe it as eventually drying up and disappearing. The high density of Harappan sites in the Sarasvatī basin is noted as visible on the chapter's map.

Town-Planning

This section describes the sophisticated urban design of Harappan cities. Harappa and Mohenjo-daro in present-day Pakistan were the first cities of this civilisation to be identified, in 1924. Subsequent discoveries extended the civilisation's known range, including major cities like Dholavira in Gujarat, Rakhigarhi in Haryana, and Ganweriwala in Pakistan, along with hundreds of smaller sites. This is why the term "Indus Valley civilisation" is now considered obsolete, as the civilisation extended well beyond the Indus valley. The larger cities were built to precise plans with wide streets oriented to cardinal directions. Most were surrounded by fortifications and divided into an upper town (housing the elite) and a lower town (for common people). Buildings of both small and large size were constructed with the same quality of brickwork. Large structures served collective purposes such as warehousing. The section discusses the famous Great Bath at Mohenjo-daro — a waterproofed tank measuring 12 × 7 metres, surrounded by small rooms and a well — whose purpose remains debated, with proposed interpretations including a royal bath or a tank for religious rituals. A student activity (Let's Explore) invites debate on the two remaining interpretations. Dholavira is noted as a unique city with three distinct zones rather than the typical two.

Water Management

This section highlights the Harappans' advanced approach to water management and sanitation. Individual homes often had bathing areas connected to a city-wide drainage network that ran beneath the streets to carry waste water away. In Mohenjo-daro, hundreds of brick-lined wells provided water. In other regions, ponds, streams, or human-made reservoirs were used. Dholavira is described as having an exceptional system of at least six large reservoirs built with stone or cut directly into rock, connected by underground drains for water harvesting and distribution. The largest reservoir at Dholavira measured 73 metres in length. A student activity asks students to measure spaces in their school and compare them to this reservoir's size. A Think About It box encourages students to reason about the administrative organisation needed to build and maintain such a complex infrastructure.

What Did the Harappans Eat?

This section examines Harappan agricultural practices and diet. Harappan settlements were established along rivers both for water access and for the fertile soil that rivers create. Archaeological evidence shows that Harappans cultivated barley, wheat, millets, sometimes rice, pulses, and various vegetables. They were also the first in Eurasia to grow cotton, which they wove into clothing. Farming tools including the plough have been found at sites like Banawali in Haryana. Agricultural activity was managed by numerous small rural villages that supplied the cities. The Harappans also domesticated animals for meat and fished in rivers and the sea, as shown by large quantities of animal and fish bones found in excavations. Scientific analysis of clay cooking pots has revealed diverse food remains including dairy products, turmeric, ginger, and banana. A student activity asks students to imagine preparing a meal using Harappan ingredients.

A Brisk Trade

This section describes the extensive trading network of the Harappans, both within their own civilisation and with distant cultures and civilisations inside and outside India. Exports included ornaments, timber, objects of daily use, and probably gold, cotton, and food items. Carnelian beads, made from a reddish semiprecious stone found mainly in Gujarat, were among the most prized trade items; Harappan craftspeople developed special drilling and decorating techniques for them. Shell bangles made from conch shells were another trade product requiring sophisticated craftsmanship. Imports likely included copper. Trade was conducted overland, along rivers, and by sea — the section notes this as the first intensive maritime activity in India. Lothal in Gujarat had a large dockyard basin measuring 217 × 36 metres. Thousands of small steatite seals bearing animal figures and undeciphered script signs were used to identify goods and traders and are linked to trade activities. A student activity invites students to interpret the animal imagery on Harappan seals. The section also notes that Harappans mastered copper and bronze metallurgy, using bronze for tools, pots, and figurines.

The Lives of the Ancients

This section presents a visual and descriptive survey of objects made and used by Harappans, divided into everyday items and cultural or symbolic objects. Everyday objects include bronze mirrors, terracotta pots, stone weights, a bronze chisel, a carved stone gamesboard, and a terracotta whistle, indicating that the Harappans engaged in both practical crafts and leisure activities. Cultural and symbolic objects include the famous "Priest King" statuette (identity unknown), a seal bearing a swastika symbol, a seal depicting a three-faced deity surrounded by animals, the celebrated bronze "Dancing Girl" figurine from Mohenjo-daro (10.8 cm high), a terracotta figurine in a namaste pose, and a design on a Lothal pot that seems to depict the story of the thirsty crow. Student activities invite examination of these objects to identify aspects of Harappan life that were important to them.

The End or a New Beginning?

This section explains the decline of the Sindhu-Sarasvatī civilisation around 1900 BCE. Cities were abandoned one by one, and the population appears to have dispersed into hundreds of small rural settlements, with no evidence of the earlier government or administration. The section notes that early theories attributing the decline to warfare or invasion are now ruled out, as there is no evidence of armies or weapons of war — the civilisation appears to have been relatively peaceful. Two factors are currently accepted: a global climatic change from around 2200 BCE that caused reduced rainfall, making agriculture more difficult; and the drying up of the Sarasvatī River in its central basin, which led to the sudden abandonment of cities such as Kalibangan and Banawali. The section concludes by noting that much Harappan culture and technology survived and was passed on to subsequent phases of Indian civilisation.
