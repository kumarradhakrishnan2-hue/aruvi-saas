Chapter 10: Grassroots Democracy – Part 1 Governance

Introduction

This section establishes why rules and governance are essential to human community life. The chapter opens by drawing on familiar contexts — rules at home, school, and on the road — to show that without rules, society cannot function. It introduces three key terms: governance (the process of taking decisions, organising society's life with rules, and ensuring they are followed), government (the group or system that makes and enforces those rules), and laws (the more important among those rules). The section also establishes that rules are not fixed forever — citizens, like students who challenge a school rule, have a say in the laws that govern their society. A student activity asks learners to contrast two images showing order and disorder in traffic and connect this to the discussion on rules.

Three Organs of Government

This section introduces the three branches of government through the example of cybercrime. The chapter explains how digital technology transformed everyday transactions like money transfers but simultaneously created new forms of crime. Governments responded by passing new cyber laws, police arrested offenders, and courts convicted them — illustrating how all three organs operate together. The legislature is the organ that makes and updates laws, done through an assembly of people's representatives. The executive implements those laws and includes the head of state, ministers, and law enforcement agencies. The judiciary is the system of courts that determines whether the law has been broken and what punishment is appropriate; it can also review executive decisions and legislative laws for fairness. The section introduces the concept of separation of powers (illustrated through Fig. 10.3, a pie diagram showing Legislature, Executive, and Judiciary around a central Governance core), explaining that keeping these organs separate provides a system of checks and balances so no single organ can act beyond its expected role. A student activity invites learners to apply this framework to the cybercrime example.

Three Levels of Government

This section explains that governments operate at multiple tiers. While any government functions at a minimum of two levels (local and national), India has three — local, State, and Central or Union government. The chapter uses the analogy of a faulty electrical bulb handled first at home, then by an electrician, then by the Electricity Board to make the tiered structure intuitive. It then applies this to a real scenario: a district flood may be managed by local authorities alone if minor, by the State Government if it affects multiple towns, and by the Central Government if the disaster is massive. The section includes Fig. 10.5, a summary table showing how the three organs function at national and State levels: the legislature comprises Lok Sabha and Rajya Sabha at the Centre and Vidhan Sabha at the State level; the executive at the Centre is led nominally by the President and effectively by the Prime Minister, while at State level the Governor is nominal head and the Chief Minister is executive head; the judiciary is represented by the Supreme Court at the national level and High Courts at the State level. Key executive functions of the Central Government include defence, foreign affairs, atomic energy, communications, currency, interstate commerce, education, and formulation of national policies; State Government responsibilities include police and law and order, adaptation of central laws, public health, education, agriculture, irrigation, and local government. A sidebar provides the governmental mottos Satyameva Jayate and Yato Dharmastato Jayah. A biographical feature on Dr. A.P.J. Abdul Kalam illustrates how even a nominally positioned President can make a significant impact; the feature uses his story and quoted thoughts to exemplify humility, dedication, and civic inspiration.

Democracy

This section introduces democracy as the overall framework within which governance operates. The word 'democracy' is traced to the Greek roots dēmos (people) and kratos (rule or power), meaning literally 'rule of the people'. The chapter acknowledges the practical impossibility of everyone ruling directly and uses the classroom analogy of a class representative going to the principal to explain representative democracy. At the State and national levels, citizens vote in elections to choose Members of Legislative Assembly (MLAs) and Members of Parliament (MPs), who then discuss laws and solutions through dialogue and debate in their respective assemblies. India is described as a representative democracy and the world's largest, with approximately 970 million voters in 2024; all citizens above 18 have the right to vote. The section also introduces direct democracy through the example of a class voting by show of hands to choose a picnic destination. It concludes by defining grassroots democracy as a system that enables and encourages ordinary citizens — the base of the governance pyramid — to participate in decisions that affect them. The chapter closes with a Before we move on summary reinforcing the four key takeaways: no country can run without governance and government; modern government has three organs that must work together; the Indian government functions at three levels; and democracy, operating through elected representatives, is the framework for the whole system.
