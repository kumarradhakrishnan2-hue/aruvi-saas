Chapter 02: Oceans and Continents

The Distribution of Water and Land on the Earth

Water is the dominant feature of the Earth's surface, covering almost three-fourths of the globe — which is why early astronauts called it the "blue planet." The largest water bodies are called oceans, while large continuous expanses of land are called continents. Oceans and continents are not distributed equally between the Northern and Southern Hemispheres; the Northern Hemisphere holds significantly more land, while the Southern Hemisphere is predominantly water. Smaller extensions of oceans carry various names — sea, bay, gulf — but all are interconnected parts of the same global body of water. The section contains a student activity asking learners to examine polar-view maps and estimate the proportion of water to land in each hemisphere.

Oceans

The chapter identifies five named oceans on the world map: the Pacific Ocean, the Atlantic Ocean, the Indian Ocean, the Arctic Ocean, and the Southern (or Antarctic) Ocean. Despite being named separately, the oceans are not truly distinct — the boundaries drawn on maps are conventions, and seawater flows continuously across all of them, sustaining a rich diversity of marine life. The Pacific is the largest ocean, followed by the Atlantic, the Indian, the Southern, and finally the Arctic, which is the smallest. The chapter introduces the terms marine flora (algae, seaweeds) and marine fauna (fish, dolphins, whales, and deep-sea creatures), noting that every layer of the ocean — from sun-lit surface to dark depths — hosts its own diverse life forms. Seawater is salty and unfit for consumption by land animals; freshwater, by contrast, makes up a very small proportion of the planet's water and is found in glaciers, rivers, lakes, the atmosphere, and as groundwater underground.

Oceans and Disasters

While oceans are life-sustaining, they can also be the source of destructive natural events. Clouds that form over oceans bring monsoon rains to continents — India's summer monsoon, for instance, originates in the ocean — but oceans also generate cyclones, violent storms with extreme rainfall and strong winds that damage coastal regions. Tsunamis are another ocean-born hazard: massive waves caused by strong undersea earthquakes or volcanic eruptions that can travel thousands of kilometres and submerge coastal areas. The 2004 Indian Ocean tsunami, triggered by an earthquake in Indonesia, killed more than two lakh people and severely affected India's Andaman and Nicobar Islands and the coasts of Tamil Nadu and Kerala. The chapter notes that international collaboration through early warning systems, including the Indian Ocean Tsunami Warning System, helps protect lives, and that India's National Disaster Management Authority oversees response to all such natural disasters.

Continents

A continent is a large continuous landmass. The chapter explains that the count of continents is not fixed and varies from four to seven depending on how landmasses are grouped. North America and South America are usually counted as two continents but can be seen as one; Europe and Asia form a single landmass but are historically and culturally treated as two (geologists often call the combined mass "Eurasia"); Africa and Eurasia are generally regarded separately but sometimes as one. The most widely adopted system recognises seven continents: Africa, Antarctica, Asia, Australia, Europe, North America, and South America. A proportional diagram in the chapter shows their relative sizes — Asia is the largest and Australia the smallest of the seven. The section contains a student activity using the diagram to compare continental sizes and rank them.

Islands

Islands are pieces of land surrounded by water on all sides that are smaller than continents. There are lakhs of islands on the planet, varying enormously in size. Greenland is the world's largest island — its area equals that of the ten largest states of India combined. India itself has more than 1,300 small islands, including two major groups: the Andaman and Nicobar Islands in the Bay of Bengal and the Lakshadweep Islands in the Arabian Sea. The chapter also notes India's Antarctic engagement: since 1981 the Indian Antarctica Programme has been conducting scientific research on the continent of Antarctica, establishing its first base station, Dakshin Gangotri, in 1983, with about forty research teams studying climate and environmental evolution in that extreme environment.

Oceans and Life

This section draws together the broader significance of oceans for life on Earth. Oceans drive the water cycle by generating rainfall — without them the Earth would be a desert. More than half of the world's oxygen is produced by the ocean's plant life, earning oceans the title "the planet's lungs." Oceans have also shaped human history: from earliest times, people used seas and oceans to migrate, trade, conduct military campaigns, and source food through fishing. Coastal cultures around the world have developed rich traditions, tales, and legends centred on the sea. At the same time, the chapter raises concern about human-caused threats to the ocean environment — millions of tonnes of plastic waste are dumped into oceans annually, choking marine life, and overfishing further depletes marine populations. The chapter calls for collective responsibility to protect oceans for the future of both the planet and humanity.
