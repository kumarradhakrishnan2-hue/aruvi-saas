Chapter 1: Locating Places on the Earth

A Map and Its Components

This section introduces maps as representations of an area viewed from above — ranging from a small locality to the entire world. It establishes that an atlas is a collection of maps and introduces three main types: physical maps (showing natural features such as mountains, oceans and rivers), political maps (showing countries, states, boundaries and cities), and thematic maps (carrying specific information such as vegetation or population patterns). The section identifies three key components of all maps: distance, direction and symbols. Distance on a map is expressed through its scale — for example, 1 cm on the map may represent 500 metres on the ground — which allows a vast area to be compressed onto a small sheet of paper. Direction is conveyed through the four cardinal points (north, south, east, west) and the four intermediate directions (northeast, northwest, southeast, southwest); most maps carry an arrow marked 'N' pointing north. Symbols are standardised pictorial representations for features such as railway stations, roads, rivers, forests and settlements; the Survey of India maintains an official set of symbols for maps of India. The section contains a student activity in which learners draw a map of their school and the surrounding streets using the symbols introduced.

Mapping the Earth

This section explains why mapping the Earth is more challenging than mapping a flat area: the planet is approximately spherical, and a spherical surface cannot be accurately flattened onto paper without distortion. The section uses the analogy of peeling an orange and trying to flatten the skin to illustrate this impossibility. It then introduces the globe — a physical spherical object on which a map is drawn — and explains that because the globe and the Earth share the same shape, a globe represents the Earth's geography more faithfully than a flat map.

a) Understanding coordinates

This subsection introduces the concept of coordinates through two familiar examples — locating a shop in a market by specifying its row and column, and recording a chess move using the letter-number notation of a chessboard (for example, a pawn moving from d2 to d4). The point is that two values together — one for each axis — allow any position on a grid to be precisely identified. This coordinate idea is then carried forward to the globe.

b) Latitudes

This subsection defines latitude as the angular distance of a point from the Equator, measured in degrees. The Equator, lying exactly halfway between the North Pole and the South Pole, is latitude 0°; the poles are 90°N and 90°S respectively. Lines running parallel to the Equator at a fixed latitude are called parallels of latitude; they form complete circles around the Earth, with the Equator being the largest and the circles growing progressively smaller towards the poles. The section also explains the connection between latitude and climate: equatorial latitudes have hot or torrid climates, mid-latitudes have temperate climates, and high latitudes near the poles have cold or frigid climates.

c) Longitudes

This subsection introduces meridians of longitude — imaginary half-circles running from the North Pole to the South Pole — and explains that because all meridians are of equal length, a reference line must be chosen. Since 1884, the internationally agreed Prime Meridian passes through Greenwich, London, and is marked as 0° longitude. Longitude is measured in degrees east or west of the Prime Meridian, ranging from 0° to 180°E and 0° to 180°W; 180°E and 180°W coincide. Example longitudes given include New York (74°W), Delhi (77°E) and Tokyo (140°E). The subsection notes that because the Earth rotates, longitude also encodes time — a place's local time advances as its longitude increases eastward from the Prime Meridian. Taken together, latitude and longitude form the two coordinates of any location on Earth. The section includes a "Don't Miss Out" box on India's own ancient prime meridian, called madhya rekhā, which passed through Ujjayinī (modern Ujjain) and was used by Indian astronomers — including Varāhamihira — as the reference for all astronomical calculations long before the Greenwich convention.

Understanding Time Zones

This section explains the mathematical relationship between longitude and time. The Earth completes one full rotation of 360° in 24 hours, which means it rotates 15° per hour. Moving eastward from the Prime Meridian, local time advances by one hour for every 15° of longitude; moving westward, it falls behind by one hour. The section illustrates this with the example of two friends, one in Porbandar (Gujarat) and one in Tinsukia (Assam), for whom sunset occurs at different moments despite both being in India. Because it would be impractical for a country to use a different local time at every longitude, most countries adopt a single standard time. India uses Indian Standard Time (IST), which is 5 hours 30 minutes ahead of Greenwich Mean Time (GMT). All countries' standard times are organised into time zones that broadly correspond to bands of 15° longitude, though the boundaries of time zones often deviate from straight meridian lines to follow international borders rather than cut through countries. The section also introduces the International Date Line, located approximately at 180° longitude directly opposite the Prime Meridian: crossing it eastward subtracts one calendar day, while crossing it westward adds one day. Large countries such as Russia (11 time zones) and the USA (six time zones) use multiple standard times within their borders.

Before we move on …

This closing summary section recaps the chapter's three main ideas: that maps are tools representing areas of the Earth with distance, direction and symbols as their core components; that every place on Earth can be precisely located using the coordinate grid formed by parallels of latitude and meridians of longitude; and that longitude simultaneously defines both location and local time, giving rise to the system of time zones and the International Date Line.
