Chapter 9: Family and Community

The chapter opens with two "Big Questions" that frame its inquiry: why is the family unit important, and what is a community and what is its role? These questions guide students through a progression from the intimate sphere of the household to the wider networks of social life.

Family

The chapter introduces the family as the fundamental and most ancient unit of any society, noting that nearly all people live within one. It distinguishes between two principal types found in Indian society today. A joint family brings together several generations under one roof — grandparents, parents, uncles, aunts, brothers, sisters and cousins. A nuclear family, by contrast, consists of a couple and their children, or sometimes a single parent with children. A student activity invites learners to observe the types of families in their own neighbourhood, count households of each type, and discuss findings with classmates to identify which type is more frequent and why.

The chapter then draws attention to the richness of Indian languages in naming family relationships. While English has relatively few terms for relatives, Hindi offers words such as bua, tau, tai, chacha, mausi, nana, and nani, and Tamil distinguishes elder and younger siblings with separate terms. Notably, most Indian languages have no separate word for "cousin" — cousins are simply called brothers and sisters — which the chapter uses to illustrate the depth of bonds among all children in a family. A student activity asks learners to list family members, record the terms used in their mother tongue or regional language, and find English equivalents, observing how a single Indian-language word often requires several English words to describe precisely.

Roles and Responsibilities

This section establishes that family relationships are grounded in love, care, cooperation and interdependence. Each member carries roles and responsibilities towards the others. Parents bear the primary responsibility of raising children into happy individuals and responsible members of society. As children grow, they take on household responsibilities and, through daily participation in domestic life, learn to contribute to the family. The chapter also notes that children absorb traditions and practices passed down across generations within their family.

The section links family life to broader Indian cultural values. It states that following one's dharma — doing one's duty — is a central principle of Indian culture, and that the family functions as a kind of school where children learn values such as ahimsa, dāna (giving), sevā (service) and tyāga (sacrifice). Individuals in a family often subordinate personal needs to the family's collective needs. Two narrative vignettes illustrate these ideas in contrasting regional settings.

The first story features Shalini, a girl from a town in Kerala, who lives in a joint family. Her uncle has lost his job, and when the family shops for new clothes ahead of the Onam festival, Shalini's parents choose to buy clothes for the financially stressed uncle, aunt and cousin as well. As a result, Shalini receives a plain cotton dress instead of the silk one she had hoped for. Her grandmother explains that this is how families support and share with one another, and Shalini accepts the outcome contentedly. A student activity based on this story asks learners to draw the family tree of seven members and reflect on why Shalini's parents acted as they did.

The second story features Tenzing, a boy from a village in Meghalaya. His mother's involvement in a local handicraft cooperative prompted his father to take on cleaning, gardening, cooking alongside the grandmother, and other household chores — demonstrating that domestic responsibilities can be shared across gender lines. His grandmother is described as a storyteller of wisdom and humour, while his grandfather helps with homework, accompanies Tenzing to the school bus, and participates actively in community social work, including registering complaints during power failures and collecting funds for storm-damaged neighbours. Financial decisions in the family are made jointly between the parents, with the mother emphasising the importance of saving for unexpected future needs. A "Think About It" box asks students to reflect on the father's consultation with his wife, his participation in household chores, and the grandparents' multiple roles. Student activities following both stories invite learners to create their own stories or dramas about families, and to consider why some modern couples opt for nuclear family living.

Community

The chapter broadens its focus from the family to the community, defined here as a group of connected people and families. It emphasises that community is a flexible concept with many contexts and meanings. Members of a community come together to celebrate festivals, organise feasts, weddings and events. In rural and agricultural settings, people cooperate in land preparation, sowing and harvesting. Over time, communities have developed shared practices — informal "rules," rarely written down — governing access to natural resources such as water, grazing lands and forest produce, particularly among tribal communities. These practices ensure collective access to resources but also impose specific duties on each member.

A "Don't Miss Out" feature highlights two real-life examples of community action. The Bhil community of the Jhabua region in Madhya Pradesh used their halma tradition — coming together to serve any individual or family in crisis — to address a severe water shortage: they planted thousands of trees and built water-harvesting structures across hundreds of villages without payment, as a duty to community and environment. A separate illustration shows the halma practice through photographs. During the Chennai floods of 2015, private groups and spiritual and religious organisations cooked and distributed food to people cut off by the flooding, illustrating spontaneous community solidarity in an urban crisis.

The chapter then presents an urban community example. In Ahmedabad, Kamal Parmar, owner of a small auto-fabrication workshop, began offering free evening tuition to underprivileged street children — both dropouts and those who had never attended school — serving up to 150 children nightly and providing free dinners. Local school teachers and older student volunteers eventually joined the effort. The story is used to prompt classroom discussion about community attitudes, the values it reflects, and the question of whether society has been unfair to underprivileged children.

New forms of community are also noted. Residents' Welfare Associations in urban areas create their own internal rules on waste management, cleanliness of common areas and pet care, with community members participating in rule-making. The chapter stresses that communities are ultimately interdependent — a Residents' Association depends on trading communities for supplies and on municipal workers for waste handling. A student activity asks learners to list all the people outside their family who support them through their work, illustrating this web of interdependence. The section closes by showing the breadth of the community concept: a jāti or subdivision, a religious or regional group, a professional group, or even a school club such as National Service Scheme, National Cadet Corps, or a science or drama club can each constitute a community.
