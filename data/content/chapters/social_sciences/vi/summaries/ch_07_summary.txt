Chapter 7: India's Cultural Roots

The chapter opens with a metaphor of Indian culture as an ancient tree with many roots and many branches, all united by a common trunk. It introduces the idea that hundreds of schools of thought emerged in India over millennia, and that understanding a few early ones can help students understand 'India, that is Bharat' better. Some of India's cultural roots are traced back to the Indus or Sindhu-Sarasvatī civilisation.

The Vedas and Vedic Culture

a. What are the Vedas?

The word "Veda" derives from the Sanskrit root vid, meaning 'knowledge'. There are four Vedas — the Rig Veda, the Yajur Veda, the Sāma Veda, and the Atharva Veda — making them among the most ancient texts in the world. The Vedas consist of thousands of hymns composed in an early form of Sanskrit by rishis (male seers) and rishikas (female seers), addressed in poetical form to many deities such as Indra, Agni, Varuṇa, Mitra, Sarasvatī, and Uṣhas. These hymns were composed in the Sapta Sindhava region and transmitted entirely through oral recitation, not writing — a tradition so meticulous that UNESCO recognised Vedic chanting in 2008 as 'a masterpiece of the oral and intangible heritage of humanity'. The early rishis saw all deities as one, expressed in the famous line ekam sat viprā bahudhā vadanti ("The Existent is one, but sages give it many names"). Central values in this worldview included Truth, and the last mantras of the Rig Veda call for unity among people.

b. Vedic society

Early Vedic society was organised into janas or clans — the Rig Veda alone names over thirty, including the Bharatas, Purus, Kurus, Yadus, and Turvaśhas. Each clan was associated with a specific region in the northwest of the Subcontinent. Governance structures are known only through clues in the Vedas: words like rājā (king or ruler), sabhā, and samiti (both referring to collective assemblies) suggest some form of community decision-making. The texts also mention a wide range of professions, including agriculturist, weaver, potter, builder, carpenter, healer, dancer, barber, and priest, indicating a diverse and organised society. The chapter includes a student activity (LET'S EXPLORE) inviting reflection on societies where leaders are chosen by the people.

c. Vedic schools of thought

Vedic culture developed complex rituals known as yajña (offerings, often directed to Agni, the fire deity) for individual and collective benefit. Over time these rituals grew increasingly elaborate. A group of texts called the Upaniṣhads built on Vedic foundations and introduced concepts such as rebirth and karma. The school of thought known as Vedanta held that everything — human life, nature, and the universe — shares one divine essence called brahman, expressed in mantras such as aham brahmāsmi ("I am brahman") and tat tvam asi ("You are That"). The Upaniṣhads also introduced ātman (the Self), the divine essence in every being that is ultimately one with brahman, leading to the insight that all things in the world are connected and interdependent. Two Upaniṣhadic stories illustrate these ideas: the story of Śhvetaketu and the seed of reality (Chhāndogya Upaniṣhad), in which Rishi Uddālaka teaches his son that brahman, though invisible, pervades everything; and the story of Nachiketa (Katha Upaniṣhad), in which a young boy questions Yama, the god of death, and learns that the ātman is immortal. A third story, the debate of Gārgī and Yājñavalkya (Bṛihadāraṇyaka Upaniṣhad), highlights the role of women as philosophical questioners in Vedic tradition. Early in the 1st millennium BCE, Yoga also emerged as a school intended to achieve the realisation of brahman in one's consciousness. Together, these schools became the foundations of what is today called Hinduism.

Buddhism

Buddhism is one of the schools of thought that did not accept the authority of the Vedas and developed its own system. Prince Siddhārtha Gautama was born in Lumbini (present-day Nepal), approximately around 560 BCE. Growing up in a palace, he first encountered old age, sickness, and death at the age of 29, and was also struck by the peaceful bearing of an ascetic. He renounced his palace life and, after years of wandering and meditation under a pipal tree at Bodh Gaya (Bihar), attained enlightenment. He realised that avidyā (ignorance) and attachment are the root causes of human suffering. Henceforth known as the 'Buddha' (the enlightened or awakened one), he began teaching ahimsa (non-hurting or non-violence) and sincere inner discipline. He founded the Sangha — a community of bhikṣhus (monks) and later bhikṣhuṇīs (nuns) — to practise and spread his teachings. The Jātaka tales, which narrate the Buddha's former births, also convey Buddhist values; a well-known tale in this chapter features the monkey-king who sacrifices his life to save his troop, prompting a human king to reflect on a ruler's duty to his subjects. The Buddha's influence on India and Asia was enormous and remains perceptible today.

Jainism

Jainism is another major school that became widespread around the same time as Buddhism, though its roots are held to be far more ancient. Prince Vardhamāna, born near Vaiśhālī (modern Bihar) in the early 6th century BCE, left his home at age 30 in search of spiritual knowledge. After 12 years of ascetic discipline he attained supreme wisdom and became known as 'Mahāvīra' ('great hero'). The word 'Jain' derives from jina, meaning 'conqueror' — the conquest of ignorance and attachment rather than of territory. Jain teachings centre on three key ideas: ahimsa (non-hurting of all living beings, including in thought), anekāntavāda (the truth has many aspects and no single statement can fully capture it), and aparigraha (non-possession, limiting oneself to what is truly necessary). Jainism also insists on the interconnectedness of all living creatures, from humans to invisible organisms. A Jain story in the chapter follows Rohineya, a skilled burglar who, upon hearing a fragment of Mahāvīra's sermon, is moved to confess his crimes and become a monk — illustrating the importance of right action, right thinking, and the possibility of redemption. Monks and nuns of both Buddhism and Jainism travelled widely, establishing monasteries and living in rock-cut caves; archaeological evidence of these communities survives across India. The chapter also notes the Chārvāka (or Lokāyata) school, a materialist school that believed only the physical world exists and that disappeared over time, as an illustration of India's wide diversity of intellectual and spiritual traditions.

Folk and Tribal Roots

The chapter argues that India's cultural heritage is not only what is preserved in texts. India has rich oral traditions — teachings and practices transmitted through everyday life without written records. Among these are folk traditions (transmitted by common people) and tribal traditions (transmitted by tribes). The chapter defines a tribe as a group of families or clans sharing common descent, culture, and language, living as a close-knit community under a chief and holding no private property. Ancient India had no separate word for 'tribe' — tribes were simply different janas living in specific environments such as forests or mountains. Officially, in 2011 India had 705 tribes with a combined population of about 104 million.

What is a tribe?

This subsection offers formal definitions and demographic data on tribes, noting that earlier anthropological descriptions of tribes as 'primitive' have been largely abandoned with deeper study. The section emphasises that there has been constant, mutual interaction between folk and tribal traditions and the major schools of thought. Deities, concepts, legends, and rituals have been freely exchanged. For instance, Jagannath of Puri (Odisha) is believed to have originally been a tribal deity, while some tribes adopted Hindu deities and developed their own versions of the Mahābhārata and Rāmāyaṇa. The reason for this natural interaction is that folk, tribal, and Hindu belief systems share many concepts: all three regard elements of nature — mountains, rivers, trees, animals — as sacred because consciousness underlies them. The Toda tribals of the Nilgiris (Tamil Nadu), for example, consider over thirty peaks as residences of gods or goddesses. Many tribal groups also hold a concept of a supreme being: tribes of Arunachal Pradesh worship Donyipolo (a combined Sun-Moon deity), while Munda and Santhal tribals of eastern India worship Singbonga, the creator of the world. The Indian sociologist André Béteille is quoted to underline that this interaction has been mutual — tribal religions and Hinduism have each shaped the other throughout history. The chapter concludes that folk and tribal beliefs and practices are therefore integral among India's cultural roots.
