Chapter 11: Grassroots Democracy – Part 2: Local Government in Rural Areas

Panchayati Raj System

India's vast size and diversity — with approximately 600,000 villages and nearly two-thirds of its 1.4 billion people living in rural areas — makes it impractical for local issues to be resolved at the State or national level. To address this, each village has a system of local government called a Panchayat, or village council. The Panchayat system, also known as Panchayati Raj, is a form of self-government that brings governance closer to the people and enables active participation in decision-making. The Panchayati Raj system operates at three levels — village, block, and district — known as the three-tier system. Together, these institutions cover responsibilities spanning agriculture, housing, road maintenance, water resource management, education, health care, social welfare, and cultural activities.

Gram Panchayat

The Gram Panchayat is the base tier of the Panchayati Raj system and is closest to the people in rural areas. Its members are elected by the Gram Sabha, which comprises all adults from a village or group of neighbouring villages who are enrolled as voters. The Gram Sabha is the platform where women and men discuss matters affecting their area and take collective decisions. Each Gram Panchayat elects a head or president called the Sarpanch or Pradhan, and in recent years more women have taken on this role. The Gram Panchayat is supported administratively by a Panchayat Secretary, who organises meetings and maintains records, and in many parts of India by an officer called a Patwari, who maintains land records and sometimes preserves maps that are generations old. The chapter includes a "Think About It" prompt asking how such old maps might help us understand the past and the present.

Exemplary Sarpanchs

This section highlights real-life examples of Sarpanchs who have made a significant difference in their villages. Dnyaneshwar Kamble, a transgender person, was elected Sarpanch of Tarangfal village in Maharashtra's Solapur district in 2017, with the motto lok seva, gram seva (service to the village is service to the public). Vandana Bahadur Maida, a member of the Bhil community from Khankhandvi village in Madhya Pradesh, became the first female Sarpanch of her village, mobilised women to attend Sabha meetings, and addressed education and sanitation issues. Popatrao Baguji Pawar transformed Hiware Bazar, a drought-affected village in Maharashtra's Ahmednagar district, by implementing rainwater harvesting, watershed conservation, and tree planting — turning it into a prosperous green village; he was awarded the Padma Shri in 2020. These examples illustrate how committed and inclusive leadership at the grassroots level can bring about concrete change.

Child-Friendly Panchayat Initiative

Panchayats are meant to include the voices of all community members, including children. The Child-Friendly Panchayat Initiative creates formal opportunities for children to express ideas and opinions on matters affecting their wellbeing. Several states have encouraged children's participation through Bal Sabhas and Bal Panchayats, where village elders respond to their concerns. In Maharashtra, Bal Panchayats have worked to eliminate child labour and child marriage, successfully bringing children back to school by convincing parents and other adults. The Sangkhu Radhu Khandu Gram Panchayat in west Sikkim has been recognised for building school compound walls for safety and constructing school kitchens for hygienic midday meals, earning the designation of child-friendly Gram Panchayat. The section also describes the Children's Parliament, an initiative of Bunker Roy's Barefoot College in Rajasthan, which engaged children aged 8 to 14 in democratic governance processes through night schools and parliament-style elections, enabling them to address issues such as education, sanitation, and social equality; this initiative won the World's Children's Honorary Award in 2001. A student activity — "Let's Explore" — invites the class to role-play a Bal Panchayat and Gram Sabha.

Panchayat Samiti and Zila Parishad

Above the Gram Panchayat are two higher tiers in the Panchayati Raj system. The Panchayat Samiti operates at the block level and serves as the link between Gram Panchayats and the Zila Parishad at the district level. Members of these institutions are elected by local people, and may also include Sarpanchs of nearby villages and members of the State Legislative Assembly. Although the composition of Panchayat Samitis varies by State, their core function is to strengthen local participation and coordinate development across Gram Panchayats — for example, by consolidating development plans from all Gram Panchayats and forwarding them to district or State authorities for funding, including through schemes like the Pradhan Mantri Gram Sadak Yojana for rural road construction. At all three tiers, provisions exist to ensure that disadvantaged sections of society can voice their needs, and at least one-third of seats at each level are reserved for women. The chapter notes that while the structure and specific functions of Panchayati Raj institutions vary across States (since States hold authority over these institutions), their shared objective is to enable villagers to actively participate in the management and development of their local areas. The section also references the Arthashastra, written by Kautilya (Chanakya) approximately 2,300 years ago, which described a tiered administrative system from village to regional capital — drawing a parallel with the modern three-tier Panchayati Raj structure and noting how similar governance thinking existed in ancient India.
