Chapter 12: Grassroots Democracy – Part 3 Local Government in Urban Areas

Introduction

This chapter builds on the earlier discussion of rural governance by extending the concept of participatory democracy to urban areas. Good governance in a democracy, whether at the rural, regional, urban, state, or national level, aims to empower citizens so they can actively participate in their country's functioning. Urban areas are generally more complex and diverse than rural areas — cities like Kolkata, Chennai, or Mumbai contain numerous communities with varied needs — which is why the governance structures in cities tend to be more elaborate than in villages. The chapter presents a pyramid diagram (Fig. 12.2) to illustrate the full Indian governance system, from the local level at the base to the national level at the top. The left side of the pyramid covers rural governance through Panchayati Raj Institutions (Gram Sabha, Gram Panchayat, Panchayat Samiti, Zila Panchayat), while the right side, the focus of this chapter, covers urban governance through Urban Local Bodies.

Urban Local Bodies

Local government structures in urban areas are collectively called 'urban local bodies'. These bodies are decentralised — rather than operating under a single central authority, local communities have a direct say in how their areas are managed. This decentralised structure is a mechanism for citizens living in an area to come together and make decisions about what is best for them, making it a concrete expression of participatory democracy.

Cities and towns are divided into smaller administrative units called 'wards'. Ward committees facilitate local activities such as health camps and campaigns against single-use plastics. They also monitor problems in their area — a water leak, a blocked drain, a damaged road — and report them to the relevant authorities. The precise functioning of wards varies from State to State based on the rules each State makes.

Urban local bodies as a whole are responsible for a wide range of functions: maintaining infrastructure, managing burial grounds, garbage collection and disposal, checking the implementation of government schemes, and collecting local taxes and fines. They also play a role in planning for the economic and social development of the area. However, for urban local bodies to function efficiently, citizens themselves must also fulfil their responsibilities — for instance, following waste segregation instructions makes garbage collection easier, and reporting a street water leakage promptly prevents wastage of a precious resource. This reciprocal relationship between the governing body and citizens is central to the idea of participatory democracy. The chapter contains a student activity asking learners to identify four or five actions responsible citizens might take to help their city area.

The chapter notes that the Madras Corporation (now Greater Chennai Corporation), established on 29 September 1688, is the oldest municipal institution in India. It was created by a charter of the East India Company constituting the town of Fort St. George and all territories within 16 km of the Fort as a corporation; a Parliamentary Act of 1792 gave it power to levy municipal taxes, marking the proper start of municipal administration. Indore's Municipal Corporation is presented as a contemporary example through a diagram of its services (Fig. 12.4), which include property tax, water charges, solid waste management, business and trade licenses, fire services, grievance redressal (Citizen Relationship Management), and on-request services such as water tankers, funeral vans, and ambulances. Indore has also been awarded the title of India's cleanest city under the Swachh Survekshan scheme for seven consecutive years, illustrating the role citizen participation plays in civic achievements.

Urban local bodies are classified by the population size of their city or town. Cities with a population above 10 lakh have a Municipal Corporation (also called Mahanagar Nigam) as their highest body. Cities with populations between 1 lakh and 10 lakh are governed by a Municipal Council (also called Nagar Palika). Smaller cities and towns have a Nagar Panchayat. This three-tier classification ensures that the scale and capacity of the governing body matches the complexity and population of the urban settlement it serves. Students are invited to investigate which type of body governs cities in their own and neighbouring States, and also to explore how urban local bodies fund their activities — the range of paid services listed under the Indore Municipal Corporation (water tanker hire, auditorium booking, ambulance services) suggests that a mix of tax revenue and service charges sustains their operations.

A dialogue between two students — Anita, who lives in a city, and Sameer, who lives in a village — illustrates the similarities and differences between urban and rural governance. Sameer observes that the village panchayat allows more direct community participation because people know each other and can raise issues easily, with even children being heard. Anita acknowledges that urban bodies also have elected representatives who represent citizens' interests, and that a sense of community persists in cities, as shown when neighbours collectively helped clear rubble after a house collapsed during heavy rains. Both students conclude that democracy functions on the same underlying principle in both settings — every citizen's voice matters — even though the scale and complexity differ.
