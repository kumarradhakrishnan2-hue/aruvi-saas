Chapter 8: Unity in Diversity, or 'Many in the One'

A Rich Diversity

India is home to over 1.4 billion people — approximately 18 percent of the world's population — and its diversity is immediately apparent to anyone who travels through the country. Changing landscapes, different styles of dress, varied foods, numerous languages, and distinct scripts are all encountered on even a short journey. Within any single region, one can meet people from other parts of India with entirely different customs and traditions. In the late 20th century, the Anthropological Survey of India conducted a sweeping national survey called the 'People of India project' that covered 4,635 communities across all states and counted 325 languages written in 25 scripts; it also noted that many Indians live away from their birthplaces and original communities, making internal migration a defining feature of Indian society. The chapter introduces the central question that this diversity raises: how can such a bewildering variety of peoples and practices share a common identity? British historian Vincent Smith framed the same puzzle over a century ago, and his answer — that India offers unity in diversity — sets up the organising idea that the chapter then explores through several concrete dimensions of Indian life. The section contains a student activity inviting the class to map classmates' birthplaces and mother tongues.

Food for All

The section examines food as one of the most visible expressions of both India's diversity and its underlying unity. Thousands of dishes and preparations vary across regions, yet a core set of staple grains is consumed almost everywhere in the country: cereals such as rice, barley, and wheat; millets including pearl millet (bajra), sorghum (jowar), and finger millet (ragi); and pulses such as various kinds of dals, rajma, and grams. A map illustrating the distribution of cereals and pulses across different regions (Fig. 8.1) makes the point visually. Common spices — turmeric, cumin, cardamom, and ginger — are likewise used throughout the subcontinent. The section's key insight is captured in a simple formula: the same ingredients (unity) can be combined in countless ways (diversity) to produce an enormous variety of dishes. This makes food a practical demonstration of 'Many in the One'. The section contains student activities asking children to list household ingredients and to explore the different dishes that can be made from a single vegetable.

Textiles and Clothing

Every region and community in India has evolved its own styles of clothing, yet shared forms of dress reveal an underlying unity. The sari — a plain, unstitched length of cloth — is worn across most of India regardless of region, and is made from a wide range of fabrics, including cotton, silk, and synthetic materials. Famous varieties include Banarasi, Kanjivaram, Paithani, Patan Patola, Muga, and Mysore silks, alongside numerous cotton types. The cloth is produced using different weaving methods and dyeing or printing techniques, resulting in endless variation in colour and design; some designs are woven into the cloth, while others are printed on after weaving. The chapter notes that the sari has a long history, supported by a stone relief from Vaiśhali dating back a few centuries BCE (Fig. 8.2). The draping style varies significantly from one region or community to another, and women have also put the sari to a wide range of practical uses beyond clothing, illustrated by a set of photographs from south India (Fig. 8.4). A 'Don't Miss Out' box highlights that for a very long time India produced the finest cotton in the world, with a printed variety called 'chintz' becoming so popular in 17th-century Europe that England and France eventually banned its import to protect their own textile industries. The section contains student activities asking students to reflect on the sari as a symbol of unity in diversity and to explore similar patterns in the dhoti.

Festivals Galore

India's festival calendar is extraordinarily varied, yet the chapter draws attention to a unifying pattern: several major festivals are celebrated across the country at roughly the same time, even though they carry different names in different regions. The primary example given is Makara Sankrānti, which marks the beginning of the harvest season and falls on or around 14 January in many parts of India. A map (Fig. 8.5) shows how the same celestial event is celebrated under different regional names — Pongal in Tamil Nadu, Uttarayan in Gujarat, Maghi or Lohri in Punjab, Magh Bihu in Assam, Khichdi Parv in parts of north India, Pedda Panduga in Andhra Pradesh, Poush Songkranti in Bengal, Shishur Saenkraat in Kashmir, and several other local names elsewhere. Despite the different names, special foods, rituals, and the spirit of thanksgiving for the harvest connect these celebrations. This convergence of festivals around shared agricultural and seasonal rhythms is presented as another illustration of unity underlying diversity. The section contains student activities asking children to describe their own favourite festival and to list the major October–November festivals and their regional variants.

An Epic Spread

Literature provides the chapter's most detailed example of unity in diversity. Indian literary traditions are among the most abundant in the world and are written in a great many languages, yet over centuries they have shared important themes and concerns. The Pañchatantra — a Sanskrit collection of animal fables at least 2,200 years old that teaches important life skills — has been adapted in almost every Indian language and has also spread to Southeast Asia, the Arab world, and Europe, generating an estimated 200 adaptations in more than 50 languages, demonstrating how a single 'One' collection became 'Many'. The most striking examples, however, are India's two great epics, the Rāmāyaṇa and the Mahābhārata. These Sanskrit poems, which together run to some 7,000 pages in their original versions, narrate the stories of heroes fighting to re-establish dharma: in the Mahābhārata the Pāṇḍavas, aided by Kṛiṣhṇa, fight their cousins the Kauravas to recover their kingdom; in the Rāmāyaṇa, Rāma with his brother Lakṣhmaṇa and Hanuman defeats the demon Rāvaṇa who had kidnapped Sītā. For more than two millennia these epics have been translated or adapted into regional languages across India and beyond, and countless folk versions exist alongside the literary ones — a scholar's survey in Tamil Nadu alone counted about a hundred folklore versions of the Mahābhārata. Tribal communities across India — including the Bhils, Gonds, and Mundas, as well as tribes of the northeast and Himalayan regions including Kashmir — have their own oral versions of one or both epics, along with legends connecting their own history to the epic heroes; the Irula tribals of the Nilgiris, for example, maintain a shrine to the five Pāṇḍava brothers (Fig. 8.7). Anthropologist K.S. Singh, who directed the 'People of India' project, observed that there is hardly a place in India that the Pāṇḍavas did not visit according to folklore. Over the centuries these two epics created a dense web of cultural interactions across India and much of Asia, serving as perhaps the most powerful vehicle through which the 'Many' of India's communities have remained connected to a shared 'One'. The section closes by noting that classical arts and architecture offer further illustrations of the same principle, and that Indian culture ultimately celebrates diversity as an enrichment while never losing sight of the underlying unity that nourishes it. The section contains student activities around identifying a painting depicting a Rāmāyaṇa episode (Fig. 8.6).
