Section 5.2 · Prime Numbers

Rectangular Arrays and the Sieve: Who Are the Primes?

40 min · Inductive
Teacher notes

Recap GCF/LCM from the previous two periods — knowing factors deeply requires knowing which numbers are prime. The most common error is treating 1 as prime; address it explicitly when defining primes as having exactly two factors. Early finishers may self-study Figure it Out Q10, p.115.

Materials
  • 1–100 number grid (one per student)
  • Board grid for Sieve demonstration
  • Textbook pages 112–115
In class
8min

Narrate the Guna (12 figs, many rectangles) vs. Anshu (7 figs, one rectangle) story from section 5.2. Students try arranging 11, 12, 13, 15 objects into rectangles, observing which numbers allow only one arrangement — inductively surfacing the idea of a prime number.

14min

Walk the Sieve of Eratosthenes step by step on a 1–100 board grid: circle 2, strike multiples; circle 3, strike; continue. Students work their own grids; the Math Talk prompt (“why does this procedure work?”) is posed mid-activity for a 2-minute pair discussion.

11min

Students answer Figure it Out Q1, Q4 & Q9, p.114 — even primes other than 2; which of 23, 51, 37, 26 are prime; true/false on primes and composites. Review as a class, clarifying that 1 is neither prime nor composite.

7min

Introduce twin primes via Figure it Out Q8, p.114 — find all twin prime pairs between 1 and 100 — as a short exploration using the completed sieve.

Homework

Find seven consecutive composite numbers between 1 and 100.
